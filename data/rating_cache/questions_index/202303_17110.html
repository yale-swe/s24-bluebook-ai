\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1708625\n 1708625\n \n 1708625\n Solid foundation to continuum mechanics with a little bit of statistical mechanics toward the end. Learned to derive the constitutive relationship for an H.I.E. (homogeneous, isotropic, elastic) material and apply it to word problems; use of Navier-Stokes equation to characterize different kinds of fluid flows (Poiseuille, Couette, and plug flow); laminar and turbulent flows; viscoelastic models (Maxwell, Voigt and three-element); derivation of stress-strain relationships for freely jointed chains; basic principles governing thermodynamics and statistical mechanics. Also learned that hydrophobic interactions are really important and ought to be your favorite intermolecular force!) Pro tip: he does a lot of complicated (and maybe unnecessary) derivations of these equations, so try not to get too bogged down in the math and just remember the bigger picture: for any kind of material (elastic solid, fluid, viscoelastic) you're always trying to find a relationship between stress and strain. That's literally it. Remember that and things will make sense for you.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1708625\n 1708625\n \n 1708625\n This is an unpopular view but Professor Murrell has really improved his teaching method over the six or so years since he started teaching this course. He explains the fundamentals very clearly and emphasizes mastery of basic concepts (ex. Hooke's Law as a mathematical statement of the relationship between stress and strain, meaning of homogeneous/isotropic/elastic assumptions about a material) as a way of gaining understanding of the more challenging applications (ex. he explains how deriving the force-strain relationship for a freely-jointed chain takes you all the way back to Hooke's Law for an elastic solid.) He outlines very clearly what kinds of word problems will be asked on exams, most of which he solves step by step in class. Not sure if he will continue this in a future iteration of this course but for us at least there were opportunities for extra credit to boost everyone's scores. As long as you pay attention, ask questions in class and keep up with the optional homeworks, you will do very well. I do think, however, that having only three exams count for the semester grade places a lot of pressure on students to do well on each of them--I don't think I've ever studied so intensely for a single exam in my time at Yale as I have in 353--so I hope that the problem sets/class quizzes will count for at least part of the grade next year. This class is also quite inaccessible in terms of resources (notes and slides are not posted on Canvas, and lectures are not recorded) so you really, really need to attend every single lecture if you want to do well. All you need to know for the course comes straight out of Murrell's handwritten notes, so take notes copiously and diligently, and don't be afraid to ask for clarification if you don't understand something. Best of luck.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1723949\n 1723949\n \n 1723949\n A strength of this course was that the set of concepts which were explored were pretty interesting.
