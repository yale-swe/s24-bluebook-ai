I became a much better exegete.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1710208\n 1710208\n \n 1710208\n Deep read through several Greek tragedies. Really got to know the Greek tragedians and ancient Athenian history better, as well as attitudes toward tragedy and how to read a tragedy.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1711695\n 1711695\n \n 1711695\n A great survey of classic Greek plays. I definitely acquired a strong base knowledge of the works of Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides that I had not interacted with before.

We discussed the ontological and political values in each Tragedy as well as the similarities and differences between the Tragedians and their separate attitude toward Greek society. \r\nThe course especially furthered my understanding of the relationship between philosophy and literature.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1713423\n 1713423\n \n 1713423\n I became a much better writer through practice, read some beautiful tragedy, and gained insight into an art that has tragically been lost in modern times. I want to read more tragedy after this class because I found it rich in its ethical lessons, in the way it moves me emotionally, and in the way it shapes my ethical disposition.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1714999\n 1714999\n \n 1714999\n Read a selection of tragedies, and considered the reception of tragedy in comedy and philosophy. Learned how to read tragedies closely and to discuss these texts as works of political theory.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1716019\n 1716019\n \n 1716019\n Learned close reading, interpretation of Ancient Greek plays, and a better understanding of the relationship between philosophy and poetry in antiquity
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1722560\n 1722560\n \n 1722560\n Beyond the content---reading and discussing Greek tragedy and comedy---this class was an intellectual gauntlet. Was constantly pushed to think of new interpretations, consult secondary literature, sift through the texts for evidence, prepare strong defenses of my interpretations and arguments against others'. Student quality in the seminar was otherworldly. Like the best humanities classes, you got the sense that everyone could end up being a wildly successful lawyer---yet, for the moment, we were all here, talking about a bunch of plays, trying to wrench meaning out of them. People would twist the texts this way and that with their interpretations. Everyone, most of all Professor Schillinger, was such a compelling speaker, reader, and writer that I'd often leave seminar more confused about a text's meaning than I had been when I came in. But confused in a more profound way. It's a brilliant course.

RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1702536\n 1702536\n \n 1702536\n S: I love the workload, one play a week. It gives you a chance to actually dive into the story, and is very manageable. The professor was very knowledgable and offered great insight. He was always well prepared and I respect his perspective a lot. He also incorporated some other works (Aristotle, Nietzsche, Plato), that helped round out the course. \r\n\r\nW: I would have appreciated more class time devoted to some of the literary structure and poetry of the plays. We certainly focused on the plot, but I think I would have gained from learning more about the different meters in the poetry, including reading some of the original greek to see exactly what we were talking about. Also, I would have liked to watch more plays or see one in person.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1706276\n 1706276\n \n 1706276\n The syllabus is fabulous. Dan Schillinger is also fabulous. Discussions were generally very strong because the readings were assigned really perfectly. The discussion questions / introductions at the beginning of seminar from Prof. Schillinger were also terrific for framing discussion. The writing assignments were a particularly strong part of the course, as were office hours accessibility for them. I did some of my best work at Yale in writing for this class, and I think it was because the prompts (and OH) helped spark my intellectual creativity in a way that got me really excited about the work.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1710208\n 1710208\n \n 1710208\n Very strong discussions that moved along quickly and opened new insight into the tragedy. Great questions to consider. \r\nWeaknesses: never quite answered some of the big questions satisfactorily. What is the purpose of tragedy? Does it have a political purpose? Feel like there were a lot of answers but no big summary takeaway.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1711695\n 1711695\n \n 1711695\n Strengths: Professor Daniel Schillinger. He is incredibly dedicated to his preparation for class and prioritizes class engagement.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Only one seminar per week. Would have liked to meet twice since discussions were so engaging on each text! We often ran out of time at the end.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1713423\n 1713423\n \n 1713423\n Strengths: the course fosters discussion very well. There were no serious weaknesses I could think of.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1714999\n 1714999\n \n 1714999\n Strengths: insightful comments by the professor, thought-provoking discussions, good pacing for the readings, all of the texts were wonderful \r\nImprovements: although I loved reading one (usually) tragedy per week, it would've been great to read an interpretive piece (e.g., by Foucault, Sartre, Arendt) alongside it, since these thinkers were often mentioned but we did not read their actual views for class; perhaps it could be structured to meet twice a week, with the first class devoted to close-reading the tragedy and the second class to discussing the tragedy together with the interpretive piece. It would have been interesting to also supplement the readings with more historical context.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1716019\n 1716019\n \n 1716019\n Strengths: Prof. Schillinger came to every course well prepared and was very open to discussion. He also gave high-quality feedback on essays. Weaknesses: None that I can think of at the moment.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1722560\n 1722560\n \n 1722560\n Strengths: Some of the best students and one of the best Professors at Yale. And some of the most haunting, horrifying, perplexingly beautiful pieces of literature ever written. \r\nWeaknesses: I think the Nietzsche went over everyone's head. A good primer on Thucydides and Athens' imperial period would be useful historical context to have included. I'm not a fan of the once-a-week seminar format, but others may disagree.
