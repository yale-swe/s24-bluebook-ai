\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1662592\n 1662592\n \n 1662592\n Strengths: The breadth of topics and literature covered is great. Strongest part of the course is definitely the material and Professor Englishs decades of experience in the subject matter. Feedback on short papers was always lengthy and extremely helpful. Its sufficiently but not overwhelmingly rigorous.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Lectures were rather bland and dry, though informative; sometimes, they only scratch the surface of what's needed to succeed in exams/papers. Grading felt slightly inconsistent, with somewhat arbitrary score assignments (e.g., 120 total points for a single essay, with points docked off here and there without clear explanation). Splitting the course with problem sets in the first half and short papers in the second half was slightly odd. Final exam is difficult, as it effectively requires a short paper to be written in the allotted time. Students are sort of left in a free-for-all, with those that have more background at an advantage; minimal guidance given as to the depth/breadth to which we need to understand all nuances of the topics for papers and exam (basically asks us to just know everything about everything, which is a lot).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1669708\n 1669708\n \n 1669708\n Overall a solid class from a solid professor who is very knowledgeable in the realm of central banking and monetary policy from his time at the Fed; however, the 2-week successive deadlines for the 3 papers were a bit of a time crunch, especially as it somewhat meddled with spring break, though you could space your time out very well or it may be nice to space them out a bit further for next iteration of the class. Studying for the final was overwhelming as the scope of the topics was all the lectures and readings, so make sure you have enough time to dedicate to this class. But overall, if you're interested in monetary policy, this is a very informative class that makes the material feel truly relevant with consistent references to current and historical events.

You learn about how to think of a dynamic economy with choice simplifications, and from there what effects monetary policy will have in this model economy. This informs the discussion of actual policy implemented, particularly with regards to the Financial Crisis and the Covid-19 pandemic. \r\n\r\nAdditionally, this class is a WR class and is useful for Econ majors who have yet to complete this distributional requirement. There are three papers that task you to do some form of synthesis of research and economic analysis with relation to a topic covered in class. Each are limited to 5 pages in length. I personally didnt find the experience of writing these papers to be particularly insightful or useful, but I can get behind the idea that written communication is critical to monetary policy, and thus it makes sense. Consider this as you decide on this class.
