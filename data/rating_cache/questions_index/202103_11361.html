RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1522373\n 1522373\n \n 1522373\n I think the course was really effective in the points it tried to get across, and by the end what Rogers said was true: we learned a bunch of different ways to think about oil and not necessarily one large "oil is great" or "oil is the enemy" narrative. I really appreciated the complexities of the material. And I really appreciated how it wasn't just the professor constantly talking, but that everyone felt they could contribute and said really really interesting things--I feel like I learned from my classmates as much as I did from Rogers.\r\n\r\nI'd say that it'd be ideal if the midterm wasn't a separate bundle of readings, though. It was assigned ahead of time, but I spent so long going through the readings that when it came down to it I had significantly less time to conceive of and write my midterm paper.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539598\n 1539598\n \n 1539598\n I really enjoyed the diverse selection of texts for each week's topic that allowed us to have multiple perspectives and ultimately nuanced discussions on the subject.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1543667\n 1543667\n \n 1543667\n A major strength is the reading list. Although there is a lot of reading, you don't have to read all of it cover-to-cover because you aren't assessed on how well you know all the material. Instead, I think it is meant to give you a toolbox of different ways to think about oil to apply to new situations in the midterm and final essays. However, this contributes to a drawback of the class which is that the reading list might be too much. It's tough to know which parts of the readings are important, and I feel like I wasted some time trying to unpack peripheral stuff before realizing that wasn't the point of the piece. I really appreciated Prof. Roger's reading guides for certain readings to help me focus my time and I hope he can do that for more of the readings the next time around. I know that several classmates and I showed up to class sleep-deprived because there was just so much reading to do for class. Also, I can't believe we didn't cover oil and climate change! Another strength is the class is a true seminar. Every class, we just get together, talk about the readings, and develop our understanding of them together under Prof. Roger's guidance. Prof. Rogers gives students the space to talk for the most of the class, but he was certainly present to provide insight. He was a great seminar leader.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1550559\n 1550559\n \n 1550559\n Strengths are definitely the readings that are well crafted for discussing oil/capitalism. Some of those books were really really interesting, and getting to take another class with Prof Rogers is always a plus.\r\n\r\nA weakness was that at times, the direction of conversations or the overall goals from each class felt unclear. There was so much reading to get through, that a lot of it went un(der)discussed and what we did discuss sometimes felt incomplete. I'd say if we could have more clear "packaging" of the topics discussed it might lend itself to better conversations down the line. At the end of the class it felt like people were drawing from their own background knowledge or jargon to fill discussion time.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1551580\n 1551580\n \n 1551580\n One of the things I noticed throughout the semester is that Professor Rogers wants the students to drive the discussion. Often in class, he will allow a moment or two of silence to pass, waiting for someone to make a comment, rather than switching the conversation himself. I really enjoyed this, as it pushed us to think that little bit harder about the issue at hand. I enjoyed how much time we spent discussing the readings, and I loved the books that were assigned. As someone who sometimes struggles to get into books, it was refreshing to dive into these ethnographies. One weakness I observed came at the start of the semester. When we split into groups of two to analyze readings and summarize them to the class, the discussion was a bit dry. Making sure that the class isn't spent going in a circle and redoing the readings essentially may buy some more time to open new discussions.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1543667\n 1543667\n \n 1543667\n I would recommend it if you are passionate about anthropology, economics, politics, and/or oil/energy. I feel like this class presents oil in a unique way compared to environmental science or political science classes. It will help you critically think about these stories when they inevitably appear in your everyday life. We also learned about how oil's social, cultural, and political connections to concepts like freedom and the American way of life make it that much harder to eliminate from our energy mix. There are only two assignments, the midterm and final essays, and there is no homework besides doing the readings for class. However, the midterm and final essays are substantial and the readings are long, so I would just be mindful of that.
