\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1723004\n 1723004\n \n 1723004\n Strengths: \r\n- Great selection of philosophical works that enabled me to appreciate how Western philosophy developed over time\r\n- The lectures were consistently strong, and helped me much better understand the philosopher we were studying\r\n- Prof. Inwood's seminars gave mean effective introduction to the construction and analysis of philosophical arguments\r\nWeaknesses:\r\n- Prof. Inwood's feedback on essays sometimes was meager, especially compared to my other two first semester DS professors\r\n- Sometimes our class discussions strayed away from the texts, so we didn't get through all the material we read
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724181\n 1724181\n \n 1724181\n The strengths of DS Philosophy lay in the syllabus of works, the discussion-based format, the intimate classroom sizes, the professors, the guest lectures, and the essays. The syllabus of works is far-reaching, expansive, thoughtful, masterfully curated, significant, and enjoyable. Each reading built off the last in some way, and my class was always making connections between books we had read prior. The discussion-based format, in my opinion, was the best way for the class to function. The professor started by asking an open-ended question, usually on the context of the writing or author, and our answers would guide the discussion. Our classroom sizes were about fifteen students each. This was really great. Not only did I make some really amazing friends in my sections, but I also felt it gave everyone an equal chance to speak and share their opinions. The professors were amazing and nothing but helpful. This extends into our guest lectures. All were enthusiastic and filled with amazing information, allowing me to conceptualize our readings better. The essays were also a really great part of the course. Professor Inwood and my other DS professors would give us essay topics about two weeks before they were due, allowing us ample time to plan, outline, and write. The essay topics were expansive and thought-provoking, and we were also allowed to propose our own topics. Overall, I do not have any weaknesses on the part of the course that I consider. I think the classes would benefit from being longer. At the moment, they are one hour and fifteen minutes long. Sometimes, our discussions would be so interesting, but we would need more time to go deeper or address a certain topic. This is just a nitpick, however. I have no cons about the course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724610\n 1724610\n \n 1724610\n As in all DS courses, the strengths and weaknesses all tend to stem from the amount of content covered in a short time. The total page count for philosophy is probably a good bit shorter than in the other tracks, but the readings are a lot denser and require closer reading (sometimes even to understand what's going on at all). The course offers a very solid foundation in philosophical reasoning and a strong overview of the early developments in philosophy. Compared to the other DS courses, you feel like you are getting a more holistic understanding of everything you read (granted we skip a ton of important texts). Nonetheless, the main weakness is that you feel like you lose some detail with the sheer amount of information covered. Many of the late Hellenistic philosopher started to blend together just because we moved through them so fast (studying for the final helps you straighten things out in your head).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724643\n 1724643\n \n 1724643\n The strength of the course can be found in its teacher. Professor Inwood has the keenest intellect of any person I have met in my life. His powers of analysis and rhetoric greatly enhanced the learning experience. In terms of feedback, I think the class would have benefited from greater student participation in the discussions. I would attribute the lack of class-wide participation to the difficulty of some of the material. Perhaps, by beginning discussion on complex topics with a brief summary of important points, student participation could be enhanced.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1723004\n 1723004\n \n 1723004\n Yes. Of course, if you're taking DS, you'll have to take DS philosophy, and if you're not taking DS, you can't take it. But for what it's worth, this class provided me with a really solid introduction to the foundational ideas of classical Western philosophy. If you are taking DS, you do have some degree of choice about which professor you'll get. With that said, on the whole, I thought Prof. Inwood was a pretty effective instructor. He's incredibly knowledgeable about ancient philosophy, which is his area of expertise, and always provided satisfying answers to any questions I had, both in and outside of class. During some seminars, though, I think Prof. Inwood could have done a better job at managing class discussions. We didn't always get through all the readings, dwelling on some ideas at the expense of others. Especially during our Aristotle unit, seminars comprised Prof. Inwood lecturing for most of the class, with some Q&A tacked on. His explanations were very helpful and informative, to be sure, but the more lecture-heavy style of classes didn't always make for the most engaging seminar experience.
