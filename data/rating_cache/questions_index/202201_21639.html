\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1573609\n 1573609\n \n 1573609\n This was quite possibly my favorite class I have ever taken. I loved this course. I felt that Professor LaFleur was very good at balancing allowing us to speak about what we wanted to talk about and steering the class in a productive direction, although I sometimes felt like we didn't get to interesting parts of the readings because we would get stuck on a place where we had a lot to say because it was simpler than the heart of the readings. I don't know how much scholarship there is on this at this point, but I would be curious to read about how non-binary identities are/are not shaping/changing trans studies/activism/understanding.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1574382\n 1574382\n \n 1574382\n Strengths: Wide selection of challenging, meaningful, and insightful texts; a good amount of fiction and popular writing, but also, appropriately, a focus on academic writing. A good mix of foundational texts with great impact and more marginal, counter-narrative texts. Professor LaFleur does well explaining the argumentative thrust of more challenging pieces and encourages students to connect their arguments to specific moments in the texts. The amount we had to read felt appropriate.\r\nWeaknesses: Discussions sometimes departed from the texts in unconstructive ways, especially personal feelings unconnected with larger course questions. Professor LaFleur asked students a couple times to connect their points to shared texts or ideas but was, understandably, reluctant to divert students from sharing their personal experiences and opinions.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575661\n 1575661\n \n 1575661\n Unfortunately, there was not much literature on Asian experiences. However, this course touched on an immensely broad breadth of experiences. The professor has worked carefully to craft this course to be as seamless as possible. I find that the structure of the course did not work as well for me, because I learn best when working reverse-chronologically; I was a bit lost later in the semester as we read literature that referred to texts that we had analyzed previously because I had struggled to grasp the context and material of that older material. In general, I think that these texts can really benefit from a lot of context before we start reading them because I found myself really frustrated as I tried to parse out what the literature was saying. \r\nI think that the demographic of the seminar contributes significantly to the enjoyment of this course. There were a lot of strange dynamics that occurred with this group that made conversations feel uncomfortable. Part of it is because of the language divide for those who are upperclassmen and have taken several courses in these disciplines and have familiarized themselves with the vocabulary to discuss these texts and then those who are underclassmen who are grappling with how they should read, understand, and discuss the texts in a form deemed acceptable to the academic language. Additionally, it can feel incredibly uncomfortable to discuss these texts if one is a part of the marginalized identity that is discussed during that day. There is such a heavy focus on the theoretical and it can often feel as if there is an active discouragement to consider these texts in practice, particularly with what these texts mean for the every day and what they should actually /do./ It's difficult to have those kinds of conversations in this class, but it's also generally difficult to be in this class. There is a high level of academic rigor and you really have to be incredibly engaged with every single second of the course if you want to be able to say anything that you're confident in. It really shows when someone has not done the readings; this is not a seminar you can skate through. \r\nThat being said, I think that I really do value the time that I dedicated into this course because I know that I came out the other end of it far more knowledgable. It's like cough medicine; it hurts and sucks to take it, but you know it's going to help you. It takes a massive amount of humility to take this class because you have to get really comfortable being wrong and questioning the ways that you think about writing, literature, and, of course, gender, but also a ton of different power structures that are also related to these dynamics.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1581684\n 1581684\n \n 1581684\n The readings were fantastic, as were discussions. I loved the nature of our assignments (apart from the TikTok assignment which was fun, but, ultimately a bit inane and certainly too heavily weighted) and the way there were plenty of small assignments throughout. The reading responses were a great way for me Tian to ally engage with very difficult readings and form my own thoughts about them before discussion. I think perhaps a discussion format that involves less lists of speakers waiting to speak and was more free-flowing would have been nice, though I understand that everyone was always eager to speak.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1584461\n 1584461\n \n 1584461\n A great strength is that the syllabus is really varied in terms of types of texts assigned. I loved having films, novels, and intellectual works assigned throughout the semester. The reading responses are also a great way to get students to really digest the readings before coming to class. \r\n\r\nAs far as improvements go, I love that this course is a seminar, but it seemed that there was generally a pretty harsh divide between class years (of course with some exceptions) in terms of level of preparedness for a theory-based seminar. I wonder if students may get more out of this course if it was at least recommended as a higher-level seminar for juniors and seniors. \r\n\r\nAnd one more tiny note: it would be great to update the syllabus with themes or units for the week so that students can understand where we're headed.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1584640\n 1584640\n \n 1584640\n This class was so interesting! I loved going to class every week, and the readings were really fascinating. Difficult issues were approached with care but not fear, giving us the chance to really investigate them as well as the emotions and controversies they evoke. I think the structure of bi-weekly response papers forced me to think deeply about those readings in the context of previous readings without it becoming a chore. Additionally, the variety of assignments (reading responses, Wikipedia page, tik tok, final paper) kept the class engaging and gave me a chance to explore the content in a variety of mediums.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1585387\n 1585387\n \n 1585387\n I loved the clarity on readings and assignments, and I enjoyed the variety in readings, films, and discussions. All of the content was great. I think class time could have been made more interesting if there had been a little bit more reining-in of discussion about personal experience / if the content of class time had been more grounded in the readings themselves. However I understand that is not 100% Greta's fault.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1594443\n 1594443\n \n 1594443\n The readings were organized really well. I also enjoyed the assignments in this class - the Wikipedia and TikTok assignments were a fun and engaging way to bring trans knowledge to the mainstream I thought I was getting much more out of them than writing a paper only an instructor would see.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1594556\n 1594556\n \n 1594556\n Strengths: How we managed to simultaneously cover both a lot of material, as well as go into that material in-depth. There were also a bunch of course assignments that felt really relevant to our modern situation (making a TikTok, writing a Wikipedia page on something trans-related). The course also drew very intentionally away from frameworks of white transness or white feminismwhile we talked about those viewpoints, we did focus on a decolonial sort of discussion of gender as our main ideology. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: \r\nDiscussions at times were a bit hard to follow? But oftentimes, that meant that the first person would say something about topic A, and then the four people afterwards would talk about a different topic, and the sixth person who wanted to respond to the first person about topic A had no way to actually segue the conversation into talking about it. \r\nAlso, while I understand that it was important to cover a lot of material, I did think that there were too many readings. We hit 70-80 pages per class regularly, and with 2 classes per week, it was very hard to keep up with all of the readings.
