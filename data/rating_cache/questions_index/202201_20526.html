\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1563748\n 1563748\n \n 1563748\n I think despite the high workload, the problems/practice were a good part of learning. The breadth of topics covered were also good, although I felt that the last few topics near the end of the semester were a bit more rushed than, say, the earlier topics. To make this more effective, I think better course organization could have helped students learn more. More planning regarding homework release and due dates, more consistent number of problems/points, and planning a schedule for the amount of time spent covering each topic would have been beneficial for students to have good practice in all of the covered topics. \r\n\r\nTo give a few examples: HW 5 was released in the middle of the spring recess with a due date in 7 days, counting the days of the break. But since there was no active assignment out before the break it would have been better to release it on the Wednesday before the break, giving students a full academic week to work on the assignment and not necessarily during the break. The time between the last assignment and the project deadline was too short to make meaningful progress (less than a week even with counting the last minute extension) especially considering that students are often taking multiple courses in a semester. The time to complete assignments were a bit irregular; although usually following a weekly schedule, there were instances of an assignment being released on the weekend due in 4 days and the last assignment released on Monday due Friday. Although the assignments were made (relatively) shorter, it would help to just plan the assignments to take the full week and fit multiple topics into one assignment if necessary. I believe all of these things can be done with just more course planning and organization. A regular schedule can help students with planning around other priorities, and better plan to commit a proper amount of time for this course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1567513\n 1567513\n \n 1567513\n I will paint a picture of what it is like to be a student in a course such as this. We have a poorly edited text written by an absent instructor that favors entertainment over accomplishing the practical tasks of 1. introducing objects and their properties in detail; 2. showing how they are used; 3. justifying why they are used that way; 4. giving students the opportunity to practice using such objects, and most importantly, 5. giving students the tools to know if they are using them correctly. Instead, students have a textbook which explains little, rambles often, and closes each chapter with a smorgasbord of impossible problems for which practice is impossible, as there is no way to determine if one is doing them correctly if they do not already know the material. In effect, students are punished for trying to learn the material. This raises a dilemma: do the students who do well in such a course already know much of the material, seeing as how there are minimal avenues for which one can walk down to actually learn it? If that is the case, then what is the point of the course at all? Rather than provide a place for learning, such a course instead deepens, widens, and reinforces a gap between students with the privilege of prior exposure, and those who need the exposure most. A course such as this would exist seemingly for the sole purpose of separating the privileged from their cohorts and rewarding them for it. I see no utility in such a course for any party. The people who want to learn it can't, and the people who learn least do the best.\r\nAnything can be made difficult if it is explained poorly. I would propose that, as the Earth's resources continue to dwindle and the meritocracy we subject ourselves to continues to crumble, that a class such as this should offer students the means to practice, the means to perfect, and the means to critically analyze what they are doing and why. This means providing students with problems for which the mechanics are at least alluded to, and for which information at the very least exists. This means a standard text, and not a pretentious passion project. This means having the opportunity to learn by practice, and not by waiting for your grade on an assignment that took 40 hours to complete. This means giving students the chance to know why, how, for what, rather than punishing them for trying to make their lives and possibly the world a better place.
