\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546647\n 1546647\n \n 1546647\n I read some seriously illuminating and existential-crisis-provoking philosophers this semester. Plato taught me that my soul is immortal, while Aristotle and Lucretius taught me that no actually, it isn't. I learned how to not fear death from the Epicureans (I'm still scared), and that Seneca would be very angry with me if I ever expressed anger over something (...wait). I learned that Epictetus would probably agree that my anger was unwarranted if it pertained to something outside of my control, and if he saw me shouting expletives I'd probably be met with a lot of head-shaking and blushing from his direction. Sextus taught me that I probably know nothing, but I might know something! Of course, I'll never really know for sure. But also never say never? #SuspendJudgement. I learned what the word "teleology" meant and will be using it to sound intelligent for the rest of my purposeless life. I learned that Maimonides and Alfarabi had serious crushes on Aristotle and that if it weren't for all that moon juice they were drinking, I might actually be a little bit inclined to subscribe to their views of God. I learned that Augustine was doing his best to make Christianity make sense, but the mental whiplash I went through trying to reconcile God's foreknowledge with human free will was not enough to convert me. I learned that Anselm was a sneaky guy for his ontological definition of God and that nothing is more mind-bending than reading the phrase "that than which nothing greater can be thought" hundreds of times. \r\n\r\nIn all, I learned that I probably have a soul (fingers crossed), there might be a God (unless there are many, or none!), and that I should stop reading these books looking for answers to questions, because it's getting pretty obvious that I'm not gonna find any.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1537257\n 1537257\n \n 1537257\n Strength:\r\n1. All the reading material are very interesting, though I would appreciate if DS didn't asked us to read so much Aristotle lol. But overall it's a great intro course to Ancient Greek and some medieval philosophy. As a person who has never been exposed to any religion before, I really enjoyed all the reading of Augustine, Aquinas, Maimonides and Ibn Tufayl.\r\n2. The DS philosophy lectures are amazing! The lecturers always explained complicated Ideas in very simple language and greatly helped me to understand the material.\r\n\r\nWeakness:\r\n1. The seminar can be a bit disorganized sometimes and at the end of the discussion I didn't really feel like I gained much new insights or my problems got answered.\r\n2. I think the course would be greatly improved if instead of close reading a short paragraph in seminar and trying to figure out what it means, we discuss the big idea of this writer: should we accept their philosophy, why or why not? what are some good arguments for it and against it? what does it mean to accept their philosophy? what counterargument could we propose? etc. It feels a bit waste of time to clarify some obscure argument in class, a job that can definitely be done after or before the class through email communication or internet searching. Given that we only have two seminars per week, I think it is really important to use it in the most intellectually challenging way.

I am aware and acknowledge Aristotle's importance, but I think the pacing was a little off.
