The only weakness I can think of (and this was so minor) is that sometimes the subject of the night's readings will take two classes to unpack, so we'll be a bit behind in terms of class discussion. Professor Tomlinson is one of the smartest philosophy and music professors out there.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1625483\n 1625483\n \n 1625483\n I really liked how each lesson Professor Tomlinson recapped the readings and engaged us in discussion because it helped me understand all of the texts on much deeper level. I also really enjoyed that Professor Tomlinson showed us versions of arias from different productions, sung by different singers, interpreted in different ways because it gave a more 360 viewpoint of the things we studied. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627368\n 1627368\n \n 1627368\n Strengths: Prof Tomlinson attracts people spanning the range of musician to philosopher/humanities student, and it makes for diverse viewpoints in discussion. \r\n\r\nWeakness:\r\n\r\nIt was sometimes hard to follow the structure of the lectures, but to be fair the lectures were extemporaneous.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628385\n 1628385\n \n 1628385\n The biggest strength was professor Tomlinson. If you get a chance to take any course with him, do it. He has a luminous intellect and his lectures, while manic, are a treat--packed with brilliant insights and connections (about musicology, music theory, staging, art history, philosophy of art, of history, metaphysics, orientalism, psychoanalysis, what have you). Another strength was the syllabus--the four operas we studied (Don Giovanni, Tristan und Isolde, Carmen, Simon Boccanegra) were all beautiful and different and philosophically rich. \r\nOne weakness of this course is that there aren't enough writing assignments, and much of the non-reading work is watching the operas, so if you lose motivation at any point, you're on your own to develop accountability mechanisms. I think this course could benefit from weekly "reading quizzes"--for the operas, not the readings--to make sure that we've actually watched and paid attention to the shows. This would have engaged me more, at least.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630326\n 1630326\n \n 1630326\n As a student with no musical background, this course was was easily accessible and helps students learn about musical theory through classroom discussion.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630623\n 1630623\n \n 1630623\n The professor's passion about the topics engaged the class and the discussions were extremely thought-provoking. However, sometimes his passion lead him to spend significant chunks of the class time answering tangential questions. This time was so significant that the teacher had to cancel many of the readings near the end of the year so that the class could stay on track. This is much learning lost in my opinion. Additionally, Yale Course Search lists this class as having "no prerequisites." However, as much as I enjoyed this class, I am convinced that I would have learned much more had I taken an introductory philosophy class. In fact, I believe I was one of only two underclassmen in the class. Most of the students were upperclassmen philosophy majors, making it difficult to find common ground for discussion. As a final suggestion for improvement, I would mention that this teacher was not great at clarifying his prompts for the papers. Many students would stay after class to ask him clarifying questions and would leave often feeling more confused than when they entered the classroom. Moreover, his comments on my writing were, while well-meaning, often confusing and harsh. My writing did improve in this class, but only because it forced me to write about foreign topics, not because of his feedback.\r\n\r\nAll things set aside, this course has provided me with unique perspectives which are not available in mainstream academia.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1634588\n 1634588\n \n 1634588\n It might be nice to either have more regular office hours or have more regulated checkpoints before an essay is due to help with their development. Also, I wish that the prompts for the essays were available more in advance to the essay's due date, like at the start of each opera's section so we could think about the prompt as the unit progresses.
