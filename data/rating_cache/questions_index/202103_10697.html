RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1522208\n 1522208\n \n 1522208\n The strengths of the course are undeniably the knowledge of the professor and the value of the guest speakers. The latter make Northern Irish issues personal and offer rare first hand insight. I can not think of a single thing I would change, this course was fantastic.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1522365\n 1522365\n \n 1522365\n Strengths: content is fascinating and Professor Weir cares a ton about it. She's a, if not the, leading academic in peacebuilding in Northern Ireland. It's great to watch her interact with our guests.\r\nWeaknesses: WPNI felt more like a series of talks than an actual class. There are basically no assignments. That's awesome from a workload perspective, but it makes it very easy to coast by in the class. There are too many students in the class and even if you really try to participate, you're only going to talk once or twice per class. So there's no real incentive to do any of the readings on the week you're not presenting.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1525387\n 1525387\n \n 1525387\n Strengths \r\n- Professor Weir is AMAZING -- so accessible, friendly, knowledgable, loved answering questions, answered them really well, funny, kind\r\n- low stress (both outside of and inside the classroom) -- purely enjoyable!!! I loved being in class all the time \r\n- the guest speakers were really amazing. we talked to a guest speaker for half of every single class, and it's so special that you can get a firsthand account of a historical war, so it was super cool and really helped with understanding the conflict \r\n- low workload (pretty much just a 2-page reflection every week and some light readings, no final!!!), but I still learned SO MUCH\r\n- extremely interesting and fascinating material \r\n\r\nMaybe a bit more feedback for the reflections would've been nice just so I could keep track of how I'm doing in the class? But they seemed pretty chill, so I honestly wasn't that worried about the lack of extensive feedback
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527568\n 1527568\n \n 1527568\n War and Peace in Northern Ireland was organized around guest speakers. This format was excellent for learning and engagement, since each person we heard from had unique insights into the Troubles and its aftermaths. I think this format fit perfectly with the course's subject matter.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1531502\n 1531502\n \n 1531502\n It depends on how you learn, but for me the structure of the course was incredible. No long writing assignments, just an opportunity to engage deeply with the course content without stress. Clearer expectations and feedback on the assignments would be nice, and the speakers could sometimes feel repetitive. It would be nice if we could pick from the questions we had to submit each week ahead of time, so everybody got an equal opportunity to ask questions and the answers would vary.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532512\n 1532512\n \n 1532512\n Strengths: Q&As with people involved in Northern Ireland added a really cool change in format from most other seminars at Yale. Additionally, student presentations weekly on the readings helped to increase understanding and interaction with the material.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Oftentimes student questions leftover from past weeks at the beginning of class made the presentations have a bit of time crunch before guest speakers arrived, which left little room for in-class discussion on the current week's material. Additionally, feedback on assignments (more than likely because of their high frequency) was sparse, and it sometimes felt as though I was missing constructive feedback that would help me better engage with the course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532711\n 1532711\n \n 1532711\n Strengths- hearing from guest speakers every class and gaining wonderful insights from these conversations \r\nWeakness- none
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544697\n 1544697\n \n 1544697\n The strengths of this course lie in the conversations we had with speakers each week. It is one thing to learn about history from a textbook. It is another to learn about history through the words of someone who lived it. I found each class to be captivating, eye-opening, and mutually engaging. Because we were able to ask questions to each speaker, it felt more like a conversation, rather than a lecture. The class benefitted from Professor Weir's wide-ranging connections: we were able to speak to people from both sides of the conflict with different lived experiences, interests, and areas of expertise. The weakness of this course relates to it being only an hour and fifty minutes! I wish we had more time to talk with speakers and engage in class discussion about the assigned reading. We mainly focused on the former (which I'm not complaining about!), but I think it would've helped to also dive deeper into the theoretical underpinnings of the conflict as well as explored the history more.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544707\n 1544707\n \n 1544707\n The strengths are definitely just the amount of diverse learning you get from all the different speakers and topics you cover over the course of the seminar. I learned so much. However I would maybe have liked if there were more opportunities for discussion amongst the students as the class was mainly dialogues between students and speakers.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544779\n 1544779\n \n 1544779\n The content was fascinating and Professor Weir is a great instructor. We spoke with someone in Northern Ireland almost every week which made the classes very engaging. We rarely got feedback on our work, however, which made the assignments feel like a task to complete rather than an evaluation of our work. A lot of class time was often taken up by people asking Professor Weir questions, which meant that student presentations were usually rushed and class discussions were cut. Overall, the course was great, but the structure could be improved to facilitate more in-class discussion and reflection on guest speakers.
