\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1656003\n 1656003\n \n 1656003\n I think that this is overall a very good course with interesting lectures and fascinating discussions. Professor Grimstad almost always does a good job facilitating discussions. The conversation sometimes gets sidetracked, but I think thats the nature of the course. I liked how Professor Grimstad would send out extra supplementary materials for some philosophers (I particularly liked the one for Nietzche). I think the biggest weakness of the course is the syllabus; the amount of days allocated to each philosopher is unequal which caused us to skip philosophers and texts that I feel we had time to cover.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1665080\n 1665080\n \n 1665080\n Seminars are thought-provoking but they can trail off-topic too much sometimes.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1665316\n 1665316\n \n 1665316\n Discussions are led perfectly: Professor Grimstad is always beyond prepared and his responses push the discussion forward. The only negative I can think of is that sometimes it can be just a little challenging to not be interrupted by professor Grimstad, but this rarely happens.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1666396\n 1666396\n \n 1666396\n Strengths: good overview of Western philosophical canon\r\nWeaknesses: too little time for these readings
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1673237\n 1673237\n \n 1673237\n Strengths: The selection of texts for this course was well-made, where the ideas of one thinker aptly flowed into or responded to the ideas of the previous. I appreciated the informal division of the course into 1) metaphysics and 2) moral philosophy, with Kant and the Kantian revolution as the breaking-point. The vast majority of the lectures in philosophy were extremely helpful this semester: I appreciated especially when the lecturer 1) left a detailed handout/slide presentation useful for review and 2) divided the lecture into an explanation, and the a critique, of the arguments of the week's thinker. This was done for example during the lecture on Hume's moral philosophy, the two lectures on Kant and the lecture on Nietzsche. I found the structure of the final exam, which provided great opportunities to display one's knowledge on a vast array of subjects, while focussing on conceptual understanding rather than identification of passages, very pleasant and well-suited to the way DS philosophy is taught.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: The only weakness I can think of right now as to the overall structure of the course is that more of the power-points / handouts could have been posted to the canvas page. And perhaps that the Hume-books were extremely expensive both to buy and rent but that it was still insisted that we should do so, for one week of study on each text.
