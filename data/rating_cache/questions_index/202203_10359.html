\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1631848\n 1631848\n \n 1631848\n I think this was a good course topic on an often neglected subject. For the most part, the reading list was great and interesting, but it felt hard to discuss the reading in classProf. Peterson always wanted to give a lot of background information, which was helpful but turned half the seminar into a lecture and took away a lot of time that could have been used to discuss topics students found interesting in the reading. This course may work better as a 50 minute lecture plus discussion section in the future.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1632268\n 1632268\n \n 1632268\n Strengths: professor very good at giving context and highlighting important points while also allowing for student input, cool content, I looked forward to going to class every week; weaknesses: I generally prefer being given a shorter reading to read in full rather than being assigned a full book a week and being told to skim it, but that's a personal preference
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1632279\n 1632279\n \n 1632279\n Strengths included relaxed seminar setting, felt comfortable to talk. Also papers allowed you to explore topics you're interested in. Weaknesses were a ton of reading which made arriving to class fully prepared difficult.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1632331\n 1632331\n \n 1632331\n I think this course was designed to facilitate conversation and it led to interesting class discussions. The selection of materials was thoughtful and covered a wide array of topics that allowed students to pursue what they found most interesting. In that way, I think the scope of the material covered was realistic and successful. Personally, I thought the structured conversations were more engaging than fully unstructured discussions, and the debates/alternating summaries were a strong part of the seminar. I think an issue with the course was that it picked up on different levels of prior knowledge, leading to some content mismatches among students. I found it helpful when it was a hybrid lecture and seminar, starting the class with some important contextual review.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1632457\n 1632457\n \n 1632457\n Strengths: discussions. very engaging and professor Peterson was an amazing discussion leader. \r\n\r\nWeakness: weekly reading length was extensive.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1638692\n 1638692\n \n 1638692\n The course covers a totally different type of history than you learn in high school and sometimes the readings can be really thought provoking. The course shows that there's no such thing as "environmental history" because every history always relates to the environment. You start to thinking about topics like the relationship between the US and Native Americans, and the industrial revolution, in a much more nuanced way. \r\n\r\nI also liked the flexibility of the final paper. Professor Peterson is also very good at making seminar "active" and setting up debates that are a lot more fun than standard discussions. \r\n\r\nOne weakness is that, because this was a new course, the syllabus isn't perfect. Some of the books were fairly dense and it's hard to read 300 pages about forest management policy. I liked the books but, if you're not really interested in the content, it's easy to get bored. It seemed like engagement with the readings was mixed, although the canvas discussion posts helped.\r\n\r\nI think the best way to improve this would simply be to intersperse longer readings with shorter books/papers. I also really wish that looking at primary sources was more emphasized throughout the semester. The final paper was all about analyzing primary sources but we didn't have any practice doing that beforehand. Maybe some of the primary sources that were informally presented in class (e.g. the Domesday book) could be part of our assignments.
