\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1660168\n 1660168\n \n 1660168\n Strengths: Professor Bowern is super smart and very knowledgeable about the field of historical linguistics. It's cool to take a course with not only the professor who wrote our textbook, but a foremost expert in the field of phylogenetic modeling for historical language reconstruction. Weaknesses: I think this class should be reorganized slightly. The problem is that it currently is trying to cater to both linguistics majors and people who've never taken a linguistics class. For the people who already understood linguistics, the class felt largely like a review of concepts taught in previous classes (several of the lectures were basically identical to those Professor Bowern gave in Introduction to Linguistics) and the way we covered material felt too surface-level, while for students unfamiliar with linguistics much of the material was difficult to grasp quickly, because understanding it fully required discussion of linguistic concepts that the course didn't delve into (i.e. phonological features, phonological processes, morphology, etc.). In an ideal word, I feel like this class should have a pre-req of some sortIntroduction to Linguistics or Phonetics of Phonologyso that students taking it have enough of a background in linguistics to engage fully with the concepts. I felt like the class in its current form needed either a lot more depth or an opportunity (i.e. a paper or a final project in place of the final exam) for students to engage with the material to the degree that they want to and practice applying the concepts we'd learned to a real historical linguistic analysis. Also, I don't think nearly enough time was spent discussing the process of historical reconstruction, and the methods used by linguists to perform such analysis. During that whole unit, I felt like we were sort of flying blind, in that it didn't feel like we had nearly enough discussion of the logic necessary to justify subgrouping, or the direction of sound change. Historical reconstruction very much felt like the heart of the course, and I felt like we spent too much time talking about things sort of tangentially related to the actual reconstruction process (i.e. writing systems, the origins of language) that we couldn't really go into depth in this area.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1681611\n 1681611\n \n 1681611\n The courses major strength is in its material. The classs later focus on sociolinguistics, endangered languages, semantic change as well as regularly interacting and learning about the subtle yet interconnected nuances of different languages (i.e., sound change, IPA, etc.) made it easier for me to be invested in the course. Although major concepts like comparative and internal reconstruction were hard to learn, because an interactive mood had been fostered early on, the class itself was never too stressful. The encouragement of class participation also contributed to a low-stress environment that was sometimes even fun. At times the lectures did not communicate some key information that was in the textbook, which was a little frustrating, though this flaw became less apparent later in the semester. As a lot of homework material was dependent on the textbook, at times this made the class really difficult, although it was never too bad that one couldnt catch up later. Another major issue was that the class itself was not an intro linguistics course at all even though students with no prior knowledge of linguistics were encouraged to take the course. Encouraging (not really requiring) prior knowledge of linguistics might prepare future students for the difficulty of the class.
