\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624406\n 1624406\n \n 1624406\n The strength of the course is Professor Okihiro--he's a phenomenal and engaging lecturer. The biggest weaknesses of this course were its organization and the sections/teaching fellows. This course does not practice what it preaches. Not only did the teaching fellows assign an unreasonable amount of dense, theoretical readings, their sections felt poorly organized and not at all oriented toward generating productive student conversations. This felt deeply ironic in a course that emphasized new and radical pedagogies--that in such a course as students we were often talked at and not to. Although the lecture environment was wonderful, the sections felt like opportunities for the teaching fellows to speak at us and left little room for students to offer their own insights. After our first midterm, the teaching fellows team spent a considerable amount of time belittling our work--saying things like "I was afraid to start grading your midterms because I knew how bad they would be," etc., etc. The teaching fellows then decided to single out the two top-performing students on the midterm and have them run outside, mandatory sessions with the chance for us to do a write-up and earn up to 10 points back on our midterm. I have never experienced anything like that in my time at Yale and found it deeply inappropriate. The final assignment (to cite and engage 15 to 20 readings from the course within 8 pages) was simply unreasonable. Again, for a class that ostensibly rejects the disciplinary nature of academia and seeks out radical and transformative ways of learning, many aspects of this course felt incredibly punitive. Another essential issue with this course was that although it was labeled introductory, it was taught in a way that would be highly inaccessible to any student without substantial background in critical theory or ER&M. Ultimately, the course could be improved through a new pedagogy which is more in line with what it teaches--assign less and more accessible readings and delve into them more, have teaching fellows who are sincerely interested in student learning, and be clear about standards and grading (rather than grading exams arbitrarily and scolding students who are simply doing their best).
