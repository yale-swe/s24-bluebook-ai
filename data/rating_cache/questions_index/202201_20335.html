\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1561942\n 1561942\n \n 1561942\n Without question. It is the platonic ideal of a Yale first-year seminar. Take this course, if you are the slightest bit interested in history. It isn't just talking about early New England. It is discussing the basic questions and ideas that motivate historical inquiry, trying to grapple with how we remember things, as historians and laypeople. It is the best course I have taken yet at Yale. \r\n\r\nThe course and its syllabus is also just beautifully crafted. Professor Peterson is a truly gifted writing instructor and lecturer/ discussion leader. Every reading flows so wonderfully into the next; we were constantly bringing the sources in conversation with one another. He is so enthusiastic for this material, and that enthusiasm spread to the course as a whole. Every class, I would come away with insights from him and my amazing classmates. I felt that the level of engagement in the discussions was extremely high, and Professor Peterson played a huge part in motivating that discussion. Even as we were engaging in serious, deep discussion of weighty matters, everything felt informal. It was the perfect environment for investigating history and how we talk about it. Professor Peterson is also a hilarious guy.\r\n\r\nThe amount of writing you will have to do (for a WR credit) is very reasonable, and all the prompts fit well into the discussions and readings. The reading load was very manageable. The final project has a really open prompt, which is an amazing opportunity to have in a first-year seminar.\r\n\r\nThis is a fun, intellectually fascinating course taught by one of Yale's best professors. Take the course.
