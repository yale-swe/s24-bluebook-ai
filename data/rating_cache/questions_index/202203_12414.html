\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1610065\n 1610065\n \n 1610065\n This class was challenging, but it was engaging.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1616436\n 1616436\n \n 1616436\n The strength of this course lies in its explanatory style. Unlike in previous physics courses I've had, PHYS 260 went into great detail about how exactly certain physical principles can be derived and what specific assumptions must be made along the way - in so doing, the course reframed Newtonian mechanics as an intricate logical system in its own right, rather than leaving it as a set of loosely connected arbitrary principles. I'm very excited for the framework built up in this class to be extended in future semesters.\r\n\r\nThe weakest part of the course might lie in the disjointedness between the lectures and the textbook, and sometimes within the textbook itself. While I understand that the lectures and readings are meant to be complementary, some intriguing topics in K&K that the authors explained rather confusingly (momentum flux, normal modes, etc.) were never clarified in class, occasionally leading to some panic.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618681\n 1618681\n \n 1618681\n Strengths are the choice of content was made for intuitive understanding of physics. However, topic presentation and testing had massive room for improvement. The professor is kind but he has not thought sufficiently about what content is interesting or difficult in standard mechanics. While his added topics are frequently engaging and well taught (eg his coverage of oscillator equations and special relativity), more than half of his lectures are on standard textbook topics and these are not high quality. Generally, the textbook explanations were both more intuitive and more detailed for such varied topics as kinematics, momentum, angular velocity/momentum, rotating reference frames, central/2-body lotion. This isnt some brutal critique of his teaching; the topics were discussed and his added content was great. But for intro classes, especially intensive ones that are a lot of work, I think it would benefit if Professor Harris (who is wonderful) spent more time thinking about this. Further, tests were really mediocre; the first test was easy but good questions, the second test was good but way too hard for a class that claims not to curve (the high was a 90, an A- by his initially stated standards, average was 72=C-), the final was fine but weird and had no questions on angular momentum (except a simple integral to calculate inertia) or noninertial reference frames and only one weird question on central body forces, which were half the course. I know though writing tests is hard, and its not the most important thing. In conclusion, it is a good class but theres a lot to improve :)

The peer office hours are very helpful! It's a great course to take!\r\nWeaknesses: there are errors on the lecture slides occasionally, and the slides were too math-heavy (especially during the lectures where lots of mathematical derivation/manipulation were shown at once, it is a bit hard to follow.) Mid-term 1 was set too easy while mid-term 2 was set too difficult. Topics tested in the final could have been made clearer; topics not tested were only announced the day before the final exam. I learned more things by grinding the textbooks.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624560\n 1624560\n \n 1624560\n This course had great problems that made me develop as a problem solver. If I had any complaints, it would be that I felt that the difficulty of the second two exams was perhaps a bit too high without any set curve. Furthermore, the lack of practice exams (or extra assigned problems) was frustrating when studying for the exams.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639761\n 1639761\n \n 1639761\n Yes! This was my favorite class during my first semester, and I don't even love physics (and am not a prospective physics major). Taking this class will give you a very deep understanding of classical mechanics, and a few topics (special relativity and especially the last section on determinism if Professor Harris chooses to teach that again, which I hope he does) may change the way you view the world more than any humanities class you take. As all the reviews will tell you, the workload is pretty intense, and the hardest problem sets can take many hours to finish. From what I've heard, many consider 260 to be the most difficult class in the Yale physics department. If you're taking 5+ classes, or you don't feel super confident in your math ability, you should definitely take 200 (from what I've heard, the two classes are both good and teach very similar topics). However, if you are willing to put in the time for a challenging class, you will not regret taking 260. Also, lots of people switch to 200 after a few weeks, so there's no harm in at least trying out 260.
