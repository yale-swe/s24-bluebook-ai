\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1681438\n 1681438\n \n 1681438\n This course benefits a lot from intense class discussion on very personal topics. A lot of the time, when we're talking about cultural phenomena, many of my peers would chime in with valuable insights based on their own lived experiences. In this sense, this is a huge strength for the course. You're not only benefitting from Professor Gorski's knowledge, you're also benefitting from real people's experiences.\r\n\r\nThe only weakness I'd attribute to this course would be in regards to the reading discussion posts. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1682070\n 1682070\n \n 1682070\n I enjoyed the critical theory unit the most, as it encompassed the reflexive theory and black, feminist, decolonial perspectives that I was looking for in a course on contemporary social theory. There was a good attempt to include a more diverse canon of theorists in the curriculum, though I believe there is still more room for more critical theorists and theorists of color so that students can be exposed to a wider ranger of theory beyond the primarily white, Western canon that most sociology students are likely already familiar with from other classes or 151. The work of these critical theorists could be elevated as well, and put in more direct conversation with the theories they are criticizing. When evaluating theories, I wish we had paid more attention to the significance of platforming potentially harmful theories and evaluated them by their social impact/reception outside the academic sphere rather than just their intellectual rigor or academic merit. What does it mean to have these theories be the "foundation" of modern social theory? How does the absence of certain thinkers and knowledge projects frame what is in the center? \r\n\r\nI enjoyed the course and professor overall, but I'm not sure if I would have continued with the sociology major had this been one of the first courses I took. The course and other students felt out of touch with other courses I've taken in sociology, which function on the understanding that the theories and principals we talk about do not exist in a vacuum and are grounded in lived experiences. I wish this course asked its students to think about the power social theories hold in shaping institutions and policy, and for whom social theory exists for, rather than asking us to apply the theories we talked about to our lives inside the Yale bubble. Also just thinking more critically about the academy as a space of knowledge making and exclusion.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1685352\n 1685352\n \n 1685352\n Strengths: Professor Gorski is a PHENOMENAL lecturer. It is clear that he is sincerely interested in, knowledgeable about, and invested in the work that he discusses. His ideas are expressed with a great amount of clarity and linearity. There is also a clear, sincere interest in the thoughts and ideas of his students, as he asks meaningful questions of us to discuss (either as a class or in groups) and was extremely receptive to questions or criticisms. There was also a pleasant contrast from SOCY 151 in that we covered a very broad range of topics (including gender/sexuality, race and its relationships within systems and societies, critical sociology, public sociology, etc.), with multiple perspectives provided for each topic. It was clear that the syllabus was thoughtfully curated and developed. I really did love this class, its lectures, and its accompanying discussions. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: I would have loved to study a wider range of "forms" of writing; I think sociology could be studied in more different forms than just long essay/research paper formats (however I understand that this just also is generally a pretty uncommon practice in the field). I also think the pieces within each "unit" of the course could be ordered a little bit more coherently.
