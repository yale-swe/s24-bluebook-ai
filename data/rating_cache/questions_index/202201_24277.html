But now, I don't want to stop dancing. I want to roll across the floor with batidos. I want to be a banana. I want to give people massages that activate their centers. This might sound crazy, but it's just a very freeing thing to have a space where you feel comfortable to dance, to learn through dance with others, to press the idiot button and let go but also to learn these various physical movements and skills and awarenesses. Although I think I didn't engage in the readings as much as I would have liked to, from the ones that I did, I did learn and see interesting notions of dance and democracy although I think the link between them sometimes felt elusively abstract or metaphorical rather than something we acted out actively in the course.

Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1563368\n 1563368\n \n 1563368\n Strengths: Eryn's energy is delightful. She hopes to enact Freirien learning (breaking down student teacher boundary) and I think in many ways, she does. She dances with us and infuses the class with energy and provoking activities. She tries to be very aware of our own needs, pushes us to take care of ourselves, take breaks when we need it. The dancing is also just so fun. Like in what class do i get to just roll over the floor and my classmates. Make moving, active art and then engage with readings on democracy and try piecing them together while drawing on paper in conversation with my classmates. every week i had something new that I was excited about that I wanted to share, and the physical movement of the class made me feel energized and expansive. I loved everyone in the class and although we didn't become besties I think that the environment was in general really amazing and supportive and everyone was just so cool.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Grades. I think that grades definitely got students stressed and I think the timing of assignments was confusing. I think there was ambiguity on how long you should work on assignments, when you should work on assignments, and what we hoped to get out of them. I think that students' preocupation with grades coupled sometimes with unclear directions or just the general open-ended nature of the course made the environment strained. I think that in general, ironically, students who cared more about their grades struggled more because they weren't acting out of true curiosity or care. Eryn doesn't care if your sentences in your reflection are perfect, but she does want to push you and question you in ways that I think Yale teachers don't normally. She wants you to think and care and actively take control of your learning, enact in Freirien learning. I think that's difficult tho because true freirien learning requires a lot of energy and independence and agency that most yale students don't have the time/energy/desire to enact. it's great in theory but in practice when there's this external notion of a grade, it's hard for students to fully engage in that. I think in general tho, the class was still realyl amazing and all students brought unique perspectives, care, and passion to the table in ways that never failed to amaze or inspire me.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1570540\n 1570540\n \n 1570540\n Only the "movement" part of the course was worthwhile. Contact improvisation is an interesting "sports-art" and I am glad to have gained exposure to it. However, anything else that did not have to do with movement was absolutely worthless. The weekly readings, while overwhelmingly abundant, contributed very little to the design of the course. The only thing more pointless than the weekly readings were the in-class "discussions." Their ridiculousness is hard to describe, but I will try. Our discussions mostly involved us sitting on the floor, doodling on a large piece of paper, and having a meaningless conversation. There are never any guiding questions or bigger goals we are supposed to build towards. We just sit there, chat, while trying to ignore our aching back pains from sitting on the floor. \r\n\r\nHow can the course be improved? Just rid of the useless "discussion" portion of the class. Make it a two-hour, movement-only course. Assign only CI-related readings. All of my qualms with the course can be solved if the instructor stops trying to make the course more than it is by tacking on "democracy" as if she feels the need to justify the course's existence.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1581510\n 1581510\n \n 1581510\n It could tie more strongly readings with movement exercises
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1583012\n 1583012\n \n 1583012\n The highlight of this course would be how focused it is on individual growth and development. Eryn is a very supportive instructor and pushes every student to be the best they can be. The course content can be molded to the interests of every student, which also makes the topic and class discussions very engaging.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1593054\n 1593054\n \n 1593054\n This course always felt like there wasnt enough time to do everything in the agenda. It seemed like 3 hours for a course would be a lot, but they went by so quickly. So a strength is how engaging and interesting the course material and exercises were. But a weakness is the time, if anything the course should be extended another hour per meeting!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1599852\n 1599852\n \n 1599852\n This course offers students a rare opportunity to engage our entire bodies (including our hearts) in intellectual and social-justice oriented engagement with deeply important issues. I really cherished the chance to move and dance each week, and to consider the role of movement in social transformation. This course draws on a wonderfully broad range of readings/sources, and is both interdisciplinary and transnational.
