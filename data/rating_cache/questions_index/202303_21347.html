John is a wonderful TF, he is extremely responsive, caring, and helpful. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses:\r\n\r\nOverall: Right now the course tries to be a research methods course that teaches students how to use a bunch of different tools of probabilistic programming for cognitive science research. While I learned a lot about cognitive science from the lectures and readings, I don't think I developed a robust set of research skills (and the theory covered was not enough to understand what was actually going on behind the hood in many of the readings).\r\n\r\nThe Location: The Wu Tsai center is great for grad students, but it is very far for undergrads and having to go 3 times a week during the middle of the day (11:35-12:50) made it difficult to schedule amidst other classes (not to mention dealing with lunch). \r\n\r\nThe Labs: It was pointless to walk all the way to the Wu Tsai Center when they could have been completed asynchronously to the same effect. More of the labs should have been like the last lab (which was less about being along for the ride).\r\n\r\nThe PSETS: These were in very few cases I actually felt like I learned something that I couldn't have just used ChatGPT to generate. In other words, these PSETS felt more pedantic than intellectually challenging. They were frequently released late and due at odd times like 8:00 on Sunday even if they weren't actually graded until a month later. \r\n\r\nThe Final Project: There were 2 weeks of school between the day we submitted our project proposals and the day of the presentation. One issue with the Final Project was that it was an engineering project, but because the PSETS/Labs were not sufficient to teach many of these engineering skills needed, and because the time frame was so short, everyone sort of relied on skills they had from past courses (or built a POMDP lol) to varying effect. Really though, the final project was a dumpster fire because of the YCRC maintenance, the lack of cluster support for Julia in December and the variability of the last minute course container. Trying to work on the project only for the cluster to crash or container to break caused me deep anxiety and dread. For the vast amount of time I spent on the project, in the end I was unusually dissatisfied with my result. It was a cool model, but building it was a labor of pain not a labor of love.\r\n\r\nAlright that's enough complaining . . .\r\n\r\nSolution:\r\n\r\n-S&DS 238 Prerequisite. Assume knowledge of Bayesian statistics and cut 2 weeks of review material.\r\n\r\n- No lab section. Instead, offer extra office hours on Mondays. Use the first few office hours to help set up the container (this container setup would be associated with the first pset released on day 0 of the course). \r\n\r\n-Readings every week, but students must turn in only 6 reading responses to get full credit (more than this is not required). This solves the problem of having weeks where PSET + reading is too much. Now students can decide when to spend time close reading and crafting a response vs. when to spend time on the PSET etc. \r\n\r\n-5 well-designed problem sets (TWO WEEKS PER PSET -- one every other week with the last one due on Week 10). Each problem set should include a lab at the top and then involve using the ideas from the lab to solve problems down below and answering conceptual questions. PSETS should entail a comprehensive amount of writing code from scratch, not just filling in the blank. As described above there seemingly was little reason to make the psets due within 7 days every time, when the next pset wasn't going to be assigned for another week. \r\n\r\n-Project proposal due anytime from Week 9 to the end of Week 10 and meetings with Ilker during Week 11 to discuss the project. This gives an extra two weeks for project work.
