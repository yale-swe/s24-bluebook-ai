\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1652643\n 1652643\n \n 1652643\n The weakness of this course is that it is pitched (and listed) as an advanced mechanical engineering course, but non mechanical engineers are encouraged to take it (perhaps 5 out of 40 students were) and non mechanical engineering professors teach it. Because of this intellectual diversity, very little advanced mechanical engineering can be taught in the class because most students are about 17 courses behind the engineers, and indeed this is not the focus of the class. The focus, and strength, of MENG404 is its dive into the considerations and goals of the medical device industry which has its own systems of regulation and ideation and many of its own challenges. Students receive an overview of the *business* of device design, which makes this class great because indeed medical device design has many stakeholders. People in the class may well go on to become doctors, lawyers, or investors in a life-saving medical device, and this class may set them on that course or give them a solid background if it comes up unexpectedly. \r\n\r\nThe final project in the class, however, is a design project, and that requires engineers who will learn something from the act of doing a funded and holistic project (i.e. not designing a classical mechanism in a vacuum, but one with stakeholders, doctor input, etc) but will not learn new design skills from lecture. This class should not count as a thesis for BS12 or BA students, because not much is demanded from the mechE's, and not much is learned. \r\n\r\nIf this class is to be listed as a MENG elective, there should be a separate course code with separate requirements available to the mechanical engineers, one that expects students to have previously taken Strength and Deformation (MENG I), Dynamics (MENG III), Mechanical Design, Machine Elements, and Materials Science. Those students would be qualified to receive lectures on fatigue failure and the advanced analysis of hip replacements; medical device component manufacturing techniques, surface finishes, quality assurance, and metrology, all of which differ from standard industry; biocompatibility concerns of different parts, their materials science origins, and testing for 510k; design methodology, pFMEA, dFMEA. If a mechanical engineering professor were conscripted to support this splinter section of the class, and all of the groups were guaranteed at least one engineer, then they could put together more workable and thought-through prototypes. \r\n\r\nI agree that having diversity in the class is very important, and if this class were 100% engineers, than they would not engage as deeply in interviewing doctors and patients, considering stakeholders and business concerns, etc etc; but as it is currently taught this class is a wonderfully fun, highly organized, overview of a complicated industry. It is not a high level mechanical engineering class.
