\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1562096\n 1562096\n \n 1562096\n So much! This course is nominally about agriculture but really also about democratic change-making, spheres of influence, and how we go about confronting the biggest issues of our time. I learned a lot about various alternative agricultural movement -- local and organic, vertical farming, cellular ag -- and mainly learned to analyze + be skeptical of most narratives of agricultural transformation. There's a little science involved, but mostly you gain insights into the narratives we use/values we bring to discussions about food and how powerful/dangerous those can be.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1566879\n 1566879\n \n 1566879\n I learned about different theoretical frameworks and approaches to the future of agriculture. These visions had many facets, including political, philosophical, social, and economic consequences. The class sessions at the Yale farm also provided me with a hands-on experience.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1574773\n 1574773\n \n 1574773\n I was challenged in my assumption that sustainability is simple. For a long time I think I've felt like there is a way to fix things, and the problem is that we're just not doing it. My thinking is more nuanced now. I understand that there are paths to healing and progress, but that they are neither universal, simple, or easy

I enjoyed learning from my classmates in the discussions we had, and also from Professor Bomford.

Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1562096\n 1562096\n \n 1562096\n Strengths: so many! The professor is one of the most ~professorial~ people I've met -- i.e. he seems to know everything about everything -- so it's always fun to listen to him lecture. The farm sessions at the end of the semester are a ton of fun and very illuminating. Many of the readings are fascinating and the reading load isn't ridiculous. Lots of flexibility with the final project, which is very cool if you have a directed sense of what you want to do. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Discussions were sometimes great, sometimes stilted. Sometimes it felt like we were rehashing the same themes multiple weeks in a row -- at first they were fascinating, but by the third or fourth time they just felt disheartening (*is* there any way to solve the world's problems?). Felt like at times we would have benefited from a little more technical specificity instead of spending so much time on the abstract.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1566879\n 1566879\n \n 1566879\n Strengths: The course structure and contents are laid out very clearly, and the weekly readings and discussion posts worked really well for sparking conversations in seminar. Writing the reflective reviews was challenging some weeks, and pushed me to think more deeply about the readings. The mixed-methods aspect of the course also provided a really unique perspective on the course content. \r\nWeaknesses: At times, the material felt very theoretical or abstract.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1574773\n 1574773\n \n 1574773\n The strengths of the course are really both mark bonfords personal level of experience and the embodied experience of classes on the Yale farm.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1576389\n 1576389\n \n 1576389\n Strengths; Fantastic TF, great trips to the Yale Farm, cool final project, the other assignments keep you engaged with the readings, and the class structure is pretty good (mix of presentation, discussion, and small group discussion).\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Some of the readings can be super dense and difficult to get through (or hold your attention). Also, I think the small group discussions went on too long--30 minutes is a long time to be in the same group! The reading response comments seemed kind of like a throwaway assignment...we weren't required to do them on certain weeks, so there was less accountability, and it seemed like people were just posting for the sake of it (which we were). I wish we covered more on food justice. I also wish we zoomed out sometimes to look at current food policy proposals or systems, or that we did a case study of New Haven food systems and policy. Lastly, Professor Bomford can sometimes be hard to follow, and I think slides or a printed out lecture would be helpful for us to follow along.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1586588\n 1586588\n \n 1586588\n I think the flow of class topics was very successful and that they built on each other well. I think that the small group discussions were not always the most fruitful because we ended up just talking about what we understood the best - maybe more classes with large group discussions or other forms of conversation would work better. I also wish that we had had class presentations on our final projects at the end of the course: we talked about them a little bit on the last day of class, but I would have loved to learn more about what people were doing, since we had such different projects. I also think working in more different types of reading material (like the Portlandia sketch lol) could be effective: seeing what farmers are reading about organic through an organic certification manual or a FERN expose on CAFOs might have worked well to break up some of the more theoretical readings.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1593124\n 1593124\n \n 1593124\n Wonderful (imo ideal) balance of lecture/seminar discussion and farm sessions; really liked alternating between breakout group discussions and whole-group discussions, reasonable workload and assignments, huge freedom in final project. accessible for students who don't have a background in agriculture.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1593641\n 1593641\n \n 1593641\n Strength: if you are interested in the ideas presented in this class (I highly recommend checking the syllabus before you sign up), then you will not be disappointed with the work we do to study these topics. \r\n\r\nWeakness: there was a good deal of pessimism sourriunding almost all the approaches we studied and it often felt hard to imagine what options we might have left.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1598355\n 1598355\n \n 1598355\n I think this course is very unique and unlike many other courses at Yale. I really enjoyed the field experience at the Yale Farm. The readings in this course were also really engaging and a great balance between theory, research, and more casual writing. The in class discussions were usually interesting, but sometimes difficult to engage with. I think sometimes Prof. Bomford would offer an overly complex topic to introduce the weeks theme and then it was difficult to base our discussion off of something we knew nothing about.
