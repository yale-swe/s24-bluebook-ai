\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617708\n 1617708\n \n 1617708\n Strengths: ASL at Yale is structured such that you're required to interact with the Deaf community, and I thought that aspect of class was a brilliant touch for cultural education. We were required to do a Deaf community service project, which taught me so much more about Deaf culture than I expected to learn from ASL, but I'm very glad it did. Prof. Rubin is also a very sweet and understanding person (I had multiple problems/questions throughout the semester, and she always helped me quickly and kindly), and she will always review signs to make sure we get them. She'll slow down if you think she's signing too fast as well, IF you ask!! \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: The structure of the course was a bit disorienting at times. I always struggled to review signs because 1) I couldn't access the homeworks without going through and re-doing the homeworks 2) all the class slides were on the Modules tab on Canvas, but they were all just labeled as "Lecture ##" so that if you didn't remember exactly what day you were looking for it would require a bit of scrolling to review. The Deaf community service project was also a structural weakness; it's intended to be student-driven, but there were so many of us working on the same things that we often found ourselves stepping on each other's toes. That being said, the service project is very new and will get better with every passing semester, so don't let that deter you from taking ASL!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618630\n 1618630\n \n 1618630\n The weekly field journals could be reduced to every other week and still help students track their engagement.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1619035\n 1619035\n \n 1619035\n There were only strengths, I felt very immersed in the deaf culture and I loved how the classes are completely silent, it was hard at first but as I got better at sign language I realized how much it helped.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1619155\n 1619155\n \n 1619155\n The course is well structured, and Professor Rubin is very engaging and a great professor. I do think that the workload is harder than other ASL batches, and while this was a bit frustrating at times, overall, I think I learnt more as a result of it.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1619244\n 1619244\n \n 1619244\n The instructor is easy to understand and very committed to us understanding and engaging with the content of the class. Professor Rubin's signing was easy to follow and she really encouraged us to engage with the material and Deaf community beyond the classroom, which was crucial to growing in our language skill.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1619510\n 1619510\n \n 1619510\n Strengths: very welcoming and accepting community, good pace\r\n\r\nWeakness: Canvas was a little disorganized at times and there was some confusion about assignments but I think this will get better with time!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1619801\n 1619801\n \n 1619801\n There was a general lack of organization for the course. There are so many things you have to keep track of over the course of the semester journal entries, four different types of 'hours' that you have to fulfill, a service project, homework, filming yourself, etc. We would sometimes be notified of something we had to do by tomorrow at 10pm or of something we should prepare for class the next day that we never ended up doing. The grade weight of our midterm changed after it had been submitted but before it could've been graded. Also make sure to get a good seat in the center of the room because if you sit on the sides she won't ever look at you or sign to you so you won't learn as well. Basically, this class kind of has to become your whole life if you want to take it.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1625203\n 1625203\n \n 1625203\n A big strength in this course, and more broadly the entire ASL department at Yale, is the plentiful learning opportunities outside of class. There are so many potential community events (movie nights, athletic events, baking nights, signing tables, etc.) that students can engage in, and it made learning ASL much more fun. Interacting with Deaf/HoH folks, as well as fellow ASL students, gave me insight into the accessibility barriers facing the community. However, there is room for improvement, especially with the service projects. At times, it felt a bit disorganized and rushed, especially toward the end. Additional planning toward the beginning of the semester and specific assignment of tasks to students would alleviate the issues. Additionally, some of the learning materials -- PowerPoint slides and Signing Naturally homework -- felt a bit hard to follow through during independent study. Perhaps making the slides more relevant (and less repetitive) and allowing students to revisit SN without repeating it all (if possible) would be highly beneficial. Otherwise, I've had a great experience thus far, and I'm looking forward to ASL 120!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627613\n 1627613\n \n 1627613\n Weaknesses: I think maybe making the syllabus a bit clearer. While we had a syllabus, the course itself did not follow everything on the syllabus and while what was expected to review was reviewed, the order and the actuality was very different. The other issue is the correlation of the course to the Signing Course on Canvas, as some of the signs learned in class did not correlate to those online and vice versa, especially since it used a different variant. I think some more practice of just fingerspelling would also be a huge boon to the course, as many other students I have talked to, including myself, had a bit of an issue following fingerspelling in the latter half of the course when learning new signs.\r\n\r\nStrengths: the course was well done to be taught by a deaf professor, as it really fully emersed the students into the culture of the deaf and gave us what we could look for and study. It felt more that there was aid than I have seen with other classes, which are usually taught by someone who is not usually a native speaker but rather someone who learned it. It felt more personal in that way, because we did not have to search for the culture to engage and spoke only in academic terms, but also culturally and socially which is often a problem I have with learning other languages in an academic setting. Engagement with the community was also very great as we were able to really get to know others who used ASL as a primary form of communication and there was a large community that we could learn with.
