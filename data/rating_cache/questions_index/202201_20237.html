A weakness of this course is the lack of problem sets. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1599808\n 1599808\n \n 1599808\n Though Professors Turner and Ezenwa brought a lot of interesting material to the course and delivered lectures that were indeed engaging, I'm not sure I would recommend taking the course for a grade for two primary reasons: grade scheme and organization. (If one isn't taking the course for a grade, then I would indeed recommend, see below.) Though the course appeared to have been billed in previous reviews as a gut, it certainly is not from a grading perspective; in fact, for a 200-level E&EB course, the class presented an academic challenge that was surprising and not because of the difficulty of the content. A number of factors, including a grade structure that leaves little room for any kind of feedback, a lack of clarity on certain key concepts that led to class-wide confusion on exams, as well as a fairly shocking lack of any kind of curve (despite a midterm distribution that was certainly not toward the upper end of what I have seen at Yale), make this course unnecessarily difficult to read in terms of what one might come away with. I took the course Credit-D, and I'd certainly caution against those (including students in biology majors) thinking that this course is as straightforward as the syllabus makes it seem. The grade distributions that the professors make available from past years cannot possibly be representative of what the final distribution looked like this year, and unless they make changes to the way the course is graded, be forewarned: it will not be an easy (or at least straightforward) A.\r\n\r\nAdditionally, the presentation of material certainly suffered from organizational issues. Concepts that seemed logical to pair together or would have flowed into or from each other in natural ways were separated into distant parts of the course. Lecture material tried to balance background from peer-reviewed work and specific experimental or observational examples with theoretical concepts, but these lines were often blurred such that it became difficult to understand what material was considered important knowledge for class assessments.\r\n\r\nThat said, Professors Turner and Ezenwa do really give absorbing lectures on topics of crucial importance for the past, present, and future of biology (from ecology to molecular bio) and for global public health. I would certainly recommend the course from a content-based standpoint; it will likely make a good fifth class if one plans on using a Credit-D (or if the grade structure is brought closer to the standard of most other Yale courses). Certainly, give it consideration if infectious diseases or even broader precepts of ecology or evolutionary biology are topics of interest, and keep an eye on how the structure of the course might otherwise have changed.
