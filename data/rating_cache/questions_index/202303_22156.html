RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1695505\n 1695505\n \n 1695505\n This course is rich in intellectual conversation and academic insight. Not only does H&P allow you the chance to look at such a broad and interactive span of history through the books we read, but H&P's discussions really invite you to be a part of the course. Why does Thucydides have so much credit when he practically states that he is saying what he thinks people said, not what they actually did? Is custom truly king? These are the questions that will be answered during this course are are among the many lists of strengths this class has to offer. As for weaknesses, all I really have to say is I wish I had more time for it. The readings are very thick and sometimes there really just isn't enough time to interact with the text as much as you'd like to.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1707800\n 1707800\n \n 1707800\n Without hyperbole, I can state that this course has no weaknesses. The discussions are greatly elevated by Professor Schillinger's comments -- and he does a fantastic job at ensuring that students participate fully. The "starter questions" are a fantastic addition that distinguished our section from my other DS classes. Furthermore, Professor Schillinger has a breath and depth of knowledge that stands out -- even among the DS faculty.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1716361\n 1716361\n \n 1716361\n The greatest strengths of DS H&P are the reading list, the professors, and the lectures. You read a ton of amazingly interesting things, have 8 incredibly brilliant and accomplished people teaching you about them, and have a weekly lecture from one of those people that is often earth-shattering. Its awesome. Prof. Schillinger, my professor, is especially a strength.\r\n\r\nThe only weakness is the workload. It is a lot of reading, and H&P readings are often both longer than Phil and more complex than Lit. However, thats what youre signing up for, and theyre not actually that bad! Besides, the readings are the core of the course, so DO THEM, and theyre also generally enjoyable and necessary for the course to function. Honestly, we read too little (theres so much more cool stuff that we dont read), but reading any more would be untenable and onerous.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1722871\n 1722871\n \n 1722871\n Strengths:\r\n- On the whole, amazing texts this is true across all three DS tracks, but the texts in H&P were generally super interesting and thought-provoking. If nothing else, H&P is great because it affords you the opportunity to read many amazing, seminal texts.\r\n- Professor Schillinger Prof. Schillinger is phenomenal, and he made H&P this past semester that much better. He comes to class with introductions to each thinker prepared, which were all deeply insightful and helped frame our class discussions. Prof. Schillinger does a great job at ensuring everyone's ideas are valued in class discussions. His feedback on essays is some of the most thorough and thoughtful I've ever received. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses:\r\n- H&P identity crisis H&P, as a DS first-semester track, I think, experienced an identity crisis on two fronts. First, the historical thought vs. political thought fault line. We went back and forth between reading works of history and works of political philosophy in the first semester. The class effectively illustrated the connections between the historians and the connections between the political philosophers, but not the connections between the two groups. Second, with respect to methodology. Not only did it sometimes feel jarring going back and forth between reading historical texts and philosophical texts, which require vastly different approaches, but I dont think I was ever entirely clear on what constitutes valuable analysis in H&P in general. Unlike in DS literature and philosophy, the way we discussed texts varied. Sometimes we took a more literary approach, close reading passages, discussing the texts characterization of historical figures, and reasoning associatively. Other times we took a more philosophical approach, endeavoring to fully unpack a thinkers position on an issue in more precise terms. Other times still our approach to texts resembled neither the literary approach nor the philosophical approach. It was often difficult to predict which approach to texts we would pursue, which sometimes made the class feel like a moving target.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1725056\n 1725056\n \n 1725056\n A notable strength of this course was the texts; I found them to be the most interesting of the three DS tracks. That said, the curriculum is not perfect, and towards the end of the course you have to work hard to contextualize and understand texts that become more isolated and spread out over history. That said, for the breadth of time and the genre of content that it covers, the curriculum is extremely high-quality and will prepare you to engage in conversations of historical political theory and philosophy.
