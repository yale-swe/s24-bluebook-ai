\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1521011\n 1521011\n \n 1521011\n Professor Velasco was definitely a strength of this course. He was dynamic and engaging, and he made everyone in the class feel truly welcome. I also really appreciated how he shaped the curriculum to include not only literary "canon" works like Don Quijote, but also a wide array of works by female and minority authors. The only weakness of the class was that occasionally the readings could be a bit long.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527505\n 1527505\n \n 1527505\n I loved how Professor Velasco engaged the class through a wide variety of mediums. Not only did we read Spanish texts, but we also listened to music, acted out plays, and interpreted art. I also think a strength was the class rapport. I felt very comfortable in this environment, which enabled me to participate more openly. One weakness is that we did not really receive grades throughout the semester, so I never really got a sense or benchmark for how I was doing in the class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527957\n 1527957\n \n 1527957\n Strengths: Amazingly passionate and fun professor, reasonable workload and accessible for people of many skill levels in Spanish, good syllabus, very chill class. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Sometimes I felt like reading one text for each lecture didnt give us time to fully explore each work and forced us to gloss over some of the longer novels.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536136\n 1536136\n \n 1536136\n The class had three main strengths: firstly, the professor and the TA (Professor Velasco and Yelsy) made the class extremely engaging by opening the floor for interesting discussions and facilitating an environment in which everyone was able to contribute their opinion. Secondly, the class covered a wide range of fascinating works, and students had the opportunity to delve deeper in any of the works of their choosing, as neither the essays nor the final had an extremely rigid structure that limited us our exploration of the literature to specific works. Lastly, the class made an incredible use of the Beinecke's resources and allowed us to examine (in person!) several ancient manuscripts and rare books. It was an incredible experience. \r\nThe one area for improvement is the amount of time spent on each work of literature. Understandably, covering one work per class period allows us to explore a much wider breadth of literature. However, the depth at which we can discuss literature is sacrificed when we can only spend seventy five minutes on it. It's also difficult for students to read and fully comprehend two hundred pages of literature and pick out what might be important for a single lecture period. It might be easier if, instead, the professor narrowed the selection of literature a little bit to allow for more discussion, or he chose slightly shorter works so that students can read each work in-depth and then have a rich discussion about it in class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536458\n 1536458\n \n 1536458\n Strengths: Prof. Velasco is really empathetic towards students and a phenomenal (and hilarious) lecturer. His knowledge about medieval literature -- not to mention his linguistic abilities -- is astounding!\r\nWeaknesses: A lot of the readings are in old Spanish, which I don't think helped improve my overall level of Spanish
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536684\n 1536684\n \n 1536684\n Strengths: Professor Velasco is definitely the biggest strength of the course: he's amazing! Very engaging, funny, and knowledgeable -- makes class so fun. I also loved the Beinecke sessions: it was so cool to be able to see the works of literature!\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: My main difficulty with the course was the large size. ~40 people in a lecture hall trying to have a small-seminar discussion just doesn't work as well -- although Professor Velasco and Yelsy were amazing and made it a good experience even so. It's a little intimidating, especially in a foreign language, and impedes active and equal participation. I would have preferred the course as a capped seminar (or at least a lecture with official sections). I think the readings could be adjusted -- some are very long and not as engaging as others. Finally, I would have preferred more structured assignments (the in-class essays felt vague), and more detailed feedback afterward to know what to improve. I wish my Spanish would have improved a little more, and I think more feedback (and a smaller course size) could help with that.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1537473\n 1537473\n \n 1537473\n There are so many strengths to this course, the most important of them being Prof. Velasco himself. I think a potential weakness of the course is that we only get to spend one class session on one work, so there isn't much time to go into depth with any of the works, but at the same time the breadth of the course allows us to survey a wide range of texts. One thing that may have been helpful is for Prof. Velasco to provide some of the historical context before we read the works, because I often found myself somewhat confused by the readings, and they often had to be clarified in class. Also, some of the readings felt slightly too long to finish in one homework's worth of work, but the overall workload in terms of other assignments was incredibly manageable (I loved that we had no exams!)
