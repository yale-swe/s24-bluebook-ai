\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1613340\n 1613340\n \n 1613340\n I wished the course could have been at an earlier time, so I would have personally been more engaged.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617114\n 1617114\n \n 1617114\n A strength of this course was the organization of assignments. It was useful for students to be able to choose when they will have their presentation in section and archival research paper and presentation, because they were able to chose a time that works best with their schedules, and during which they could put the effort and time into their assignments that the assignments required of them. The other deadlines were clearly communicated, including the deadline for the critical paper on liberation and final project. A weakness of this course was the workload, particularly the amount of readings that was assigned. Most weeks, the amount of pages of reading that were assigned exceeded the typical reading load for a humanities course in Yale College, and made it difficult for students to truly understand and analyze what they were reading, given the large number of pages assigned. Additionally, the directions for some assignments were not very specific and lacked clarity. Although this gave flexibility for students to pick and chose what to focus on for their assignments, it did not make the expectations behind the student work clear.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1622233\n 1622233\n \n 1622233\n A couple of major strengths of the course are the classroom environment and the syllabus; the readings were always interesting and worth it. A fault is the organizational structure. It seems to me that in the past, the course agenda was crafted with the thought in mind that students would want to stay past the class time to continue discussion and learning, and so by the time class was over this past semester, we would not be near to done with the scheduled events. I think it's fair to have after-class discussion for people who want it, but I don't think it's the best model to account for students wanting to be there for that time, especially considering it's a class that ends at 8:50 at night. The course would benefit from organizational streamlining so that determining logistics doesn't take up class time, and a simplified, realistic agenda so that people don't feel they're missing out if they leave at the time the class is supposed to end, presenters don't get cut off, and there's class time reserved for the discussions that are supposed to happen.
