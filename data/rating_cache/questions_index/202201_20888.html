\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1562399\n 1562399\n \n 1562399\n This course is genuinely one of the best courses I have taken so far at Yale, even though it was VERY high workload and challenging topics wise towards the beginning to the point that I was considering dropping the class (and the chemistry major itself too). I went into the course thinking it was just something I would have to get through for my major but ended up loving it so much that I am reconsidering what type of research I might be interested in doing in the future and also what type of classes I want to take going forward. What kept me going though the initial hardship was the teaching staff who really was so so supportive. Somebody was always there to help you with your questions and furthermore, all of them were also very approachable; they were always eager to help and welcoming, so I never had to worry about being a nuisance or asking "stupid" questions. One of the biggest strengths was also the biggest weakness. What I'm referring to is the grading and assignment system. Even though the 85% threshold and 3 tries system for psets was absolutely pain in terms of workload but also for morale (when you keep getting back failing grades for psets you put a lot of effort into, then that doesn't necessarily make you joyous about taking the course), then I must admit that the system did do what it was set out to do, which is make sure that you really(!) do know the material. I can say with complete confidence that the content for this class is solidified in my brain so well solely because of the sheer amount of practice that I was required to do to pass the psets. So I have a very love-hate relationship with that system. I guess if just one of your classes has that system, then it is great, however, I cannot imagine doing that for multiple classes at the same time.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1582531\n 1582531\n \n 1582531\n Pat was a really nice professor and tried hard to make the class fun/get us to learn. It was nice that we were encouraged to collaborate so much in class/in discussion. I thought the class environment was overall really friendly and welcoming. What I didn't enjoy so much was the grading scheme and lecture organization for this course. The lectures sometimes felt a bit scattered and the slides weren't always super thorough, but we did a lot of group activities in class which might be why it was thought that the slides didn't need to be so instructive on their own. In terms of grading, I like the idea of specification grading (giving students three chances on all homework and exams to master the material) as it relieves some stress if you were having a bad day on a homework or exam...but it sometimes felt like the thresholds were a bit strict and we ended up having large time commitments if we decided to go to all the exam retakes. It was a bit stressful to me that some of our exams were out of a small number of points (around 30), meaning that we could only lose a couple points to be over a 90% for the A-grade specification. I understand that I should be able to score nearly perfectly if I truly get the material, but I find that even on my best of days, I am still capable of missing a few questions just by a stupid math error or coming across a concept that I somehow missed in studying (after all, we do have multiple other classes besides this one and can't expect to thoroughly cover every subtlety that might come up). In the future, maybe the exams could be out of more points (50-100) to allow some more flexibility (though I understand that this might be slightly more time-consuming for TAs to grade). Or, when we do the exam retakes, maybe we could just re-do the types of questions that we missed on previous attempts in order to minimize TA grading/extensive studying (students could just focus on the material that they didn't get to study as thoroughly before). Maybe there could also be a study guide/review sheet before each exam that very explicitly lists the things we need to know (e.g. key acids/bases, model crystal structures, ligands, etc.) so that we can prepare most efficiently to hopefully reach the pre-determined threshold for grade boundaries.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1595870\n 1595870\n \n 1595870\n A big strength of the course (and Dr. Holland) was the receptivity to feedback and usage of the feedback to better the course organization as it was still happening. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1583762\n 1583762\n \n 1583762\n I would, but it's important to understand how this class was structured. The way that Prof. Holland ran the class is specification-based grading, meaning that there was no cumulative grade. Instead, one had to get a certain grade or above on midterms, problem sets, the final, and a paper-review assignment. Everything except the last two had three tries, problem sets could be redone by doing new problems akin to those missed on the first try and the midterms were full retakes. Answers were posted after the originals of each assignment were due. Having said that, I think the structure worked really well. It was conducive to actually learning everything in the class, and the multiple tries took a lot of the stress out of things. At first, we had to redo the entire problem set if we didn't hit the mark, but after that was changed things became even more manageable. \r\nAs for the teaching itself, I'd say it was generally good but that it could sometimes be hit or miss. Some classes we would walk out gushing, some we would walk out having no idea what just happened. I think the class hit its stride halfway through the year and things started to come together and make sense--and the structure of the class became more regular then as well. It was also Prof. Holland's first time teaching the class, and I think it will get tighter and better as time goes on.
