\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1528318\n 1528318\n \n 1528318\n I loved this course! The readings were varied and interesting. The in-class discussions were enjoyable and never felt like a waste of time. The writing assignments provided good opportunities for practice and feedback on writing. The professor was amazing. The course and assignments were impeccably organized. I always knew what to expect and what was expected of me. The only unit I didn't like was Dante, because it's impossible to provide all the context necessary to enjoy the piece in the short time that we had. Otherwise, I loved the poems and poets that we focused on in class.\r\n\r\nThe writing labs could be improved if they were half as long and gave students more flexibility. Sessions about things like writing emails are great because they provide valuable skills to students who might not have come from privileged backgrounds, but students who already know what is being taught should have the option to spend the allotted time on other things. The zoom speakers during labs were also not enjoyable. Covid precautions are necessary of course, but zoom for three hours on a Friday afternoon is a bit of a stretch for students.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1529720\n 1529720\n \n 1529720\n Strengths: Professor Bender is an amazing instructor who explains concepts in such a concise and illuminating manner that brings you into a text really quickly. The TAs have been really helpful and patient in their feedback. The poems and readings in this class are also very engaging - it offers a much more inclusive body of text as compared to DS and enables you to see the diverse forms of poetry and how people attach different concepts and norms to it, rather than being fixated in a usually euro-centric standard. You get to practice being on your foot and engage with other peoples observations.\r\n\r\nWeakness: \r\n\r\n1 the sheer breadth of material we cover in this class means we cant engage with any of the texts deeply - not the kind of depth where one spends an entire year just studying one text and knowing it at the tip of your tongue - but just the depth where one would feel confident explaining why reading this poem is important i.e. why should we care about this poem, knowing that you are actually convinced of its worth even though you might not personally resonate with it. I feel that level of engagement is a minimum for the meaningful approach to any text at all. If not, it would be no different from just reading sparknotes and FEELING like you know everything about it already, when in fact you only know fragmented bits where the text appears interesting. \r\n\r\n2 a seminar needs to be driven by student input, and a group made up of only first-year students might not be the best deal where we tend to make scattered observations here and there - sometimes they are illuminating - but not conducive for developing a coherent understanding of the text as a whole. \r\n\r\n3 although it is a writing credit, I feel the writing workshops offer limited help in terms of actually improving our writing skills. Peer review is great, but not so much when everyone in the room is a first-year student who isn't entirely clear what is required in a college-level essay. The best way to learn writing is not to listen to a thousand tips and advice but to actually write something, get feedback, work on it again - but the chances to get feedback in this class are really limited, not to mention that two out of six assignments are about writing museum labels which seem relatively irrelevant. Im not sure whether there could be better ways to teach students how to cite secondary resources in an academic article. Functional writing and academic writing are very different practices - yes there are similarities and its important to learn different genres of writing, but Im not sure whether its worth this huge detour and it remains a rather inefficient, confusing approach within the scope of this class to me. \r\n\r\nIn general, I believe the entire six pretty good series could have been a wonderful opportunity for first-year students to engage with the broader humanities subjects primarily through their selected perspective (e.g. poems, journeys), but I feel the current curriculum design is slightly too ambitious, resulting in a highly condensed and thus somehow chaotic experience - think about 100 students trying to have a discussion, or having 4-5 consecutive loosely-related curator talks on a Friday afternoon. Our seminar and the entire six pretty good series would have benefited from a more focused and coherent approach, and possibly a smaller class size. I wouldnt say I regret taking this class - but I genuinely believe the six pretty good series has a lot of potentials to be a much more inspiring class.
