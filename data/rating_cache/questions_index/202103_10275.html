\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1543044\n 1543044\n \n 1543044\n This course was very strong because it combined chalk talks which were great for conceptual learning and slides which had many examples and cool things that got me excited about the content. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544001\n 1544001\n \n 1544001\n Strength is the spread of knowledge which does not force someone to try to be a botanist overnight, but allows for appreciation to the width of the field. Weakness is the amount of information given orally or written on a board which makes it slightly harder to go back and pick up details when compared to courses with all published slides and texts.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548711\n 1548711\n \n 1548711\n The slideshows showcasing the breadth of diversity were really interesting, and the board lecture format was really useful for learning the biological processes we discussed in the class - it was a good pace, and the drawings helped to explain the material. The quizzes were definitely helpful in preparing for the midterm and final exams. For the slideshows and overall, a bit more structure would have been helpful for connecting the classes together, since there was so much information presented that it was sometimes hard to know what to focus on/take away from a specific lecture and how it related to other concepts we discussed. For the papers, it was interesting to read them and see what types of papers are written in this field, but sometimes they felt disconnected from the topics we discussed in class since they seemed to be discussing topics at either a much more specific or much broader level than the material we covered. It was great to watch everyone's creature cast projects at the end of the class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1551078\n 1551078\n \n 1551078\n This class is extremely heavy on memorization and testing to the extent that it detracted from the learning experience. I also felt like the expectations for what we were supposed to study were never clear in advance. There were two midterms, three quizzes, and then a take-home final exam. It felt like we were being tested relentlessly almost every week in the middle of the semester, and studying (even just for the quizzes) was a vast undertaking, given how hella confusing literally everything was. When we got tests back, they were scored without a lot of feedback, so there was no way to know the correct answers (or even trust that things that were marked correct were actually right, as the TFs were known to let accuracy slide on tough questions). So it was hard to learn from the tests/quizzes, and there was minimal feedback provided beyond them. There was SO MUCH to memorize all the time--confusing life cycles, names and characteristics of clades, phylogenies, etc. These challenges were compounded by the fact that we were given very confusing expectations about what to study for each test. There was one test where we were explicitly told that we only needed to know stuff that we'd learned since the previous midterm, and then guess what was actually on the test--stuff from before then! So the tests were very frustrating, and they were a very significant proportion of the class. \r\n\r\nIn the future, I would strongly recommend replacing the quizzes with problem sets. This would help students engage with the material in a constructive way and help students learn what to expect from tests in a lower-stakes way. This would make the class a significantly less stressful experience, and would be way more helpful than blindly studying everything before a quiz and then realizing that you'd studied all the wrong things, which is how I felt after every quiz.
