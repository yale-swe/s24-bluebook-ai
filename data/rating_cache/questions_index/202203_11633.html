\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627725\n 1627725\n \n 1627725\n Absolutely not. I consider myself to be a rather calm person, but this class brought me dangerously close to the level of fury only ever reached when I was dumped during finals week last year. The first few weeks of the class function as an intellectual indoctrination into the professor's preferred evolutionary theory, the Extended Evolutionary Synthesis as opposed to the Modern Synthesis. The first two classes or so are feigned as a collaborative effort to debate the two theories and reach a conclusion together, but the ending will always be the same. This class operates under the lens of the EES, and no amount of valid rebuttal will change that. You will be dragged along into a semester's worth of reading, papers, and discussions all engineered to bolster a theory you never got the choice to believe in. Giving us the illusion of choice and intellectual discussion put a bad taste in my mouth right from the start. \r\n\r\nThe rest of the semester was more digestible since we moved away from theory for the most part. Readings were interesting sometimes. Other times they were about theoretical archaeology and performance theory. Oftentimes I wondered to myself "what is the point? why is this even an issue?" with the "this" being whether the archaeology is better defined as the performance of doing archaeology or the study of artifacts in the archives.\r\n\r\nThe papers are another gripe I have with this course. They're short, thankfully, with the first two being 3-4 pages and the final paper being 4-5 pages. But the prompts are incredibly vague, and to this day I still don't understand my feedback. Feedback consists of lots of red strikethroughs that replace words and phrases with synonymous words and phrases. It feels like you're being graded on the extend to which you write in Professor Tomlinson's voice. The only good feedback I got was when I regurgitated the things he said in class. The assignments occupy an odd space, being largely about topics in evolutionary study but requiring the humanities-style prose of a music professor.\r\n\r\nI couldn't tell you how other students felt about the course, because each 2-hour class turned into Professor Tomlinson's personal echo chamber / fan club as the semester went on. Never have I felt so outcasted in an academic environment. Perhaps it is because I am not a humanities major, so I am unfamiliar with the flattery and factiousness required to succeed in the classroom, but it was jarring to say the least.
