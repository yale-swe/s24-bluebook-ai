The grading distribution is very manageable and allows for engagement with a number of different paper styles and research.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1613786\n 1613786\n \n 1613786\n weakness: African history - very broad a lot of info so hard to cover
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1615093\n 1615093\n \n 1615093\n I wish we had more grades than just the three papers, such as weekly reading responses. I also wish the lecture style had varied more, as it could get very monotonous. Finally, I thought that the spacing of the due dates of the three papers in addition to the final exam was a little too close together. It felt like everything was lumped in at the end. I also do not think there needed to be a final essay exam, as I had already written about two of the 4 prompts in my papers.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617054\n 1617054\n \n 1617054\n I really enjoyed the lectures, I thought they were very engaging and interesting. Professor Machava was a great lecturer as well and discussion sections and readings were interesting and relevant to the course.\r\n\r\nThe course got a bit confusing at the end, which I think is due to the nature of the history of postcolonial Africa - sometimes it was hard to keep track of what events were happening in each country/what systems of governance each country had. Also, in terms of the work for the course, we always focused on broader topics (the prompts were very broad) which posed challenges.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617750\n 1617750\n \n 1617750\n Strengths: Professor Machava is a great lecturer and we should absolutely have more classes on African history. There were so many times when he would discuss something in lecture and I would wish we could spend more time on it. Weaknesses: I'm guessing this is universally applicable to history lectures, but most people came to section unprepared and did not engage in discussion.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627018\n 1627018\n \n 1627018\n I found the lectures to be super engaging and the material to be highly interesting. I think one small procedural change could be editing the Canvas page to make it more obvious which readings are for which lectures. I think it also would be helpful to have a bit more guidance and early discussion about final papers to make that process more straightforward.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1638279\n 1638279\n \n 1638279\n The strengths of the course were Professor Machava's lectures and the course material that we read. The class was incredibly engaging, and the teaching team and our material greatly enhanced my understanding of each lecture topic. However, the weaknesses of the course was that I feel like it didn't dive as deep into the material as I wished it had. My teaching fellow didn't provide much analysis or ask us very many questions during our discussion sections, often leaving me feeling dissatisfied with the level of depth with which we explored the material. Moreover, I sometimes felt that class was cancelled when it didn't need to be in order to allow us to work on our assignments. The subject matter of the class was so interesting that I just wish we had had more of it--I felt like I didn't learn as much as I hoped I would at the beginning of the semester.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639067\n 1639067\n \n 1639067\n Strengths - nice professor, approachable TFs, interesting readings, variation of assignments\r\nWeaknesses - unclear/slow feedback, too broad a time and geographic range
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639567\n 1639567\n \n 1639567\n Where this course excels is as an entry point for Yale students interested in African history but without any prior knowledge or background. This course was nothing if not accessible, and gave me a clear and solid understanding of how colonialism varied across the continent and has continued to harm Africa. That said, the course was perhaps a little too accessible. I rarely felt challenged or pushed intellectually. While its understandable that a course on such an enormous topic would default to broad strokes, I do feel we could have gone much deeper. One good example is Timothy Snyders Eastern Europe to 1914, which also covers a sprawling period but does not sacrifice detail or rigor.
