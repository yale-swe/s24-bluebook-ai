\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527927\n 1527927\n \n 1527927\n I thought the course was incredibly well taught. The class was rigorous, but still had an emphasis on student learning and not simple busy-work. I truly believe that everything we did had a purpose and was part of a larger mission to gain an understanding of environmentalism in China. \r\n\r\nI enjoyed how the class was largely discussion-based, but still incorporated elements of lectures. Going into the class I did not have an understanding of the nuances of the Maoist era, but the "mini-lectures" Li Laoshi conducted allowed me to understand the context of the literature we were reading and allowed me to participate in the discussion better. \r\n\r\nLi Laoshi also required us to post discussion posts before every class, which I found incredibly helpful. It kept me accountable for the readings and allowed me to know generally what I'm getting into before the class starts to better prepare for discussion. \r\n\r\nI would say the biggest strength of this course is having Li Laoshi as a professor. The class's topic in and of itself was interesting, but Li Laoshi really made the class an environment where every student can succeed and take something away from each class meeting.\r\n\r\nThe only weakness of the class is that it was too small. With only 5 students, there sometimes weren't enough differing ideas to have a rigorous discussion, resulting in a potentially interesting conversation dying out.

I think this class is a great introductory class into the myriad of environmental problems in China, from plastic waste to air pollution to ethnic conflict to the Belt and Road initiative and my only suggestion would be to offer more higher-level courses on the specific themes we touched on, so that we could study them in greater depth
