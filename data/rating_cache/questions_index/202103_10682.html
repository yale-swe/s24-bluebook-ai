\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1522339\n 1522339\n \n 1522339\n A far deeper understanding of how, why, and who we punish as a society. A comprehensive overview of the prima facie justifications of punishment (retributivism, rehabilitation, deterrence) and the ways they compliment/contradict each other. A chance to apply theory in more practical case studies of the death penalty, sex predator laws, and prison abolition.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1530385\n 1530385\n \n 1530385\n The course covers all traditional theoretical justifications for punishment-- retribution, deterrence, etc.-- and introduces students to evolving understandings of punishment, such as moral education theory. We discussed different actors involved in punishment, institutions used for punishment, and alternatives to our current carceral system.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1530496\n 1530496\n \n 1530496\n A review of penological literature and the reasons why a government punishes its citizens. Probably one of the most insightful courses I've taken at Yale.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538115\n 1538115\n \n 1538115\n I learned more about the theoretical justifications for punishment and both the strengths/implications of these justifications as well as the weaknesses of/challenges to them. I've gained insight into how society's justifications for punishment don't necessarily match up with its actual practice, and how we need to truly think deeply about what the purpose of punishment is.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539243\n 1539243\n \n 1539243\n I really learned how to deeply examine my normal way of thinking and how to communicate my ideas based on the experiences I've had. I really learned how to listen and be open enough to what I heard in order to change my thoughts.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1547643\n 1547643\n \n 1547643\n engage critically with the theory of punishment and build a more nuanced understanding of what justifies punishment
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549431\n 1549431\n \n 1549431\n An overview of the different main purposes of punishment (retribution, deterrence, incapacitation, rehabilitation, etc.) along with various case studies like the "battered wife", acts of a sexual nature, the death penalty, and more. We worked within all these ideas in a very philosophical/normative framework.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549433\n 1549433\n \n 1549433\n Deepen understanding of theoretical questions around difficult ethical legal questions. Didnt find any answers but thats part of the draw learned so much about my own moral tendencies from discussions in class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549705\n 1549705\n \n 1549705\n I rethought the fundamental underpinnings of the connection between crime and punishment. This course allowed me to understand criminal justice through numerous new lenses and question the justifications for punishment in the American system.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549863\n 1549863\n \n 1549863\n A deep dive into the legal, political, and philosophical theories and justifications for punishment, with a focus on American legal jurisprudence. Topics included the death penalty, deterrence and incapacitation, prisoners' rights, forgiveness and mercy, the role of vengeance, humiliation, and shame, and sex crimes. This class really taught me to think critically and analytically about law at a higher order of complexity than I had before.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1550344\n 1550344\n \n 1550344\n A really challenging and unique course it taught me to think critically about punishment, carceral systems, and the way we apply punishment/shame/discipline in our daily lives, perhaps without thinking. We talked about punishment in theoretical/philosophical terms, but also in more applicable ways (looking at law and policy), so people who are interested in law as well as people who just like political theory/philosophy can get a lot out of this course.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1522339\n 1522339\n \n 1522339\n Only positive things to say about this course. The readings, both in a particular week and in how they built off-each other over the course of the semester, were chosen well. The discussion, with facilitation from Prof. Rosas, always got into the meat of a given issue, forced people to challenge their views, and generally was of higher quality than anything else I have seen in the PLSC department. The major assignments were also well-designed to not just test our knowledge, but our ability to tease out connections between different concepts. The final paper was also a great opportunity to deep-dive.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538115\n 1538115\n \n 1538115\n Professor Rosas was a huge strength of this course; he took the comments students made seriously and often challenged them in class or asked follow up questions (not in an intimidating way at all, but in such a way that it made students think more deeply about the arguments they were making). The readings and the way the course was clearly divided into larger subtopics was also a huge strength. The final paper also lends itself to a lot of flexibility and so you're able to explore anything that interests you. The Oral Reading Reaction Assignment was also a huge strength! It sounds incredibly intimidating, but Professor Rosas makes you feel very comfortable and it's very low stakes; it's a great opportunity to have a one-on-one conversation about a specific issue that particularly interests/concerns you. The only weakness of the course I can think of is not really the course's fault, but I think the classroom we were in made it difficult to have seminar discussions given that our table was a long rectangle-like table and it was difficult to see each other's faces. I think it would be helpful to shift to a larger (or at least, not as narrow) classroom.

Also, Rosas' continuous feedback is incredible, and it's clear he puts a lot of time into giving the fairest, clearest feedback he can to each student, and absolutely takes their ideas and work seriously.
