\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552799\n 1552799\n \n 1552799\n One strength of this course was that a very close-knit dynamic developed among the students, due to the variety of activities (acting, brainstorming, discussing, movement, etc.) during and outside of class. However, the four-hour class time felt unnecessarily long at times, especially since 2+ hours of outside group meetings were often required each week. The class length make the course inaccessible to students with time conflicts and other commitments several friends have told me that they were considering the TAPS major but weren't able to take this class specifically due to the length. Additionally, I found that class time would be better used if a substantial portion of it were set aside for group meetings without outside meetings required. Scheduling those outside meetings was always difficult, especially since most students were involved in extracurricular theatrical productions with extensive rehearsal schedules. Otherwise, if outside meetings were kept, this course would work just as well, if not better, in a 2-hour, once-a-week format. By the third and fourth hours of class, I often found it harder to focus, despite having taken breaks, and I felt that some activities could have been cut or shortened. \r\n\r\nAdditionally, I would have preferred to do the consent workshop earlier on in the semester, and to accompany it with a discussion about consent and immersive performance. For both of those classes, a second option (or a whole roster such as the ones offered for the projects) would help to accommodate students who felt uncomfortable with the first option; for example, one of my peers was highly triggered by the suicide-related content in Night, Mother, and as a Jewish student I felt deeply affected by Fires in the Mirror and uncomfortable when the themes of Jewish trauma and modern antisemitism went entirely unmentioned in the class discussion.
