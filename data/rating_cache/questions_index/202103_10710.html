Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1531421\n 1531421\n \n 1531421\n This course was strong in that it offered an excellent range of literature and taught me a lot about how to dissect real political science articles. I feel well-prepared to critique research and understand it better now. \r\n\r\nHowever, I feel like too much of the class was dedicated to critique, and there was little to no explanation. Sometimes we would jump into a very complex article without any preparation, making discussion confusing and disjointed, as everyone had different understandings. I also feel like my knowledge was never systematized. There was no overarching structure to the course, so every week felt like a new and disconnected strand of information. Even when there were intersections between articles, I feel like I never got a comprehensive picture of the field.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532856\n 1532856\n \n 1532856\n The readings are simply unmanageable. Certainly they are very interesting, and beneficial to our class discussions, but regardless of how much time I allocated to doing the readings for this class I was never able to finish them. What I ended up having to do was pick about half of the required readings and read them in full, and then just skim over the remaining ones. Considering that I did not know ahead of time which readings were more relevant or interesting or academically sound, I would have preferred if we had simply been assigned less readings so that I could give them all my full attention rather than abstractedly picking and choosing which ones to read and which to merely look over. Beyond this, I have no complaints. The class was well-structured, the lectures/discussions were engaging and informative, and the paper topics provided a fair opportunity for us to demonstrate our understanding of the content.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1547602\n 1547602\n \n 1547602\n I loved the interdisciplinary nature of the course. The readings are very interesting and cover anthropology, history, game theory, economics, and political science. Class discussions are great in facilitating understanding of the readings, which can sometimes be difficult and dense to understand. Professor Queralt is great at explaining the papers and helping the class understand the material. The only thing that could be improved is if there was a bit more time for students to discuss the readings amongst themselves.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549039\n 1549039\n \n 1549039\n Strengths: The course readings are very well curated and always super interesting. The students in the class were also one of its best qualities -- everyone brought super interesting lived experience and academic backgrounds to the class, and that made seminar discussions a real highlight of my week. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: The course was not well organized to facilitate student learning as well as it could be. While seminar discussions were excellent sources of clarification on different theories and ideas, there was very little written or oral feedback on assignments. The bread and butter of the course are 2 page reading responses the critique the readings, and while Professor Queralt grades those papers quite harshly on a consistent basis, he provides very few comments on what students could do better.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1556354\n 1556354\n \n 1556354\n I must say the readings are a relative strength, I nearly looked forward to them (especially the non-econometric papers, as my background in statistics was not as strong, and even some of the readings were characterized as much harder, particularly in this statistical aspect, than others). The response papers got pretty annoying, pretty fast. However, I do recommend doing them, and doing as many as you can. I found that I was often more prepared for the discussion the next class period after doing/preparing a response paper.\r\nTo the professor, I do recommend making fewer response papers mandatory, and assigning a constant number of extra credit points to be given for each additional response paper turned in (assuming some minimum threshold is met; I think this might help get the incentives "more right"). Additionally, I found the grading to be QUITE HARSH, in just about everything except attendance (but I'll be honest, I thought that was going to be a lot harsher than it was). The other graded assignments (besides attendance) were response papers and short papers, and at least for what I got feedback on, I often and DEEPLY questioned whether the feedback given matched the points deducted, with some instances being that I thought the instructor simply got it wrong. I don't know if English is the professor's second language, but it often felt like some things were "lost in translation". \r\n\r\nAdditionally, expectations for assignments were not nearly as clear as they could have been, which may tie into the grading woes (especially on the response papers and short papers).\r\n\r\nGoing back to the grading woes, I think part of the frustration was attributable not really having any idea where you were at in the course as it progressed (response papers were graded out of three, and it was as hard as anything to get a three, which means students were potentially out on a limb with a bunch 66% for assignments, in what was then expected to be an un-curved class), and no grades were posted to canvas or anything else. \r\n\r\nAdditionally, I do not know if the professor went through with this, but they suggested curving the class late into the semester, which would have brought some grades up, but it seems at least unheard of to curve a class of around 12-15 students.
