\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1695655\n 1695655\n \n 1695655\n It's hard... I took AP Physics C in high school so I thought I was ready for this class. I think PHYS 200 is too familiar for me and PHYS 260 is too hard. Prof. Harris is great at explaining things but it's hard to keep up.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1705020\n 1705020\n \n 1705020\n The biggest weakness of this course is that lectures are not recorded - if you miss a class you're behind the entire semester and it gets very difficult to understand the material on your own.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1706650\n 1706650\n \n 1706650\n Strengths were that it lived up to its name of being an intensive course. It embraced that messy math or physics that other classes probably would've handwaved away, which is good, and it really did feel like I was learning more about how we formulate physics on top of just learning how to use it to solve problems. Weaknesses were that classes could be overwhelming sometimes, especially with Jack Harris never solving problems out on the board and just having avalanches of typeset math on his slides instead. Exams also felt disconnected from the actual content, as many of them relied on problem solving experience and mathematical tricks instead of the ground-up derivations we were doing in class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1708877\n 1708877\n \n 1708877\n The many, many office hours offered by this course were extremely useful in helping me catch everything that was being thrown at us throughout the semester. The difficulty of this class was actually a blessing in that it made us get together to knock out the problem sets, which was great for making friends and for eventually studying in groups for the exams.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1712977\n 1712977\n \n 1712977\n Strengths: the material and slides were most excellent. In particular, I enjoyed Prof Harris's way of inquisitively explaining things. It felt like he was walking us through the reasoning used to derive the equations, rather than simply throwing the equations at us and expecting us to memorize them. Many of my previous instructors (both at Yale and elsewhere) would do well to emulate his style.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses and ways to improve: I legitimately can't think of any.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1719381\n 1719381\n \n 1719381\n I think that the preparation for the midterms can be improved and the course is very challenging but my knowledge of how to deal with that has also greatly improved.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1721728\n 1721728\n \n 1721728\n I think the strength of the course is it is intellectually challenging and the topics are arranged in a good order. However, I think it could be improved if some of the topics we covered in homework/exams was more physics concept based and less of just mathematics problems phrased with physics concepts.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1722591\n 1722591\n \n 1722591\n Strengths:\r\n- The content is good and it is quite a solid and fun introductory physics course, the problem sets are also good practice of the things we learned in class\r\n- The instructor explains how theorems come about (e.g. their basic assumptions and their derivation)\r\n- The support for this course is quite good (e.g. the TA office hours)\r\nWeaknesses:\r\n- This course is pretty good actually
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1723994\n 1723994\n \n 1723994\n Strengths: Lectures were engaging and interesting; I gained a different understanding of mechanics and greater intuition of core topics\r\nWeaknesses: Lectures felt a bit all over the place at times in terms of pacing and how long we would spend on things; sometimes problems that we focused on seemed very esoteric and exams tended to be centered around those specific problems and examples we worked through in class rather than testing the concepts we were learning more broadly. Additionally, practice problems provided before the exams were very disconnected from the actual exam content.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724367\n 1724367\n \n 1724367\n I think the main strength of the course might also be the biggest drawback for some. This course is made as a challenge and it expects a lot of knowledge beforehand. However, it is also really fun and the lectures by Prof. Harris were always something to look forward to.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724694\n 1724694\n \n 1724694\n The course was extremely well-taught. Dr. Harris explained topics in a very deep and ground-up way. The homework and exam problems were usually very interesting. The class was conceptually challenging but very manageable with sufficient effort, due to Dr. Harris's awesome teaching skills. Dr. Harris allowed a lot of time for questions from students during class and always gave deep and helpful answers. He also clearly cared a lot about helping students learn and about helping students keep their confidence when they encountered challenges. Furthermore, while I did not attend TA or ULA office hours often, I heard from other students that office hours were great and the TAs and ULAs were wonderful.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1710880\n 1710880\n \n 1710880\n Yes - with a few caveats.\r\n\r\nFirst, I would only recommend this course to people who've taken physics in the past. Although you do learn many of the concepts from the ground up, I would say it's somewhat difficult to apply them to problems unless you have experience with how a system behaves on a bigger-picture, more simplified scale, and consequently what assumptions you can make. Because I found myself learning familiar concepts with greater detail and rigor in this course, I'd imagine it would be easy to get "lost in the weeds" of the math and derivations for someone seeing these for the first time. Still, learning where formulas and equations came from and how they could be generalized to different situations was very illuminating and worthwhile for my understanding of physics.\r\n\r\nSecond, you should have a solid calculus foundation to at least Calc 2. It's definitely doable to take this concurrently with Calc 3, because div/grad/curl only show up for certain computations, but you should understand infinitesimals, limits, and where derivatives/integrals come from, not just how to do them. It also helps to know some differential equations (second order in particular), but that can be picked up throughout the course.\r\n\r\nThird, if you don't want to major in physics, or are very confident in your skills, this class might be a much larger workload than it's worth. Still, I would enroll in this class if you're interested and determine whether or not to switch to PHYS 200 or PHYS 180 as it progresses. In general, talk to previous students, go to office hours, and work with your peers to make a judgement for yourself.
