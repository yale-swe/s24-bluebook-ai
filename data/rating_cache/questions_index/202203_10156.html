\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618256\n 1618256\n \n 1618256\n I personally loved every aspect of this course; I think the assessments were fair and I appreciated that there were multiple opportunities to improve your grade. As I mentioned before, I was especially fond of the final term paper.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1623410\n 1623410\n \n 1623410\n Strengths of the course: teacher, content, grade structure/graded workload.\r\nWeaknesses of the course: pacing of the course, non-graded workload.\r\nImprovements: Spending less class time on the actual translation (students should be required to prepare three questions about the translation before each class and we can go over that instead). The rest of the time could be spent on actual analysis of the text, which sometimes was lost given the amount we had to get done each night. Also, maybe spreading out the material a bit more. Some passages did not feel as necessary for us to read, while some extremely necessary passages were lost or under-examined.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624549\n 1624549\n \n 1624549\n This was a fantastic course. Prof. Freudenburg is both an extremely insightful authority on Vergil and a very chill, laid-back professor. It's a lot of daily translation (as expected for an upper-level Latin seminar), but the discussions are great. I came away from almost every class feeling like I'd learned a lot and grappled with some really interesting stuff.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627348\n 1627348\n \n 1627348\n It was consistent, well-taught, and enjoyable. The course material was reliably interesting. Really, no weaknesses come to mind.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627509\n 1627509\n \n 1627509\n Love the format of this course with reading every time we meet usually preceded by a brief lecture from Prof. Freudenburg about relevant historical/mythological/artistic content.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627917\n 1627917\n \n 1627917\n The course is very well taught, the seminar style is conducive to the learning objectives, the material is interesting. I loved the multimedia elements and wish there could've been more, maybe as enrichment options.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630567\n 1630567\n \n 1630567\n Strengths: Professor Freudenburg is a wonderful teacher. He is friendly to his students, and everything he has to say in class is fascinating--it is a pleasure to hear him talk about the Aeneid. He is also very responsive to the class's needs and ideas. I liked the quizzes and thought these were a great way to check my learning every few weeks that wasn't incredibly stressful. I thought the Aeneid excerpts were well-picked. I loved the YUAG visit. Weaknesses: I would have appreciated more direction on how to do a close reading, and how to formulate this into a final paper. Simply a one-page instruction sheet with some do's and don't's would have been helpful for people who haven't done a paper in this style before. I also thought the readings got too long towards the end of the semester. We started out with 60-80 lines per reading, but by the end of the semester, more of the readings were over 100 lines which became overwhelming as workload increases around this time anyways.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1636308\n 1636308\n \n 1636308\n The strength of this course is that it goes so much beyond being a simple Latin course to become a course devoted to understanding the mind of Vergil (as much as possible in a semester's work) when writing the Aeneid by analyzing the text in its original Latin. By translating and examining key passages from all twelve books of the Aeneid, we both looked over the breadth of Vergil's range without sacrificing much of our ability to examine the depth of his writings in our haste to get through the entire text. By having many students in the class translate and comment on the text, we were able to bounce ideas off of each other and consider how the Aeneid fit into the broader framework of classical literature. However, at times we spent too much time discussing one element of the text such that later we had to speed through the latter half of our translation.
