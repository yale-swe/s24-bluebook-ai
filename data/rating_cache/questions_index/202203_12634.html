\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1607222\n 1607222\n \n 1607222\n The Grand Strategy program is the best course I have taken at Yale. Definitely apply if you are interested!!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1608103\n 1608103\n \n 1608103\n The small group nature of the modules was one of my favorite formats of any class at Yale, and our practitioner Kica Matos has been a massive personal, professional, and educational inspiration.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617862\n 1617862\n \n 1617862\n Love the module style and the weekly reading responses, though sometimes tedious, did ensure that I read the readings thoroughly
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618136\n 1618136\n \n 1618136\n I thought the readings and class discussions were really enriching, and the summer research paper felt like a good length. I think the class gave me a really robust understanding of the current problem of U.S. immigration, particularly focusing on the immigrants who are already in the U.S. I do wish the class had devoted at least one week to discussing the question of border policy and how we can make a fair and sustainable system for processing people who are not yet here but want to come here. I felt like that was the biggest gap in our strategy briefing, and I think it would have been really useful when thinking about the challenge and potential solutions of immigration.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618261\n 1618261\n \n 1618261\n This semester was designed incredibly well: we had a manageable and well-curated reading load, professors that cared, assignments that were about depth of knowledge rather than busy work, and a format that allowed us to meaningfully delve into our interests. Our final briefing assignment, for example, was only ten pages split among seven or more students. The assignment was about precision and prior design rather than throwing words on the page. The only "weakness" I suppose would be sometimes the seminar sessions followed by sometimes four hour long events. Shortening the excellent (but sometimes quite long and mandatory) outside class events would likely improve engagement.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618652\n 1618652\n \n 1618652\n Strengths: Second semester GS exceeded my expectations! A major strength is the small module size. Last semester, with such a big class, discussions sometimes felt a bit stagnant. With smaller class size, I felt like us students could steer the dialogue and explore ideas in more depth. I enjoyed both of the major assignments (summer research project and strategy briefing) it felt like an appropriate amount of work and I learned from both. Aaron was amazing, meeting with all of us to talk about our career plans he's a real asset to the course and I think future TAs should follow his lead. Professor Brenes and the GS team do a great job with the dinners as well; Professor Brenes is a great interviewer of guests! \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Sometimes the readings were very long and we wouldn't really touch on them in class anyway I think making the readings shorter, and cutting out any readings that aren't essential for class discussion, would be great. Also, when we met as a big group all of GS plus several professors for class, the discussions lacked the same depth as the small group discussions. Sometimes it felt like nobody was on the same page, even about what GS is, and not in a good way. I have no idea how to fix this problem but I definitely think as much time in small groups as possible is ideal. I think I learned more about GS from *doing* grand strategy through projects and small group discussions, than from talking about it abstractly in a big circle.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618695\n 1618695\n \n 1618695\n Strengths: Got to work closely with practitioners; small class size; very interesting and relevant content. Weaknesses: Readings for the great power competition module were almost entirely Foreign Affairs thinkpieces. It would have been nice to have a greater variety of readings, including more historical background and academic texts. Also, more guidance on the structure and format of the strategy briefing would have been helpful.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618740\n 1618740\n \n 1618740\n Strengths: Guest professors, events, meals, class diversity
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618947\n 1618947\n \n 1618947\n Strengths: 1. Practical problem solving, with feedback from relevant experts. 2. Small classroom setting, opportunity to get to know your classmates and professors well\r\n\r\nHelpful to know that this course requires you to think about strategy in a very particular way defined by different practitioners definitions of grand strategy (which may be slightly or largely different). I think this push is welcome and helpful, but it is very different from what other Yale classes ask of you so be prepared to engage in this exercise and ask for continuous feedback if you hope to perform well on major assignments, and feel as though you've mastered the thinking expected here.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1622244\n 1622244\n \n 1622244\n I liked that this section of GS is split into modules. The smaller size made it easier to interact with everyone. I think a lot of prior knowledge is assumed, and it would be helpful to provide more background information. Additionally, the instructions for assignments were often unclear or nonexistent, so it would be helpful to have better guidelines for expectations for assignments.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624008\n 1624008\n \n 1624008\n This course is amazing! I wish I could take it again!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1625048\n 1625048\n \n 1625048\n Having Professor Brenes and Professor Sky co-teach the module was a great way to approach both the historical and contemporary angles of refugee policy issues. The Grand Strategy program includes opportunities to meet with activists, policymakers, authors, and academics in small group settings, which was really exciting and helped inform how I thought about the policy issues we discussed in seminar. The second semester (in the fall) also involves breaking up into modules, with each one focusing on a specific policy area. I really enjoyed the smaller group setting.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1631670\n 1631670\n \n 1631670\n I thought that the Spring seminar could be improved. I sometimes struggled to find continuities between the seminars, and felt that many of the invited guests/co-teachers used the opportunity to discuss their own academic niche rather than engage with the framework of Grand Strategy.
