Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1613350\n 1613350\n \n 1613350\n This course flies through such a comprehensive look at NAIS that I left the course feeling far more literate in the terms and subjects relevant to North American Indigenous communities. The course was well-structured as well as a consequence of the assignments; I felt as if the assignments held me accountable to engage with the readings and gave me a lot of room to improve my analysis of the course content and my own writing. I had a lot of fun with the Perusall assignments because they helped me understand how other students were interpreting and engaging with the material.\r\nHowever, the course could definitely benefit from engaging in more work about South American and African Indigenous communities. While I learned a lot about North American and even Asian Indigenous communities, I felt as if we didn't have as much investigation into African and South American content.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1615801\n 1615801\n \n 1615801\n Strengths: Prof. Andrews understands how to craft dynamic class conversations regarding the readings. She has the ability to bring the excitement to the class through her passion and knowledge of Indigenous studies. I also appreciated the kindness she always extended to us because she can recognize that we are people, too, and Yale is not always the easiest place to exist. Lastly, the creative project was the perfect fusing between theory and practice. It felt like an opportunity to embody the knowledge we had engaged with throughout the semester. \r\n\r\nWeakness: One point of tension in the course had to do with the fact that many of us were entering this seminar with different degrees of knowledge about Indigenous Peoples and NAIS more broadly. It would have been beneficial to offer a lecture course first--- possibly make it a series? By doing it this way, there is a greater chance that the students in this course will be (mostly) on the same page and time doesn't have to be taken away to catch others up. Just a minor suggestion!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1631424\n 1631424\n \n 1631424\n This course was great because Professor Andrews was very responsive to student feedback and needs. She also did a great job at selecting our readings. However, sometimes the texts we read were kind of dense and difficult to parse through. Additionally, there were a lot of graded assignments.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1631548\n 1631548\n \n 1631548\n Strengths: Incredible professor, engaging readings, insightful assignments and assessments, fair grading, thoughtful and understanding professor. \r\nWeaknesses: N/A
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1631919\n 1631919\n \n 1631919\n The theories covered in this class are very progressive. Prof. Andrews offers great guidance and a safe space for our discussion. I think the depth and elaborateness of class discussion are more important than how many topics we have covered. Sometimes to cover the readings assigned I feel like we don't have enough time to dive deeper.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639668\n 1639668\n \n 1639668\n The use of Perusall was a strength of this course. It allowed students to interact with each other and build thoughts off of each others' thoughts in a more conducive environment than a simple discussion board. I very much enjoyed reading the comments and annotations on the Perusall page. It helped me to understand my classmates' perspectives better. \r\n\r\nI think that the biweekly discussion responses were less valuable to my learning. It felt more a rote endeavor than the Perusall annotations. I would have liked to see more Perusall annotations and perhaps a short freehand response at the end of each week/section of learning to encapsulate the broad themes that were presented and learned.\r\n\r\nThe other weakness of the course was the division that existed within the class--both physically in the classroom and in discussion. The Indigenous students in the course wound up sitting on one side of the classroom and the non-Indigenous students on the other side. This presented a kind of weird energy in the classroom. Though this is not the fault of Dr. Andrews, maybe some thought could be given to how to facilitate more discussion and better discussion in future renditions of this class. Discussion did improve towards the end of the semester. Maybe it just takes time.
