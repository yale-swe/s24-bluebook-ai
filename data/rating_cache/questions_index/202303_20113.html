RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709987\n 1709987\n \n 1709987\n It would be borderline impossible to express in words how much I developed as a student and as a person through my time in this class. I came into college with a very narrow and instrumental view of what my education should be for; namely, a certification of my intelligence and work ethic that I could present to employers in four years' time. But over this semester, as I grappled with the ideas of Christian, East Asian, contemporary, socialist, and feminist writers and forced myself to become receptive instead of dismissive, I began to pick apart the protectionist shell that sheathed my identity and critically examined a good chunk of my identity. What were my core values? Why was I placed here at Yale? Who did I want to become in four years, and how should I shape my education to realize that future self?\r\n\r\nBeing my first and only college course with a writing component, I can confidently say that I've learned to gravitate towards substantial rather than flowery writing, to outline properly rather than diving headfirst into an essay, and to seek help (thanks Poorvu!) rather than rely on self-evaluation. Although it wasn't easy to snap into it at 9 AM on Mondays, I learned to craft responses to my classmates during discussions that were constructive and thought-out. and I realized the value of taking notes and listening with intent, as being required to do end-of-week self-assessments compelled me to not zone out.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709987\n 1709987\n \n 1709987\n Strengths: The selected readings represent a wide variety of viewpoints, and the questions that we are asked to discuss often lead to some very interesting tangents. More generally, I think the broader topics that the course covers are necessary for every freshman to reflect on, and while doing so might stimulate more questions than answers, the process of finding said answers is the essence of college, in my opinion.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: It was not uncommon for 2-3 students to completely dominate the discussion and leave other students without space to make comments. Towards the end of the course, when we got into Paolo Freire and bell hooks, it sometimes felt like students were throwing around the same politically correct buzzwords to signal their alignment with the authors' viewpoints, making for an echo chamber of sorts.\r\n\r\nImprovements: The instructor could be more deliberate in encouraging roundtable discussion instead of discourse among a select, vocal few, either by breaking the class up into smaller groups or calling on people who haven't spoken yet to share their ideas.
