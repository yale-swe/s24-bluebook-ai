\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532528\n 1532528\n \n 1532528\n I gained a broad exposure to the pillars of ancient philosophy and their core ideas. In particular, I learned quite a bit about Platonic and Aristotelian philosophy. I also learned how to write a paper in philosophy, which is rather different than papers in other disciplines.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539355\n 1539355\n \n 1539355\n I learned how to write an essay in a traditional philosophic manner, and I also developed a basic understanding of ancient philosophy that will greately help me going forward. Prior to coming to Yale I had never taken a philosophy class, but now I feel ready to go further into philosophy and really explore the texts we have looked at in more detail.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548922\n 1548922\n \n 1548922\n A good survey of Ancient Greek and Roman philosophies as well as some medieval and Islamic philosophies with a focus on the original texts.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548928\n 1548928\n \n 1548928\n I developed my philosophy essay writing skills, which meant learning to scrutinize another person's argument more thoroughly than ever before. Specifically, the structure of a philosophical essay (listing premises/conclusions, making steps in an argument explicit) helped me make essays for other DS courses more clear. This course also developed my knowledge of how philosophical thought developed in ancient Greece and spread to the rest of the Western world after Plato and Aristotle; tracking their influence of later philosophers (Skeptics, St. Augustine, the medieval theologians) was helpful for understanding how reactionary and/or communal philosophy is. The close alignment of opinions on the highest good being the attainment of Happiness, the repetition of sun analogies, and the emphasis on knowledge as a distinctly human gift/attribute to hone convinced me that human thought is truly an arrow, with various points of view converging on foundational ideals.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549164\n 1549164\n \n 1549164\n This course taught me a considerable amount about the origins of western philosophy, and provided an excellent overview of the classical part of the western canon. Despite having never studied philosophy in any depth before, I quickly acquired a strong understanding of the basic thoughts underpinning philosophers such as Plato, Aristotle and Aquinas. I also learnt how to read well, since the quantity of reading is very high and, because of the dense nature of philosophical treatises, it is difficult to skim read, meaning one naturally is forced to learn to read efficiently. Beyond this, I learnt how to write an analytical philosophical essay.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549354\n 1549354\n \n 1549354\n Logic, philosophical argumentation, close reading, textual analysis, seminar-style engagement, discussion, & analysis, and writing skills.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549681\n 1549681\n \n 1549681\n I developed the ability to write a clear and concise college paper, arguing for a certain position. Additionally, I learned to logically analyze arguments and find flaws. I gained an understanding of several schools of philosophical thought that have been influential.

Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532528\n 1532528\n \n 1532528\n I think the texts we read are largely well-chosen: while a month each of Plato and Aristotle felt like a lot relative to how much time we spend on other authors in DS, in retrospect, the time does seem well-spent. Additionally, I think the lectures in DS philosophy were consistently the best among the three DS tracks, as they were almost always really helpful in helping us parse the often-dense texts we read. One change I think would be really helpful would be to have the philosophy lectures before the sections discussing a particular texts, so we could have a better basic understanding of the text going into section (as it is, section was frequently a lot of "I don't understand this").
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539355\n 1539355\n \n 1539355\n The strength of this course is the amount of material we cover and the professors that cover it. Directed Studies philosophy essentially goes from Plato and Aristotle to Dante, which is a truly amazing amount of texts. . Another strength of the course are the lecturers. With a new lecturer every week, each a leading scholar in their field, the amount of detail they go into is truly incredible. That being said, an issue with the course is that it might cover too much in too short a time. There were some texts that definitely deserved more attention than we were allowed to give them, and I left many of the classes disappointed that we couldn't keep talking about authors like Seneca, and only devoted two classes to the incredibly influential Stoic philosophy.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548922\n 1548922\n \n 1548922\n Strengths: Covers most of the major authors and works in ancient philosophy\r\nWeaknesses: The distribution of texts in regard to their themes is not balanced. In this course you will read Aristotle's Categories, Physics, Metaphysics, On the Soul, and Nichomachean Ethics. I agree that Aristotle is a great philosopher, however the contents of those texts very much overlap and are arguably outdated in our contemporary context based on the principles of modern scientific methodologies. I suggest that the course should substitute some of these readings with parts of Aristotle's Politics and Poetics, texts that are much more relevant to the present day and echo the other two DS courses. Also, I cannot see the reason for including Anselm in our curriculum. Anselm has a rather ridiculous religious logic; I would rather spend more time with ancient Greek mathematical logic then reading Anselm. SJM.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548928\n 1548928\n \n 1548928\n The strengths are the breadth and depth of content covered in such a short time, but one major weakness is that timelines and connections between philosophers can get lost in the sheer volume of content. For example, how much was Epictetus influenced by his predecessors, even while under imperial Roman rule? The through-lines we looked for were not always adequately explained for retention.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549164\n 1549164\n \n 1549164\n The readings are spectacularly interesting, especially some of the later ones that focus on the nature of the soul and the philosophy of religion. My only real qualm with the course was the quantity of reading - it was very difficult to keep on top of and even when I did achieve this, I never really had the time to really start to analyze in proper depth the works in the way that I would have liked to (one seminar is NOT enough time to really understand someone like Maimonides!).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549354\n 1549354\n \n 1549354\n It's a very thorough survey of western philosophy. Greater inclusivity of non-white or non-"western" authors would be welcomed, but I suppose that would be in contrast to the spoken aim of DS.
