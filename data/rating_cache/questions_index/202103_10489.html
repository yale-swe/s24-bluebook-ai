\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532507\n 1532507\n \n 1532507\n I learned so much about both educational systems and the skills necessary to be a successful advocate / leader within educational structures.\r\nIn regards to educational systems, this course teaches you about the instructional core (think, what happens inside the classroom between teachers, students and curriculum), the school as a larger institution and the school district as an organizational method. I debated the merits of Charter Schools, the Finnish model of education and the backlash to school equity movements.\r\nIn regards to advocacy and leadership skills, I learned about the importance of framing problems for policy makers and educational leaders. I thought critically about the history of U.S. educational reforms, their political motives and what can be done better for our schools in the future.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544791\n 1544791\n \n 1544791\n First and foremost, this class gives you a number of indispensable frameworks for analyzing education systems all over the world. While you are learning this content, you also learn the case method and how to write an effective memo. These skills are critical for business school/work and anything in policy. This class also features a group project that lasts for most of the semester in which you research a problem that your group identifies within education and you offer possible solutions. This hands-on learning teaches you how to develop a theory of change and apply design-thinking to create your intervention. Plus, you get to present it to a group of really experienced and influential educators/superintendents from around the state!

The strength of this course is its case-based and project-based pedagogical model. This is an engaging and interactive seminar where you get to know your peers and your professor.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544515\n 1544515\n \n 1544515\n It's clear that Professor Lemons has invested a LOT of time and energy into designing this course. We were able to dive into several hotly debated issues (critical race theory, charter schools, etc.) while staying focused on how they show up in the real world.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544791\n 1544791\n \n 1544791\n This course is really interesting and teaches a number of skills not covered in any other class at Yale. Dr. Lemons is really cool, understanding, and knowledgable. The structure of the course, including the long-term project and the case method, make it feel really refreshing and fun. In terms of improvement, I would suggest introducing the memo in a different way. I felt really confused throughout the whole semester about what made a good memo. Often, the memos I thought I would do well on got okay grades and the ones I didn't think so much about got better grades. I think explaining more about what you are looking for and what makes a good memo (versus a bad memo) would be very helpful. It also still feels very unclear about what our final project grade is on are we being evaluated on our final proposed intervention, on our presentation, or only on what we wrote for our final group reflection paper?

This truly was one of the best classes I have taken at Yale. \r\n\r\nThe group projects are a highlight -- though it is nerve-wracking to present in front of education leaders across the state their feedback is so helpful and this experience helped place the course content into a real-world context (something that rarely happens on this level for Yale courses).

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546909\n 1546909\n \n 1546909\n Strengths: A major strength of this class is Professor Lemons. He is empathetic and flexible, carefully intentional about creating an environment suited to fulfillingly self-guided student learning, an engaging instructor, and an expert in the content. The semester-long group change project was definitely the best part of the class. Presenting our work-in-progress solution to a panel of education experts and then sitting down with them for a more in-depth conversation afterwards was extremely fulfilling and felt surprisingly low-stakes, since there was no grade attached to the presentation. Further iterating on our project and turning in a (still work-in-progress) final product that we were proud of was incredibly rewarding as well. The classes themselves flew by, despite being 1 hr 50 min at 7:00 on Wednesday nights. Professor Lemons struck a great balance between guiding the conversation at times, but also leaving plenty of room for student direction and discussion - it may have been the first class I've ever taken that truly felt student-led. The cases we studied were interesting, and I really did learn a lot from our conversations about them. The other assignments - the case analysis and weekly case memos - were instructive and were good opportunities to critically apply our knowledge of the material, even if they were also a bit intensive. The self-grade (as awful as the title of the assignment sounds) ended up being a welcome opportunity to reflect on the semester. \r\n\r\nRoom for improvement: First thing - the work for this class felt extremely back-loaded, with the case analysis, final project submission, and self-grade all being due rapid-fire, one after another, the final week of the semester. It's true that the case analysis is available the entire semester, but most students are deadline-driven, and this sequencing of deadlines sets them up for a very stressful end of term. If the case analysis deadline could be a bit sooner (perhaps before November break, or one week after it), this could help alleviate the heavy end of semester workload for this class. Second thing - it is *shocking* that this class is not a writing credit! Expect to write quite a bit for this course: ~20 pages *single-spaced* individually, not including writing for the group project. It would be great if this class could be a writing credit, but I don't know if Yale's criteria for this designation are oddly specific/restrictive.

Extremely rewarding to be able to work on something long term so that knowledge throughout the class is able to culminate, and then have the opportunity to present it to experts. Not only this, but then following the presentation to further discuss the policy proposal at length and then be able to modify based on feedback from those with knowledge of real-world implementation and challenges. Incredible class.
