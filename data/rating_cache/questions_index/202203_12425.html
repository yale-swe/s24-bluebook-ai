I could tell Keith was trying to make the course more accessible to the (significant) population of undergrads in the course, but at the end of the day, this is a graduate level course, and there is already an undergraduate equivalent that undergrads can take if they'd like a course that is not at the level of a grad student. I wish the course had been a little deeper on some topics, and I wish the exams had been closer to the difficulty level of the problem sets. I was hoping that this course would help me hone some mathematical skills that would help me in future topics, but I think that the difficulty level of the exams was a big reason it fell short of that goal. \r\n\r\nI did have some major issues with the lecture portion of the course. I love Keith so so much, and I can tell he's a super nice guy, but I do not think that lecturing in a course like this is his greatest skill, and I got the sense that this course was not the best fit for him or his skillset. I'm sure there's other courses that he would be a phenomenal prof for, perhaps some particle class, but for math methods, I do not think math methods was the right course for him. Keith is an excellent storyteller and very good at giving insight and intuition to broad topics and the philosophical parts of physics, but he struggles with the fine details and minutia of calculations. Unfortunately, MM is a course that is full of nitty-gritty details and careful calculations and more-or-less devoid of broad-strokes insights and philosophy, which I feel makes it orthogonal to Keith's style: it ignores his strengths and relies on his weaknesses.\r\n\r\nI feel so bad for Keith for being in the position he is in. I can tell he tries extremely hard and prepares greatly for the lectures, but at the end of the day it just simply isn't the right course for him to teach, and I worry that he might feel badly about his performance. I also expect a lot of the responses to this to be unfairly harsh towards Keith for how this course went down, which makes me sad. I wish we had gotten to see Keith in a different course where his skills and talents can really shine. What happened isn't fair to the students, but it really really isnt fair to Keith.

Keith is great, but this course isn't right for him, and I feel students would get more out of it if it is led differently. \r\n\r\nWhether I would recommend a hypothetical different prof version of the course, I cannot say. I think a math methods course is a useful course to have in a physics grad program, but that there are so many different topics required and not everyone has the same background or needs, so it can be tricky to tailor it well to the class. A similar course that had an extra level of rigor and covered more advanced topics while forgoing some of the basic vector algebra could be a strong course. I think many people would have seen manipulation of vectors and matrices, and probably also vector calculus, multiple integration, and stokes/greens/divergence theorem. Removing or de-emphasizing these topics to make room for more discussion of complex analysis, PDEs, and other advanced topics could be a way to make this course stronger.
