\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1610410\n 1610410\n \n 1610410\n Strengths: discussion moderation, group spirit, good guiding questions, grading was fair and feedback was incredibly useful; weaknesses: pace was too quick and sometimes discussion felt shallow
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624312\n 1624312\n \n 1624312\n This course was amazing. I enjoyed the readings even when on my own, and coming to class to discuss with the Professor Schillinger and my classmates made it all the better. Our discussions were definitely my favorite part of the course, as we all had listened to a lecture about the material prior to coming to class and Prof. Schillinger provided even more context at the beginnings of each seminar.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624322\n 1624322\n \n 1624322\n Strengths: DS teaches you how to write a lot of papers and write them well. It teaches you how to engage with, challenge, and grapple with difficult ideas, and it exposes you to a canonic school of thought. Few (if any) similar programs exist, and the experience you receive from DS is truly unparalleled.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: Too much reading. I fell into a pattern of completing all the reading before beginning to write papers, and that hampered my paper-writing abilities. I'm not sure there is a solution, however -- DS is meant to be a broad survey, and the mountains of reading are part of the program. Perhaps it would be helpful to know that we don't need to read everything.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624326\n 1624326\n \n 1624326\n Strength was seminar with professor Schillinger. Schillinger grounded the discussions in the text and facilitated great dialogue between the students.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624537\n 1624537\n \n 1624537\n DS is a fast-paced course, and this not only makes it difficult but also can leave you feeling like youre moving onto another material before youre able to engage with a text or author in depth. Since all this process of reading and discussing texts is accompanied by paper assignments, it might also feel overwhelming. On the flip side, this fast-paced structure and the demanding reading load compels one to become a faster reader and writer, as well as making it possible to see patterns and draw connections between a broad body of work since one goes over so many texts and different thinkers.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624577\n 1624577\n \n 1624577\n There was not enough time allocated to each book (especially the Arabic philosophers) for me to understand them completely. However, overall this was a really great course to understand ancient philosophy as a whole with no prior experience.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627733\n 1627733\n \n 1627733\n There were no weaknesses with the course within control of the instructor. Professor Schillinger was an incredibly intelligent and kind instructor, and made the class interesting and worthwhile. He understands what it's like to be a student and could empathize with our experience in significant ways. One time, for example, he kindly brought us donuts at a time in the semester when he knew we were mentally exhausted. The class itself was run very well. The books we read were carefully chosen by the administration, and gave us an incredible background in philosophy.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1629259\n 1629259\n \n 1629259\n This course did an excellent job of going with the flow of student ideas while sticking close to very complex yet intriguing texts. I feel much more comfortable understanding the variety but also the depth of multiple ancient philosophies and the work of several philosophers. Still, I hope that future DS philosophy courses may be more strategic about how much reading is assigned, especially given that so little of the text is actually able to be meaningfully covered in class.

I don't mean to say that the DS content is better than anything I've ever read (and I've certainly read more interesting texts), but the ideas embedded within DS works are beautiful and multilayered and, quite frankly, fascinating. So my recommendation for someone considering DS would be this: take the course if you are interested in grappling with new ideas, and take the class if you want to improve your writing. Only if they meet these two criteria would I recommend DS.
