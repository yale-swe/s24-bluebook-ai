\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1607463\n 1607463\n \n 1607463\n The moral and cultural aspects of race relations in the USA were front and center. I was also given effective instruction on improving my writing.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1614553\n 1614553\n \n 1614553\n I got to read some critically underread Black literature published since the millenium. What I learned, both from the books and from my peers, is the way that the genre of Black literature is continually evolving with the contemporary moment. Even as books tell stories from the past, those narratives are touched by the salient topics of the present day. I also expanded my notion of what counts as literature, as the course included poetry and children's books. I learned to critically evaluate my own relationship to race and the tension between racial identity as something produced/constructed vs racial identity as a very real force which shapes our day-to-day social interactions and reactions.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1623601\n 1623601\n \n 1623601\n Analyzing text and thinking, talking, and writing about it- great course to learn how to communicate about a text outside of the traditional essay
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1633789\n 1633789\n \n 1633789\n I learned a lot about close reading, and literary analysis. We also discussed the process of knowledge-making in general, and many other topics since student presentations were a major part of the course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1636029\n 1636029\n \n 1636029\n more insight into perceptions of contemporary Black literature, and learned a lot from the student presentations every week
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639196\n 1639196\n \n 1639196\n Perhaps the most effective element of ENGL 266 was the variety of genres on the syllabus: even looking only at literature from the past 23 years, we tackled and formed through-lines between memoir, children's literature, novels, poetry and poetic memoir, and cultural criticism. The ENGL 266 supplied breadth without sacrificing depth, and what was perhaps most valuable was that my personal feelings about each week's readings never impacted my excitement for the class conversation to come. Even those readings I wouldn't personally return to for matters of individual taste were granted serious, full-bodied, and layered discussions in class.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1636029\n 1636029\n \n 1636029\n strengths: strong reading list, amazing professor, individualized student presentations; only suggestion for improvement is that some weeks, our discussions could have struck a better balance between being anecdotal vs. rooted in the text. Some part of me also wanted class twice a week to get to be in that space and engage with the text/my peers more often, but a once a week seminar also has its own benefits
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639196\n 1639196\n \n 1639196\n This semester was the inaugural offering of ENGL 266, and it goes without saying that the syllabus could easily have a hundred other iterations: there is no one way to wholly encapsulate 21st-century black literature over the course of fourteen weeks. That said, I thought this semester's syllabus was wonderfully varied, rich, and effective for generating discussion (and, just as importantly, overall a delight to read). As is often the case in seminars with syllabi this good, students this committed, and professors as enthusiastic as Prof. Mahurin, I left most class sessions wishing there'd been more time for discussion. As such, I can see a course like ENGL 266 benefitting from twice-a-week scheduling, but beyond that I have no major qualms or suggestions for improvement.
