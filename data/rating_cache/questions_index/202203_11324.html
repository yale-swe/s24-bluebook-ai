\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1616445\n 1616445\n \n 1616445\n Strengths: James Barnes is definitely one of the better math teachers I've had at Yale. He's a pretty nice guy and has many intuitive explanations for set theoretical concepts for people that don't really have an intuition for the abstract concepts in set theory (like me). Additionally, office hours (both with James and the TA's) were really helpful, so there was always a place for help if you were struggling with the problem sets. Weaknesses: the course moved a bit too fast at the end. We were basically just speeding through theorems in order to reach the final topic of the course: the Von Neumann Universe (as well as completing the axioms of ZFC). However, James didn't test us much on the final concepts of the course on the final exam, so it wasn't too big of a deal, and it was pretty satisfying to realize we'd just covered the fundamental axioms that make most of the math we know today possible (although I'm still pretty confused as to how ordinals work, specifically ordinal arithmetic).

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1616445\n 1616445\n \n 1616445\n It really depends on the type of math you're interested in. This class is not like other math classes you'll find at Yale. Set Theory is the basis for most, if not all, of the math you've encountered in your life, so you're defining many intuitive things that become much less intuitive from a set-theoretical standpoint. For example, addition on the naturals needs to be defined using the Recursion Theorem, and addition on the other number systems is defined from the naturals, but proving properties of addition varies across the number systems because of how they're defined. Additionally, the grading scheme is not very forgiving. 75% of your grade is made up of exams with both midterms having around 4 questions and the final having 8 questions, so very minor mistakes are costly and major mistakes can ruin everything. However, I don't know if the class is curved, and James gives the option of replacing one of your midterms with a final project, so there is some room for leniency. Regardless, James is a great teacher, and if you really like pure math, you're looking at the right class. I enjoyed the experience (although my grade probably didn't), and I think it's pretty interesting to realize that math is simply held together by a set of axioms that we defined, which can lead to both flaws and perfections in our mathematical system. I can't say I'm an expert at working with ordinals and cardinals now, but it was interesting to take a look at the limits of math and more. I would just recommend shopping the class and seeing if Set Theory is interesting to you, but be aware that the course is much easier in the first half of the semester in comparison to the second half.
