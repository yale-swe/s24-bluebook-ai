\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709992\n 1709992\n \n 1709992\n A weakness of this course is that it just feels like a filler course for the abet required topics. Because of this, we spend no significant amount of time on learning any one thing and the lectures feel insubstantial compared to the projects we're working on. I think the lessons learned during this class should be taught much earlier in the curriculum, and leave this class for actually discussing topics that we will use on our projects, such as advanced engineering design concepts, design for manufacturability, parts and hardware selection, more advanced stress/fatigue/cycle analysis for more complex mechanisms, etc.\r\n\r\nI didn't mind making slides every two weeks to keep instructors updated, but all of the design reports felt like nearly pointless filler work, with many of the categories not applicable to all teams, and our team spending more time trying to write anything down we could to fill out a report rather than actually work on our project. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1711809\n 1711809\n \n 1711809\n The problems with this course lie both with the content and the instruction. First and foremost, the content is elementary and should have been covered in well-taught 185 and 325 courses. Ideation, pugh matrices, gantt charts, motors and pneumatics, should all be covered in freshman year in 185. Materials selection and failure theories should be covered in 325. Arduino and 3D printing are already covered elsewhere in the curriculum. Students from our year do not know how to make, dimension, or tolerance engineering drawings properly -- but this is not covered in our current curriculum either -- we will graduate without it. Engineering economics and engineering ethics should each be a mandatory half-semester class. The fact that any of these topics are covered in the senior design class demonstrates the organizational shortcomings of the major, its sequencing, teaching, and rigor.\r\n\r\nInstead the class should cover advanced topics, including systems engineering and engineering management (explaining how team projects are organized at large companies), control and quality systems, IQA, verification vs. validation, different ISO certifications, the fine details or working with suppliers and subcontractors. Toward the end of the semester, Dr. Booth asked us about the strength and weaknesses of the class, and multiple students raised the concern of topics being basic, to which he responded, "what is an example of an advanced topic." We do not know, because we have not been taught them. Whoever is in charge of the curriculum should look to the curricula of leading institutions (MIT, Stanford, Michigan), and see what topics they cover in their advanced seminars. They should not be asking the students, who frankly do not know the expanse of what they have not been exposed to. \r\n\r\nThis difficulty of students understanding whether or not they have received high-quality teaching unfortunately casts a shadow over the entire course-review based system. The lectures that Dr. Booth gives are not very carefully planned, are usually surface-level, and do not always demonstrate a mastery of the material by the lecturer. Dr. Booth is clearly knowledgeable and I am sure a capable researcher and engineer, but teaching is an entirely separate skill, one that does not come easily nor always yield to effort or support from non-engineering poorvu 'teaching experts.' Moreover, students in his projects may feel that their projects are sufficiently advanced and well-run because they require and reward effort, but they do not require or reward thought. Not all mechanical engineering projects need to be materials- or research-focused, but if they involve mechanical design and product design, then they should also do the work of market analysis, tolerance analysis, manufacturing analysis, that is, take advantage of the relative simplicity of the project to engage with more advanced topics. \r\n\r\nThe fact that we are students with limited knowledge means that more than anything we do not know what we do not know. That means that it is very important for the professors to clearly define the scope of the project for the students, because students should be told what is expected of them. Students without experience who choose their own scope can easily end up doing very basic work because they are not expected to push themselves, to learn and teach themselves new topics. \r\n\r\nIn summary, the course must be improved against a trusted benchmark - a new professor, highly regarded by their peers, and students. Dr. Booth cannot be expected or allowed to improve himself because he is not a quality of teacher who can be, as he is now, in charge of the entire mechanical design curriculum of the department. This may seem harsh, but I urge all mechanical engineering professors and relevant administrators to sit through the final presentations at the end of Spring semester. If these are all projects that you would brag about to colleagues in industry, then I retract my words
