\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538434\n 1538434\n \n 1538434\n This course could be appropriately subtitled what wisdom Professor John Lewis Gaddis, having read many books and both studied and taught many leaders, thinks that you should keep in mind as a student trying to understand the world. Professor Gaddis teaches some other courses one on biography, one on foxes and hedgehogs (leadership), one on time travel, and of course Grand Strategy that are more focused on a specific subject. This seminar seems to draw bits from all of them to impart a broad but complex sense of his worldview. It bounces back and forth between the specific and the general. It brings you into a world of "big thinking" that isn't pedantic or bound by discipline a world in which the ancient Greek wars and chaos theory and ballroom dancing are all connected and leaves you with a deep understanding of the importance of that kind of intellectualism, and of course history's place at the center of it all. The course has improved my writing in similar ways: Professor Gaddis himself is one of the most elegant writers I've ever read, and he instills that value in students as well. I've learned the power of simplicity and conversational, vivid clarity even in the most academic writing, which is something you don't always get in the (social) sciences. This probably all sounds extremely vague and abstract, which is certainly true at times; I'd be hard-pressed to say what hard facts I learned in this course. But I feel far more educated than before when the semester began.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534708\n 1534708\n \n 1534708\n Strengths: the content, the professor, and the class size.\r\n\r\nWeakness: the workload was significant.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538434\n 1538434\n \n 1538434\n The course got better and better as the semester progressed. Initially, there were often days when I left class wondering what exactly I had actually learned class time was taken up with students throwing out their ideas often without too much connection to others points, and the topics from one class to the next didnt always feel clearly connected. But then there was a point about halfway through the course when everything began to click, both in terms of the connections between the material and the cohesiveness of the students. Everything we had talked about and were talking about began to come together, and what had seemed like disorder before suddenly seemed like a very consciously assembled pathway. It was immensely rewarding when I started noticing references in our later readings to things we had read earlier -- or better yet, making my own theoretical connections. The end of the course (especially the touchingly personal last class!) was truly bittersweet and left me feeling both satisfied and yet sad that there wasnt something more. Professor Gaddis clearly loves the classroom, and is someone who really believes in learning from his students, even after (or perhaps because of) over fifty years of teaching.\r\n\r\nIts hard to judge something that felt more like an experience than a normal course on mundane things like workload. But as for that: Its simultaneously difficult and easy. The reading load, as others will undoubtedly mention, is very heavy (think a full 500-page book in five days, and of course the entirety of War and Peace over the semester). The upside is that youll never fear a big reading load again. And its not like youre tested or graded on memorizing exactly what happens Professor Gaddis just wants you to gain insight from, and I think sincerely enjoy, the things youre assigned (its clear that some of the books on the syllabus are mostly there, or at least there in full rather than as excerpts, because he liked reading them, they influenced him, and thinks you should read them too). Besides the reading, though, the writing isn't onerous at all: the only graded assignments are a few short papers, all of which are on extremely broad/open-ended topics (being told simply to "write a classic" as the first assignment was, to be sure, a bit frightening) and all of which encourage creativity. Professor Gaddis is not only amenable to but also encouraging of outside-the-box thinking, which made the writing actually a lot of fun.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538620\n 1538620\n \n 1538620\n One major strength of the course is its ability to invoke self-reflection and reflection on the world around a student. The readings consist of many books and articles that I think should be read by anyone during their lifetime, and the discussions are some that I think everyone should experience. Although the direction of the discussions in the first few weeks may not be clear, the course comes full circle towards the end in a very enlightening way. Additionally, I think the writing assignments are quite enjoyable! The prompts are creative and fun while still seriously engaging us with the course material which I really loved.\r\n\r\nThe weaknesses of the course likely include the massive workload especially in terms of readings. I'd say it is possible to complete the course without completing all them all, they really are very enriching and the discussions flow based on them and so I'd encourage anyone taking the class to come into it ready to dedicate many hours a day reading for the class. Another potential weakness is the fast-paced of discussion that while quite exciting can sometimes leave the quieter students, who do want to share, feeling unable to do so.
