\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1700655\n 1700655\n \n 1700655\n Strengths: Very well organized, and even tangents are relevant in the sense that even if we don't strictly follow the syllabus, the discussions are always expounding on some part of the EU that is fulfilling some broader goal of the course. \r\nThis is one of very few (count-able on one hand) courses at Yale that focus specifically on the economics, politics, and/or history of the European Union. Even the European Studies classes are often hyper-focused on one country or on another subregion, like post-Soviet states. \r\nThe readings are not too heavy, and Marnix explains them thoroughly, so you'll be fine if you have to skip a couple of readings. (But don't make a habit out of it.)\r\nThe course is a very good mix of lecture and discussion. The students are also very much self-selecting. Everyone participates and asks really, really thoughtful questions -- not the kind of questions that you ask just for brownie points or to get a participation credit.\r\n\r\nNote that the goal of the course isn't to indoctrinate students into loving the EU or thinking it is some magical super-democratic, super-efficient utopia. Criticisms are very welcome, and Marnix is very upfront about legitimate reasons why someone might be pro-Brexit, for example. But the course helps you distinguish between economically sound anti-EU arguments and incoherent populist nonsense. That's a good skill to have.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1700655\n 1700655\n \n 1700655\n Yes! If you're interested in the econ, politics, and/or history of the EU, this course is perfect for you. Intro micro and macro are needed, but you don't need any intermediate or advanced econ knowledge. You don't need to be European or have lived in the EU at all. In fact, the students are a good mix of Americans, EU citizens, and other international students, which is really refreshing because you get a wide variety of perspectives, which Marnix does a great job of including in the discussion. \r\n\r\nThe workload is low, and much more focused on practical skills than busywork. You have to write two 2-page (single-spaced) policy memos, and Marnix gives you feedback, returns each one, and you get to revise it, with the original and final each counting for 50% of that memo's grade. Then there is a final oral exam for which you get 45 minutes to prepare (closed-book but you can write out talking points during your 45 minute prep time) and 45 minutes to speak, including a Q&A from Marnix and one other highly-rated professor or Jackson School Fellow. The assignments and exam are designed to simulate real-life policy situations. Or, I guess, McKinsey casing, if that's how you want to look at it. The syllabus says there might be problem sets but my class didn't get any.
