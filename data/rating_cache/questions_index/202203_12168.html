\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1619101\n 1619101\n \n 1619101\n Strength: Both instructors did a great job modeling the thinking that syntacticians do. They approached new material generatively, describing a shortcoming of the model we had up til a certain class and offering different suggestions for how to change it, then making an evidence-based and accessible argument for one particular change over others. The impression I got was that that is how academic linguists actually do think about, and ultimately resolve, new controversies, so watching that thought process be modeled out in front of us was fantastically helpful, and being asked to go through that thought process on our own, for new and different theoretical gaps, on our homeworks was also a great learning experience. \r\nA fun, engaged, and active classroom, where student participation guides what topics are focused on in a given lecture and big, wide-scope questions are often taken at face value. \r\nThe framing of the class and the project of generative grammar in the first few weeks was particularly thought-provoking, though it could have accommodated a few more details on the cognitive side.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1625130\n 1625130\n \n 1625130\n The course feels very well-honed in its structure - there's not much I'd want to see changed. It smoothly builds up from fairly obvious and familiar concepts at the beginning of the course to complex syntactic operations by the end, and Prof. Zanuttini and Ka Fai (the TF) made sure that no one was lost along the way. Perhaps my only suggestions for improvement are that a few classes felt like they spent too much of the time reviewing the previous day's material at the beginning, and the readings from the non-Carnie textbooks could be extremely hard to follow (but fortunately, they weren't really necessary at all because the teaching in-class was so clear!). Moreover, I might particularly have enjoyed more optional readings/in-class information about the history/development of the field, the origins of the theories we learned, how they were built on or contrasted with previous work, and what assumptions they made - since it sometimes felt like new theories/principles/rules were introduced as Laws of Nature, despite the fact that they're seemingly all pretty ragged around the edges in the end. If you accept the theories as-is, you'll leave the course feeling like you have witnessed the hidden architecture of the beautiful system that is language. If you're like me and are not completely sure you accept them, you'll still have an appreciation for the beauty of the theoretical program that this course follows, but you may also leave with a nagging feeling that something you can't place doesn't quite add up, and wish you could learn about some alternative programs (maybe that means I need to take Syntax II, though...).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630594\n 1630594\n \n 1630594\n Strengths: the homework assignments were always just challenging enough, never felt unfairly difficult or long, but were always thought-provoking. Professor Zanuttini is also excellent, and happy to discuss any of the material. The overall atmosphere of the course is very friendly and collaborative--I especially like the option to work with a group on some of the homework assignments.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: the course moved a little too fast in some places, especially in the week before the midterm and the week before the final, where a lot of information was conveyed very quickly. I think some of the movement units could have used an extra week (especially DP-movement), and the beginning of the course could move a little faster to make up for it.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1632059\n 1632059\n \n 1632059\n The biggest strength of this class by far is how organized the class is. There is always a pset every week, there will always be office hours, there will always be section and handouts for help, et cetera. There are so many resources to help you succeed on the psets and on the exams. The psets were also quite fun in my opinion.\r\nThis next point is both a strength and a weakness. At first you are taught very basic syntactic theory, and as the course progresses the things you learn earlier in the semester are rendered obsolete by the new theories you learn (like X-bar theory). I think this is a very good teaching model, but it also means that the gross amount of content you learn in the semester is not maximized to the length of the semester. If we just started with X-bar theory we would be able to cover a higher gross amount of content, but this would be at the cost of both a steady introduction to syntax and a chronological trip through syntactic theory. So, I think this is a subjective matter.
