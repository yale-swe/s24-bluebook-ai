Often, with the heavy reading weeks, however, the discussion of novels feels short and I wish we had more time to unpack each of them.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575196\n 1575196\n \n 1575196\n Strengths. Professor Cleary is an excellent discussion leader; he guides students through central themes and also allows us space to engage on our own terms. I appreciate that the register of literary analysis in this course ranged from political to philosophical to emotional. He is also kind, warm, and willing always to go above and beyond to help with writing assignments.\r\nThe other seminar members were extremely engaged as well, and the syllabus was excellently crafted, one of those syllabi in which you feel gratified by themes returning throughout the course. I also appreciated our discussions of contemporary literature. \r\n\r\nI cant think of many weaknesses for this course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575309\n 1575309\n \n 1575309\n The syllabus was incredible but we read through novels too quickly and often didnt have enough time to properly reflect through more personally chosen canvas questions.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575346\n 1575346\n \n 1575346\n Basically no weaknesses. I know in class that we discussed a better integration of secondary sources; this would be nice. I also wonder if maybe, so as to ensure some subjects are covered, there might be a poll or something before each class so that students can vote on what part or theme of the novel they wish to discuss most.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575449\n 1575449\n \n 1575449\n Excellent course. Only additions I would suggest are on the writing front: doing peer critiquing of essay drafts to get further feedback on our writing and introducing a writing assignment where students get to do some reflection on their own educational experiences.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575470\n 1575470\n \n 1575470\n The main strength of this course is Professor Cleary, who also comes to seminars prepared to lead a thorough and engaging discussion. He not only gives great feedback on essays, but takes the time to read through canvas posts and comment on them too. The syllabus is very well-selected and covers the university experience over a range of continents and historical periods. The only weakness of this course that I can think of is the reading load, which can be 400 pages a week, not to mention the critical readings. To improve the class, I would maybe cover Jude the Obscure in just one week and make more room for class discussion on the critical readings. I personally found 'Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man' and 'Disgrace' the least enjoyable to read and arguably focused the least on the university novel, so I would suggest cutting those too. In addition, as so many seniors take the class, I would love to see some of the reading responses encourage seniors to reflect upon their own university experience and relate the novels to Yale today. Finally, it could be interesting to incorporate other forms of media into the class, maybe as optional reading response prompts, such as the television series of normal people or The Chair.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1587334\n 1587334\n \n 1587334\n I thoroughly enjoyed the course. The readings were fascinating and felt especially pertinent to a graduating Senior looking back on both my time in University as well as my decades of 'education' in and outside of the classroom. Professor Cleary is a great and engaging teacher, and I always looked forward to class discussions.\r\n\r\nMy only main pieces of feedback (really already covered in our last Canvas post) would be to reduce the reading load on some weeks and to consider ways to sometimes vary up assignments, perhaps looking beyond the novel form and/or allowing students to try out a creative assignment. Besides that it was a wonderful course!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1598124\n 1598124\n \n 1598124\n The discussions were great--students were very engaged, and Prof. Cleary facilitated the discussions in such a way that they were open to student input but also felt purposeful and built toward a deeper understanding rather than going in a bunch of different directions. The novels were great individually and in connection to each other--it was interesting to see how texts from a variety of places and time periods dealt with similar themes. The only thing I think could be improved would be to reduce the volume of reading/written responses on Canvas a little.
