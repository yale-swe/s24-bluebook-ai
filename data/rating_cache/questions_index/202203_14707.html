\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624303\n 1624303\n \n 1624303\n I really enjoyed this course and how discussions went.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627235\n 1627235\n \n 1627235\n Strengths: \r\nEncouraged back and forth discussion between students without a professor to moderate at all times which led to engaging discussion sections. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628193\n 1628193\n \n 1628193\n Strengths: Barasch, the history texts. Weaknesses: The Augustine reading.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1629706\n 1629706\n \n 1629706\n I truly did not understand half of what I was reading--the class went by too fast and there was so much information to remember and process that it was basically impossible. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1636958\n 1636958\n \n 1636958\n Strengths: Most of the readings were great; the lectures are absolutely superb. Professor Barasch is wonderful -- funny, kind, forgiving, but deeply deeply knowledgable. I loved his lecture on Plutarch and Caesar.\r\nWeaknesses: 'Historical and political thought' is a pretty vague category, and sometimes one had to stretch a bit to see how a few readings fit into it. I thought the coverage of a lot of the Christian works, apart from Exodus and Genesis, was a little weak. I also think it would be amazing if broader, survey-course-style history was taught in concordance with learning history via a few primary sources. It's sometimes hard to get a real sense of the sweep of history, and what exactly is going on in the context of the books, if one's only source of knowledge is the books themselves. For example, it would have been nice to have learned about the whole Greco-Persian war from a textbook or a lecture, in addition to reading excerpts from Herodotus' interpretations of it. Being able to better understand the 'great books' within their historical context would make it easier to understand their political and intellectual implications.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1636978\n 1636978\n \n 1636978\n I enjoyed the course material, and I especially liked when the material was made available to us as digital copies. I hope Directed Studies can provide more flexibility and affordability with the course texts in the future, whether its making more texts available on Canvas or subsidizing book-buying. I also think that there could be more of an emphasis on the political science aspect of the course; it focuses quite heavily on history, and there is less emphasis on writers views on various political systems.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1638004\n 1638004\n \n 1638004\n There were no weaknesses with the class. The professor used the canvas discussion posts to get us all involved in the class, and we were all encouraged to participate actively. I never felt discouraged from raising my hand out of some fear that I would be wrong. Though my interpretations of the texts were likely misplaced sometimes, the professor and my classmates always wanted to hear what I had to say. I loved every second of the class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639106\n 1639106\n \n 1639106\n I think the course did a great job of improving students writing regarding historical and political texts. But I do think that sometimes the readings assigned were too long given how dense the content was, it was often difficult to give each reading the attention it deserved given the length.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639812\n 1639812\n \n 1639812\n I think the intellectual rigor of the discussions was very high and the sophistication of thought made for a super interesting course. However, I think a little more structure in the discussions would have allowed students to grasp the main concepts and ideas of each text in a more complete way.
