\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1530875\n 1530875\n \n 1530875\n I have developed and learned so many new skills, knowledges, and insights by taking this course. Firstly, I learned how to really get at the heart of large texts and find what they're really trying to say. I can fully parse through a text and say "This is important, this is what the author is trying to drive home." and I can also say "This is cool, but it's not a big deal in the grand scheme of the author's goals and motivations." I've learned the importance of historically situating a piece of literature or an archive while analyzing and examining it so I can piece together all of the moving parts, some of which I can't directly find in the pages. I learned how to analyze a primary source and quickly get at what the goals of this source are, what about this source complicates preexisting literature, and what about this source is relevant to the questions I want to answer. I wrote my first ever historiography in this course, and learning that skill was so intellectually stimulating and while writing this paper, I truly found my voice as a writer and stood confident in how I carried the reader through my argument as a writer. All of these skills beautifully and eloquently culminated into my final project and essay, where I learned how to transpose the histories and handlings of histories from one moment of time onto another moment of time to trace out discontinuities and continuities and figure out what that means when it comes to the questions I want to answer through my project or essay.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1530875\n 1530875\n \n 1530875\n Everything about this course is an absolute strength. Readings were selected carefully and creatively and they really challenged us to look for what things tie (or don't tie) multiple sources together. The course format was also a great strength. We started off the class each week with one student giving an oral presentation on the readings and someone else responding to the presentation, and the ideas brought about from the presenting and responding is what would lead in to our discussion on the readings. We also took class time to talk about what was supposed to go in papers, which was really helpful as someone who never was assigned a primary source analysis that couldn't be over 2 pages or who didn't know what a historiography was. At the end of class every week, we would write in groups a definition of the word "abolition," and as the weeks went on, this practice became more and more formative especially at the end when during the last class, all of us had to come up with a collective definition for the word. We couldn't come to a concrete, finished definition ... but that was the whole point, and although that was frustrating at the end of class it was also such a powerful and formative way to send us off at the end of our last class. Everything about this class was truly a strength.

Focusing on the experiences of Black women throughout the seminar is something I will NEVER forgetthis class and the syllabus and ESPECIALLY Prof. Micah Khater really went out of their way to make this class interrogate the ways in which history is taught. As in, this class utilized sources that didn't just focus on history we just easily know or take at face value: this class engaged scholars that are doing the hard work of finding the pieces of history that have been ignored and bringing them to the fore.\r\nAlso, the class asked us to think about our own relationships to abolition and how we can be a part of the continuing fight for abolition, including understanding what abolition is and how it evolves over time. This class made me both hopeful and apprehensive but mostly hopeful. I'm a hug fan.
