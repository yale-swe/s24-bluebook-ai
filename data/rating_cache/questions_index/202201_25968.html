\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1564138\n 1564138\n \n 1564138\n The flexible schedule of this class can be either a strength or a weakness depending on how you are as a student. As JDRS states himself in one of the first lectures, if you want, you can work through all the pre-recorded lectures and do all the assignments at a faster pace and maybe even finish everything in as short as a couple weeks. But the non-requirement of going to class also means that you can easily fall many lectures behind and then do the minimum to complete the assignments (e.g., skipping through the videos on 2x speed and copying from the instructor's class codebase, which is often nearly identical to the code needed for the assignments). \r\n\r\nOne definite weakness is the factory-farm style grading; students are seemingly encouraged to copy as much of the code as possible from JDRS's class examples for use in the problem set to verbatim replicate the output so the graders have an easy time, and the regrade request culture is a little toxic, with tons of kids grubbing for that extra 1% at office hours if they got 99% and the graders threatening that "the whole pset will be regraded if you request a regrade for a single question, so your score might go down." Here at Yale there's already enough grade toxicity and disincentive to do original work if risks decreasing an assignment score; this class shouldn't contribute to that but it does.\r\n\r\nOne strength is JDRS himself - a funny and engaging and consistent lecturer, clearly with a strong understanding of all the material being taught, and a savvy with Canvas that sets the class page up a lot like an easily navigable webpage and takes advantage of other online resources, like his Intro to Stats class.
