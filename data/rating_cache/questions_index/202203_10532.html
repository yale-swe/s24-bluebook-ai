I think that what you get out of this course depends a lot on your familiarity with the material coming into the course: if you're new to this material, you get a ton from the information presented about music in Europe from 1600-1800, and learn a lot about how to listen critically to classical music; if you have familiarity, some of the discussions on cannon/music from the Americas is very interesting.

Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1614280\n 1614280\n \n 1614280\n I think this course would do very well as being broken up into two courses, nor not de-facto required by the music major. I think it was taught very well this semester, and the material is engaging, but if you're someone who is coming into the class with knowledge of a lot of the material already (but have to take it for the major), time could better be spent elsewhere. I also feel like the quizzes, which are pretty much weekly, and administered in sections, would be much better off being online to open more time for discussion in sections (though I realize there are questions about academic integrity were that to happen).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1621208\n 1621208\n \n 1621208\n This course did a really good job of focusing on the big picture of music history, not just making us memorize individual pieces of music. There is always the question of what gets included and what gets left out of a music history survey course, but I think this course did a really good job of selecting pieces to give us a decently broad overview of the time period.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630393\n 1630393\n \n 1630393\n I thought it was a great class! It was also taught with a sense of self-awareness. We engaged with the course material but also asked why we considered this was the material we consider important to study. We engaged with the study of music history itself and frequently questioned the canon, what it was and why we study it.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630395\n 1630395\n \n 1630395\n I really liked the structure of the class. The way the quizzes were spaced out made things much easier to manage. I also thought the lectures were extremely engaging.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630411\n 1630411\n \n 1630411\n Strengths:\r\n- Excellent lectures, with very engaging lecturers (both prof Peritz and Allison). Even though this isn't the type of music that is most interesting to me, I always found myself really engaging with the lectures and always looking forward to class. Professor Peritz is great at keeping class interesting and fun, and she always has high energy that brings up the mood of the whole group.\r\n- Interesting questions that went along with each lecture, about the broader themes in music at the time. I liked that these were broad enough that we could think about how they connect to more modern styles, so that those of us who are more interested in modern music could connect with themes directly.\r\n- Helpful sections (when we didn't have a quiz) that clarified terms from lecture and let us dig deeper into contect. Allison did a great job of facilitating these and bringing everyone's voice in, especially after lectures where only the same couple of people spoke over and over again. Those of us who don't have extensive background in western art music had more of a place to contribute in section.\r\n- I definitely learned A LOT of content over this semester overall\r\n\r\nWeaknesses:\r\n- There was a huge focus on memorization, which I felt did not contribute to my critical understanding of the content. The requirements of memorizing dates for every piece and composer, cities for every piece, along with term definitions, composer/song names in different languages (especially for those of us who are not familiar with western art music or europen languages) felt excessive and unnecessary. Even though it was emphasized in class that this was NOT about memorization and was really about understanding, with dates/cities/composers/songs/terms on the quizzes, I spent the large majority of my studying time memorizing. When I tried to explain a concept in my own words instead of a memorized restatement of what was said in class, I would often get points taken off on my quizzes, so I found it safest to just memorize everything. \r\n- I felt that there could have been a lot more resources for studying for quizzes, such as study guides, lists of songs/terms that could be on the quiz, etc. The work for this ended up falling on a couple students who put in the work on these and helped the rest of the class; this could have easily been solved by giving these resources to students directly so that they could learn exactly what will be on the quiz. \r\n- I found the 'no technology' rule in class to be a weakness of the course. When studying for quizzes, my written notes prevented me from searching for specific words in a document or copying information into a study guide. I also think that eliminating technology creates a weird divide between people who have accessibility accommodations due to disability and those who don't.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630541\n 1630541\n \n 1630541\n I expected this course to involve a huge amount of memorization of pieces of music and facts, and while there was a good amount of that, the things we had to memorize were always deliberately chosen to be the very important subset, which I am now grateful to know, rather than the old approach to this class, which would have had us memorize tons of useless facts and pieces of music. The lectures and sections were interesting and engaging. It was a bit odd to have two sections for such a small course, but it was nice to have a very small class size.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630650\n 1630650\n \n 1630650\n Overall I loved this course and I don't have many suggestions for it. I like the listening and term quizzes--I appreciate that there were two different kinds of quizzes and that the lowest grade from each was dropped. I liked the research paper as an opportunity to explore something new, and the final analytical essay was a great way to wrap up the course. I also loved the trip to the Beinecke. I always learned a ton in the lectures. \r\nI have two suggestions for the quizzes. For the listening quizzes, I think it could be cool to have the unfamiliar listening on more than one quiz. I think it could be really useful for life and for extending our knowledge beyond this class to have more practice using our skills to analyze something new. And for the term quizzes, those sometimes felt really laborious and just that we were regurgitating stuff we had memorized. So it could be nice to add some sort of short analytical question at the end of each quiz that forces us to think about larger themes from the past two weeks or so instead of just memorizing definitions.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1632344\n 1632344\n \n 1632344\n At first, the (almost) weekly term/listening quizzes seem like a lot, but in the grand scheme of things it is much more manageable than a huge final and it also ensures that you're keeping up with the material on a weekly basis, as opposed to cramming everything at the very end of the semester. Having not taken MUSI 350 prior to this course, I wish there had been a little more upfront discussion of the historical context of the music we studied, but as the semester went on, the dots started to connect themselves.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1633472\n 1633472\n \n 1633472\n Weakness: content could be more inclusive. \r\n\r\nStrength: structure and delivery of the class was great.
