\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1562212\n 1562212\n \n 1562212\n This course was better than PHYS 205L, but PHYS 205L was the worst class I have ever taken in my life, so it is a low bar. \r\nThe experiments in this class were for the most part educational, illuminating, and fun. The lab guides were well-written and very helpful. \r\nIt is a complete joke to label this class a 0.5 credit with no writing distributional credit. Each week we write a lab report from 7-20 pages. The analysis alone has taken over 10 hours. I have lost points in the grading for my writing style, despite it being a Physics lab without a writing distribution. \r\nMy suggestion for this class is to only assign the analysis for most of the labs, rather than the full report. For maybe 2-3 labs over the semester, have the students write a full writeup. For all the other labs, only assign the analysis and maybe some of the discussion. Writing an introduction, procedure, abstract, and conclusion for every lab was a tremendous amount of work and really not educational enough for the effort. Instead, have the students only do this 2-3 times over the semester. \r\nIt is also ridiculous to tell people to do all of the analysis and writeup separately. This is not realistic at all for a lab environment. I agree that people should not be able to put all the work on their partner, but the suggestion that people don't work together for the analysis is insane for a class that is this much work.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1567077\n 1567077\n \n 1567077\n The course gives students a wide choice of experiments to run, which lets them choose which areas of the physical sciences they are more interested in and gain exposure to techniques and theory they might not get from lecture courses. Lab time is structured efficiently, with more than enough time to complete most experiments with high quality data, and letting students immediately start on their experiments once they get to class is very convenient. Support with the experiment and apparatus was also immediately available to students. The experiment guides handed out were also very helpful and contained the right amount of information.\r\nHowever, the workload for this course was by no means commensurate with 0.5 credits as it consistently took more time outside of lab to prepare the lab report than it did to run the experiment. Having to prepare a full lab report for each experiment added on a large workload overhead to the course but was not particularly educational because: a) feedback was only provided once at midterm so it was hard to learn from mistakes; b) many experiments did not warrant a full-fledged report format, e.g. there wasn't much new theory for introductions, the apparatus was too simple for a Methods section, or in general there wasn't enough to say to need an abstract and a conclusion; c) there wasn't much left for students to learn about scientific writing after about the fourth report, and the remaining five did not really develop any new writing skills. In addition, the amount of time needed for data analysis varied wildly between experiments (e.g. Nuclear Reactions and Momentum Conservation took maybe triple the time required for Digital Logic, Electric Fields, and Planck's Constant). \r\nIn my opinion, either the credit value of this course should be increased (i.e. to 1 credit and/or grant a Writing credit), or the workload of writing reports should be decreased, e.g. just the quantitative analysis and presentation of data with a discussion and error analysis writeup for most experiments, and two to four full-format reports per semester.
