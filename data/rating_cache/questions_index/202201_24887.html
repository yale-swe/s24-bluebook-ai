\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1568471\n 1568471\n \n 1568471\n This course could be improved by decreasing the discipline specific readings and shifting those into recommended reading guides for students' with particular research interests. If more emphasis can be placed on discussion posts, that can be a space not only for academic reflection but also for personal connections. In Education and Empire, the class is split into Team A and Team B, with Team A writing their posts before class (on the readings) and Team B writing their posts after class (reflecting on class discussion and in response to Team A's posts). Then they rotate. I found this to be a very useful and intentional way of being present with one another, while still maintaining a structure for incentives and accountability for students to do their readings.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1565103\n 1565103\n \n 1565103\n Yes!! I loved how this class focused on anti-eugenic public facing work. In other words, we didn't just write papers and do the readings to complete the class, but we actually engaged with the community and brainstormed ways to make our work more impactful. One of the projects we did was host Naugatuck High School students and teach them about the pervasive impact of eugenics. I helped give the students an anti-eugenic tour of Yale where we pointed out key buildings and professors at Yale that had contributed to eugenic work. Some students also presented work at the Yale & Slavery Research Project Student Symposium in April, which was very fascinating. We were given a lot of freedom in our final project to do what we wanted, which I appreciated as I was able to develop a curricular unit for high school students on eugenics and immigration. As Yale students, it's very important that we expose the way in which Yale continues to perpetuate eugenic thinking and this class helps do so.
