\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1567437\n 1567437\n \n 1567437\n A major strength of the course is the material, which is objectively really interesting. It was exciting to learn about language in the brain. One thing that got in the way of me fully enjoying the course was the workload. We had about double the amount of assessments compared to other classes, and the class required all of my academic attention at all times. Cutting down on some of the assessments would make the course much more doable, and I don't think we would really lose a lot of the learning experience.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1568100\n 1568100\n \n 1568100\n Strengths: You'll learn so much about this field that you'll leave the class feeling like an expert. Professor Piango doesn't treat us as incapable of absorbing this difficult material--she believes in our ability to comprehend the material. She cold-calls a lot during class but it's never mean, and when she keeps pushing your answer, it's because she knows that you'll get there eventually. I really enjoyed certain units in this class, like when we learned about Broca's patients for the first quarter of the semester. Weaknesses: I don't think we got enough feedback during the semester, which made writing the final paper very hard. I also thought that the tests sometimes had confusing questions, which was hard because a lot of them were true/false (and I'm not good with true/false questions). I honestly couldn't tell you what my final grade will be in this class as we get no feedback, but I really enjoyed taking it! Sometimes I wish we had a TA or someone else who could explain the material, because if I didn't understand Professor Piango's explanation, there was no one else I could ask.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1572392\n 1572392\n \n 1572392\n Strengths: The material is very interesting, and I really enjoyed reading most of the papers in this course. The professor is also a wonderful asset -- she actually wrote or co-wrote several of the papers in the course and knows many of the other authors personally, which was very cool. She is extremely knowledgeable about the material and I really enjoyed learning from her insights. The pilot study was also structured so that we did a little bit of work on the project every week, which was at times a lot of work with the reading, but I appreciated it being spread out.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: a lot of work that wasn't spread out through the semester. The first six weeks or so we had a Pset every week (graded for completion) covering 100-200 pages of reading, which was a lot considering the last half of the semester we had much less reading and less homeworks. The pilot study was also a lot of work to do on top of the reading/psets every week. I also felt that we could probably have one less exam. Sometimes the material is gone through quickly, which is understandable because there is simply so much of it, but I did get confused here and there. Other students in the class may have much more linguistic/neuroscientific background than others, which may feel a little frustrating if you have not done that much coursework, but I found that to actually be stimulating.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575323\n 1575323\n \n 1575323\n The main strength, but also main difficulty, for this course was the sheer amount of content covered and the amount of work we were able to complete. I feel like for a semester's work, I have a really robust understanding of how language happens in the brain, but it was difficult at times to tell what takeaways we were trying to get from each paper because we could get a little bogged down in the details. A lot of the larger picture only coalesced for me when I was studying for the final exam. One weakness of the course was that after a certain point, we were expected to do multiple assignments for one day, e.g. read the papers and do work for the pilot study, and then we got stretched too thin and did neither very well.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1580121\n 1580121\n \n 1580121\n The strengths were in the material itself and Professor Pinango. She's great at explaining difficult concepts, putting everything together, and always gets excited about the material. The homework assignments, although often very time-consuming, always led me to develop a basic understanding on the topics before going into them in depth in the classroom. \r\n\r\nThe main weaknesses were about feedback. Through the semester, we only got grades and feedback for maybe 2 of the weekly homework assignments. The main group project and the final project proposal ended up being educational, but it would have been great if expectations for them had been explained earlier. We would often be working on these projects trying to guess what the assignments were, since they were never explained explicitely in class or section.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1596077\n 1596077\n \n 1596077\n This course covers a lot of material and is very dense. While the readings were difficult, I found that class time helped me understand them more thoroughly. The course also requires that you compete a pilot neurolinguistics study, which can be a lot alongside the course material, but I found it enjoyable.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1598659\n 1598659\n \n 1598659\n strengths: breadth of material covered; hands-on research experience
