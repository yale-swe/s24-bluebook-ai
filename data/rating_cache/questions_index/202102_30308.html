\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1515163\n 1515163\n \n 1515163\n Initially, I found this course a bit uncomfortable because of the subject matter and my personal identity. I'm a white guy and I consider myself progressive and in no way sympathetic to U.S. imperialism, but certain themes in the course challenged me. I was slightly uncomfortable with the emphasis on masculinity and whiteness, but after completing the readings and hearing discussion, I felt like my eyes were really opened. I found myself subconsciously defending certain events before I really considered them, but once I truly investigated the facts rationally I felt enlightened. Perhaps the most formative moment in the course was when I read that the Texas Revolution was the most successful filibuster action. For me, this was revolutionary on a personal and intellectual level because one of my ancestors died at the Alamo. That was always a cool fact growing up and I was taught by my very liberal parents that it was a heroic act. Learning more about the general trends of the antebellum era showed me how wrong I was. I fell much more thoughtful about American foreign policy following this course. I don't think I will ever look at things in black and white again and I think thats a big deal.

We read a diverse range of articles, book chapters, and essays from writers in fields covering history, foreign policy, and gender, and often complemented our readings with in-depth coverage of primary sources.
