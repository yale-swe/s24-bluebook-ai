\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1616475\n 1616475\n \n 1616475\n This course was well-organized with clear instructions and expectations. You have access to all the lecture slides and download each one before class. You receive a helpful study guide before every quiz and the final exam, highlighting important questions to focus on as you review. There are also three extra credits distributed throughout the semester with hard deadlines but clear instructions (no science background is required).\r\nMy favorite part of the course is definitely the sections (a combination of discussion and lab)! In discussion sections, we talk about so many interesting topics I never thought about before, such as debates surrounding genetic ethics, anthropological collection ownership, and the actual biological consequences of the constructed social concept of race. In lab sections, we got to examine real specimen of Darwins finches and try to figure out the phylogeny tree as he did; we got to examine casts of Lucys bones and compare with modern humans skeleton; we got to practice forensic anthropology with casts (its basically trying to determine the age, sex, height, identity, cause of death of deceased individuals from bone remains etc. it was mind-blowing - like in a crime movie!!!). I would take this class even for sections alone! They opened my eyes to so many cool things out there and made me view many important topics in a completely different way.\r\nI personally found the quizzes (11 multiple choice + 3 short answer + 2 bonus in 20 minutes) a bit tight in time, though they are not designed to be. Sometimes I struggle with finishing in time, especially when the short answer questions require some more thinking - not really slow-thinker-friendly, but still doable.\r\nAnother thing to note is that Professor Thompson stopped posting the lecture recordings publicly halfway into the semester due to low in-person attendance. You can still request access if you miss a lecture occasionally (I did once and Professor Thompson was extremely supportive and helpful in making sure I can get access to it). I have nothing against her decision (in fact, Im quite supportive of it) since its totally understandable and reasonable, but I have to admit that it posed a small challenge for me as I previously relied on recordings for quiz review. It was not a big deal as I changed my study strategy soon after to focus more on class notes and slides, but if you have to rely on recordings every time, then you have to rethink before taking this class. However, Professor Thompson did make all lecture recordings available again before the final exam.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630799\n 1630799\n \n 1630799\n The regular short-assessment aspect of this course is a unique strength. It drove learning in a non-stressful way.\r\n\r\nUnfortunately, this class also has a lot of room for improvement. The slides are very disorganized, making it difficult to study for short exams even when topics are provided ahead of time. The disorganization also made it difficult to situate topics chronologically. I have now completed the class and still have very little clue when everything we studied took place. This could have been remedied with a simple timeline that is shown consistently and indicates when the current topic dates to in a way that makes it easy to place in the broader course context.\r\n\r\nThe lack of coordination between content covered in lectures and content assessed on exams was a constant source of frustration. Little effort was made in lectures to distinguish between important take-home lessons and tangential information that wouldn't appear on exams. Questions that were completely unaddressed by class content appeared with shocking frequency on exams. It was a regular and frustrating experience to see a question that clearly dated from a previous year when that topic had been covered but had not been covered at all or in the necessary depth this time around. Even though I studied thoroughly for each exam from the materials provided, and have thousands of words in study documents to substantiate that claim, I found myself guessing on about a quarter of exam questions.\r\n\r\nI also felt that the class didn't take the students' time commitment seriously. We regularly spent the first 10-15 minutes of class addressing questions that could have been answered in office hours and were therefore behind on lecture content for almost the entire semester. This meant the syllabus was less helpful as a reference and that we had to watch an entire lecture on our own time at the end of the semester to prepare for the exam. The assignments themselves also often felt like busy work where the time investment to complete them was not proportionate to the learning opportunity. This is the only class I've had at Yale where the principal source of required readings is entire chapters from an introductory textbook. The time/learning ratio is worse for this kind of learning than any other. The section activities also felt more like high school labs than a real college course. One section was even justified as an opportunity not to learn the material, but to gain an understanding of how difficult that kind of task must have been for early scientists to conduct.\r\n\r\nFinally, I think the content of this class could be so much more interesting and pertinent. Most of the heart of this course tracks the human ancestral fossil record, what anthropologists have discovered when and where. While I understand this is central to the work of those in the department, it is far removed from what most students will find interesting and helpful. I took this course because I wanted to learn how human evolutionary history influences the world today. I wanted to know how it might explain why we are so addicted to our cell phones, why we have an obesity crisis in some parts of the world, and why we are so quick to form prejudices and stereotypes. We addressed none of these questions, with the exception of one short lecture on the neolithic crammed into the final day where we touched on the most obvious aspects of the second one. I am much more likely to find answers to those questions useful than I am information on the names and dates of specific fossils.
