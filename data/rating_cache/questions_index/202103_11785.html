\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1521544\n 1521544\n \n 1521544\n In general the course is pretty good. It's not vey much work, which is nice but also doesn't force you to engage as hard with the material. If you do the readings you'll be fine, and they aren't overwhelming. The course is a cool idea and brings together different perspectives. If you're a history buff, then much of the factual historical lecture may not be new to you, but the literature probably will be, as will some of the interesting historical narratives woven during lecture. The midterm and final assignments offer creative options, which is nice and can be a fun way to engage with the material.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532223\n 1532223\n \n 1532223\n The unique texts that I found in this as opposed to other Medieval courses were the Arabic texts, such as 1001 Nights and the background of Saladin. However, I failed to really grasp the "multicultural" aspect of the class because all of the texts still centered around the Mediterranean, and the degree of cultural variation studied was almost entirely Islamic vs Christian kingdoms (and an odd divergence into gender?). I found this disappointing because there is such a diversity of traditions within Europe - comparing Nordic scripts to Spanish poetry, for example - that was washed over, not to mention diversity within the Islamic world and the entire Asiatic and African experiences of the middle ages.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1533395\n 1533395\n \n 1533395\n Strengths - the Astonishing Women unit and the final lectures based on the movie; weaknesses: could've explored more of the "multicultural" perspective. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1545355\n 1545355\n \n 1545355\n This course is absolutely amazing in the sense that the instructors truly care about the course and the material covered. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546584\n 1546584\n \n 1546584\n Its a pretty good class. Theres a lot of reading but the amount is inconsistent week to week which makes it hard to plan out your time.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546612\n 1546612\n \n 1546612\n Strengths: \r\nProfessors: Marcel Elias literally adorable and Ardis Butterfield is a badass. \r\nPolitics: relevant takes on history-present day convergences, but also not being indoctrinating presentism. \r\nSupplemental stuff: great recommendations for supplemental readings without it being overwhelming\r\nTransmission of knowledge, treatment of literature: pretty darn decent. I'm constantly seeking classes/teachers that will help Make Ideas Real, especially Past Ideas/Past Realities. It's simple to do, but not easy. One success in this class is the focus on Artifact. E.g., visiting Beinecke and YUAG, and lectures focused with photos etc. Some professors, so knowledgeable about their field, don't really get that sometimes, reading non-contemporary literature is like being dropped into the middle of the Atlantic Ocean and being expected to know the coordinates from the water temp. Historical context babyyy. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: \r\nI hate digitized readings so much and I just want to have short little readings that I can actually freaking finish. I don't really know why that is so impossible in every English class. Also, lmao, I genuinely thought this was a history department course for several weeks because I'm an idiotso I was sort of confused by all the literary readings for a little bit and then I realized what was up. I think this about every literature class, but: I could've used a timeline and a map for a syllabus, instead of a list. Smartie expert professors need to better realize how little some students know about their field, and just spoon-feed certain foundations of clarity. TIMELINES! MAPS! This is now a rant about syllabi formats. People need visual or pictorial or at least SIMPLE-textual versions of information in order to contextualize something as nebulous as literature. It doesn't solidify into object without this. However, as mentioned, seeing artifacts in the Beinecke is part of that real-ifying of literature.... and they really did a good job in my opinion, I just crave it in syllabus form.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546962\n 1546962\n \n 1546962\n One strength was the syllabus - the readings covered a huge amount of ground, but each week felt grounded in a particular theme. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1547459\n 1547459\n \n 1547459\n I thought that having both professors was an incredible advantage to the course. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548491\n 1548491\n \n 1548491\n The strengths of this course are that the lectures do a great job of elaborating on the weekly readings and putting them into context. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552371\n 1552371\n \n 1552371\n I think that this is a fine introductory course, especially for an English major hoping to fulfill the Medieval period requirement.
