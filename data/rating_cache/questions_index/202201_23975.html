\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575916\n 1575916\n \n 1575916\n This course completely revolutionized the way I grapple with texts, made me much more confident in my ability to analyze texts, and instilled me with a newfound appreciation for political philosophy. Coming into the course, although I already had a semester of Directed Studies under my belt, I did not yet feel completely confident with tackling the great books unaided. Throughout first semester, I often relied on reading the introductions of the texts we read to help guide my interpretation of the texts, and felt that I was incapable of forming my own unique interpretation of the great books. In H&P class last semester, I would often allow the lecturer to inform my view of the text -- in my mind, they were always correct, and I deferred to their intellectual authority. But right from the get go, Professor Schillinger encouraged every student in the class to read actively and engage with these works in such a manner that would allow us to arrive at our own unique understandings. For example, in my first paper, I allowed an insight made by the writer of our book's introduction to serve as the basis of my argument because I did not feel confident that I could come up with my own unique idea. In his feedback on that paper, Professor Schillinger said that he felt that my own voice was missing from the paper and encouraged me to bring more of my own unique thoughts and ideas into the next one. This encouragement really made a big difference for me confidence-wise. After that, I felt capable of confronting these books head-on and forming my own interpretations of these difficult texts prior to listening to the lecture. Professor Schillinger also encouraged individuals to confidently grapple with these texts by having every student in the class lead a class discussion and come up with questions beforehand. \r\n\r\nI became a much better reader in this course than in any of my DS courses so far, and I think that's partly because Professor Schillinger reads the texts so closely that I was inspired to do the same. Professor Schillinger was constantly bringing the text back into the conversation -- at times he would say in response to a person's comment, "that reminds me of a quote on pg xxx", and sure enough we would flip to that page and there would be relevant information there (he truly knows these texts like the back of his hand). The questions that he would send out prior to our seminar discussions would really help guide my annotations and allow me to develop a nuanced understanding of each author's central questions that I wouldn't have had otherwise. For example, prior to our Marx reading, Prof. Schillinger sent us out a list of questions to keep in mind as we read, including something along the lines of "what's the difference between ideology and philosophy?". This was a nuanced distinction that I may have never caught without being prompted to look for it, and it ended up being central to my analysis of the text. In this class, I analyzed central ideas from the semester like, what is the best regime? How should people be organized into states? Is there/what is human nature, and how does that inform our understanding of how to form states? What does it mean to be free? What are the crises plaguing modern history? Has there been progress over history? I also learned how to pay attention to key passages and details such as the camera obscura metaphor in Marx, or the metaphor about beauty and ugliness (and how it relates to Nietzsche's "Yes!") in Genealogy of Morals.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575916\n 1575916\n \n 1575916\n In general, I think Directed Studies should slightly change the H&P syllabus next semester. I would recommend taking out Sieyes' The Third Estate -- I just didn't feel that it added that much to my understanding of the semester's themes and it didn't feel that related to the other texts on the syllabus. Perhaps it could be replaced with either Frederick Douglass's Narrative (since then it could be put in conversation with Hegel's slave dialectic) or with Franz Fanon's The Wretched of the Earth. Also, retrospectively, I think DS finals are actually really helpful. Especially for H&P, re-reading all my annotations and studying with friends helped me really understand how these books work in conversation with each other. \r\n\r\nIn terms of this particular course's strengths and weaknesses, I can really only think of strengths. I am not being hyperbolic when I say this -- this was the best DS section I was in all year. Partly, this is because class discussions were amazing! All of my peers were so enthusiastic and engaged in the discussions. I think part of the reason why is because Prof. Schillinger makes the classroom feel like a safe space where people feel comfortable to participate and contribute. Professor Schillinger is also really incredible at leading discussions and keeping the momentum going. He strikes the right balance between letting students take the lead in discussion but also interjecting with his own thoughts and ideas (either building off student ideas, adding nuance to ideas being discussed, asking clarifying questions, and contributing his own ideas prior to discussion to help introduce the text). \r\n\r\nI thought another strength of the course was Professor Schillinger's essay feedback. He gave really detailed comments and would annotate our papers so that we could see feedback regarding specific lines. I also found office hours to be quite helpful. Professor Schillinger always made himself available to meet with students to discuss papers, and also would ask me my opinions on that week's text in office hour meetings. These questions would always push me and encourage me to develop my understanding of the themes further.
