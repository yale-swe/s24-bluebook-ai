\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1698608\n 1698608\n \n 1698608\n Strengths: Scott is super knowledgeable, we are exposed to some cool composers and music, class is pretty open ended to be shaped towards your interest, SuperCollider, while confusing, is really powerful so it's good to get some experience using it \r\nCons: I almost wish the class was more geared to composers rather than computer scientists - what we were learning was really cool but the musical applications felt a bit superficial compared to what is possible with these rich systems we were learning about
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1701120\n 1701120\n \n 1701120\n This course was a little difficult to relate to as the content is very niche and non-intuitive.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1701677\n 1701677\n \n 1701677\n Strengths: Classes are interesting and varied. Some days will be group work, other days will be lectures, and if you're lucky, sometimes half a lecture will just be Scott playing Minecraft! Talking about how the game uses features like random systematic generation, and how those features can be applied to music composition, of course. Scott is a very knowledgable and engaging professor.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: This is the only course I've taken at Yale that doesn't really use Canvas. Instead, we use Scott's Notion. It's clear that he has put a LOT of time into developing and organizing his Notion, and that it offers certain features that Canvas doesn't, which make the integration of code, video, and sound snippets possible in a way that Canvas couldn't replicate. That said, it's also clear that his Notion has gotten unwieldy, and his tendency to "save as" led to repeated problems where links or forms were out of date. The learning curve for understanding which things were due when was steep and unnecessarily stressful. To compensate for this, Scott had a generous lateness policy, but overall I think the Notion either needs a major overhaul, or this class should just try to make the best of using Canvas so students don't need to learn an entirely new system just to perform the meta operations of being a student in the class (like figuring out what the homework is and when it's due and how to submit it).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1706407\n 1706407\n \n 1706407\n A strength of this course is its assignment-based format each of the assignments, coupled with a blog post, allows you to dabble in a different area of computer music each time that builds atop the last. Another strength is the lectures, which are some of the most interesting that I've had for a computer science course at Yale. Moreover, the differing lecture formats is a strength, as it keeps you engaged with the material.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1714331\n 1714331\n \n 1714331\n The course's only merit is, Scott showed some responsiveness by canceling one assignment after feedback, although the workload remained excessive. Scott's enthusiasm and ease in presenting topics were notable, yet his interest often seemed singular within the classroom, particularly outside of initial classes and one discussing video game scores and sound effects. A significant drawback was the strict attendance and punctuality policy, demanding high focus in every session. Scott's insistence on traditional note-taking methods, prohibiting mobile phones and requiring paper, aimed to ensure engagement but primarily served to enforce attention. These aspects significantly influenced the final grades.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1716261\n 1716261\n \n 1716261\n very interactive
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1718158\n 1718158\n \n 1718158\n Strengths: freedom and flexibility in projects\r\nWeaknesses: During class Prof. Petersen would go on tangents and I would struggle to follow along.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1722609\n 1722609\n \n 1722609\n The main weakness of the course is SuperCollider itself. SuperCollider is the best software out there for algorithmic composition, yet it's not a very good one. The documentation is mediocre and confusing at times; the error reporting is terrible and opaque; the syntax is annoying; and (though this is not very relevant for 95% of the course, sans the final project) a lot of packages are deprecated. As a result, there is a steep learning curve to being able to program in this language.\r\n\r\nScott is also an idiosyncratic professor who may not be everyone's cup of tea, but I personally think he's awesome and will be taking 432 with him next semester.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724279\n 1724279\n \n 1724279\n The blog notion format is a great way for students to interact and work off of eachother given blog posts and formats to learn strategies for future projects.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724420\n 1724420\n \n 1724420\n Lectures are enjoyable and there is time for open discussions during class as well. Plenty of materials and code examples are given, and Prof. Petersen and Nicole were always happy to help. This course's main deliverables are projects, which I believe suit this class well. Each main topic and composition idea gets its own project, and the final project allows you to combine a number of smaller components to create a more elaborate music generator. I had no complaints with this class' structure.
