\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1567431\n 1567431\n \n 1567431\n The course was a good introduction to the field of pragmatics. I learned a lot about the history of the field and its connections to semantics and other subfields of linguistics. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1582023\n 1582023\n \n 1582023\n We were given an extensive overview of speech acts, presupposition, implicature, experimental pragmatics, etc. from an amazing professor!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1589727\n 1589727\n \n 1589727\n I learned about the use of language in context and different types of meanings that are contained in any utterance- presuppositions, implicatures, etc. We talked about the interaction/overlap between semantics and pragmatics, conversational maxims and how we intend and comprehend utterances, and different types of speech acts.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1592784\n 1592784\n \n 1592784\n This course offered a fantastic insight into the relationship between language and its usage. The central topic of this course was meaning in language and how it relates to both context and speaker intentions. The course was broadly divided into three sections: Speech acts, presupposition, and implicature. Speech acts are rituals like naming, marriage, inauguration, agreement, and other instances of linguistic meaning that change the world instead of communicating information. A presupposition is a condition that both speaker and hearer must believe to be true before a particular assertion can be made. An implicature is meaning that is beyond the literal sense of what is said. Throughout this course, we developed philosophical and cognitive pictures of the use of language to mean more than what is said.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1593547\n 1593547\n \n 1593547\n Some linguistics, a notion of what pragmatics is (not triviail!), and some of the fields in pragmatics.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1594763\n 1594763\n \n 1594763\n how context affects language and what we take for granted in speech: the underlying theory of speech acts, presupposition, implication, and reference. some fun stuff about what constitutes lies and truth!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1594822\n 1594822\n \n 1594822\n This course teaches you a lot about the unsaid parts of language, at the crossroads between linguistics, cognitive science, and philosophy. Not only was this helpful for me academically--for instance, learning how papers are written in those fields--but I also now use these things in my daily life, such as how to reiterate something to someone, and how to be more direct.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1594846\n 1594846\n \n 1594846\n Lots of pragmatic insights!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1595620\n 1595620\n \n 1595620\n I developed knowledge on the path of the study of pragmatics as well as perspectives on current topics in pragmatics.

This course has changed the way I think about language, I keep finding myself thinking about it in my daily conversations.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1596476\n 1596476\n \n 1596476\n We covered speech acts, presupposition, implicature, the distinction between what is said/what is implicated, and reference. I became able to read and interpret work at the boundary between linguistics and philosophy of language, and even write my own.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1596476\n 1596476\n \n 1596476\n Strongly! A surprising number of non-linguistics-majors took the course and all seemed to enjoy it greatly. What was most special, in my opinion, was that it was an introduction to a subfield that is really still in the middle of active development, even of its very fundamental principles. It became clear over the course of the semester that so much of what we were being introduced to was various philosophers of language/semanticians/pragmaticians just throwing new terms and definitions at the wall to see if they stuck - and the course made it feel like we, too, could be part of this great sandbox experiment. Josh (Prof. Phillips??) was so instrumental to creating this atmosphere, displaying unparalleled earnest excitement for the content and sending us (both the whole class and individually) lists of literature to check out on any topic we were interested in. The material was certainly confusing and chaotic at times, and I think I ended the course thinking that a lot of existing pragmatic theory was misguided in various ways. But feeling like I could articulate my disputes and imagine what better approaches I would take was possibly one of the most intellectually rewarding experiences I've had yet.
