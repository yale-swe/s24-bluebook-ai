I developed mastery of Bernard Suits' framework for understanding games and activities in general which is pretty niche but super cool and surprisingly generalizable. I also gained really cool and fascinating insights from the other students every class.\r\nMost importantly, I found this class to be a really exciting and helpful introduction to philosophical discussion and writing. Professor Maxwell is excellent at facilitating this; he elevates each students' arguments to their most refined form in class without overwhelming the conversation space, and he gives helpful and guiding feedback both in comments on the midterm paper and in one-on-one meetings in preparation for the final paper. I learned to think in terms of necessary and sufficient conditions, hypotheticals, and philosophical trial and error, all of which can be terrific tools for evaluating or analyzing basically anything in the world.\r\nWe were assigned one or two papers (or chapters of The Grasshopper by Bernard Suits) for reading before each class, and Professor Maxwell did an excellent job at walking us through the arguments we had read and making sure we understood what we were discussing before we really dived into it, again without ever pushing his interpretation or taking up a large amount of space in the discussion.\r\nThe class deals with anything you can think of that fits in the category of "playing a game" (in fact, we spend lots of time considering if this category exists and what falls inside or outside of it). This includes computer games, board games, sports, playground games, anything. We try to figure out what these have in common and what they don't and come to some very interesting and impressively relevant conclusions about activities, good, and society writ large.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548488\n 1548488\n \n 1548488\n I'm inclined to say that this class was all strengths. Professor Maxwell is very excited to engage in and facilitate discussion of this material, and he does a terrific job of bringing out the best insights and arguments every student has to offer. In some ways, it feels like the topic of games could be substituted out for any kind of category that allows for in-depth philosophical analysis, but I think that because every student has extensive personal experience with games and game-playing, everyone feels like and is an expert with truly valuable insights to offer once we are sitting down and discussing it. Additionally, the blend of low stakes and deep meaning that comes in games specifically allowed us to have truly deep and powerful conversations about justice, value, ideal societies, and a lot of stuff that come close to the meaning of life without anyone feeling unqualified our out of their depth.\r\nI felt that the class strikes a truly impressive balance between depth and breadth, allowing for a full and sturdy understanding of The Grasshopper, the book that serves as our main text throughout the semester, while also allowing us to have meaningful discussions of constitutive rules, gamification, and the slightest bit of game theory, along with every other notable definition of games or game-playing that exists within and outside of the world of philosophy. I think some students felt like our conversations became a little repetitive around the middle of the semester when we were mostly dealing with The Grasshopper, though I generally felt fulfilled and excited by every class. Nonetheless, it could have been energizing and effective if we had actually played more games, either in class or in optional out-of-class meetups organized by the Professor, especially around the middle of the semester. In addition to being fun and bonding, these common game-playing experiences can and did allow for specific points to reference and play with in in-class discussions.\r\nOne last potential field for improvementthe class, as it is now, allows for a really wonderful atmosphere between students and a kind of collaborative, fun, and deep basis for friendships through class conversations. However, this potential could be fully taken advantage of if we had done just a bit more of getting to know one another (even just our names and where we are from, etc.) in the first few classes.
