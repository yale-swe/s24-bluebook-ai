Writing multiple papers, with comments received on each, helps the student strengthen their close-reading skills, as well as their ability to form interesting questions and to write logically and lucidly.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1633738\n 1633738\n \n 1633738\n The ability to analyze complex historical situations and recordings. Others focused more heavily on political science - what is the goal of human life? How can society be constructed in a way that ensures happiness? How to achieve long term stability through the right form of government and ruler? We tracked the development of ideas from the Greek era into the Roman era, then the Middle Ages and saw how they could be reconciled with rationalist religious frameworks. The same forms of governments and early seeds of social contract theory will remain relevant as we will explore their evolution in the modern era.\r\n \r\nSkills - internalizing a lot of material on short timelines in the realms of history and poli-sci; comparative analysis; discussion of texts in a seminar setting; group-work; writing skills.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1624396\n 1624396\n \n 1624396\n Professor Freedman is fantastic and really cares about teaching. The material, however, was somewhat dry, especially at the beginning of the semester.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627379\n 1627379\n \n 1627379\n I don't really have any suggestions here, I think it was a wonderful course as is.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628495\n 1628495\n \n 1628495\n Strengths: wonderful professor, very engaging discussions in a great and collaborative environment \r\nWeaknesses: the pace and volume of reading, but this is an inevitable part of DS
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628524\n 1628524\n \n 1628524\n The course covers too much material in too little time. The readings are long and dense, which makes them difficult to get through at times. It is almost impossible to complete all of them on top of the other readings for Directed Studies, and since these are the longest, I found that with my peers they were the ones that most often fell to the wayside. This is a shame because the material is extremely interesting, but we couldnt dive very deeply into the texts because we had to move on to the next quite rapidly. \r\n\r\nThere should be more clarity as to what constitutes a good H&P paper. Though it does vary by professor, examples successful essays would be very useful to have on hand. This would help de-mystify the style of writing that is necessary for a good paper and clarify the kind of shift in approach needed from writing high school-style essays.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628712\n 1628712\n \n 1628712\n Some of the readings--Clouds, for instance--seemed a bit disconnected from the others. But on the whole, the course is marvelous. You'll read historical (Herodotus, Livy, Tacitus) and political (Plato, Aristotle) thought, both of which are wonderful and decidedly interconnected. You'll meet fascinating students, encounter fascinating ideas, and make fascinating discoveries, both about the texts you read and also about yourself.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628780\n 1628780\n \n 1628780\n I think the main strength of this course is how you grow to appreciate the connections between ancient texts and how those relate to the modern world. There aren't any glaring weaknesses, but I think students sometimes struggled to retain the big ideas and events from the readings afterwards, and it might be good if the instructors could provide more supplementary materials to this effect, like summaries or themes that could then be compiled for each work. This could also be helped by earlier access to the finals study review documents.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1629233\n 1629233\n \n 1629233\n This course is truly interdisciplinary, but this apparent lack of thematic focus can sometimes make the DS reading-load feel extra heavy.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1629779\n 1629779\n \n 1629779\n The strengths of this course were the continuity of ideas, the way the texts built upon each other, the quality and engagement of my peers, and the fascinating ideas found in the texts. The weaknesses were the lack of connections to the modern world/politics, the speed with which we covered texts (at the expense of true understanding, at least in my experience), and some readings which I found challenging to the point of complete disengagement.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1633738\n 1633738\n \n 1633738\n I think that splitting off into smaller groups was helpful, but if it occurred too often it caused the class to feel a bit more rigid in the topics we were allowed to discuss compared to other DS courses. I enjoyed a closer talk with several of my peers which I think was highly rewarding, but sometimes it felt like it took away from the large group discussion or from discussing several different sections of the text. Perhaps splitting into smaller groups could either be done once a week or possibly on alternating weeks in the future so students can experience the rewards of both formats a little more equally.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1637674\n 1637674\n \n 1637674\n DS covers broad swaths of material - that's both its strength (scope) and weakness (depth). The format of both lectures and seminar discussions helps compensate for this.\r\nH&P, by design, focuses on history and poli-sci which means some students may gravitate towards particular texts. But, it also allows students to experience multiple perspectives from each era. Personally, I thought all the texts were interesting, the lectures great and the seminar discussions of my H&P section were a highlight.
