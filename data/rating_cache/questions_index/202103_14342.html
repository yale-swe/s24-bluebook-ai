I was introduced to the seminal political and historical texts of the West (and also their relations to one another in an established tradition), yet was also encouraged and pushed to interact with them, to bring myself and my ideas to whatever they themselves had to offer. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539345\n 1539345\n \n 1539345\n The strength of this course is the amount of material we cover and the professors that cover it. Directed Studies H&P essentially goes from The Bible and to Aquinas, which is a truly amazing amount of texts. We also covered a lot of ground textually, with religious texts like Aquinas' Summa Theologiae sharing a spot on the syllabus with classic history texts like Polybius' "Rise of the Roman Empire". Another strength of the course are the lecturers. With a new lecturer every week, each a leading scholar in their field, the amount of detail they go into is truly incredible. That being said, an issue with the course is that it might cover too much in too short a time. There were some texts that definitely deserved more attention than we were allowed to give them, and I left many of the classes disappointed that we couldn't keep talking about texts like the Bible after spending only one class (essentially an hour) on the entire book of Genesis.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1543641\n 1543641\n \n 1543641\n This course had myriad strengths. As a seminar, the class learned how to keep a thread of discussion going, to listen to disparate ideas, and to also celebrate disagreement. Specifically in the context of Historical and Political thought, we also learned how to apply large, overarching questions pertaining to human political organization and historical currents to contemporary political and historical questions, which was thrilling. The course could move quite rapidly, and it was difficult at first to both delineate and relate the texts when we read them so quickly; yet such a rapid pacing of the syllabus was also a great strength of the course---excitement never waned.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1545773\n 1545773\n \n 1545773\n This course excelled at conveying its subject matter effectively and memorablyI felt that the weekly discussion posts were a great element that encouraged me to do the reading carefully even when the temptation to skim was strong. The class's discussion format was another strength, as it allowed us all to engage constructively with each other's perspectives on and interpretations of the material.\r\n\r\nI felt that class discussion, whose centrality was one of the course's strengths, could have benefited at times from more direction from the professor. This did occur to some degree, but I think a bit more leadership / directing of the conversation toward relevant subjects could be helpful in the future.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1547228\n 1547228\n \n 1547228\n The strength of DS in general of course lies largely in the texts. There are some surprising and fascinating texts in this curriculum, which combine to give you a good idea of how the ancients thought about history and politics. That said, I sometimes felt that the reading was a little too much. The theoretical bits of the texts were usually interesting, but it was often hard to focus on the historical details, because I knew I could never learn enough about the particulars to form any kind of informed perspective on a subject. I feel like any serious discussion of history requires time to familiarise yourself with the details, and time is not something that DS allows you in abundance. The only thing that gives you an opportunity to delve into a decent amount of depth into the texts is writing essays, so even though they add to an already considerable workload, I think the essays are a strength of the programme. Then, of course, the seminars are a precious opportunity to compare your ideas on the texts with others', and look at them from a range of new angles.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548983\n 1548983\n \n 1548983\n This course solidified a timeline from ancient Greece to imperial Rome for me, but medieval history was definitely overshadowed by the political thought during that era.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552322\n 1552322\n \n 1552322\n I really could have used more historical context.
