\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1608263\n 1608263\n \n 1608263\n The course was extremely well designed and thought out. Each reading was manageable and very helpful in understanding the various schools of thought on the topic. One minor point of improvement for the class is that I felt the course title could have been more specific to include the heavy emphasis on political thought that ended up being part of the class. Other than that, the political philosophy was a very welcome inclusion, and I really enjoyed every minute of this class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1610547\n 1610547\n \n 1610547\n I think the course is well-taught, well-designed, and well-assessed. The only potential weakness I could point to is that, in an attempt help students orient themselves towards both the close reading of complex theoretical texts, and their consideration at a broad, conceptual level, the course asks too little of them in terms of reading load. The reading load of the course was by not light, but I personally would have preferred a more extensive reading list, since the domains of thought in which we were moving were at times foreign to me. I can think of no other weaknesses; it was otherwise one of the best classes I have taken at Yale, and certainly the best I have taken in the English department.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1614028\n 1614028\n \n 1614028\n The course's strengths were that the material was fascinating, the readings were new to most of us in class a great mix of better and lesser known Communist texts in particular and all great, and that the resulting discussions were great too. Professor North's way of understanding complex issues and making sense of history was very interesting and inspiring to me I feel he thinks unlike many professors at Yale and perhaps in a way that more closely resembles my own mode of very associative thinking! Professor North is clearly so knowledgeable about the topics he taught and deeply committed to presenting liberalism and communism in a fair light. \r\n\r\nThe weaknesses of the course were really only that sometimes the idea of 'literary criticism' seemed a bit buried within the other material in the course and sometimes the connection between the intellectuals we treated in class and criticism seemed obscured. Professor North perhaps could have illuminated these connections a bit more for us.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1614230\n 1614230\n \n 1614230\n I wish more of this course had actually spent time talking about literary criticism. It felt like this class was more about political theory.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1616286\n 1616286\n \n 1616286\n The organization of this course was extremely poor. The semester was broken into three sectors, but it felt like we covered Marxism and a little bit of Liberalism at the end. Our midterm was to write a "pilot" paper in preparation for our final. However, at that point, all we had covered in the class was Marxist criticism, basically forcing our hands on what we knew enough to write about for the midterm paper. There were no hard prompts for the papers which is normal, for a high-level English course but there was also no guidance on what to do, what to pursue, and how to go about it. For this course to be a senior seminar and endcap to a person's Yale career I can't imagine anything at all was learned in this course, except for the prolific reading we had to do. The readings were in preparation for the course discussions in which I never had any idea what was going on because everything was philosophical.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1620316\n 1620316\n \n 1620316\n The main strength and weakness of the course is its formlessnessthe drift from thinker to thinker prompted a lot of interesting discussion, but without more structure it was sometimes easy to get lost in the weeds.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1620343\n 1620343\n \n 1620343\n The final paper for this course was the longest one most of us had ever written and there was almost no support for the writing process. Joe was available in office hours but with no prompt and all of the intermediary steps between the idea and the finished product being optional, there wasn't a lot to go on. This paper was not just a longer version of something we'd all done before, but I at least had to learn how to write this kind of paper. I think there was a lost opportunity for instruction on how higher level English papers are written and should be thought about.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1620586\n 1620586\n \n 1620586\n I appreciated getting to say any preliminary thoughts I had about the text for that week. I thought it did a really great job of getting us to start thinking about things that might have slipped past one person but stuck out to another.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1621330\n 1621330\n \n 1621330\n I would have liked to see the conservative literary theorists presented in addition to the liberals and the Marxists. It felt like an arbitrary choice to leave them out. I would have also enjoyed more conversations about the state of contemporary criticism.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628369\n 1628369\n \n 1628369\n Prof. North is definitely a strength of the coursegreat listener and lecturer. Interesting reading listId love to hear more about the interconnections between these readings. Id also like to read some pieces of literature to which the theoretical readings can be applied
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1631456\n 1631456\n \n 1631456\n Perhaps both the greatest strength and the greatest weakness of this course is the breadth of materials covered. While covering such a wide range of authors is absolutely necessary in order to gain a foundational understanding of marxist versus liberal perspectives (especially since most students lack exposure to marxist literature prior to this course), doing so limits the time that can be spent toward synthesizing and comparing these two perspectives once the foundational knowledge is gained. One way to address this might be to shorten the works read by each author and to add small, weekly discussion essays, a solution that may also help to alleviate some of the pressure underlying this courses lack of assignments. This solution doesnt necessarily subtract or add time to the students workload, but I do think it could redistribute time in a way that leads to an even greater grasp of the perspectives discussed. For example, rather than reading multiple chapters of I.A. Richards, perhaps the syllabus could be limited to one chapter, yet students could write a one-page discussion essay (half-page summary paragraph with a half-page reflection/criticism paragraph) prior to class, an assignment which would serve to further solidify the students understanding of Richards in comparison to the many other authors discussed and encourage the formation of questions while reading.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1632547\n 1632547\n \n 1632547\n Strengths great spread of reading, great two-part structure, great essay assignments, great discussion leading by Prof North\r\nWeaknesses potentially too much reading/lacking direction in what to read for (maybe add blogposts and reading guides?)
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639105\n 1639105\n \n 1639105\n This course is meant to be an overview of liberal and Marxist literary and arts criticism. It was a bit more haphazard than that, because not all students had the same knowledge of 20th C intellectual history. This made it difficult to have productive discussions in class. The syllabus was also a bit disjointedit seems difficult to provide a coherent overview of all of 20th C literary criticism in an undergraduate course.
