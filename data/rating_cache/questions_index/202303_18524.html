\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1705337\n 1705337\n \n 1705337\n It's mostly strengths! I liked the organization of the class, with weekly discussions led by students, and the readings were really interesting. I think the readings are unfortunately difficult to get around to for many students because of how long they often are, but i don't think the reading should be cut back, it just comes with the territory.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1706049\n 1706049\n \n 1706049\n The strength of the course is that everyone is allowed to become an expert, meaning a lot of learning from others. The other aspect I thoroughly enjoyed was the presentations given, being able to do a lot of different types of activities! Also, the final project being self-exploratory was really nice! For improvement, the only aspect is having more guest speakers, since those were some of the most memorable parts of the class!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1706909\n 1706909\n \n 1706909\n A substantial strength to the course were the weekly "co-facilitations," which were weekly presentations by students. Each week, different students presented on the readings and facilitated an activity of their choosing. This implementation was especially helpful for my learning and diversified the way we engaged with the class. \r\n\r\nA weakness was that sometimes, the classes were on zoom. Although I understanding, given the commitments of Professor Hidalgo, I wish that classes would be held in person whenever possible.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1718730\n 1718730\n \n 1718730\n Strengths: Flexibility in the topic/deliverable product for the final project, the breadth of information covered (e.g readings on different kinds of informality: from street vendors to housing justice advocates to sex workers), the opportunity to facilitate classroom discussions, and the support from professor Hidalgo in crafting a final project. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: In person-classes were not structured well/didn't require much valuable participation (sometimes it felt like students would bring up an interesting point but it wasn't explicitly connected to the course material or they didn't explain the connection well and professor Hidalgo would not intervene thus the conversation fell flat), lack of breaks during in-person class (a two hour class needs a break or else students won't focus well for the latter half), and lack of feedback on discussion posts (only in the first few classes did professor Hidalgo pick out a few posts and talk about them). \r\n\r\nImprovements: Implementing a break in the class, crafting a more structured agenda for each class AND sharing it with the class, verbally or visually keeping track of time (this is a way that structure can be communicated to students and in my opinion consistency makes students look forward to class because they know what to expect), *providing more feedback on discussion posts (commenting under one post each week and providing feedback/grades on discussion posts halfway through the semester). \r\n\r\n*In addition, the discussion post format could be altered. We suggested this while giving in-person feedback but to reiterate: you could split the students into two groups, Group A and Group B. Both groups are expected to do the readings but only one group does a reading response (meaning that each student in that group posts a reading response) and the other group responds to the first group's post (meaning that each student in that group posts a response to another post). Each week the groups would alternate. This way the discussion posts would facilitate a conversation between students.
