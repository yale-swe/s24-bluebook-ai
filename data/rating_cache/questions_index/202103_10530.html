\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1556225\n 1556225\n \n 1556225\n I learned how to close read, how to connect my analysis of a text to secondary sources and to create a full argument from that analysis, and then how to turn that argument into a paper. I experienced quite a bit of important literature/authors that I had never read before; I also learned an incredible amount of music & literature related information not just from Professor Skorupa, but also from the other students in the course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1556357\n 1556357\n \n 1556357\n The class provides an extensive introduction to the world of music and its interplay with literature across different genres and forms. Overall, this class was immensely satisfying for me, as I went in wanting to explore both music and literature in detail, and Professor Skorupa's discussions accomplished that very well.

Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1525388\n 1525388\n \n 1525388\n The strengths of this course were the professor, the exposure to literature and space to talk about it in a comparative way, and the small class size with lots of field trips. If I were to mention one weakness about this class, it would probably be the mandatory movie screenings. I LOVED the movie screenings as a concept but many of the films we watched were violent and included depictions of sexual violence, or other potentially upsetting themes, and we weren't given any sort of content warning. My class bonded a lot during these movie screenings, and I loved them as a feature of the class, but if you are worried that you might be made uncomfortable/upset/unsettled, or otherwise disturbed by content relating to those things, you may want to look up information about the movies ahead of time to make sure that you are prepared.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527923\n 1527923\n \n 1527923\n Strengths: very fun course, lots of interesting stories and texts, fun little writing assignments to keep us engaged, great class camaraderie\r\nWeaknesses: The curriculum could use an update. Most of the texts we read about the connection between literature and music were about classical music and French/German (and some other European) literature, and the few texts that we read written by people of color were not the core of the class ("Sonny's Blues" was in the "intro week"; we didn't discuss "The Second Bakery Attack" in class at all because we spent all of the class talking about the other readings for the week, Thomas Mann's "Tristan" as well as a very long letter Baudelaire wrote to Richard Wagner; Toni Morrison's Jazz was during the final week of classes, which was fun but meant we didn't have the option of writing any of our essays about it, so it felt like a token/bonus text). I wish we read a more diverse range of texts (same with the weekly movies)
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1531958\n 1531958\n \n 1531958\n Strengths:\r\n- The weekly reader's responses are engaging and progressively help you improve your close reading skills.\r\n- The professor is flexible when it comes to office hours and is always willing to help out with the essays or assignments\r\n- I found the material we read very interesting, making it easier to go through it even on hectic weeks.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses:\r\n- The workload can vary week to week, especially when an essay is due. There were weeks where we had to revise a paper, read and annotate a book, and write one or two weekly responses, while there were weeks where we only had to write one reader's response. It made it a bit hard to plan too much in advance how much time to dedicate to the class.\r\n- The time it sometimes took the professor to grade the papers made it hard to know what we had to improve before handing in the next essay.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1551982\n 1551982\n \n 1551982\n The course was taught and organized really well and Professor Skorupa is great. Sometimes I felt overwhelmed by the workload, though, and the amount of reading I had to do didn't seem that necessary.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1553862\n 1553862\n \n 1553862\n This course does have interesting units and the class usually fosters good discussions. This class also naturally had a tight bond, which made some of the difficulty in the work and reading worth doing. I would say, however, that I didn't look clearly in the syllabus, and I slowly came to realize that the movies, readings, musicians, and most of the work we read in class were heavily European. I do appreciate learning about this part of the musical canon, but I would have appreciated more than only about two authors of color in the curriculum, both at the beginning and end. However, there were many delightful strengths that gave the class life, such as a big trip to New York and the trips to museums and Yale resources.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1556357\n 1556357\n \n 1556357\n The strength of the class were certainly the discussions with my classmates shepherded by Professor Skorupa. I had an amazing, amazing set of classmates in the seminar, equipped with differing varieties of backgrounds and experience with music to fuel debate and discussion about the texts we discussed. The range of texts we read was quite nice, covering a wide swathe of well-known authors. I would, however, add that a large number of the authors we read were French, German, or American, but that "bias" was actually a result of Professor Skorupa being a polyglot who was able to analyze those texts in much greater detail. We watched a movie every few weeks on a specified movie night, and these were some of my favorite parts of the class--not solely because of the films, but also because we got to interact with each other outside of class. Likewise, the class involved engaging with multiple different forms of art (music, sculptures/paintings at the YUAG and the Met, opera at the Met Opera, and of course, literature)\r\n\r\nTwo semi-weaknesses of the class might be the heavy workload and Professor Skorupa's strict grading rubric: there were occasionally upwards of 20-30 pages of reading due between classes (within the same week) so it did get a tiny bit hectic if you tried to analyze every text/poem (whilst also attending the weekly movie nights.)
