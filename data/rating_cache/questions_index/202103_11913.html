Homework expectations were clear and there were ample review sessions. I think the overall mix of computation and theory was about right -- not just rote work, but not strict proofs either -- even if the teaching was not ideal in other respects. Weaknesses: It might just be inevitable, but engagement with the class was very poor. Often, not a single student would answer the professor's questions. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548819\n 1548819\n \n 1548819\n Professor Schacht spent the time to derive many of the rules and concepts we used which gave us a great background for each topic, but also made pacing feel slow at times and caused us to not cover some of the topics we planned to review at the end of the semester. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552991\n 1552991\n \n 1552991\n This course was an abysmal introduction to linear algebra and should be redesigned entirely. Lectures were unclear and unhelpful, and little to no syllabus organized the topics covered. The textbook was closely followed, though it would have been great if another book was chosen (even Gilbert Strangs other textbook- I read all of both and prefer that one as a reference). This book has great intuitive explanations of the way many concepts in linear algebra overlap and interact, but has little structure and reads as a brick wall of prose without any theorems or key points highlighted. Unfortunately the intuitive (albeit dense) explanations of the textbook were not presented in lecture. To make matters worse, assignments and exams focused on computation, rather than understanding of the material. Its here where I start to wonder the reason for the existence of this course. We live in the 21st century. Computers were *literally* designed to deal with busywork matrix operations. If one isnt to take MATH 225 to explore the formal foundations of the field of linear algebra, the alternative should be a course that teaches the intuitions with real world applications. Yes, techniques such as gaussian elimination, factorization techniques, and inverse calculation should all be learned by hand initially. More important, however would be to actually explain when these techniques are necessary, and teach how one would use them in life. The concepts of linear algebra overlap and interact so beautifully, and it was a shame that this beauty was lost in a sea of pedantic busywork on every pset. Make me calculate a 4x4 inverse once on a pset and once on an exam, sure. Then teach me how to do it in matlab, and make me use matlab to solve an actual conceptually nontrivial problem. Meanwhile, for a course allegedly focused on applications there were no applications to be seen. I feel like the course not only failed to teach how linear algebra works, but also why anyone should care to learn it. I feel like I thought myself from the book and would have been better off learning entirely on my own.

You learn all the mechanics of solving problems, while also getting intuitive explanations without needing to rigorous proofs. I cant say there are any glaring weaknesses.
