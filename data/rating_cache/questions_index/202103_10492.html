\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527502\n 1527502\n \n 1527502\n The course starts with a historical overview of the impacts of imperialism and colonization on education, focusing on Native American experiences, the experiences of enslaved people, and other marginalized groups. This creates a base for more theoretical critiques of education and the ways it has affected our cultural, social, and political reality. The class focuses on the ability to pick out ways that our current system is plagued by an imperialist past.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1533816\n 1533816\n \n 1533816\n Content the history, mechanisms and motives of U.S. education as an arm of imperialism and settler colonialism (read: Black vocational schools, Native boarding schools, imperial schools in the Philippines)\r\nSkills how to theorize contingently and how to build your emotional and intellectual capacity for the contradictions of life as we know it as\r\nInsights this course is a collective and affirming experience that forces you to reconcile with the meaning of your education as a Yale student, this course will not lie to you.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1537850\n 1537850\n \n 1537850\n I learned how frightening and terrible the world can be, and how compassion and imagination can combat that terror. I learned a lot of cold hard history and social theory, and I worked on considering myself within the structures around me. I learned about the American education system, and as a class, we tried to imagine alternatives.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1542562\n 1542562\n \n 1542562\n Education and Empire really transformed how I think about education, pedagogy, and curriculum. tzb is amazing and takes you through deconstructing many different aspects of learning fundamental epistemologies (and epistemicide), the American history of education (including the US's legacy of colonial ed and Indigenous boarding schools), discipline and surveillance, alternate imaginations of education, and what we (as students) are to do about this. It is a really empowering experience that pushes you to think hyper-critically about future careers (particularly in education), as well as your own past experiences with education. Some classes are solemn and saddening, others are energizing. This course reveals things to you that everyone interested in education (and honestly every person at Yale) should take.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544301\n 1544301\n \n 1544301\n This class really helped me think critically about U.S. education and its role in imperialism and colonialism. TZB talked about wanting to give us tools, and I feel like this class really did that, both to analyze the existing relationship between education and empire but also to look towards something more for education. This class challenged me to question a lot of things that are treated as standard or objective aspects of education; it also challenged me to want and think about something better.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546468\n 1546468\n \n 1546468\n This class changed the way I think about education and about social reform and taught me about stamina and education in movement spaces. The class first gave background historical knowledge about education in the U.S. then readings provided insight into the unspoken and hidden ways education was used as a tool to oppress or ensure that the status quo remained the same, valuing order over change. From large scale educational and imperial movements and history the class also zoomed in to dynamics of control and surveillance within classrooms. Discussions often focused on what reforms are possible or if reform is possible at all. Alongside insights into the uses of education I gained confidence in grappling with large, difficult, and pressing questions about educational reform.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527502\n 1527502\n \n 1527502\n The course allows students to think critically about a history that is often ignored and a current education reality that is often glamorized. Students are able to unpack their own educational experiences and better understand the myths of education we often fail to recognize and buy into. I think the class was also a safe and supportive learning environment for many. That being said, the class did have a number of weaknesses. I felt that professor tzb didn't always teach in a way that aligned with the very pedagogy we were learning about. Knowledge felt like it was held by the professor, rather than created by the class. Even though the class was a seminar, it sometimes felt like a leminar in a way that didn't really allow me to fully grapple with the readings and ideas. I wanted a more collaborative environment that emphasized class discussion over understanding of specific theoretical concepts. Lastly, and this might just be my misunderstanding of the class, but I was frustrated by how theoretical many of the readings and class discussions were. I wanted tangible ways to apply the more theoretical concepts about education.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1533816\n 1533816\n \n 1533816\n professor tzb cultivates a learning environment in which you WANT to participate, not for points, but to challenge the way you think about yourself. this classroom has, without fail, created a community amongst its students over the years. there are no words that will properly do justice to this experience. know that the syllabus is intellectually rigorous, the professor is intellectually generous, and that you will be changed as a person.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534950\n 1534950\n \n 1534950\n i think the readings could be cut down a bit and i think that sometimes there was a lot of groupthink happening, but the class is truly full of strengths and i really enjoyed my time.

TZB is awesome and encouraging and sincere and brilliant. \r\nWeaknesses: The nature of the intellectual work in the class is such that it sometimes makes it hard to see concrete solutions to the problems that the class poses. Not really a weakness, but something to be ready for.

In terms of improvement, I would just support tzb's own efforts to change the structure of the course to actually put some of its ideas into practice. It sounds like next year will be a real reimagining of what this course will look like.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544196\n 1544196\n \n 1544196\n Strengths: Discussion formats and overall flexibility!\r\nWeaknesses: Occasionally I found myself a bit confused by what the reading assignment was but this is so miniscule in the grand scheme of things!

She has really carefully curated a syllabus that is challenging but also empowering. I honestly cant think of any weaknesses right now.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546468\n 1546468\n \n 1546468\n The strengths of the class include Professor TZB and the selection of readings on the syllabus. In seminar people felt comfortable sharing their own experiences, mistakes, and reflections on changes in thought from the readings. The readings on the syllabus are all relevant and there is a balanced mix of dense/theoretical texts alongside personal narratives and accounts which made the heavy reading load manageable; I think the reading load could be considered a weakness of the class as it can be quite heavy at times. Another strength of the course was the flexibility for assignments and the opportunity to make friends through group work and group discussions.
