Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1526590\n 1526590\n \n 1526590\n This course is well structured with a great balance of discussion time and student involvement.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1528370\n 1528370\n \n 1528370\n 1. The strengths of the course mostly related to Professor Eccles. Everything from her readings on the syllabus, to her informal assignments and essay prep, to her incorporation of midterm feedback (the only professor I have ever seen actually incorporate midterm feedback, in real-time), to her incredible feedback and support in office hours and in grading, and her facilitation of discussions.\r\n\r\n2. My first suggestion is that Professor Eccles should teach more. Follow or even replace the debrief with your own context, expertise, etc. My only real complaint about this course was that I felt there was too much discussion. Sometimes I was itching to hear Professor Eccles own take or contribution to a topic, only to have to listen to another student talk for 2-3 minutes and not provide much substantial material to the discussion. I understand and appreciate the need to engage more students in discussion, but let students hear you, professor!\r\n\r\n3. My second suggestion is that the grading scale be adjusted. The idea of grades being distributed evenly across 10 percentage points doesn't quite sit right with me. If 10 future Nobel Laureates in literature take this class and perform accordingly, all of them should be able to earn an A. A curved grading scale doesn't allow for this and may leave students feeling like their hard work and results are not accurately reflected. I personally felt my grading was accurate enough, but I can easily see how others can suffer (or unfairly gain) from it.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1529620\n 1529620\n \n 1529620\n Well organized, receptive and thoughtful Professor, overall an awesome course! Definitely worth taking 126 with prof Eccles. It was incidental, but occasionally the poet pairings were hard to connect to each other.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539208\n 1539208\n \n 1539208\n I thought the syllabus was very well crafted, and the informal assignments we had every couple of weeks were incredibly useful for writing papers. I felt like I came out of this course with a new appreciation for the amount of time it can sometimes take to understand a poem, and I look forward to exploring the poets we read more in the future.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546521\n 1546521\n \n 1546521\n Professor Eccles was great! The course material was incredibly interesting and engaging. It was always a wonderful intellectual challenge to engage with the poems.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1550662\n 1550662\n \n 1550662\n Strengths: Professor Eccles is truly wonderful. She's a strong discussion leader and is very receptive to student feedback on what is and isn't working. We had informal assignments which were so helpful for noticing new things about the poems, coming up with paper ideas, etc.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1556158\n 1556158\n \n 1556158\n I think the course has a bit of odd pacing, especially compared with other sections of 126. We spent a lot of time on Wordsworth and Pope, two authors that other sections moved through quite quickly. I also would have appreciated more time spent on fundamentals of analysis before jumping into specific authors; it felt like, on some occasions, everyone in the class was working with a different vocabulary. While that is somewhat inevitable, it can be helped to a degree. I did enjoy the discussions I had in the course, though. They were quite productive, as were the smaller assignments leading up to larger papers.
