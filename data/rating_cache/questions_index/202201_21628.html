\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1583982\n 1583982\n \n 1583982\n Through taking this course I learned about the vectors and vector spaces, their properties, how to prove that a set of objects is a vector subspace, and how to use vectors to solve geometric and algebraic problems in mathematics. I learned about linear spans and linear dependence, how to determine if a set of vectors are linearly independent, and the consequence of linear dependence or independence on the properties of a vector subset. I learned about functions and linear transformations, what it means for a function to be injective, surjective, and bijective, and how that influences the behavior of these functions in proof and problems. I learned about the matrix representation of a linear transformation, the properties of a matrix, how to perform matrix multiplication and how it differs from scalar multiplication , the conditions under which a matrix is invertible and diagonalizable, how to invert an diagonalize a matrix, and how to use different matrix operations to reveal properties of matrices in proof and problems. I also learned about eigenvectors and eigenvalues, how they are determined from a matrix, and how they can be used to diagonalize a matrix, solve systems of linear equations with matrices, and how they can prove properties of a matrix when used in proofs. One of my favorite aspects of the course was how the material is applicable in a wide range of disciplines. While we learned about the structure of matrices and eigenstates, I was able to use these objects to solve relativistic and quantum mechanical problems in my physics class. The interconnectivity of linear algebra is fascinating, and the way in which we built each topic from the ground up using concrete derivations and solid proofs definitely helped me better understand the material and be able to use it in other contexts. Our use of proof writing in derivations and problem sets will help prepare me to take other proof-based mathematics classes down the road.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575715\n 1575715\n \n 1575715\n Strengths: Ebru was very understanding about the fact that proof-writing and theoretical linear algebra are pretty difficult to grasp. She dropped the weighting of the first midterm from 25% to 10% (unless keeping it at 25% benefited your grade, obviously) after a good number of people bombed the midterm, and the succeeding exams were a lot more reasonable. Additionally, Ebru was not really focused on if you were using proper proof language and structure and was more focused on if you were making valid arguments with your proofs, which is how an introductory proof-writing course should be. Ebru is also very kind and pretty funny if you get to know her. Ebrus office hours were great, and while I have a few things to say about her lectures, she was pretty helpful in office hours, so if youre struggling in the class, go to her office hours with some practice proofs and just run through them with her. Also, Linear Algebra is a really cool subject, and while I love Calculus, learning Linear Algebra is kind of the point where everything in math seems to tie together and make sense in my opinion. Also, the benefit of taking a theoretical class like Math 225 is that there isnt as much emphasis on computation, so any computational problems are really just to see if you know how to do the math, so you dont have to worry much about getting computational problems that are easy to trip up on because of how annoyingly long they are.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses:\r\nOne thing that really annoyed me was that it took the TAs around a month to grade each problem set (this is not an exaggeration), so you were barely getting feedback on your proofs in time for exams. We even had a problem set due 3 days before our final, which only added onto the stress of studying and was pointless because it was obviously not graded in time. Also, there were no practice exams for the 2nd midterm and final. We only got practice problems to do in the book, but you couldnt check your answers because they were never sent out, so Im not really sure how those would help. There should be a lot more resources for writing proofs for practice given that this is an introductory proof writing course. Also, Ebru was not the best instructor, but before I say more on that, this was her first semester teaching the course, and she was open to suggestions on how to make the experience better, so if she continues teaching the course, I definitely think she can make it better. One of my criticisms on her teaching would be that lecture often moved way too fast for students that have never been exposed to Linear Algebra and the way she taught the concepts didnt really align with our textbook, so you could only look at her lecture notes (which were extremely incoherent) to review the material. I think that either she needs to make her lesson plan align more with the textbook or make her lecture notes a lot more thorough and detailed. I dont blame the pacing of the course entirely on her, though, because honestly, this course tries to accomplish way too much in one semester given that its supposed to be an introductory Linear Algebra class. Teaching all of the computational and theoretical aspects of Linear Algebra from elementary Set Theory to Spectral Theory while also teaching proof-writing is not really achievable in one semester if youve never seen Linear Algebra before unless youre Srinivasa Ramanujan. We actually didnt even get to Spectral Theory, and Ebru said in an office hours that this was because she was trying to take the beginning of the semester slowly so that we could grasp the fundamentals better but that she eventually had to speed up if we were going to cover all of the topics specified in the syllabus. I thought her idea of taking it slowly was great because the theoretical component of this course takes some time to digest. Math 222 or some form of computational Linear Algebra should be a prerequisite for a more thorough coverage of the theory in this course.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1575715\n 1575715\n \n 1575715\n If I had to describe my experience with this course in one sentence, I would have to quote Tyrion Lannister: I found it surprisingly beautiful...in a brutal, horribly uncomfortable sort of way. Honestly, Linear Algebra is a really cool subject, but Id be lying if I said this class isnt hard. A few reasons to take Math 225 would be: you need it because its a requirement for your major, youre deciding between Math 225 and Math 222, or youre a computer science major (linear algebra is not required for CS, but it helps A LOT, and if you didnt know, Linear Algebra is really important in implementing complex concepts of various mathematical fields into computers). I say that you should take Math 225 if youre deciding between Math 222 and Math 225 because Linear is probably the only math subject where knowing why the math works is actually pretty important in being able to apply the math. I would only recommend Math 222 if they change the structure in a way that they actually cover the theory behind the techniques in Linear Algebra without including the proof aspect. However, I will also note that Math 222 is easier because it lacks the proof aspect of 225. Overall, though, both classes are not great in terms of teaching quality, so be prepared to teach yourself most of the content. Proof writing is what makes this course difficult because most people have never written a proof before taking this course, and you essentially get a two-week introduction to writing proofs focused on simple number theory at the beginning of the semester and are all of a sudden expected to know how to produce proofs on Linear Algebra concepts like youre in Good Will Hunting. The only people I know who found this course on the easier side had taken Linear Algebra before. In other words, taking this course without any exposure to Linear beforehand is difficult (as a warning, there are a good number of people that fit this profile in the course, so you will have to work harder than them). Unfortunately, you cannot take both Math 222 and Math 225, so you dont have the choice to get some exposure to the computational aspects of Linear Algebra before taking this theory-focused course. This course also lacks the resources of the introductory math courses at Yale, so dont expect to find as much help as you could in a course like Math 120. Also, your experience in this course is going to be a lot different than any other math class youve taken if youve never taken a proof-based course because there is no algorithmic approach to solving proof problems. Any question in this class regarding writing a proof is problem-solving in its purest form, and you cant get by with plugging and chugging and simply accepting that the math works like you could in previous math classes (a little tip: DO NOT try to just memorize theorems and think that is enough. You should understand why the proofs of those theorems work and maybe even try to prove them on your own in creative ways because when writing proofs, you really need to have an understanding of everything in order to draw connections and solve problems). I still think this course is better than Math 222 from what Ive heard, though, so definitely take it if you need to take introductory Linear, but if you don't need to take Linear and don't have that much interest in the subject, "fun" is not how one should think about this course. Also, make sure you have enough time to commit to the class because, seriously, you will need to teach yourself a lot of the course material. The computational aspects are pretty easy to learn through YouTube, but to get better at writing proofs, you really just have to practice a lot.
