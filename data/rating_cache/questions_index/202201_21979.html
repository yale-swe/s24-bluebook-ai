In a pandemic it's important to have a remote option, and I'm not suggesting that this be taken away - I only mean to point out that by design, a literature course which is 75% recorded lectures is going to be disengaging. We only wrote one essay, and it was very late, so there wasn't any chance to grow within that form. In a 182 level English course students are already going to be familiar with longform essays, so I don't see the reason to "build up" to them with the critical reflection papers. This isn't the only way, but I really like the format of 3 long essays over the course of the semester. Because the form is the same, students are able to take criticism and apply it to the next assignment. Re the critical reflection papers, I was a little confused what to do at first. We didn't receive instructions / a rubric for what these were supposed to be until after the first one was due, so I went of my TAs suggestion to not worry about it, and I wrote a relaxed, discussion-post style response and got a 2/3. Once I learned we were supposed to make an organized argument / a short paper, I was fine for the next two, but I wasn't told anything about what I was supposed to do until after the first assignment. Additionally the syllabus repeatedly changedon the original syllabus we were supposed to have 4-6 critical reflections, 2 critical essays, but the deadlines /requirements were constantly changing. I think the course would really benefit from having a simpler structure decided on at the beginning of the semester. Re the final: I personally don't like quote IDs - it was fine cause it was open book and we could read for context and write that important context into our responses, but the implication was that normally these are in person, without the book - I don't think quote IDs are representative of text comprehension or writing ability. I did really like the final, though, given that it was open bookit allowed me to reflect on the texts on a large scale in a way an individual final paper wouldn't.\r\n\r\nI really respect all of the instructors / TAs as people and every student I know loved the material; I think that with a little more focus on syllabus / course design this course would absolutely shine, especially as a seminar.
