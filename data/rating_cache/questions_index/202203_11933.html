RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1612193\n 1612193\n \n 1612193\n The reading material and class discussions are interesting, but at the end of the day it is run as your average History seminar, with student discussions taking up most of the class, framed by remarks from the professor. Compared to other classes I have taken at Yale and in the History, AFST, and Poli-Sci departments in particular, this format is just less engaging and less exciting. The classes are also hugely shaped by the students taking the class, so I assume it is vastly different from year to year.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1615936\n 1615936\n \n 1615936\n The content was extremely interesting and was a very unique approach to understanding the conflict of the African states during this time period, as well as the political involvement of many prominent figures of the anti-apartheid movement. The guest speakers added a very interesting dimension to the class discussions and Professor Steinberg's intimate knowledge on the subject was extremely enlightening.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1631960\n 1631960\n \n 1631960\n I think that while this course was extremely informative and covered an extensive amount of South African history, the transitions and connections between the Mandelas, Fanon, and Gandhi were a bit abrupt. I am not well-versed in political theory or philosophy so it would have helped to assign some easier background readings to supplement or have a brief discussion in class. I think I connected my thoughts the best when Prof. Steinberg got up and drew diagrams or shared his own personal thoughts to help us visualize what we were learning--examples include the spheres of Winnie Mandela's existence, Nelson Mandela's political ideology, and understanding the racial segregation of South Africa.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1637931\n 1637931\n \n 1637931\n Strengths: content is so engaging, its great to spend such a prolonged amount of time with two political figures. This is not an opportunity you often get in PLSC classes and it makes for some in-depth learning.\r\n\r\nI only wish I had gone into the class with a stronger grasp of South African history and the events of the anti-apartheid movement. You certainly have the opportunity to learn this as the class goes on, but I feel that coming in with that background would have made the learning even easier.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1640091\n 1640091\n \n 1640091\n The strengths of this course were its discussion format and the texts that we studied. I loved reading the material for this class every week, and our discussions greatly enhanced my understanding not only of the texts that we read, but also of bigger questions of the historical time period, political theory, and the politics of representation. It was incredible to read and hear from Professor Steinberg's deep knowledge about this history, and it was equally fascinating to talk with the guest speakers he brought to class. While focusing on two figures alone was new for me as a history student, I do not think that this class had any weaknesses!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1640570\n 1640570\n \n 1640570\n I liked the readings and structure of the course. I wish our conversations could have had more structure - I would have liked a set of questions to discuss. As it was, our conversations could be free form and devolve into a lot of nothings. But Steinberg was kind and interesting at every turn.
