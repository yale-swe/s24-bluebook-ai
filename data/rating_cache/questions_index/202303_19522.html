\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1705796\n 1705796\n \n 1705796\n In general, I appreciated Professor Lu's teaching style, as she always made lectures interesting and found ways to make the content more accessible and relatable to us. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1716388\n 1716388\n \n 1716388\n Strengths: Prof T is an incredible person, very kind, approachable, also can be humorous, sarcastic, and funny! Lectures are a combination of lecturing and also a sort of discussion, though it may not feel like it just because we are in a bigger lecture hall. Relatively low workload depending on your engagement with the class, and the guess lectures and class "field trips" to the Beineke and YUAG were already great.\r\nWeaknesses: Not much in my opinion, but I can imagine some students not liking the discussion participation part of the class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1719708\n 1719708\n \n 1719708\n One strength of this course was how focused our readings were. While many survey courses pile on the reading and consequently see students skip much of it, that was not a problem in this course. Our readings were consistently short, curated, and on-topic. It was clear that Prof T had thought each and every one out with care. This made it easier to engage deeply with the material for any given class meeting despite whatever other pressing obligations one had that week. \r\n\r\nThe greatest weakness of the course was how easy the assessments were. Our only graded assignments were three short papers of 3-4 pages, each of which focused on close textual analysis, and so there was little incentive to maintain broad knowledge of the course materials. The path of least resistance was to develop enough deep knowledge on one or two authors to produce a very short paper on an as-needed basis rather than maintain and build the deeper, comparative understanding of how the Chinese tradition evolved over time. It would have made more sense either to incorporate comprehensive exams or longer papers that engaged a broader scope of knowledge covered. While it may seem cynical to assume that students are unlikely to bother cultivating this sort of deep knowledge without extrinsic motivation, Yale students are very busy people, and we simply tend to jettison tasks we dont absolutely need to complete. So, I really do think we could have benefitted from tougher assessments. We would have risen to the challenge, and been better off for it!
