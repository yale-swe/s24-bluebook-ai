\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532488\n 1532488\n \n 1532488\n The course is good at introducing students to math in conjunction with the life sciences, though it was very dissimilar to 115 and traditional Calc 2 courses.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1533810\n 1533810\n \n 1533810\n Since we had to read scientific papers some class time was devoted to discussing those and that took away from class time learning about non-reading related material. \r\nIt felt like we did not spend a lot of time on any given topic, some more than others, there was a lot of time spent on phase planes. This was fine but it just took some getting used to and a lot of practice. Another major weakness was how everyone felt very unprepared to do the problem sets and it was almost like office hours were a required part of the class. \r\nIt would have been way too difficult to complete any problems set Without attending at least one full office hour session.However even though the class did not correlate with the homework, the homework problems definitely translated to what would be on the test. So did the practice tests. So it kind of balanced out and I was able to be well prepared for the test.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536097\n 1536097\n \n 1536097\n This course is strong in the applications of the math; it made things more manageable and useful. The weakness comes primarily from the instructors' blindspots. At this point in their math careers, they probably see things a lot easier than students. As a student, however, a lot of the insights the professors insisted were intuitive did not seem intuitive at all. In fact, in a lot of situations, if you gave me what was given on an exam, I would not be able to draw the conclusions our professors drew in just a few minutes. As an FGLI student coming from a low-income, public high school, I did not feel like there was an adequate enough effort to make the math less foreign, especially in a room filled with peers who attended the top private schools in the nation. Another weakness came from the professors' lack of knowledge in the science aspect of the class. They were mathematicians before they were scientists, I'm assuming, so it makes sense. I feel like we missed several opportunities to have engaging discussions on the science or social science aspects of what we discussed. For example, the ozone reading we did resembled a free radical reaction discussed in my chemistry class. Predation, especially, could be a cool science discussion.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536435\n 1536435\n \n 1536435\n The content is so far removed from anything I will have to do outside of the classroom, and even from other math classes, that my friends in the math levels above me couldn't even comprehend what I was doing. In addition, the problem sets are so difficult that going to office hours is basically a requirement for getting a decent grade on them, which took up an extra 8-10 hours of my week. I did not enjoy this course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1537425\n 1537425\n \n 1537425\n Very streamlined and all assignments and tests and reading connect well

I dont think we spent enough time on the readings in class because the professors were hell-bent on cramming a ton of content into each class period. I would have also liked to see more time devoted to understanding the material being taught in class by giving us time to work on example problems in class. Sometimes, this is was allowed but simple things such as how desks are arranged can affect the facilitation of group work.
