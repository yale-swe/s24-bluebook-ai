RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534811\n 1534811\n \n 1534811\n The history, theory and analysis of opera was extremely interesting, although oftentimes the connection to philosophy was less clear.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534824\n 1534824\n \n 1534824\n Strengths: the operas chosen span a wide range of genres, plot structures, and philosophical issues\r\nWeaknesses: the readings tend to be almost entirely from the same people, all from the past 50 years, with the exception of a smattering of shorter contemporary sources (mainly in the first 2 operas); the balance between "opera" and "philosophy" varied greatly from class to class, making it difficult to prepare and thus difficult to engage; too much time was given to the first 2 operas, leaving little time for the latter 2--which is a same, as it seemed to be a universal point of agreement in the class that Carmen was our favorite opera; the "philosophy" of the latter 2 operas was much less engaging, relying mostly on sources that did not engage in the source material directly
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534827\n 1534827\n \n 1534827\n The chief strength is Professor Tomlinson's mastery of the subject matter.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534839\n 1534839\n \n 1534839\n No weakness.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534871\n 1534871\n \n 1534871\n I liked the pacing and structure of the assignments/syllabus. My only suggestion would be to allow a little more time for the essays (10 days instead of 7, maybe?), because arranging meetings with Writing Tutors for them was a little hectic and sometimes not possible within the timespan. The prompts stumped me at times, but ultimately you have a bit of freedom within your responses.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535050\n 1535050\n \n 1535050\n This course excels at its interdisciplinary approach, and is best as a way of using philosophical questions to introduce students to music in a unique way, and using musical works to introduce students to a number of distinct philosophical approaches in a way that is engaging and concrete.\r\n\r\nIn terms of improvement, this was the first semester this course was taught, and so if it is taught again (and I hope it is), I believe that it would benefit from more transparency in its organization and structure, so that at any given moment in the class, there is a clearer idea of not only what operas but which philosophical approaches and ideas lay ahead. A similar thing could be said about the placement of assignments throughout the semester.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535340\n 1535340\n \n 1535340\n This course was very interesting, and the professor kept it engaging the whole time. I had a really fun time in this class, but I noticed that despite being a music class, there were no music majors. This is because this class doesn't count for the major. If somehow that could be changed, I think I know a lot of people who would really love to take this class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536311\n 1536311\n \n 1536311\n strengths - Professor Tomlinson is a gem of knowledge about both the opera and philosophy. A good lecturer, and very kind. Great & diverse (musically speaking) selection of operas.\r\nweaknesses - I think the different relationships of the philosophical texts to the operas made the organization / larger themes of the course harder to pick out (ie: some philosophers were writing ON opera, some were generally philosophy the professor could connect to the music, etc). I think a more specific philosophical focus would help provide a greater feeling of "cling" between the disparate elements of the course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1537580\n 1537580\n \n 1537580\n Strengths: First, Professor Tomlinson is awesome. He's brimming with wit, knowledge, and passion for his subject, and is overall just a fantastic lecturer. If he ever made a podcast, I'd definitely listen to it. (Also, he's very, very nice!) Second, the workload is manageable. You watch operas, have weekly readings, and there's only three short papers spread throughout the semester. Try to take the class with a friend so you'll have someone to watch the operas with, it makes it even more fun.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: First, sometimes (though not always) the lectures fell into fairly discrete "music" and "philosophy" boxes, and I definitely preferred lectures that balanced the two rather than sticking to one or the other. Second, it'd would've been nice if Tomlinson had been clearer regarding his expectations for the papers. Be sure to ask what he's looking for! (He's not a harsh grader, he just doesn't give much guidance on the papers unless prompted.)
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538739\n 1538739\n \n 1538739\n This class was well suited to students of all levels of prior familiarity with music. There was a great mix of lecture, when necessary to teach key concepts, and discussion, allowing students to engage directly with the course's intellectual material. Frequent opera viewing and listening in class kept the core of the class in focus without feeling like it was just filling time (as often happens with in-class videos).

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534824\n 1534824\n \n 1534824\n Honestly, this one is hard to say. I have a slight background in music, and a greater background in philosophy, but even with both of those, it could be really hard to engage with the class at times. The main reason for this is that it is clear that Professor Tomlinson is more interested in the music than the philosophy; on days when we were meant to discuss readings, for example, the first hour of the class would usually be dedicated to talking about the opera, and the last 15 minutes to the readings. As a result, he expected absurdly high engagement with the opera at times--usually, he would require that students watch the opera at least twice, sometimes in two different versions, and during the weeks of discussion would ask us to rewatch certain acts (keep in mind that operas can run for over 3 hours, and the acts for over 1 hour). What made it more frustrating is that he would often assume that, after watching it, we would somehow have the music completely memorized, often playing certain tunes or leitmotivs on the piano (or singing them aloud) and commenting on them as he went along. But the most frustrating part was that several other students had an extremely high degree of knowledge about opera--keep in mind that this is a 100-level course. A lot of the time (multiple times each class, in every single class), at least one of these students would raise their hand and talk about any opera OTHER than the one at hand. (Section asshole + opera = a terrible concoction.) Professor Tomlinson would actually engage with these students, in the middle of class, for quite some time, meaning that he was essentially having private conversations with a portion of the class, making it unclear what was necessary to know and what was not. Recommending this is only "hard to say" because some of the content really was interesting. So here's what I suggest: shop it, and see for yourself--if, in the first class, you realize that there are a bunch of music snobs with you, and Professor Tomlinson races through (I should note that he speaks very very quickly) the material, then don't take it; if the class is more balanced, and Professor Tomlinson has learned to balance the content better, then check it out more seriously.
