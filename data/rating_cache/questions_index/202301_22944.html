The course does push you to think more about your personal finances (great), but most of the information is obvious or may not be relevant (e.g. not having credit card debt or the formulas to calculate thresholds for mortgage refinancing). Other than a few practical pieces of advice, the course offers very minimal intellectual engagement and is poorly structured. Attending lectures and reading the slides packet are basically interchangeable. Professor Choi is a decent lecturer, but he has poorly written problem sets. The problem sets confuse students not due to the difficulty of the content, but rather the wording and inconsistencies in terminology. Many of the TF OH questions were just clarification questions on the writing. The course is also incredibly rigid and runs through exam week for undergraduates. This is stated upfront, and I would recommend students not underestimate the inconvenience that this causes when considering the course.\r\n\r\nAll else aside, the worst part of the course was the quizzes. I assume that the purpose of having beginning-of-the-class period quizzes is to encourage lecture attendance and see if students have an understanding of the materials. The quizzes accomplish both--however, poorly. Yes, twice the amount of students shows up in quiz lectures than in non-quiz lectures. However, some students leave right after the quiz, and lecture participation is no better even with more people. Students who attend regularly, but a missed quizzes due to unforeseen conflicts fare worse than students who show up for the first 10 minutes. Moreover, reading the lecture notes briefly vs. paying attention in class results in the same level of preparedness for quizzes since the questions are either common sense or specific to one bullet point on a slide that you happen to flip to. Life happens, so know that there are better classes out there where students are not graded on having perfect attendance. Class quizzes are stated directly on the syllabus, so it's perfectly fair. However, I would caution students to carefully consider whether taking a mediocre course is worth dealing with glorified attendance quizzes.
