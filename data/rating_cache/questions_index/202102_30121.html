\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1512569\n 1512569\n \n 1512569\n Strengths: Interesting material; thorough handouts for reviewing; well-written tests; Haig struck a good balance between providing details and big picture ideas.\r\nWeaknesses: Lecture format! Haig frequently switched between documents he'd share on zoom, and would often draw out diagrams live during lecture to explain the concepts. I found it sometimes difficult to keep track of the overarching organization of the lectures as a result. Using slides instead would've provided a smoother, more cohesive, and more efficient experience, in my opinion (our pace was a little behind the syllabus, and I'm guessing some details had to be cut as a result). \r\nAlso, I feel like the handouts, while helpful, were a little too text heavy, and could have been improved with more diagrams and figures. \r\nLastly, I felt like Haig occasionally dwelled on topics more than necessary - the history of neurobiology is interesting, yes, but probably not as important as the material itself.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1513385\n 1513385\n \n 1513385\n The course was taught in the span of five weeks and that time really is extremely tight for the amount of material that the course covers. Even though Professor Keshishian is a very knowledgeable and clearly excited instructor, the lack of visual material, such as slides, to accompany his lecture sometimes make the lecture hard to follow. This is exacerbated by the summer session course format which requires the course sessions to run for 2+ hours. The lack of visual guidance and clear segmentation and highlighting of the main ideas that are to be covered in each session prevent students to fully follow and take away from the lectures everything that is taught. The handouts are extremely comprehensive and that helps a lot when it comes to recapitulating the concepts that are covered during lecture. Nonetheless, they are very densely written and sometimes hard to follow as well. If I were to make any suggestions in terms of things that can change for this course to convey the material more effectively those would definitely entail making slides with colored pictures and clear diagrams a priority and organizing the lectures in a way that presents the main ideas and key takeaway points clearly and succinctly. Overall, I feel that students have a lot to learn from Professor Keshishian and I truly hope that the course can get to improve from an organizational perspective as that would much better allow students to take away everything that Professor Keshishian has to offer.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1515209\n 1515209\n \n 1515209\n The material was pretty difficult, but it was manageable day to day. Sometimes, it was a bit hard to stay engaged/focused in class because it was a lot to sit through for 2+ hours, three days a week. Professor Keshishian, however, is a good professor and is very understanding. He makes sure to answer questions fully and is always offering to set up extra meetings to further explain topics. The exams could be difficult and were long: he gave extra time to handle formatting and submission, but it felt impossible to complete the exam without using this time to continue to work on the exam itself. That said, he generally doesn't ask trick questions. More practice problems and P-Sets would've been appreciated and gone a long way, as we somewhat felt like we were left out to dry after the first two weeks, at which point the practice problems and P-Sets stopped. (They were only available for the first two weeks of the course, which is the material on the first exam.)
