\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1704390\n 1704390\n \n 1704390\n Brilliant course. Spitz and Bloch were phenomenal. The syllabus was the most thematically cohesive I've ever seen for a literature course not about an individual author. What I mean is that every week, we talked about the readings of the week, and the conversation naturally incorporated readings from previous weeks. I've taken English courses where each week you read a new thing, and you talk about it, and that's that. In this course, every week built on the last. \r\n\r\nAnd the readings are incredible! I got to read some works I've loved before, some classics I'd always meant to read, and some gems I'd never heard of. This is the only class I've taken at Yale where I got the sense that everyoneor almost everyonein the room had done the readings every week. There were no superfluous readings, either; the page counts were comparatively light, which gave everyone a chance to go deeper on each week's topic. Everyone spoke, because the professors guided conversations in such a way that contributing was easy and rewarding. There were emotional moments, and emotional days. There was an undercurrent of good feeling in the room that started up around week 3 and never went away.\r\n\r\nNo weaknesses I can identify.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1705390\n 1705390\n \n 1705390\n Strengths: So, so many. This class meant a lot to me this semester, and encouraged me to think about my relationships with my friends, family, and partner more deeply and in a new light. Many of the works we read were familiar, and many were new to me, but regardless of whether I knew something before I would inevitably think of it in a different light after Professor Spitz's comments, Professor Bloch's wry jokes, and my peers' points in class discussion. \r\nWeaknesses: None that I can think of. One warning I will give to potential students is that you must be prepared to take this class seriously and dedicate time to the readings, essays, and coming to class. Professors Spitz and Bloch do not tolerate absences, and expect students to not only do the readings, but personally engage with them and think about them deeply. This is not a class to take for an easy A, or for fun (it is fun, at times, but it also requires serious emotional and intellectual investment).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1705670\n 1705670\n \n 1705670\n Strengths: Professors who really, really care about the content and discussions. Great reading list (especially Fun Home and A Raisin in the Sun). Classmates who really care about the material. Writing assignments that truly deepened my engagement with the texts. Not any weaknesses that were really worth mentioning, although I think maybe asking more pointed and difficult questions would lead to a more enlightening discussion. I personally prefer questions like "Why did character X doY" to questions like "What did you think when you first read this passage?" The former type of question came up plenty in our class though, and I felt like those questions often stumped us, leading eventually to a deeper insight.

Every student in the seminar left the final class with tears in their eyes - Professor Spitz and Bloch created a classroom that echoed the content whereby we became our own version of a loving family.
