\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1528333\n 1528333\n \n 1528333\n Sometimes, we do not even receive feedback on an assignment before the exam that the content from the assignment is on, and we definitely do not receive feedback on problem sets before the following is due. When we do receive feedback, it is often illegible and typically only tells us how many points we lost next to the problem with little to no explanation. For the problem sets, we are told to compare with the solutions posted after the due date, but that seems to defeat the main purpose of receiving feedback. For the exams, we don't even get solutions; I did not know why I got most of the points off on my last exam when I got it back, and it took over a week to schedule a meeting with my professor because he doesn't reply to emails (or when he does, it is usually at least 2 days later).\r\n\r\nAdditionally, this class is a lecture consisting of majority upperclassmen at 9:25, three times a week on science hill, which I feel sets students up to fail. Most upperlevel students don't have other classes that early in the morning, making it difficult to build a routine around it when extracurriculars can routinely go to midnight. Based on the typical schedule of upper-level college students, it seems unreasonable to expect students to be able to attend all three lectures while still getting a reasonable amount of sleep; even when I did attend lecture, it was often too early for me to focus well. Additionally, our problem sets are due on Friday, meaning that the reality is probably that most students are staying up to work on them late Thursday night, making it even more difficult to attend class on Friday. \r\n\r\nFurthermore, the instructor only posts the slides that he projects rather than his lecture notes. Though the visuals are helpful, when reviewing material, it can be very difficult to recall what is going on in the slides/the explanation behind it without any verbal explanation. Sometimes, he also mentions details in class that, again, are not on the slides, and can have a very negative impact on our performance in the class if we missed them. For example, on the last exam, remembering one such detail was crucial to being able to perform well on a question worth 18/100 points. Because the class is not recorded, there is no way of going back over what he said and catching the details, unless we are expected to retain 100% of the information brought up in class, which again is extremely difficult given the time of this class.\r\n\r\nFinally, there is virtually no interaction in this class. I would say that he maybe asks us a question once every two weeks in this class, which is often just trivia regarding the content we are learning. People also almost never ask questions during the actual lecture unless something written on the board is wrong; most save their questions about content for office hours or other times. Thus, I think that the in-person component of this class does not benefit our learning, and we only do it in this format because it is the professor's preference. In fact, the in-person, non-recorded format of this class is a detriment to me and my peers' learning for the reasons listed above. I understand the desire to encourage students to attend lecture by not recording, but most students who don't attend lecture choose to watch the recordings at a time that works better for their own personal learning schedule, meaning that the only component they are "missing out" on is any live interactive component, which I already explained we do not really have. The issue is not that there needs to be interaction, as I actually tend to prefer my lectures this way, but that the class needs to be recorded or at a different hour. Thus, a recorded format, whether that means recorded live lectures or asynchronous, is objectively superior for this course.\r\n\r\nIn conclusion, I believe this class, which already has fairly challenging content and is typically taken by students just fulfilling a major requirement, sets us up for failure.
