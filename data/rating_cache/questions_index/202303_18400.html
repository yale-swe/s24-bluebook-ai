\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709104\n 1709104\n \n 1709104\n Strengths: Material itself isn't hard to understand. \r\nWeaknesses: Academic support (professor, TAs, graders) were faulty in all aspects and course overall was not designed for student learning.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1711505\n 1711505\n \n 1711505\n This is a flip class, therefore the learning is done via lecture videos outside of class time. I am not a fan of this type of learning, therefore, I think this class would be better taught during actual class time. The homework for this class takes up a substantial amount of time, far more than I was expecting for an introductory astronomy course. I think the problem sets should better reflect the material that will be on the exams. The exams being only four questions each require intense amount of studying for only a small subset of the material to be tested on. In class we attempt to solve problems at the white board, yet it tends to feel like weve never been taught how to solve these math problems. The lecture videos tend to not go over the math processes were required to use, so its very easy to get left behind in class. Working the problems on the white boards rather than in table groups hinders students from writing down and saving problems worked on it class for additional practice outside of class. I attended the TAs discussion section each week to receive help on the weeks material and problem set, and reflecting on the semester, it seems that I learned the majority of the content from the discussion sections. Additionally, we did not receive the grades for the last two problem sets prior to the final exam, and it generally took numerous weeks or even a month to receive the grades back on a couple of the problem sets, making it difficult to receive feedback in time for the material to still be relevant. Overall, I think this course would be benefitted by an adapted version of the syllabus that more accurately reflects the course load and exam types.

It is not that the math is particularly hard, it is just that, for the most part, you are left to figure out how to do the math on your own with very few if any examples in lectures and in class. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1711505\n 1711505\n \n 1711505\n If you are a prospective astrophysics major, then absolutely, however, otherwise no. This course has a reputation as a GUT science course, however, this is in my opinion incorrect. There is a substantial amount of homework involved and the course material is not necessarily easy. Participation is a part of the overall grade, thus you should always come to class, however all of the learning is typically done outside of class in the hours spent watching lecture videos, taking notes from the textbook readings, and the bi-weekly optional discussion sections. While attending the optional discussion sections is not required, in my opinion, if you want the best shot at receiving at least an 85 on the problem sets, you should be attending one or both sections each week. The homework is certainly required as you will be frequently out on the spot in class and will be expected to solve math problems each class. This class has caused me far more stress than I had anticipated, so as a student not exceedingly interested in astronomy, I would not recommend.
