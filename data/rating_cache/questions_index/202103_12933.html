\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527189\n 1527189\n \n 1527189\n I loved the way this course was organized! The syllabus is very well planned out and clearly thoughtful.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1528213\n 1528213\n \n 1528213\n I felt like this was a super fun course; I really enjoyed the books we read and I felt like I learned a lot from the discussions. I also enjoyed the freedom to write about whatever we wanted for the final paper. I probably could have done more writing for this course and not felt like I was overwhelmed (eg some smaller essay where we could choose which text from class we wanted to write about) and I would have liked more guidance on the queer idyll assignment: I haven't done any creative writing since elementary school so I struggled to come up with cohesive ideas and a way to format them.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535241\n 1535241\n \n 1535241\n I think Professor Herring is great, but wish he acknowledged his own privilege at times when talking about sensitive subjects. The class was very interesting though and he was very accommodating

I loved that discussion on intersectionality and positionality occurred multiple times throughout the course. I also really liked how much discussion happened in the class. The discussions helped me learn a lot from my classmates.\r\n\r\nOne weakness of the class was that the conversation of why wed say certain words and not others (for example: saying the d-word for lesbian woman but not the f-word for gay man) didnt come up until about midway into the semester. I feel like the class, with the focus it had on words, could really have benefitted from this discussion taking place early in the seminar.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535292\n 1535292\n \n 1535292\n I think there could have been a bit more clarity on some of the assignments. There were some resources through office hours, but some people, like myself, did not have the time to make it to those.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535385\n 1535385\n \n 1535385\n Strengths: Extremely diverse curriculum, engaging discussions, the professor \r\nWeaknesses: At times, the readings are a bit long which causes students to not do them in their entirety, which in turn leads to a less robust discussion.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535593\n 1535593\n \n 1535593\n I think a larger recognition of positionality would benefit the course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536013\n 1536013\n \n 1536013\n Strengths: clear assignments for grades, meaning all the readings were done out of interest and pleasure; phenomenal atmosphere in which everyone could speak as much or little as wanted; very diverse curriculum \r\nWeaknesses: reading load occasionally overwhelming
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536573\n 1536573\n \n 1536573\n This was an amazing course. The syllabus was rightfully diverse in reference to the different types of queer lives/spaces we talked about, and all of the readings were interesting and relevant. Additionally, Professor Herring prepares stellar presentations and guides conversation well.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1553063\n 1553063\n \n 1553063\n This course was always intellectually stimulating I came out of every class session having genuinely learned something new or changed my perspective in some way. The readings were on the whole very interesting, although many of the articles were too long and dense to read thoroughly. The combination of academic and literary texts was a strong point of the syllabus, although the organization could be improved. The discussion of decolonization during the last week, which focused on Queer Indigenous identities and spaces, would have been better placed earlier on in the semester, in order to inform discussions of other texts and spaces. Another strength of the class was the inclusion of a variety of LGBTQ+ spaces and places across the country (urban, rural and suburban). We spent much of the semester discussing metronormativity and deconstructing cultural narratives that depict cities as more accepting to queer people than rural or suburban spaces. While there was a solid foundation in urban studies with regards to New York City, I felt that San Francisco was missing from the syllabus, and learning about the queer history of S.F. (and its role in "flight to the city" narratives) would have been helpful for many of our later discussions and for students' understanding of metronormativity as a whole.
