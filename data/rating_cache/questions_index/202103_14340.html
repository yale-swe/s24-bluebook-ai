Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1525955\n 1525955\n \n 1525955\n I feel as though the strengths of the class lie in the format of the seminars. The professor would "lay the groundwork" for our conversations to get our minds thinking about the work in a way different from how we (or at least I) thought about it while reading the text. The ability for multiple people to agree and disagree with the ideas, approaches, and/or rhetoric of different writers and then in turn agree and disagree with one another was refreshing. I do feel as though a weakness was that sometimes I couldn't read and evaluate the text as deeply as I wanted before coming to class, though that is a comment more related to directed studies as a whole.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532079\n 1532079\n \n 1532079\n This class is great. The material is mostly all interesting and is heightened by the DS seminar style.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1533193\n 1533193\n \n 1533193\n Because Directed Studies History and Politics was my first introduction to political theory, I have no benchmark to compare it to. With that said, for me, this course's strength was its engagement with political thought. I feel like I can understand and and explain many tenets of western political thought, especially concerning Plato, Aristotle, and later Medieval thinkers. The course's weakness was history. I do not, for example, understand how lawmaking worked in Athens or Rome; I am also unfamiliar with the relationship between church and state during the advent of Christianity.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534050\n 1534050\n \n 1534050\n Strengths: high energy discussions, usually reasonably paced readings, variety of perspectives in the classroom\r\nWeaknesses: the City of God reading load is nearly unmanageable (there may have been other works with similarly overwhelming reading loadsoften we dont cover even a quarter of what we read, so it may be helpful to select more representative sections of an authors work rather than reading a wide selection)
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538740\n 1538740\n \n 1538740\n Lectures given by different people every week alongside small seminars that allow for engaging discussion on the text. Course could be improved with a greater level of spoken cognizance about sometimes uncomfortable concepts like the "west," the "western canon," etc.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538855\n 1538855\n \n 1538855\n Strengths:\r\n-some of the most fun 1st semester DS texts are here (in my opinions), lots of hidden gems\r\n-H&P is the DS course where there is the most consistent overlap between the the remaining two courses; it constantly feels like the texts from Literature and Philosophy are in conversation with the H&P texts \r\n\r\nWeaknesses: \r\n-same issue with all 3 DS courses: too much content too little time, we inevitably miss out on a lot that could be said because of time constraints
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539001\n 1539001\n \n 1539001\n This course benefits from a close-knit cohort of students and a very engaged professor. As with other DS classes, I was often left frustrated by not being able to dive deep into individual aspects of the works we read.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539118\n 1539118\n \n 1539118\n Strengths: The breadth of the course gave me a broad knowledge of many important historical thinkers. The frequent essays, though challenging, significantly improved my writing skills and my ability to critically analyze historical and political thought.\r\nWeaknesses: Given the enormous breadth of the class, I was often left feeling unsatisfied with the time we were given to discuss certain texts, and the readings were so hefty that I often felt like I didn't have time to really engage with them.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539133\n 1539133\n \n 1539133\n Strengths: energy put into the the class was high, it could be improved by commentary from Professor Schillinger that contrasted student points with what the devil's advocate would say.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1549865\n 1549865\n \n 1549865\n Of the three DS tracks, Historical and Political Thought felt the most rushed. While it was gratifying to study for finals and see just how many historians and thinkers we had covered, I did not feel as though I had enough time to absorb the ideas of the historians and thinkers we only spent one or two days on, especially those medieval thinkers we studied at the end of the term.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1551336\n 1551336\n \n 1551336\n The strengths of this course are innumerable. It provides an excellent foundation for historical and political thought. The biggest strength is that it is a survey course, surveying the extent to which politics and history are imagined, interpreted, and reinterpreted over time.\r\n\r\nThe largest weakness of the course is that there is not enough support for the level of writing required. I felt that I was thrown into the deep end, and I watched as others who came from more writing-intensive backgrounds (read: elite private schools) thrived with the tools necessary to tackle the writing from day one, whereas I struggled to understand what exactly each paper wanted from me. The requirements seemed vague and lofty, and it seemed at times as if the goalposts for each essay shifted on a whim.\r\n\r\nThis course needs to devote some class time, no matter how precious, to explaining, in clear and concise terms, how a model paper is written. That time would be better spent than pretending to read Maimonides.
