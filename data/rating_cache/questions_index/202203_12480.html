\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1611879\n 1611879\n \n 1611879\n I loved the scope of the course and how much time we dedicated to the various revolutions. We did go through 1848 a bit quicker than I would have liked, and I would have enjoyed spending another week on it. Overall, this course strengthened my historical knowledge of countries other than just the U.S. and made me feel like a world citizen. In terms of workload, the readings were digestible in only 2-3 hours a week. Most people in the seminar were able to get through all the texts, and there weren't any weeks with an obscene workload. The papers are pretty hefty, especially for a non-writing credit course. But the topics for the papers are so open-ended (literally anything revolutionary-related) that you can choose something that interests you, which makes the workload seem less.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1612316\n 1612316\n \n 1612316\n The class is challenging. Though the readings are not always incredibly long, the content is dense and most of it requires some prior knowledge of the revolutions we were studying. It is the sort of class you have to come prepared in order to meaningfully contribute, which is a good thing and I could see my classmates really understood the concepts we were discussing. I think the grading distribution could be improved. Despite the amount of reading and discussion, the only grades beyond participation are the midterm and final paper, which can be stressful if the midterm doesn't go well.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628430\n 1628430\n \n 1628430\n I had a lot of issues with this class, the first being it isn't so much a history course as much as it is a political science one. The level of knowledge required for this course is really high, specifically when it comes to global political history and just an understanding of government and politics. Further, and this is not the fault of the professor, but class conversations were oftentimes dominated by a select few students, who tended to be male. This is an issue of class makeup, but I do think this class attracts a certain type of student that is comfortable taking a lot of airtime and not really considering other classmates. I also think the class wasn't really focused on collective learning as a group, but instead unnecessary debates that pushed the thinking of the two people participating in said debates. However, the class did not feel like a cohesive community or group, which is unfortunate because I usually leave seminars feeling the opposite.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1629900\n 1629900\n \n 1629900\n This course was difficult for me because I only really knew the American revolution. It was difficult to think about the historiography of the French Revolution when, frankly, my understanding of the major events was a little hazy. There is a general understanding that you have prior knowledge of most of these revolutions. I wish the professor would spend 20 minutes at the beginning of the seminar lecturing about the major events so that everyone is one the same field.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630166\n 1630166\n \n 1630166\n The classroom atmosphere here was unlike any other history seminar. Professor Aslanidis is not interested in wasting time or filling two hours with talk for talk's sake. You will be encouraged and pushed to engage critically with the texts, with the political ideas being discussed, and with your peers. The room was always fun because I never knew what the discussion was going to look like ahead of time.\r\n\r\nThe trouble with the class's survey nature is that we occasionally found ourselves in discussions about the political ramifications of the details of a revolution's development without having really studied said development. So sometimes, for example, we might be asked to discuss the political ramifications of our interpretation of the cause of a particular revolution, having read only five or ten pages about the cause.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630891\n 1630891\n \n 1630891\n The class is incredibly inclusive and engaging. I feel as if every student in the class participated extensively. Professor Aslanidis is truly gifted at helping to facilitate class discussions, and is a gem in the Yale faculty. Every student should take at least one of his classes.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1630995\n 1630995\n \n 1630995\n The readings were a major strength of the course. They were from a wide range of perspectives and schools of historiography. The course did a good job of adequately covering a range of views, from conservative to Marxist. Another major strength was the classroom discussions which Professor Aslanidis led extremely well. I always deepened my understanding of course material from the questions the professor asked and how he guided discussion. I can think of no major weaknesses of the course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1636795\n 1636795\n \n 1636795\n I loved how the class was discussion based and all the readings were very interesting. Sometimes discussions could get repetitive so maybe for some of the class having some structure could help with that.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628430\n 1628430\n \n 1628430\n If you are looking for a political science class, sure, but this class caters to a certain type of student that is very comfortable dominating a conversation, and if you are that student, great.
