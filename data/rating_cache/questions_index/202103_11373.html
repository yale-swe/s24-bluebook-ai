\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1529006\n 1529006\n \n 1529006\n I found 267 to be a really helpful overview of paleoanthropology both what we know and how the field operates. It was as much theory-of-knowledge (as in, "how do we know what we know?") as it was anthropology, and looking back I think that was rewarding.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539273\n 1539273\n \n 1539273\n I learned a great deal about the fossil record and the current state of paleoanthropology. I learned about the classification system for ancestral hominin and what evidence we have that each existed. I learned about the anatomical differences between us and these ancient hominin as well as what cultural materials each was known to use. I learned about when each type of stone tool was discovered and which hominin were likely responsible for them. I learned about different dating methods for paleontology and their limitations. I learned about why it is so difficult to reconstruct our ancestral lineage, and why there is a so called "missing link". I also learned about the subjectivity of science. I think that lesson brought me some of the biggest insights because, as a scientist, I put a lot of weight on quantitative, objective data. This course taught me that it's really the interpretation of this data that matters to the outside world. Wrong interpretations from influential figures can cause a great deal of misinformation and can really hurt a fields overall image to the public.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548019\n 1548019\n \n 1548019\n I learned a lot about human evolution and was able to squash a lot of my misconceptions about the field! We also got to do a lot of analyzing and putting ourselves in a paleoanthropologist's shoes.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548759\n 1548759\n \n 1548759\n I developed a deeper understanding of conducting paleoanthropological research as well as the current state of affairs of human evolution discoveries.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1531200\n 1531200\n \n 1531200\n Extremely good professor - workload is a lot at times, but manageable if done in advance. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539273\n 1539273\n \n 1539273\n The class taught so much and the information was fascinating. Professor Thompson was really great and it was clear she was passionate about her work and educating us about it. She was a very big strength of the course. The biggest weakness overall was the time commitment this class had. Professor Thompson said she significantly decreased the workload for this semester but it was still a lot of work. It also felt like a lot because there were so many types of assignments- preparation and posting article summaries on canvas for three different debates, five different take-home assignments that each took several hours, two news article summaries AND a canvas post for each, all on top of weekly section assignments that would take at least a couple hours outside of section to complete. Honestly it felt like there should have been a lab component to this class just for the section assignments (sections were actually done in the paleo/anatomy lab too). There was a lot of work and I feel like it was more than most classic stem classes I've taken at Yale. I will say though, to defend the course, it was super nice not having any actual tests! As a student with test anxiety this class showed me that it really is possible to learn just as much, if not more, without tests. This is a lesson I will carry with me and advocate for in the future.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548019\n 1548019\n \n 1548019\n Strengths:\r\n- Prof. Thompson is INCREDIBLY approachable and passionate about work. She is very gracious and understanding and puts a lot of thought into what we do/discuss as a class\r\n- Lots of assignments mean we had lots of chances to engage with material and that one thing wouldn't make or break us\r\n- Really fair graders too- they understand this isn't our area of expertise and are genuinely interested in our thoughts\r\n\r\nWeaknesses:\r\n- Section was a MESS. It was obvious that it should have been coded as a lab because there was not one week where we left on time and finished the section assignment during the allotted time. They usually gave us incredibly complex assignments (think giving us 150+ tiny pieces of paper with things like discoveries, tools, and locations on them and asking us to match them in less than 30-40 minutes. always a nightmare and us and our TA always ended up scrambling for time)\r\n- The amount of small assignments REALLY adds up. With news summaries, section assignments (we always had to put in 1-2 extra hours to finish these outside of section), debate prep, and take home assignments, it just stacked up to be stressful at times. Sometimes we'd have like 3-4 single assignments due over the course of several classes which was stressful. They weren't individually difficult but the volume and timing made them basically endless. On this note though, they are NOT impossible. The grading is SUPER fair
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548028\n 1548028\n \n 1548028\n Great class overall, though I think that it really should be made clearer that this is a class for people who want to be anthropologists, and seemingly not so much for the casual interested observer. Though of course these are very important skills for an anthropologist to have, they do not fall under the category of human evolution so much as they do field anthropology. I wish there had been more focus on human evolution and less on what it's like to be an anthropologist. Though take this with a grain of salt, as I am not an anthropology major.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1548845\n 1548845\n \n 1548845\n I think the entire class was taught very well, and it was very clear that Professor Thompson was very passionate about this topic. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552901\n 1552901\n \n 1552901\n This is a great course.
