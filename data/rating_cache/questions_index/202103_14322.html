\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1538592\n 1538592\n \n 1538592\n It is a common complaint of DS that the program sacrifices depth for breadth. I agree with this wholeheartedly, and nowhere was it more evident than in the Literature course. I found that none of the texts we read was apportioned enough time to be truly mined for its layered meanings and nuances, and that the speed of the course made it hard to even maintain clarity about through-lines and context. I think this issue could be ameliorated by the removal of 1 or 2 texts from the syllabus, though I know such a decision would be a difficult one to make.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539350\n 1539350\n \n 1539350\n The strength of this course is the amount of material we cover and the professors that cover it. Directed Studies literature essentially goes from Homer and the Bible to Dante, which is a truly amazing amount of texts. We also covered a lot of ground textually, with long poems like Ovid's Metamorphoses sharing a place with the lyric poetry of Sappho in our writing. Another strength of the course are the lecturers. With a new lecturer every week, each a leading scholar in their field, the amount of detail they go into is truly incredible. That being said, an issue with the course is that it might cover too much in too short a time. There were some texts that definitely deserved more attention than we were allowed to give them, and I left many of the classes disappointed that we couldn't keep talking about texts like the Bible after spending only one class (essentially an hour) on the entire book of Genesis.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1553843\n 1553843\n \n 1553843\n I felt this course was overall perfectly balanced: it exposed students to a host of complex and rich readings, helped them to think about how others learned from said readings, and also to display their conclusions in clear essay form.
