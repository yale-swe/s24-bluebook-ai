\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539576\n 1539576\n \n 1539576\n Strengths: You are allowed to pursue your passions and you will be surrounded by peers who are just as passionate as you are. The course genuinely gives you skills that are useful and teaches you how to be self-initiative. You get to see a ton of cool projects and make a ton of cool things. It combines STEM and creativity!! How rare is that at Yale? I wish there were more courses like this because it's what I'm genuinely interested in.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: It's a lot of work, even with a more reduced curriculum compared to the last time it was taught. I understand why everything is there though but everyone faced burnout. I think more required pair-based projects would reduce workload! I think everyone was hesitant to work with others even though it was an option and over a certain number teamwork becomes hard so pairs are good. Sometimes it was too self-directed and I felt I wasn't able to utilize all the info thrown during lecture at us. For example, I would have loved a demo on how to use a voltage regulator or a battery charger. It got too engineering-focused at times.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1550340\n 1550340\n \n 1550340\n The strengths of the class are that it's super open-ended so that you can really make it what you want. I learned a lot about outlining a project for myself and self-critiquing to make sure it would get done on time. This was also a bit of a weakness, since it was sometimes unclear how we might be graded artistically on a project.\r\n\r\nThe class also involved a lot of self-teaching, so I wish the lectures might apply more to the work that was assigned.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552048\n 1552048\n \n 1552048\n The biggest strength was the small size of the class and the resulting interactions with classmates. It was great to see the projects that everyone else came up with, and get high quality feedback and help brainstorming for my own ideas. It really felt like we were all there to help each other and learn from each other, and we bonded over many late nights in the CEID. The group project was one of the highlights of this class, as we all put our heads together to come up with an immersive experience and used each of our unique strengths to accomplish it. One of the biggest weaknesses was that I didn't feel like I got much useful information from the lectures, which were very long. Most of the things I learned in this class, I learned on my own by looking things up and by trial and error. The labs were helpful in promoting this, but it seemed like lecture time could have been better spent or made shorter to match a normal class period, as many of the concepts we covered never came up again and/or weren't relevant to the things we were trying to build. Also, while our projects were all judged on how "creative" they were, we weren't given a clear understanding of what this meant or how we could improve in this area. While my hands-on skills greatly improved in this class, I don't know if my ability to think creatively has improved in the same way.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1554290\n 1554290\n \n 1554290\n It was amazing just how many tools we covered in one semester! It was super exciting to have so much freedom in our projects, save for the specific technologies that we had to include. Presentation days were really fun; I loved getting to see what everyone else had made. Towards the end, people were making hamster ball robots, music synthesizers/remixers (idk the exact term), educational, light-up plushies, and interactive generative art systems. Just so much fun to see. \r\n\r\nDespite the frequency of deliverables, this class was honestly not that stressful b/c Scott is a chill and capable teacher. Lab sessions were a great opportunity to learn from classmates-- the pair programming aspect was really helpful, esp. as someone coming in w/ no experience working with a Pi, the Arduino IDE, or hardware in general.\r\n\r\nI wish we could have gone deeper with looking at examples of work by established artists in lecture. Maybe some close analysis could be helpful-- it felt like we sort of breezed by most of the examples. Would have loved to have more inspiration for our projects. \r\n\r\nThe first task of the first module was lowkey the hardest part of the class, b/c of the weird limitations on the Leeds computer (why isn't python installed?) I'm not sure if that's meant to be a feature of the assignment rather than a bug, but it almost convinced me to drop the class back then... Wondering if there's a way to ease students into the task with hints (maybe a few days into the task) as to what the pitfalls are for working on that particular computer. The rest of the class was nowhere near as painful.\r\n\r\nAlso, for the students who have never done circuit stuff, maybe emphasize that resistors can burn up if you don't choose the right level. In Module 3, my project started smoking from the inside, and it took me way too long to realize why.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1556008\n 1556008\n \n 1556008\n The greatest strengths of this course were the overall structure and module design, the open-ended projects, the flexible deadlines and reasonable grading, and the great atmosphere for learning and working together. The course builds on itself in a really awesome way, moving through a variety of electronics techniques that you can continue to use in later projects after mastering them earlier on. But by focusing on one specific component or technique in each module, you can really dive into it; furthermore, this provides good direction for what would be an otherwise daunting set of possible project ideas. As a result, the projects have the perfect amount of open-endedness, with clear direction from the required technologies but complete freedom in terms of how you use those technologies to make something creative. This made it super fulfilling to complete each assignment, and by the end of the course I had a portfolio of exciting electronics/art projects to show off. While these projects could be extremely time consuming because of their self-directed nature, the deadlines were generally quite flexible, and the professor was very amenable if you needed a bit of extra time to finish up your work. The grading was also quite reasonable and generous, with consistent criteria across a great variety of projects. But most of all, I really enjoyed the collective experience of creating and sharing our projects as a class, and the many late nights together at the CEID and the OMI lab are what Ill remember most. Despite these strengths, there were still weaknesses from the time crunch of so many large projects in such a short semester and a sometimes frustrating lack of electronics review in class. The time requirement of this course is quite high, with the possibility to explode at any given time if a project idea or implementation gets out of hand. While properly scoping the projects helps a lot, it would be nice to have a bit more time for each project, rather than rushing through 7+ projects throughout the short semester. One thing that sometimes exacerbated the time commitment was limited discussion and instruction about the required electronics components in class, e.g. batteries or motors. It was good to do so much self-directed learning, but going over fundamental component operation and tolerances would make the course smoother for everyone. Something that could help significantly with both these issues would be changing the first tasks of each module such that they are about getting the required components working at a basic level, rather than implementing anything more complicated (e.g. getting joystick readings rather than demonstrating its functionality via some program). Moreover, instead of using lab classes to present our work, it would be better to present exclusively on discussion days (maybe the day they are due so we can actually share operating projects), allowing more lab time for the aforementioned component/technical instruction.
