Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1529839\n 1529839\n \n 1529839\n Strengths -- we covered a remarkable amount of ground, and it was amazing how we spent a ton of time on the work itself, but also so much time on secondary literature. It really gave us a sense of the great work at hand, but also how it's been interpreted/used/taken in the centuries since.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses -- the beginning of the course should be rethought. There was little to no introduction to Hegel or to the Phenomenology, making it slightly jarring diving right into such an infamously hard work. Furthermore, I wish we had more formal/comprehensive lessons on the parts of the Phenomenology we missed. I understand it would've been too much to get through every paragraph of the text, but I feel like we barely got any information on the parts we didn't get to, making it difficult to have a smooth, complete understanding of the text as a whole.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535373\n 1535373\n \n 1535373\n The course is designed to be intensive. This is a double-edged sword. If you do all the work, you get a lot out of it. Professor Havranek is also extremely generous with office hours and willing to respond to every question. But if you slip behind, the class quickly becomes slightly more incomprehensible (which can be fatal since Hegel is already so incomprehensible).
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1541539\n 1541539\n \n 1541539\n One of the biggest strengths of this course was the classroom discussions. Hearing others' views on the usually difficult material was always helpful and insightful. I also liked how Hugo tried to make sure that everyone got a chance to speak. It was also very helpful to ask Professor Franks and Hugo questions on the material, both in class and in office hours. \r\nRegarding weaknesses, most of what I'm going to say should not be understood as weaknesses per se but rather things that I wish were different. The first of these is the perusall deadline. I felt like the (at first 6 but then) 8pm deadline the day before class could sometimes be difficult to meet. Some of us have student jobs and other assignments, so consistently meeting that deadline could be a challenge (and we often would not get instructor responses until close to before class time). It usually felt like a race to finish the reading and get comments in, and I sometimes I found myself rushing and asking questions that were not at the quality I would like because of time constraints. \r\nI also thought that the three presentations may have been one too many. Doing three a semester is rough, and some of us never got feedback on our reports, so we were in the dark regarding how we did and ways we could improve. I also thought that the presentation expectations could have been clearer (e.g., what should we prioritize, clarity on one of the key issues, or breadth?; is a handout appropriate?, etc). Additionally, maybe having one or two max presenters a day with one less presentations would help open more class time for actual discussion. \r\nAnother concern I have regards the final paper in this class. I felt like there was little opportunity to try to write on Hegel and receive feedback in preparation for the final. The seminar reports of course helped, but some of us never heard back in time for the final paper, and these assignments also did not give us the space for any detailed analysis of the Phenomenology. The final seminar report did help with this, but, again, I would have liked to start practicing writing on Hegel a lot earlier, rather than the last week of the class. Perhaps substituting one of the presentations with a ~5-6 page paper would be helpful for the undergraduates. I feel like I personally would have benefitted from this a lot, and the experience would also have helped lesson my stress as I wrote the final.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1550191\n 1550191\n \n 1550191\n This course benefitted a lot from its rigorous structure, although this structure also made the course high-workload and incredibly intellectually challenging. The weekly Perusall assignments and monthly seminar reports forced a thorough examination of the text, so that there was no gliding by without understanding, but they, combined with the high reading load of the course, led to this being one of the most difficult (while fulfilling) seminars I have taken at Yale.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1551967\n 1551967\n \n 1551967\n Great seminar structure, though I think more in class work on writing could be helpful
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552990\n 1552990\n \n 1552990\n This course was very well organized and the small amounts of reading made for much richer and more careful discussion than many of my other classes, where the readings often strayed above 100 pages per week.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1555855\n 1555855\n \n 1555855\n My only complaint is that I wish we had more time to discuss the reading. Hugo and Professor Franks did an incredible job and divided up the lecturing workload well. They would do well to have plenty of time beforehand to discuss exactly where their interpretations of the text diverge from one another to ensure the class gets as broad of a range of approaches as possible.
