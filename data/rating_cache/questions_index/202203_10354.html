I personally came out of the class fascinated by the convergence of chaos theory and history.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1629862\n 1629862\n \n 1629862\n I learned to think critically and creatively about the importance of history, as well as its limitations, and about the role of the historian. The assigned readings and discussions held in this course were very intellectually challenging and pushed me to think more deeply about history than I ever have before. Through this course, I also became a better writer. Professor Gaddis chose really open-ended essay prompts that seemed rather daunting to tackle, but they pushed me to write imaginatively and develop my writing skills. This course additionally emphasized the importance of being a generalist, having knowledge about many different disciplines, and not sticking to a super specialized path in life. I really appreciated this message, as someone who is interested in many different areas of study and isn't sure what they want to major in, much less do beyond college.

I would not say I developed too many particular skills, but I did gain some insight on historical philosophy and how different types of work (archival work, fiction, music, math) can contribute to our understanding of history.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639400\n 1639400\n \n 1639400\n It's hard to pin down exactly what I gained from this course, and that's probably by design. Professor Gaddis teaches new ways of thinking about the world and the past through the medium of an exceptional range of readings. You won't learn about one history in particular, and you won't read only one kind of book. You'll instead be treated to the probing questions, remarkable thought process, and wonderfully disjoint interests of a most wonderful historian.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639542\n 1639542\n \n 1639542\n Better writing skills and analytical skills.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639985\n 1639985\n \n 1639985\n As many other reviews allude to, it's hard to pinpoint a specific skill or insight you learn from this course. As soon as you think there's a takeaway from the class, Professor Gaddis will introduce a completely new idea about, well, what history teaches. Most of the specific history you'll learn mirrors the readings: Herodotus and Thucydides, Churchill in WW2, the Cold War, and of course, Napoleon's invasion of Russia. However, it's the big-picture insights that you will take away most from this course: "power-law relationships" in history, self similarity across scale, the "Malkovich maneuver," gold-digging ants, and many more classic Gaddis insights.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1607737\n 1607737\n \n 1607737\n Reading a lot of interesting texts that I would've never read. Made me a better writer. Discussions with Prof. Gaddis were really engagin.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1610379\n 1610379\n \n 1610379\n Absolutely amazing course. Not sure it could be improved much. Maybe some colloquia or field trips.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1612024\n 1612024\n \n 1612024\n Strength: Professor Gaddis is legendary. His insights and stories make for wonderful discussions. Also, you'll quickly notice his humor and wit. Much of the reading is rewarding (and some have barely anything to do with history; they will still change your thinking). The assignments were all thought provocative. Being in the same room as him is a privilege.\r\n\r\nWeakness: There really isn't any. You just have to be ready for doing the work.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1612434\n 1612434\n \n 1612434\n The professor is very passionate about teaching the class. The course discusses bigger philosophical questions about what is history, which are not typically addressed in history classes. Because of the focus on methodology, you do not learn much about any historical period in great depth. Many of the assignments are very open and include creative writing, which can be difficult for people who prefer standard history writing. A weakness of the course is that it is focuses almost completely on the Western canon. I also think that the inclusion of critical theory would have added a lot to our discussions. For example, although we discuss limitations of archives, we barely touch on the race, class, and gender biases of archives. I think that scholarship on critical fabulation would have deepened our discussions of archives.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1615017\n 1615017\n \n 1615017\n Strengths -- really engaging conversations, creative essay topics \r\nWeaknesses -- there was so much reading that you did not have the time to fully engage with each one. Felt like reading load was quantity focused over quality.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617792\n 1617792\n \n 1617792\n I love the seminar format - each class is a dynamic and intellectually stimulating journey, starting from an initial idea and evolving based on the contributions of students. Professor Gaddis masterfully guides these discussions, and we are able to have conversations about particularly complex concepts. The only issue I have with the course is the workload!
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1627404\n 1627404\n \n 1627404\n This class may prove to have been my best at Yale by the time I graduate. It made me excited to discuss history and to simply hear Professor Gaddis's insights and anecdotes, and it fulfilled its secondary role, being a fall first year seminar, of creating a new community of first year students. My one recommendation would be to provide more clear feedback on work. The gender split was also heavily skewed male, but I don't know how that could be fixed.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1629862\n 1629862\n \n 1629862\n Strengths: the reading list!!! There are so many readings assigned for this course, but they are all amazing and worth the time spent. Reading War and Peace was a particular highlight, especially since Professor Gaddis is so passionate about Tolstoy. The small class size is another strength - it allowed us to get really deep into intellectually challenging discussions. Of course, the biggest strength is Professor Gaddis himself. There's so much to learn from him, and our class also enjoyed getting to hear about his personal life, hometown, how he got into history etc.\r\nWeaknesses: Our in-class discussions, while always engaging, were not always related to the readings we prepared. Often times I came into class with many thoughts on the readings, but then the conversation centered around something different and I wasn't completely sure what to contribute to the discussion. I also wish I had received more in-depth feedback on the writing assignments.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1635926\n 1635926\n \n 1635926\n Strengths: professor Gaddis is an interesting person with decades of experience and wisdom under his belt.\r\nWeaknesses: discussions felt meandering and largely pointless. They added little to the content introduced in the (hefty) readings.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639400\n 1639400\n \n 1639400\n This course is fantastic! I wish Professor Gaddis would push a little harder for more engagement with his questions, though they are admittedly super hard to answer. I always thought the best classes were the ones where there was lots of back-and-forth.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639542\n 1639542\n \n 1639542\n Strengths - great readings, interesting papers. \r\nWeaknesses - too much reading so that students do not complete all of it and so class discussions are not worth the long readings. Class discussion have lots of long often irrelevant tangents.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639985\n 1639985\n \n 1639985\n The reading list is interesting, but you take this class for one reason: Professor Gaddis. However, because of this, class discussions are often pretty disconnected from the readings, which can make the very intense reading load even more difficult to slog through.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639985\n 1639985\n \n 1639985\n Definitely. This class was a true intellectual experience and exactly what I hoped I would find when I came to Yale. Yes, the workload of this class is very high, bordering on ridiculous at times. You will read the entirety of War and Peace over 3 class periods, and some classes require multiple books' worth of reading (not to mention that many readings are incredibly dense and difficult to read). While you only need to write four five-page essays, the prompts require a lot of creativity, and Professor Gaddis's high standard for writing quality means that you will probably spend hours writing and revising each one. However, if you're willing to do the work, you will get an incredible amount out of this classfrom neat historical tidbits to fundamental lessons about how history works. Don't be turned away by Professor Gaddis's legendary status; he is actually very approachable, and the class dinner we had with him was a highlight of the semester. If you get in, take ityou will be glad you did after the semester.
