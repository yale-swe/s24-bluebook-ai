RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1523244\n 1523244\n \n 1523244\n This is the only English class I have ever taken that has actually taught me how to become a stronger writer. The Genre of the Sentence hones in on the words themselves, what you are trying to say, what you are intentionally not saying. Now, I feel as though the sentences I write have meaning. Beyond the improved execution of writing, the consistent weekly assignments spurred my creativity. Selecting a topic to write about totally unprompted was a struggle in the beginning weeks, but soon, topics come naturally. In the middle of the day, something happens to you and you think "Oh, this would make a great piece." That is what makes this class stand out you become a creative.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1525545\n 1525545\n \n 1525545\n this course helped me become more confident in the act of writingsometimes it is enough to just write something down instead of waiting for what feels clever or beautiful to hit you.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1525546\n 1525546\n \n 1525546\n I learned how to write a sentence. I got much better at listening for the rhythm in my writing, at reading prose through the lens of the sentence, and observing without exaggerating or sentimentalizing reality.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1525610\n 1525610\n \n 1525610\n I became a much better writer through this course. Verlyn stripped away almost everything that was hindering our writing and then helped us build back up towards complexity, all while teaching us how to write as clearly as possible. I also learned to trust myself more as a writer.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1525668\n 1525668\n \n 1525668\n You will learn to write much more succinct and powerful sentences. Throw away everything you know about writing academic papers. Beautiful language is born from beautiful ideas written simply.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1527883\n 1527883\n \n 1527883\n A big increase in my ability to craft excellent sentences and to write creative nonfiction as a whole. My prose got much clearer and more efficient.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1536612\n 1536612\n \n 1536612\n I certainly became a better writer! I feel like I have a better grasp at writing interesting, engaging, narrative prose, and have a better ability to write about myselr.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539359\n 1539359\n \n 1539359\n I learned to be both a better writer and a better reader. I also learned how deeply intertwined those two skills are.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1544445\n 1544445\n \n 1544445\n I became a more confident writer. I learned how to edit pieces, what questions I should ask when I'm reading, and what stylistic choices I should be paying attention to. I developed practical skills on reading and writing. Additionally, I learned how to have a feel for reading and writing that only comes with continuous practice.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1546720\n 1546720\n \n 1546720\n So much, of course, so I probably don't need to join the chorus. But obviously this is the best creative writing class at Yale. Why? Because Verlyn holds his line. He is a hard-ass in the exact was that he is a softie. And I think that is a combination that makes most people (subscribed and sucking up to a more conventional work-writing practice...) feel very *weird*. By weird I mean, insecure. By insecure I mean, threatened. Anyway: that's all to say: it's possible to encounter all sorts of defensiveness and untoward criticism and stuff when walking Verlyn's path, so, that's the only Warning I'd give. Can you tell?: The insights gleaned here are mainly psychological. They are insights about what motivates writing, what goes into and what comes from writing, and also how much random whackassery is smothering writing/writers/artists/creative people/everyone. It's about being gentle with yourself, which a very scary concept for everyone deeply invested in disciplinarian penal workaholic low-self-worth capitalist culture. I'm loading this with my own opinions, obviously. But, see, I feel alright about my own opinions within the context of Verlyn's class -- there's a good gardening practice that he invites, cultivating things like opinions and perspectives. Oh, I should just say what we really learned: to notice. That's what he would say probably. To notice things and write them down. It's just that being able to do that requires a slow deconstruction of mental/emotional/psychological blocks. Sometimes it's not slow; sometimes it's precipitous, but there's care in it. Etc etc! Take this class if you can get in and leave mommy issues at door: humility or bust*. *waste effort resisting the good.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1539359\n 1539359\n \n 1539359\n Only strengths. Of any Yale course, Genre of the Sentence has the most intentional workload. Theres no busywork. No final project. The reading load is light and always helpfulan unusual occurrence at Yale. There are no reading responses. Just two pages of writing a week. Two pages of supposed-to-be-perfect sentences.\r\n\r\nEvery week, Verlyn will line edit the sloppy metaphors, dependent clauses, and poor word choices. The first few pieces have many embarrassingly bad sentences. Verlyn shares these miserable bits of writing with the classeveryone contributes. Over time, the poor-sentences handout becomes shorter, then disappears. Verlyn, the scribbling editor, becomes Verlyn, the confidence booster. \r\n\r\nMidway through the course, everyone sounds alikelots of short sentences. Should I have listened to all that advice? Its a bit disconcerting. At the end of the course, those doubts vanish. Everyones writing truly becomes their own. The sentences get longer, but remain clear. Garbled writing transforms into writing that shows humor, sorrow, love, kindness, joy, and happiness. There were more laughs, awws, and even some tears during the weekly workshops.\r\n\r\nIts impossible to not become a better writer during Genre of the Sentence. I enjoyed every moment of the class.
