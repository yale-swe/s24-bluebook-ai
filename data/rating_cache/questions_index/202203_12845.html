\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617266\n 1617266\n \n 1617266\n I am a bit frustrated because much of the material is very similar to the Paul Kennedy course. For the ROTC cadets, who are the majority of the class and are required to take it, many of us are left wondering when we're going to learn the important information we need to know for starting active duty. Last year, the 400s learned all the practical things they needed to know, like different scenarios with enlisted people, forms and awards, how to navigate activity duty finances, bases, etc. Are we just not going to learn that stuff? I thought it was part of the curriculum. To me, that is much more important than learning about WWII bombing, again. \r\n\r\nI am also unhappy with AS class now being for credit. I don't have room in my schedule to take a class that is not really that relevant to active duty life (compared to, e.g. AS300 or 200 class). The class being for credit takes away time for things that are actually important to my course of study. And, because it is so repetitive, it is even more frustrating. \r\n\r\nI would very much prefer to just learn the practical information I was supposed to learn before starting active duty rather than having a class that adds more work and takes away from my focus on my actual degree. Especially because the majority of the content was already covered in previous years.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1618305\n 1618305\n \n 1618305\n This course is extremely well-organized, allowing students to get an overview of the use of airpower in a given conflict each week through both background reading and the weekly class, which consists of both instructor-led and student-led discussions. Students can then specialize in the conflict or topic most interesting to them by choosing to lead the respective week's discussion or by writing their paper about it. The exams focus primarily on the themes of the course; as a result, they encourage students to synthesize what they've learned from the course to write about a given topic, rather than focusing on rote memorization of random facts and dates. Overall, this course does an excellent job of helping students understand the role of air and space power in both shaping American grand strategy and setting the world order.

This is probably the point, a millitary needs to constantly be under self-examination and correction. But just a few months away from commissioning, the study of our repeated failures as a force was enough to give me pause.
