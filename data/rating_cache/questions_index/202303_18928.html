\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1698692\n 1698692\n \n 1698692\n This was the best fucking course I have ever taken at yale. Professor skiba is a wondeful teacher always prepared each class to share the secrets of the universe. He treats each class as an adventure through physics and is dedicated to making the learning experience the best he can for each of his students. He is friendly and devoted to making the class a fun and inclusive space. He understands that we are students who want to learn and respects that in the way he sets up his assignments and exams. This class made me want to continue studying relativity and one day aspire to be a professor like him.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1705441\n 1705441\n \n 1705441\n There is a nice balance between phenomenology and math. The course catered well to physics majors with all different specialties and to math majors with a physics interest. At the end of the course, some of our material on cosmology felt a little smushed in, so maybe a bit more organizational planning could be done so that the end of the course doesn't feel rushed.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1707790\n 1707790\n \n 1707790\n Strengths: Professor Skiba is a fun and engaging lecturer, and happy to stick around after class answering questions. There was also a nice class dynamic, I think facilitated by Professor Skiba's jokes. The material is inherently very cool (black holes! gravitational waves!). We spend a lot of time with the mathematics of tensors, and by the end of the class, I had some real understanding of what physics tensors are and how to use them. I think splitting the class half-SR, half-GR works fairly well - it's nice to get some sense of the ideas of the course with the more familiar special relativity, before moving on to the unfamiliar GR. The TA was wonderful, and together with the professor provided clear feedback.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: during a few of the more tensor/blackboard-heavy lectures, I was almost completely lost. I think this is in part because of a lack of physical intuition - the physical situation we're dealing with often gets abstracted away under a mountain of tensors, such that it's hard to see what's really going on. This is more about the material than the teaching, I think - humans aren't really built to think in 4D, and there's only so much you can intuit about a 20-component tensor. \r\n\r\nOne area for improvement: there were a few lectures in which we made first-order type approximations, and it was somewhat unclear to me what we were and weren't assuming. Perhaps the lecture could begin with a list of assumptions/approximations? I'm honestly not sure how helpful this would be, but I think it might provide a little more intuition, and help prevent some "why can you do that?" moments.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709590\n 1709590\n \n 1709590\n Strengths: Professor Skiba is likely the most funny and entertaining professor I've ever had at Yale. The content of the course is like a different language and rather intriguing. Professor Skiba is also very kind and willing to help his students. Naveen, the TF, was also awesome and super intelligent\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: At times, it felt like Professor Skiba and Naveen were both too smart to the point where I couldn't understand what they were saying. There were times where I felt extremely lost -- probably more lost than I've been in any class in my life and I thought I was screwed. But I did feel like Professor Skiba and Naveen were there to help in those times
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1711032\n 1711032\n \n 1711032\n The structure of the course (half the semester on SR, half on GR) works well, with the midterm serving to split the topics in two. The order of topics covered is for the most part well thought out, and each week tended to build on the previous weeks' material so that was satisfying.\r\n\r\nI would have appreciated having the reading laid out at the start of the semester, instead of starting to receive page/chapter numbers by piecemeal halfway through. Turns out the newest edition of the GR textbook also covers SR, so 1) I didn't need to buy the "recommended" SR textbook and 2) I could have started reading from the GR book at the start of the semester. Reading associated chapters BEFORE the lecture would have been super-helpful to me, but we didn't find out which pages were connected until after the class - sometimes not until the end of the week.\r\n\r\nProblem sets were sometimes extremely difficult to parse - there were problems I legitimately didn't know how to do, but there were others I only thought I didn't know how to do because I couldn't understand what was being asked of me. That extra mental load made the weekly homeworks stressful. There was also sometimes an expectation that we knew or remembered certain concepts from other areas of physics which definitely made the problems more difficult! Exams were rough, but I feel like the curve was significant and fair in consideration of that.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724360\n 1724360\n \n 1724360\n Strengths: Skiba and Naveen, as well as the material itself. I can't think of many weaknesses other than the fact that I would love to learn more about GR, especially the cosmology unit at the end.
