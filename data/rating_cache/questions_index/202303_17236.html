\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1704561\n 1704561\n \n 1704561\n An array of topics: Fourier Series/Transforms, Neural Networks (CNNs, ResNet, lateral inhibitory networks, non-linear networks), anatomy of the eye and visual system, Principal Component Analysis, K-means clustering, Gaussian distributions/blurring, Laplacian operator, Edge detection
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709564\n 1709564\n \n 1709564\n General aspects of computer vision, with a focus on how different biological systems may perceive vision.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709796\n 1709796\n \n 1709796\n Computer vision
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709886\n 1709886\n \n 1709886\n Everything under the soon: an intro to calculus, a proof of properties of the convolution operator, the history of human neuroscience, implementing binocular vision from scratch, the biology of neurons, efficient solving of systems of constraints, optical illusions, and the cannot edge detector, to name a few topics we discuss.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1709966\n 1709966\n \n 1709966\n How basic computer vision works and a biological context for vision.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1710110\n 1710110\n \n 1710110\n The class is about 50% math, 30% biology, and 20% coding/computer science. The PSETs skew CS (mostly they're done in Python), while the lectures are mostly math and biology. Topics include: multivariable calculus applied to gradient ascent/descent, neuron basics (synapses, action potentials, excitatory/inhibitory), Fourier transform applied to linear systems (impulse response), linear algebra applied to principal component analysis (principal components are eigenvectors of a covariance matrix), the visual system (mostly V1), edge detection, deep neural networks (perceptron, CNNs, are DNNs good representations of biology?), stereo (using two images of the same scene).\r\n\r\nThe topics are in general quite scattered, although they all apply to vision in fairly fundamental ways. The math is more about intuition than understanding - this isn't a good class to learn about eigenvectors, or learn how to do a fourier transform. However, this is a good class to understand (once you already know about gradients, eigens, fourier transforms, etc.) what these things are actually up to. I took this course at the same time as quantum mechanics, and while I learned the formula for a fourier transform in quantum, I got more intuition for the operation here (we talked about Nyquist's theorem, e.g, and I now have an intuitive understanding of how things go wrong when you don't sample frequently enough).\r\n\r\nOverall, I developed a bit of intuition for some math, an idea of how organisms start processing light, and a sense of how insanely cool and complicated the brain is.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1711501\n 1711501\n \n 1711501\n I learned about various aspects of computational vision in this course. Building progressively on models for vision as the course went on, I gained insight into image filters, basic neuroscience, sampling, template matching, and edge detection. I was also introduced to a variety of different types of neural networks. In this process, I was able to build upon my MATLAB skills.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1710110\n 1710110\n \n 1710110\n Strengths: the material is fascinating, and Professor Zucker really knows his stuff - he's been in the field for a long time, and has an understanding of the fundamentals, which is very valuable in a field often focused on highly-specialized optimization. The course covers math with the goal of developing intuition, rather than teaching students how to perform computations (more on this later), which means students who already know the math still have something to learn. I didn't really learn to do anything new, but I think the class has made me a better physicist, and certainly left me excited to learn more about vision and the brain.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: This course just doesn't spend enough time on math to teach students new mathematical techniques. This is overall a strength of the class, I think, but it means that when Professor Zucker does try to teach math, it doesn't go very well. Even in the best of circumstances, it's difficult to learn math from slides, and Professor Zucker isn't great at explaining step-by-step proofs/derivations of formulas. In preparing for the final, I had more luck learning PCA from the internet than from the lecture/notes. However, the lecture and notes gave a much better intuition for what PCA is/why it's useful than I found on the internet.\r\n\r\nThe last PSET was assigned too late, and was due during reading week, the day before I had my first final. It was also quite long, and significantly bit into my studying. I'm not sure what should be done about this - the PSET was certainly valuable, and I wouldn't want it to just disappear, but also this was really poor timing. Also midterms were never handed back, so I never found out where/why I lost points, which would have been nice to know.
