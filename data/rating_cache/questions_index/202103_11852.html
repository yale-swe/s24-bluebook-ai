\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1528709\n 1528709\n \n 1528709\n All in all, I thought the class was very good. Definitely the best of the semester. Professor Tipton was a very capable and engaging teacher, and the TAs (particularly John Garmon) covered class material and built on it during office hours to compound the ideas from lecture and improve understanding of the material. The method definitely worked and focused on the idea of practice, where answering problems would teach the material. The problem set questions for the year were very good questions, and I felt I learned quite a lot from them. I'm always a fan of learn through problem solving. \r\n\r\nHowever, I do feel Physics 200 also has some room for improvement too. My greatest gripe with the class came from the lecture slides. I learned most from Professor Tipton's lectures when he used props, answered questions, and deviated from the slides. The reason being that he tended to explain why something occurred and how it did so in much greater detail than the slides or when he was reading off them. I found that the slides generally covered what was happening in a concept but not why, which I found tough to follow. Also, very often they were a mix of formulas and bulky paragraphs that were tough to read while following the lecture. It felt like the idea behind a concept should be intuited from the formulas, which I found very challenging to understand at times. To use an example, slides 11 to 13 of lecture 22 introduce the 1st law of thermodynamics. Heat is introduced, a formula derived from previously seen principles is shown, and paragraphs bookend it with things to note about the formula and things of note about heat. Now, this is a good introduction to the material (remember I think the class is quite good), however it was much better explained by John Garmon during an office hour session as Heat being the sum of the change in internal energy of the system and work done on or by the gas (where the sign of the work changes depending on what is doing the work). He took the time to explain what heat is in a system, how it is transferred and related to internal energy, and why work would subtract or add to it. The inclusion of "why" and "how" into the equation as opposed to "what" made a world of difference in understanding it and more of that in the slides would very useful. That's not to say there was never a "why" in class, only that more time spent on it makes understanding the rest of the material much easier. I apologize for the essay. \r\n\r\nTo summarize, there were a lot of formulas that detailed what was happening in a concept. Deviating from that, with an explanation of "what" happens because "why" and "how" that occurs would help. Despite being one of the toughest concepts in the class, I feel Professor Tipton taught relativity very very well, because every other sentence was about "why" something happened, rather than just what. I didn't always understand it, relativity is tough, but the actual teaching part was great.
