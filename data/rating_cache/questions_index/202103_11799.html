\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1535041\n 1535041\n \n 1535041\n strengths: \r\n- Professor Cleary is an amazing educator: he evidently cares so much about making school meaningful for us, and about helping us to get the most out of his class. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1551668\n 1551668\n \n 1551668\n Strengths - Firstly, any chance to take a class with Professor Cleary is a gift. He does a great job of guiding the class through dense, complex readings. He does not patronize you; he holds you to the standards that he holds himself, which means that you have to do the readings. This meant that nearly everyone came prepared, and our discussions often felt like they had real stakes (an experience hard to find in an English class). Secondly, most of the readings were really provocative and fulfilling to read. I would never think to put Hannah Arendt with Barbara Ehrenreich and Frank Wilderson III in one semester, but these books enhanced one another. Many of the supplementary essays from scholars like Frederic Jameson, Edward Said, and Francis Fukuyama were so great and I never would have read them otherwise. \r\n\r\nWeaknesses - The readings for this course were **dense** and the workload could be overwhelming. The original syllabus had to be cut down because we could not keep up with the pace of readings assigned to us. Moreover, despite Cleary's best efforts, there was not an obvious sense of direction to the material itself, which I think could have sustained us through some of our more intensive weeks of reading. I guess the theme could be "polemic" or "public intellectual," and we sometimes tethered our discussion to these books around these words. Often, however, it was either more necessary or engaging to discuss the content of the readings themselves, that discussions about style or broader questions of polemic seemed less relevant, or more peripheral to where the heart of our discussion lay. There were some quirks to the syllabus, too, that confused me...for example, we spent a lot of time reading William Blake, which didn't seem like the most obvious choice for a polemic poet across history / Western canon (i.e. contemporary American poets like Terrance Hayes' "American Sonnets," Claudia Rankine's "Citizen", WWII modernist poets like Ezra Pound). It also felt like a strange way to start the semester off, considering we read little poetry beyond it, besides Shelley's "Mask of Anarchy" and Lord Byron's "Age of Bronze" (which did not have the bang for its buck). Generally, the readings during the second half of the semester were much more diverse and exciting than those during the first half of the semester.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1551753\n 1551753\n \n 1551753\n The strengths are the readings themselves, and their diverse slate of backgrounds and goals. In addition, as a writer, being in a small class with a collection of similarly dedicated writers was very impactful on me throughout the semester. I learned as much from my peers as I did the course.\r\nA weakness of the course is that the intellectual path of this class was somewhat disjointed at times, and the connection between readings week to week was sometimes hard to follow. But the strengths definitely outweigh the weaknesses.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552084\n 1552084\n \n 1552084\n The course was difficult. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1552691\n 1552691\n \n 1552691\n Prof. Cleary leads fantastic discussion, and I think he did a wonderful job of selecting the texts for this course. All of the readings are interesting and make for lively discussion. The space in general felt very warm and welcoming. As for areas of improvement, I sometimes wish certain books had more time dedicated to them. This course is fast-paced, and sometimes I came away feeling like we hadn't fully discussed some interesting parts of the texts, though that's really a minor issue.
