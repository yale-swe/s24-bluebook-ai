\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617516\n 1617516\n \n 1617516\n The strengths are the syllabus and the instruction. What we learn and how we learn it is done really well. I think there could be some improvements made in making sure students understand the crucial passages in each text so we know what seminal moments to reference when studying for the final.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1617668\n 1617668\n \n 1617668\n Strengths: Professor Lamont was a really amazing, caring professor. Her enthusiasm for the course content was obvious; she'd frequently go to the map or whiteboard to explain something about the linguistics or historical context of each text. Some of her insights were remarkably illuminating. A tough but fair grader. The reading list was really great. Only Confessions and perhaps Matthew felt out of place. I learned a lot for sure. The DS lectures are really great; combined with the seminars, you should get a good sense of why these books have been so meaningful to so many people.\r\nWeaknesses: Directed Studies is a lot of work. Sometimes the class felt like a never-ending test; as though every week, I was being tested to see how much I could read, how fast I could write, whether I could show up to class every day ready to say something interesting about the hundred pages I'd just finished last night. And for all of that work, DS doesn't always feel equivalently intellectually rewarding. You might spend half of your weekend reading one book --- you might be the only one in your class to actually read the book --- and end up only speaking once or twice in the seminar, before the class moves on to a new text the next day. I think a lot of the ways in which the program is valuable won't be obvious until years after you (or I) have taken it; they certainly don't feel obvious when you've spent the whole week reading or worked yourself to the bone writing a paper. But there are books you'll read that are breathtaking and beautiful and if you don't take this class, there are probably some really special texts that you'll miss out on.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1628461\n 1628461\n \n 1628461\n The strength of this course definitely reside in the breadth, and compelling nature of the material studied. Being surrounded by very engaged students, eager and curious certainly complements in a considerable way the professor's insights. The rigor achieved by regularly having papers to write, not only obliges us to become an avid reader, but also pushes us to think outside the box, in going beyond general lines of interpretation and understanding.\r\n\r\nA weakness, like any DS course, is the deceiving rapidity by which we have to read and study books which deserve more dialogue and questioning. Sometimes, I also wish we had more time at the end of a seminar to conclude, and tie up on some initiated thoughts that were spurred at the beginning and let the professor give her opinion, or some broader questions to think about. I also regret the fact that the writing and question posts were not shared between peers. I feel it as a lost opportunity to learn and elevate our thinking outside the classroom, as all the content can not be exhaustively discussed in class.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1637769\n 1637769\n \n 1637769\n DS covers broad swaths of material - that's both its strength (scope) and weakness (depth). The format of both lectures and seminar discussions helps compensate for this.\r\nIn my literature section, in particular, Prof. Lamont balanced well between discussions of themes and plotlines outside the text and close-reading of particular passages. The weekly discussion posts helped center discussions around what the students found interesting. Given Prof. Lamonts expertise in Classics, she often also addressed the cultural and historical context of works, which strengthened our understanding of the texts and ability to put them in conversation with each other.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1638110\n 1638110\n \n 1638110\n There are many moments where you can see the past informing the present in the syllabus; however, the jump to Andalusian poetry felt sudden
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1639953\n 1639953\n \n 1639953\n The strengths of DS are evident. Perhaps while the purpose and role of philosophy and h&p are also self explanatory, I wish we discussed briefly the hazy question of literature's purpose. What do we gain from reading say, Virgil. How do the authors and concept that communicate with each other apply outside the fictional universe? Also, with regard to poetry, I would much rather we focus on 2 or 3 outstanding poets, read and understand all of their works in depth than briefly skim the poems of 30 poets whose names won't register.
