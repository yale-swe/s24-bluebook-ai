\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1532261\n 1532261\n \n 1532261\n Strengths: The material was all super interesting and lots of little gems and big picture insights to be gained from engaging with the material. It raised important questions at the outset about why inequality is bad as answers to that question can potentially shape what types of inequality we find more problematic and thus determine what is most pressing to study. The course introduced me to a variety of study designs which try to measure inequality as well as theoretical frameworks to think about inequality, both of which helped expose me to different approaches which economists take to answer difficult questions, a valuable skill for someone such as myself trying to get greater exposure to such economic methods. Also, having each individuals present on papers really helped me practice reading papers and made me dive into one topic and see just how interesting it was at a level I might not have if I hadn't had to present on it.\r\nWeaknesses: Due to the grading of the course (4 psets which you only need to do 3 of and one presentation) it was easy to disengage either by not attending class or by not really spending as much time thinking about the material outside of class. This is a shame since it seemed to me that when I did really dive into the material more (either by asking questions in lecture or by the PSET questions which asked about specific results) I learned so much more. Also this grading scheme meant that we had no feedback until the last day of classes, which is pretty not ideal for knowing how you are doing. Also I think that lectures could have been more engaging, as it often felt like a sort of basic routine of running through the results of a paper in a pretty dry way. The topics are so interesting that we could (and occasionally did) have important and interesting conversations, but the dryness of lecture took away from this and made it easier to disengage.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534926\n 1534926\n \n 1534926\n Professor Attanasio is an asset to the class because of his amazing work in this field. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1534977\n 1534977\n \n 1534977\n In terms of strengths, I think the class does a good job covering economics in depth. What I mean is that Professor Attanasio tries to go into the economic models while also covering the empirical findings. I feel like he did a good job of balancing the math plus the findings. Sometimes, in other classes, professors will breeze over the math and jump straight to the findings, which I feel like is a lost opportunity. That being said, towards the second half of the semester, Professor Attanasio started explaining the models in even more depth, which I felt was very useful. The group project is also a great opportunity to meet new friends and practice reading/understanding economics research. That being said, because there was such unequal participation (some people never showed up to class), I feel like some people got stuck with partners who were just completely uninformed about what was going on. There were some people who presented who I had never seen before in the class, and I feel like that was unfair to their group partners who did attend (and got to see how the presentations should go).\r\n\r\nAlso Rosie was a great TA and very helpful with the assignments!\r\n\r\nThe main thing I would change is the flow of the assignments. All the problem sets are due at the end of the semester before reading week, and many of them were released in the 2nd half of the semester. I think the problem sets should be due at intervals throughout the semester. It also would encourage more students to meet and work together during the semester which can be very enjoyable. Plus, having feedback about grades and areas to improve would be useful.\r\n\r\nAlso, I think the class should be taught at a later time slot, if possible. It was taught at 9am, and because of this, there were only maybe 7-9 students who consistently showed up to class while there were some students who didn't attend at all. It made the class seem very unequal in terms of participation (ironic for a class of inequality), but there's technically not a participation component of our grades. Perhaps a participation component of the grade also would encourage more people to attend. Also, the fact that lecture slides are so thorough meant that a lot of students felt like they could skip and just read the slides. There was little incentive for them to come; I think most of the students who came were genuinely interested in the topic and learning.

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1533718\n 1533718\n \n 1533718\n Probably not, unless the format changes substantially. The content is interesting and the instructor is an extraordinarily talented economist. The actual problem sets were fun too, if a bit vague at times (perhaps intentionally though). The major issue for me is format. The three problem sets due by the end of the semester sounds like a great setup during shopping period, but students (myself included) inevitably procrastinate, creating a mad dash at the end of the semester. I think having the problem sets be due at set points throughout the semester would pace people's learning better and create a more organic learning culture (e.g. study groups) among students. Even if the professor insists on having the due-by-the-end-of-the-semester policy remain in place, I would strongly advise allowing students to receive grades or at least feedback on problem sets once they submit them, rather than having all feedback on them after they are all due, even if the student submits it early. For one, this would incent students to actually submit the problem sets earlier/throughout the semester. For another, students have a right to know at least a substantial portion of their grade by the Yale College midterm deadline, as is stated in the instructor handbook.\r\n\r\nI do not wish to come across too punitive -- it generally must be hard to know what a desirable course format is given what I'm sure is a capricious student body. But I have seen similar feedback for the course's format in its past iteration, and I really think these changes would allow students to profit more fully from the instructor's incredible wealth of knowledge and the course's pressing, interesting content.
