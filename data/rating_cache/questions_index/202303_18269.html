\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1705341\n 1705341\n \n 1705341\n Professor Valeggia is an engaging lecturer, passionate scholar, and a great person, period. I did love the weekly discussion boards, but they allow for everyone to provide input on the material even if they are reluctant to share in class. In summary, the course itself is strong, the smaller assignments however can be tedious.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1706275\n 1706275\n \n 1706275\n The strengths of this course are that you can really learn at your own speed. The assignment deadlines are very flexible (with the exception of the final project deadline of course), which creates a much less stressful learning environment compared to other Yale courses. You can also tell that Prof. Valeggia, as well as her TA Sakura are very invested in their students' academic and personal development. Moreover, Prof. Valeggia has done extensive research with the Qom people in Argentina, and it is a pleasure to see her passion for the research that she has conducted with indigenous populations. You can tell that Prof. Valeggia has great expertise in her field, and yet she is humble and eager to learn from her students, as well. The course also encourages students to really explore their interests and use their talents to the fullest.\r\n\r\nA slight weakness of the course was that weekly assignments were too short (usually 350-400 words) to express any nuanced opinion. I do think that learning to concentrate information is important, but the short word limit encourages regurgitation of talking points and lack of reflection. This ties into another weakness of this course: other than the really cool perspectives on cultural practices and attitudes of the Qom people, the opinions presented are often reduced to typical US narratives of progressivism are presented as "truth," while everything else should be "unlearned." I am not against teaching typical US-American progressive ideas, but I do think that they should be accompanied with more diverse perspectives. I am also all for progress, but I believe that progress can look different for different people. Having a more "conservative" perspective-- or simply one that does not fit into any clear ideological framework in the US context-- was met with derision and hostility in class discussions (exclusively by peers though, the professor was very respectful of everyone!). I think the class could be improved by providing more diverse perspectives and by focusing slightly more on evolutionary biology, rather than 2020s US-focused ideological discussions. All in all, I had a great experience, though! I want to underline that I was still able to express my own opinions and that the professor herself was very respectful of everyone's perspective.
