\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1653407\n 1653407\n \n 1653407\n The course can focus a bit more on specific policy recommendations. Would be interesting to dig deeper into how existing policies have failed to help people who believe they were being hurt by globalization, and how we can help support these people specifically.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1653841\n 1653841\n \n 1653841\n Strengths: The course covers a broad variety of topics in a short period of time and gives a strong overview of the successes and challenges of economic globalization.\r\n\r\nWeaknesses: The course is NOT structured as a debate and is NOT structured to encourage seminar-style participation. Each week, a group of 2-5 students makes a presentation on the readings assigned for that class and spends the entire class presenting their presentation. The presentation is totally useless if you have done the reading, because you already know all of the content. Professor Zedillo interrupts almost every slide (in some cases multiple times per slide) to provide his feedback, which is something that should be done privately via email or in office hours, not in class. The students then respond to the feedback/professor's questions as if they were defending a thesis, which is fine at first but gets annoying after the first dozen interruptions. Students are sometimes able to sneak questions in if they are particularly persistent.\r\n\r\nBy the way, the course involves only two days of presentations from Professor Zedillo: the very first day of class, and one class on the Doha Development Round. To be fair, Professor Zedillo's presentations are informative and he has some fun anecdotes about his experience as a Mexican minister and later president. The problem is that 90% of the course involves students presenting and the professor debating them, while the whole class watches as if it were a play in a theatre.\r\n\r\nEveryone is already required to do the readings, because you have to write weekly reflections (which, by the way, the professor refuses to provide feedback on, and it is unclear if he ever even reads them). So having to write the reflections and then watch other people present on the exact same readings for each class, for the entire class session, is a waste of time. I will admit that making the presentations is a good learning experience. But there is no need to present them to a class full of students that has already done the readings and is obviously bored.\r\n\r\nLastly: There are hundreds of pages of reading each week, much of which is very "in the weeds" (i.e. extremely detailed economic analysis of specific countries or time periods).

\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1653841\n 1653841\n \n 1653841\n I would STRONGLY advise against taking this course for the following reasons:\r\n\r\n(1) Professor Zedillo is a highly accomplished economist and statesman. No question about it. However, he only teaches in two of the ~12 sessions of the course. I would not call this "teaching."\r\n\r\n(2) Professor Zedillo refuses to provide feedback on assignments. But he also refused to provide feedback on the in-class midterm exam. When he returned the exams, his only written feedback was a number grade circled at the top of each essay booklet. One student asked how he could get feedback, and Professor Zedillo said, verbatim, "your feedback is in the grade." Then he said, "imagine that your exam were open-book. Go into your notes and readings and see what you would have written that you did not in fact write." This is a cop-out answer. I have never had any other Yale professor refuse to provide feedback (verbal or written) on a midterm exam. Now, you might say "ok, go to office hours." see point (3) below.\r\n\r\n(3) Professor Zedillo frequently ignores emails and refuses to provide office hours.
