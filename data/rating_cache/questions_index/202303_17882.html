\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1694579\n 1694579\n \n 1694579\n This course is extremely well-facilitated and encourages creative engagement with the material. The small group structure allowed me to participate fully with the readings in an intimate environment, and Prof Dembroff always took time to go through the readings with us if we asked. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1703057\n 1703057\n \n 1703057\n There are too many strengths of the course to name. I thoroughly enjoyed the focus on embodiment and bringing our full selves to class. The breath work was extremely effective as a grounding exercise to help me be present in the classroom. I really enjoyed sitting on the floor and getting rid of the overly structured classroom layout involving clunky and excessively orderly desks. Some of my classmates and I did express that, while sitting on the floor was helpful for creating a positive classroom learning environment, it could at times be uncomfortable. The bond formed in the small groups helped facilitate honest and open discussion of the course materials and their applicability to our experiences. \r\n\r\nI loved all of the activities that encouraged self-reflection. \n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1713663\n 1713663\n \n 1713663\n It was great having a small group that stayed constant throughout the whole semester. This let me deepen my relationship with others in the class and created a comfortable learning environment. I also enjoyed the different forms of learning that this course encouraged. From our deep dives into texts to small art projects, this class employed multiple strategies to improve the learning experience. The only "weakness" is that there was sometimes not enough time to discuss the wonderful texts, but that is due to the time constraints of the course and not the course itself.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1714206\n 1714206\n \n 1714206\n Early in the semester when we started each class with breathwork, I found that really helpful. We stopped doing that after awhile, and there were a couple of classes when we sat on the floor with no movement breaks, which felt drastically different from the original stated intention of the class. \r\n\r\nI appreciated that the professor attempted to create a seminar style class with a larger group, but I didn't find that was always successful. Discussion felt limited in my small group with only the perspectives of myself and two others, but without the mediation or conversational guidance of a skilled facilitator. Other times when our small group joined other small groups for conversation, nobody seemed concerned with keeping the conversation on task, and so I didn't get a lot out of our discussions. With subject matter that can be very personal, it seemed like people found it difficult to strike a balance between sharing personal stories and being more objective. It becomes difficult to communicate about ideas when feelings are involved. Additionally, the class was composed of graduate and undergraduate students. Initially, as a grad student, I was excited to hear perspectives from the younger generation, but it became clear that the gap between us all was huge, and there was little common ground. It was alienating to be one of the older students. \r\n\r\nWhile it was stated that we should move through the course forming our own ideas, it seemed to be the expectation by the end of the semester that we would all agree/align with a similar narrative, based on how conversations were shaped. A couple of students seemed to know the professor quite well, and they were vocal in expressing this shared rhetoric. When I was working on my final project, I struggled to feel comfortable sharing anything that didn't fit into the favored rhetoric of the class (that gender is an oppressive social construct). My perspective is that this has more to do with current social polarization and individual personalities than it does with the professor and the course. \r\n\r\nThrough our weekly journals, we had to be accountable to each other by completing the assignments and showing up to class. This was great in theory, because instead of completing the weekly reading and journals because it was required, I found I was more so doing it to be accountable to my small group, and to show up with coherent thoughts. A drawback to this was that one of the members of my group missed a bunch of classes, so it was difficult to exchange our journals because we had to find additional times to meet up outside of class (beyond our monthly group gatherings).\r\n\r\nI recall that one of the earlier objectives Robin stated was to integrate all waves of feminist theory. I don't think we accomplished this goal.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1715401\n 1715401\n \n 1715401\n Really interesting texts / format. I really loved my group and expect to stay connected with them in the future. I do with there was slightly more emphasis on discussing the text in class, but I do think that there was a lot of value in the different approaches to learning in this course.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1715966\n 1715966\n \n 1715966\n Similarly to what I mentioned before, one of the major strengths of the course was the approach to pedagogy. The beginning of each class started with breathing exercises to get everyone on the same page. We all lay on the floor and breathed together in order to release any tension that we may have entered the classroom with. After breathing we broke off into smaller groups to discuss questions relating to the text and our personal lives and to also deep read the text. All of these approaches felt very different from any philosophy course I have ever taken. That was very powerful of this course, to attempt something so new with such a heavy and rich history as feminism. That being said, at times I felt like that very strength was sometimes its weakness. I mostly felt this because, even though we had good conversations in almost every class, I felt like we could have gone through the text more in-depth than we did. Oftentimes I felt that questions that students would bring up regarding specific passages in the text weren't clearly answered. Not to say that the main objective of a course is to fully understand a text, however, it felt like due to the nature of the course, attempting to understand the text fell by the wayside sometimes.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1716043\n 1716043\n \n 1716043\n strength: robin's teaching style! i have never felt more present for a class
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1717880\n 1717880\n \n 1717880\n The professor was conscious of the class's position in the institutional setting and actively designed a unique learning environment that felt safe and accessible to all students. Conscious efforts were made to dismantle the typical power dynamics of the traditional classroom. As a neurodivergent student, I deeply appreciated the class's setup. Small group learning helped me feel comfortable speaking openly and honestly (and these conversations allowed me to engage with the material and my classmates more deeply), which is lost in typical seminar discussions. Letter writing assignments were effective for the same reasons. Meditation sessions every class were amazing and helped encourage mindful presence in the class (many professors tell you to be mindful/present without ever demonstrating how to do so). Weekly readings printed out were such a thoughtful gesture that also helped my engagement with the texts. This class was truly revolutionary in its approach to feminist philosophy, because we learned how to embody it in every single thing we did in class. \r\n\r\nThis is not a weakness of the class, but in my personal experience, I struggled a bit because I sometimes felt that my small group was not the best match for me. However, it was still a decent dynamic and I think most of us put our best efforts into engaging openly. I wonder if there is a way for the professor to improve the small group selection process.
