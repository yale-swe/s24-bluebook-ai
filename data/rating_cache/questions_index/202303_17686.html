Question 3 Answers

\n \n \n\n\n\n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n \n
RespondentAnswer
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1707044\n 1707044\n \n 1707044\n The workload was quite high, making it difficult to fully internalize anything.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1712841\n 1712841\n \n 1712841\n Strengths: great discussion, fantastic readings, super engaging assignments\r\nWeaknesses: A LOT of work
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1713995\n 1713995\n \n 1713995\n There are a lot of readings, so be prepared for that. The workload for this course was insane, and I found myself directing most of my energy to this course because it was also the most challenging for me. I guess that would be the main weakness: the workload (especially the readings) was so large, that at times I found it hard to meaningfully engage with the material. A writing assignment every single week, even after breaks like Thanksgiving and Fall break was also exhausting. I think fewer assignments spread a bit further apart throughout the course of the semester would've been better. I also found that a lot of the readings dealt with theories/ideas with a foundation in European art. If you don't have a solid foundation in European art, this class can be a bit difficult, especially in terms of class participation, but it's not impossible. In short, this class requires you to put in A LOT of work, but I will say it is very rewarding in the end and you come out of it feeling a lot more secure in your knowledge of art historical theory.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1716371\n 1716371\n \n 1716371\n I feel tempted to complain about the weekly writing assignments, but having to write one per week not only helped me stay in touch with the readings but also significantly improved my writing and my comfortability with writing.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1719028\n 1719028\n \n 1719028\n This class was very engaging. Sometimes the discussions for one presenter would not mesh well with the next, causing the flow of the conversation to suddenly stop, never reaching a full conclusion (or at least as close as you can get in a seminar class). Each week there seemed to be a text on theory and a text that was like a case study using that theory, but it was hard to present the case study when we were on-location in front of other interesting artwork. Maybe more engagement with the works we visit at the YUAG? Also, it may be helpful to have the opportunity to skip an essay once a semester. It would be helpful if you need to catch up on work.
\n \n Navigate to all answers for Respondent Id 1724305\n 1724305\n \n 1724305\n Under Professor Buckley, this course is an extremely thorough review of art historical methods. The readings he chose are fascinating and introduce you to a huge diversity of perspectives. Discussions were well facilitated, though it was often the same smaller group of students contributing regularly. The major problem with the course as Professor Buckley teaches it is the huge amount of work compared to when the course is taught by other professors. Weekly, you are expected to do ~70 pages of reading and then write a three page short essay. Im a little unconvinced that the weekly three-pagers produced much greater reflection than something like a weekly discussion post would, especially because it gives you less time to focus purely on the readings.\r\n\r\nMy objection is less to the workload itself than to the large disparities between the workloads of the class as taught by different members of the faculty. Given that HSAR 401 is a requirement for the art history major, I really think the amount of work should be standardized between semesters. As it is now, its unfair towards some students and produces different levels of understanding between class years. I think standardizing the workload can be balanced with still giving professors a lot of freedom in designing how they would like to teach it.
