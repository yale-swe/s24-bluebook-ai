2020-Fall FREN 170 02 Intro Study of French Lit

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 12047 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 The lessons were extremely informative and very well-structured. I always enjoyed the discussions that followed.
 The selection of literature was fantastic. We covered many of the heavy-hitters but also covered works and authors I wasn't expecting us to cover, which was refreshing. The reading questions and discussions were geared well to help me understand the texts. I also really liked the essay prompts and presentation assignments, which struck the right balance between open-endedness and limitation. The comments on our writing and the ability to turn in revisions of our work were also excellent, as well as the personal meetings on the second essay. Readings were sometimes quite long, and I noticed that the energy of some discussions suffered from our lacking time to focus on the passages in detail.
 Strengths: Professor Girard is brilliant, hands down. She knows so much about French, French literature, French art, and she so passionately imbues that into her students. Each and every class she had a PowerPoint which was accompanied by a brilliant lecture. She had so much to say that it would often bleed over after the designated conclusion of the class; while for some that would be a negative thing, it was just further evidence of how much she loved teaching the material. The assignments are incredibly manageable, and aside from a couple of lengthier readings (like Balzac's Le colonel Chabert) the reading load was never too hefty; she also made every reading electronically accessible via PDF, so no purchases were necessary whatsoever. Her feedback was incredible, too. I've never received so much feedback on an essay, and she was more than happy to sit and talk for hours at length about your writing and its various nuances, in addition to the content of the class. While this class had much less discussion that I had expected, it's a quirk of the class. You get to hear from the best of the best, and just listen and soak it all in. There are a few times where you can speak, and of course you can ask questions. But no pressure if you're a little shy when it comes to speaking. Classes were recorded, too, so if you had to miss class it was okay! She gave feedback on exposs, too -- not only on style but also on grammar. Maybe some will say she's a little blunt, but mistakes are mistakes. This is the "langue de Molire" after all, so we should be speaking and writing it properly. I'd never heard of some of the authors we read, and they were incredible. Alfred Jarry's Ubu Roi is a great addition/complement to the Trump era of politics, and Le colonel Chabert is a great reading to just think about life, status. The philosophical texts chosen, like Micromgas, or Rousseau, were actually really interesting. Tartuffe was a really great play, and while it might not be Molire's best, it was a joy to read. The more modern work was equally as enjoyable to read, like Perec and Houellebecq. Maybe there should be a few more women included on the syllabus, and it would be nice to read more full-length novels or novellas, as most of the class is drawn upon excerpts (save for Balzac, Flaubert, Molire) and I'd have loved to have gotten to read longer works of the French canon, but this, again, was a fantastic survey. Weaknesses: Technology is certainly a hurdle from Professor Girard. I feel like I know her husband as much as I know her, because he came to class and helped her with tech issues. However, this isn't really that much of a weakness at all, because none of the tech issues really impeded the learning experience. I will say, however, that the lack of discussion provided little incentive for some members of the class to be particularly engaged in the class at all, either for lack of comprehension or some other reason, but there's no way for me to really tell. Lectures could go on and on, bleeding into the next class period for some, so if that's a concern, make sure you speak up and let her know the time. Sometimes we'd even go 25 minutes over (but it's worth it!) so just be cautious. The first two essays are prompt-driven, while the final essay is open-ended; I think I would've preferred a similar set of prompts rather than having to come up with something on my own. And the expos seems a little pointless, unless it's just intended for speaking practice; it was on any topic of your choosing related to French culture, so it didn't even need to be tangentially related to French literature, which was a little surprising to me.
