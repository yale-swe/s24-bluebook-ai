2020-Fall MENG 361 01 MechEng II: Fluid Mechanics

Q:

Among the practices in this course geared toward learning during the COVID-19 pandemic, which succeeded and which could be improved? Please comment on course practices that allowed for virtual engagement.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC602 CRN: 11161 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 Having pre-recorded lectures was an extremely good idea. Learning was at our time table which was great because we were not going to class in person. A split classroom is supposed to cover just practice problems in person, so doing the same lecture twice was a bad idea.
 Pre-recorded videos and lecture slides helped a lot.
 The live zoom lectures were great, especially when the professor included videos and images related to the topics we were learning about. There were also a lot of office hours to accommodate questions and homework help during the week. More resources for exam prep would've been helpful.
 The way this class was adapted for working remotely didn't really work for me. Prof. Smooke would upload videos of him explaining the lecture slides in advance, but would go over the same exact content in class almost word for word, with the only addition being that people could ask questions. Since it was the same exact lecture as his pre-recorded lectures, I was not motivated to watch the pre-recorded videos. The problem was that since he expected us to be watching the lectures in advance with the ability to pause and write notes, he had license to speed through pages of derivations as quickly as he could speak them and finish lecture in like 20 mins. I've previously had Prof. Smooke for another class in person and students' saving grace was that he would be slowed down by his own writing (which is still blazing fast). I know most of the issue I had with his teaching style this semester was my own fault because I refused to watch the same lecture twice, but I feel like I would have benefited from him slowing down in the live zoom lectures and writing out his notes on an iPad or writing tablet instead of using slides and only a fraction of the class time.
 Professor Smooke pre-recorded all of his lecture videos and wanted us to watch the pre-recorded videos, then come to class and essentially watch the same lecture. Towards the end of class, I feel like the in-class lectures were more example-based and focused on real life applications, which was great, but at the beginning of the class we would just be watching him do the same derivation that he had us watch a pre-recorded lecture of him doing. I think having the prerecorded side focused on proofs/derivations and the in-person lectures focused on example problems would be much more useful.
 The live virtual lectures, office hours and pre-recorded lectures worked. It would have been helpful to also have discussion sections where we could go over problems using the Zoom whiteboard, step by step. While I think the format of the class worked, having the opportunities to see problems worked through on the Zoom whiteboard, similar to a chalkboard in person, could have been really helpful.
 We had recorded lectures and then had the same lectures live over zoom to ask questions and get the information solidified, which I found very useful. Taking exams if front of a camera live over zoom was not the best though.
 I appreciated that we had both lectures and recorded videos. I think it was good to be able to watch the recorded videos, which were concise.
 Prerecorded lecture before live lecture with questions was a good format. Would have liked to engage with Professor Smooke more in an office hours setting but he didn't offer any
 Similar to what I said in my last answer. If he taught the same information in the 75-minute window, it would be easier to keep students engaged. I think the combination of a full 75-minute lecture and recording the classes would be just as time-efficient for Professor Smooke (I think they would render the pre-recorded modules unnecessary and he could just post the slides from the lectures if he wanted to).
 The pre-recorded videos were great because it allowed me to go at my own pace and take detailed notes before class. Then in class, I could focus on hearing the information again, digesting it more, and asking any questions.
 Prof Smooke had prerecorded lectures with the exact same material as in lecture, so it allowed students to take notes or prepare questions before class. This was an excellent idea and should continue to be done
 Having the lectures recorded ahead of time, then also having Smooke redeliver them was a great way to learn the material.
 Again, adding videos and history snippets into lecture was huge. It's tough to stay motivated and interested when you're on zoom half the day, and these provided enough spice to lecture to make it really enjoyable. Prof Smooke recorded lectures before class that were the same as the live portion, which I would normally be frustrated with but enjoyed for these reasons: First, fluids is HARD, so seeing the topics twice is more or less necessary, and the second time round you can ask questions during lecture. Second, lecture usually ended before the scheduled time for the course, which, even though we covered a lot of material in that time, gave us a feeling that we were getting out early. Plus, you're not watching the clock for the end of lecture, you're simply present the whole time.
 Recorded lectures followed by live ones were very helpful
 Yes. The combination of prerecorded and live lectures helped
 The well made slides and pre-recorded videos explaining the lecture were very helpful. The virtual lectures were great as well and having the notes available to us a few days before lecture allowed us to take notes before so we weren't rushing to take notes during class, which can be difficult with online lectures and powerpoints.
 Regular synchronous meetings were incredibly helpful. Even if I didn't ask questions, these live meetings held me accountable and added some dynamic to the class.
 The format could be improved if this course is offered again virtually. The pre-recorded lectures and the live sessions had exactly the same material. although after the first few weeks Prof. Smooke started introducing more fun videos and example problems in the live lecture. The problem is that the live session goes way too quick to take meaningful notes, which means you need to watch the pre-recorded lecture to have all the equations and stuff written down. But once you do that, most of the live session becomes redundant because you're just staring at slides and listening to explanations you've already covered. Would be better if live lectures were not just presentations, but included hand written derivations, which allow you to take notes and engage in real time. Would also be helpful to gear the live sessions more toward solving problems and applications of the lecture material. Most of us really loved the videos and stories Prof. Smooke shared and they really contributed to our enjoyment and understanding of the material.
 The large number of office hours were very helpful with allowing me to always have access to help. The asynchronous recorded lectures made reviewing class content very easy which facilitated studying
 Taking advantage of the asynchronous portion and having many many TAs and ULAs was extremely helpful
 This course was taught as a flipped classroom, meaning that we had pre-recorded lectures to watch before class and class time was given over to a quick recap of the recordings and answers to conceptual questions. I found this course format easier to work within and better for my learning style. The plenitude of TA office hours were also quite helpful.
