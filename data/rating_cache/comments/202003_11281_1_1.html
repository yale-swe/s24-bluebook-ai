2020-Fall E&EB 654 01/E&EB 354 01 Phylogenetic Biology

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401 CRN: 11293/11281 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 I became excellently versed in the methodology and theory behind phylogenetics, which as a field is becoming more and more important to our modern understanding of biology. This class was excellently designed to get students to practice and play around with phylogenetic tools and software. Class discussions were focused on the interpretation of the mathematics and theory behind the methodologies and conclusions of phylogenetics. Our class paper presentations allowed us to really pick apart scientific literature, as did the final project. For those shopping this course, here's how it worked: Tuesday-Class discussion around the subject of interest for the week. This could either be a methodology (i.e., Bayesian analysis) or some aspect of the theory behind phylogenetics (e.g., incomplete lineage sorting). Thursday-In-class practice/problem solving. In these classes, we would start the homework in class and work through parts of it together so that all students really started to get a sense of the methods to be applied. Since the final project essentially took the entirety of the semester to complete, these practice sets were perfectly sized. Tuesday/Thursday-paper presentations. Students would lead discussions of papers they selected from the literature. This was my favorite section because it demonstrated the relevance of the material we were learning in class to the recent literature.
 I learned a lot about phylogenetic biology methods and theory, as well as how to use software to build and interpret phylogenetic trees.
 I learned so much, not just about phylogenetics, but about molecular evolution, R and Git, how to be a computational biologist, math, and even graph theory(?!) I developed so many practical and theoretical skills: I can now build my own phylogeny from molecular data, use the command line, and understand all the methods sections in phylogenetics papers that had previously mystified me. I also got tons better at coding for this class, which had a big impact on my ability to research.
 A solid basic understanding of phylogenetic methods, maximum likelihood, bayesian inference, comparative methods etc. Working knowledge of R and cluster.
 I built strong technical skills, particularly in R programming and using the cluster. I also gained knowledge of general phylogenetic biology, as well as specific case studies through student presentations. This course was not only a thorough introduction to the field of phylogenetics, but also provided more advanced material.
 A understanding of the theory behind phylogenetic, as well as on overview of what can be done with phyhlogenetics. Basic applications of computational phylogenetic tools.
 I learned a lot about the methods and software used in phylogenetics, and some more of the theory behind phylogenetics. This class was geared towards preparing people for actively doing research using phylogenetic methods.
