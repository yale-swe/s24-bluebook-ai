 No, I would never recommend this course to another student. The course readings are okay, but Christakis' lectures do not do much to add upon the readings, rather he usually just goes over them. Christakis himself is also unapproachable and not open to new ideas. He is extremely stuck in his own opinions. In a course that is meant to evaluate and discuss the impact of the role of structures like race and gender on public health, it is disappointing that Christakis fails to realize that as a white male he might have privileges and biases that he simply does not bother to question. This course has also much more busywork than it is worth, with two six-page papers as the final exam, which are assigned and released only a week before they are due. Some of the readings throughout the semester are interesting, but it is a lot of scientific studies that are difficult to comb through. This work might be worth it if the course was more well taught, but unfortunately this is not the case.

 Overall, yes, I would. Christakis is an amazing lecturer, and the material is very useful to pre-meds. However, I do have some issues with the course, although I think these will probably go away with the return to normal classes and exams. The switch to open-note exams, while usually beneficial to students, felt detrimental to me. I studied using all the previous exams, and this worked for the first exam, which was most similar to the style of past exams. But after the first midterm, it became apparent that allowing exams to be open-note also warranted a change in the kind of question being asked. Between this and not getting much feedback on exams, I felt like the course actually became harder than it would have been during a normal semester, and I would have preferred exams that were more similar to those of the past and closed-notes, rather than the second midterm we had where the questions became harder. I was left feeling like I understood the material very well, and I studied a lot, but because of the new exam style, I was getting lower grades than I deserved/earned in order to create a more significant grade distribution; my studying and understanding did not feel like it was not being reflected in my grade, which was incredibly frustrating. At the very least, providing more feedback would help improve this, but, as previously mentioned, I also think this issue could go away with the return to the normal exam style.
 I feel like most of the material we covered was fairly obvious, but maybe that's just a consequence of living through a major public health event. With that being said, it was nice to go through the material structured manner, and I found the course's emphasis on guiding concepts such as structure vs. agency helpful in framing what we learned. Take this class if you want to read some interesting papers and learn about public health in a classroom setting, but don't expect to have your mind blown every lecture.
