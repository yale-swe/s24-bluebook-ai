 The structure of the class was great, I felt that the pace was good.
 The teaching team for this course was the biggest plus. There were a total of over 50 office hours each week (this was a 5 week summer class) between prof JDRS and the 6 or so TFs for our class of around 60-70. They were extremely smart and always had a way to help me with homework, logistics, and general content questions. Prof JDRS was also super enthusiastic at all times and I could tell he was passionate to teach us - there were always inside-jokes he would make to keep us entertained. The fact that it is half coding/half stats made it very capable for being taught online - using RStudio (a coding program) for basically all work we did made the class inherently more intuitive, eg we could follow along the lecture code line-by-line in our own program window and execute commands on our own. The class overall had a TON of content, which isn't inherently a bad thing. The condensed timeline, though, made this a bit more challenging. The pacing was basically where every weekday was about the same as a normal T/Th class on campus, meaning we would go through about 2 full weeks of on-campus learning in only 5 days. We had homework assignments due about every 2-3 days that took about 5-8 hours each for me, which meant I was working pretty constantly between studying, watching lectures, doing homework, and going to office hours. I wouldn't do this course with another one at the same time. The fast pace also meant that grading would sometimes fall behind - something I wish could be better. Sometimes we wouldn't get grades and feedback back until the night before an exam. Lastly, the information in lectures is pretty dense, so it sometimes is unclear what is important and what is not, but it easily becomes apparent in the homework so you can always go back into the lecture code and look at what you need. Although learning to code in R often made the class more exciting that it would have been otherwise (I felt like a Hackerman(TM) ), coding can also be a pain. More than once I found myself staring at a line or two of code that would work for half an hour before going to zoom office hours where a TF fixed my error due to a basic mistake in less than 10 seconds. I guess that is more of an endorsements of going to office hours though, haha.
