Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 The course is really strong in its course material, but its homeworks are lacking. The undergrad ULA is amazing and incredibly smart, but there should be absolutely zero classes at Yale with an ULA making a problem set. Either the grad TA should be making them or the professor herself. Problem sets are long and they change two days before the due date, even though they take an average student probably 20-30 hours to complete. I cannot emphasize enough how much the psets detract from the rest of the course. Psets are terribly long and not optimized for learning, in the sense that much of it is going back and forth in a jupyter notebook trying to interpret what the pset is even trying to say. Instructions are vague, clarifications are desperately needed and often given 1-2 days after questions have been asked, and in general the psets give the sense that whoever made them doesn't really care about the students.
 This course had a perfect amount of material, and the content was delivered in a nice order that built upon each different step. I think that to take it to the next level, it would be nice to see more live demonstrations of the techniques, or more examples included it in the slides. Also, sometimes the math notation was confusing, and so introducing some of the symbols (specifically the more complex probability related ones) might be helpful for students who dont have that background.
 Smita's lectures are very strong and I learned a ton of interesting content. That said, I have a relatively surface-level understanding of many topics thanks to the extremely wide range of material covered by the course. Reducing the breadth of the class would help students understand concepts in greater depth. Also, the problem sets were monstrously long. While this isn't terrible in and of itself, it's unrealistic that Smita wants students to completely them *and* do the supplemental readings posted for each class.
 Topics are interesting, but prof could do a better job explaining
 The problem sets did solidify understanding by providing concrete examples of what we were working on. Some of them were difficult to understand, though, and needed lots of clarification from Piazza. They also were often very tedious and I felt like I sometimes spent more time figuring out library syntax than learning.
 The strengths of this class are the problem sets. The problem sets are very detailed and allow you to learn how to apply the material presented in lecture. Also, the papers you can read are very good at explaining things that are unclear during lecture. Another strength is that we cover a lot of exciting material and techniques in the class which are extremely applicable to a lot of research topics. Another strength is that the final project in the class allows you to explore whatever material you want, and present it in the form of an ML research paper. Extremely cool, and a lot different than assignments in a usual CS class. The weakness of this class is definitely that the second problem set was extremely long and taxing, with not a lot of knowledge gained. I would definitely have preferred a problem set on CNN's or RNN's because that was the coolest topic and the coverage of it was rushed at the end of the semester.
 Strengths: So many modern techniques that are super cool. Professor Krishnaswamy is also a GOAT. Also, the TF and ULA worked their butt off to give everyone individual customized feedback, which is pretty nuts for a ML assignment. Very rarely do I find a course where it feels like the instructor and teaching staff put in more work into the course than their students, but this is one of those courses, and it really shows in the learning experience imo. Weaknesses: it's a lot of work
 Problem sets were way too long! Having only 3 with parts of them having errors and / or not being covered in class until nearer the due date made it difficult to plan consistent work; splitting them up into more problem sets that are smaller would be much better.
