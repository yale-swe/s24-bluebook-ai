2021-Spring EDST 205 01 Effective Classroom Teaching

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 24775 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 Strengths: getting paired with a secondary school teacher as a mentor (and while it is virtual, how well Melissa matched the teacher with the student's interests/background), how Melissa organized the class itself as an embodiment of an effective classroom (in a cool meta way), how well organized each class was (literally timed to the minute), the low weighting of each assignment so no assignment was particularly stressful, the ability to revise any assignment (which also relieved stress), the spoon policy (allowing you to miss one class or turn in an assignment late no questions asked), the case study (and the creativity involved), the teaching segments (it was especially informative getting to make your own lesson plans), the extensive feedback Melissa provides on each assignment, experimenting with platforms teachers actually use for weekly reading responses, the classroom discussions, mix of readings and videos/podcasts as class prep materials,...literally the whole class is a strength. No weaknesses come to mind!
 The biggest strength of the course is definitely the case study, which involved working with an assigned teacher partner throughout the semester and interviewing them regarding the principles of effective teaching. We also had to observe our teacher partner's classroom. This was IMMENSELY helpful because it allowed us to connect these very theoretical principles to real life teacher's pedagogical practices. The course excels at margining the theoretical with the practical in a way that no other course I've taken ever has. Another huge strength of the course is the extent to which it is student-led. Oftentimes we are given options about which activities we want to do (so going to different breakout rooms for people interested in having a discussion about the readings vs. those who want to actually apply the readings to a particular case study). Professor Scheve also always took our feedback seriously and adapted the course as we went along to best met the needs of students. Thus, another strength is the extent to which the course was student-centered.
 Prof Scheve is incredible -- she truly practices the effective teaching practices she teaches. The class is well-structured but flexible to student needs, with a doable and meaningful workload, timely and thoughtful feedback. With a focus on trust and conversation, the class is more than just a collection of students, but a true learning community. Prof Scheve did the almost impossible and managed to make a two-hour Zoom seminar engaging, rigorous, and comfortable.
 Professor Scheve is absolutely amazing. She teaches by example and demonstrates exactly what it looks like to be an effective educator. The only weakness was not meeting more often! This is the one class I would have gladly taken on more time and work for, because it was genuinely a pleasure to participate in.
 1. The instructor is phenomenal. Melissa is very receptive to feedback and very supportive. She leads classes extraordinarily well, offers time for students to turn off videos and rest, creates time for breaks and room for people to share feelings. There were numerous classes where she spent the beginning of class acknowledging that we are human and affected by outside events and likely grieving at many points in the semester. She cares about you--about us--as humans before students. When someone offered the idea of the spoons theory, she was very on-board with the idea and said that if there was ever any day that we were just not feeling okay, we could request an extension on an assignment or skip class with no justification needed. Her feedback is often extensive and useful, and she's always very open to meeting with students--about anything from "I have no clue how to be a teacher" to "what am I doing for this assignment" to "I need someone to talk to" 2. The readings are focused. There aren't many readings and the readings aren't that long (or you can get the main points of the readings from the summaries/conclusions), so you really hone in on a few ideas each week. During discussion, we'd push those ideas further and/or put those ideas into practice, which made me feel like I was learning something every week. 3. There aren't many assignments, so you can really focus on those few assignments and think about the main ideas of the readings and how you can put them into practice. 4. The class really came together as a community which made discussions flow very smoothly and people felt comfortable reflecting and sharing vulnerable thoughts.
 Strengths: genuinely interesting readings, discussion posts, and class plans & materials Weaknesses: there simply wasn't enough time to do all that we wanted to do; wish there was more discussion time with classmates
 This course was very strong. The biggest strength was the teacher partner pairing. I think one of the best parts of the class was getting to interview a real high school teacher and gain perspective and context for the readings we were doing. That really solidified why what we were learning was important. One potential weakness of this class is that it was really catering to the digital format and may be hard to replicate post-pandemic when everyone returns to in-person instruction.
 This course was EXTREMELY well-organizedat the beginning of every class, Professor Scheve shared a Google Doc with an agenda for the day. Not only was it helpful to see the agenda each day, the agenda itself always included a variety of modes of learningwhole-group discussions, breakout rooms, watching videos, breaks, time with our cameras offwhich meant that there was never a dull moment and I was always completely engaged even over Zoom, which is a feat! The course had the strongest sense of community I've ever felt in a Yale course. Readings and discussions were lively and fascinating. This course not only modeled the way I want to lead my classroom as a future high school teacher, but also the way that every college professor should ideally conduct their courses.
