 Not at all. Individually, the professors are amazing. They are experts in their fields and want students to dive into their respected sections. Together, this course seemed to have two components: lectures, where professors taught us about fascinating "history of life" concepts, and assignments, that had to do with concepts within the course, but were horribly written out, explained, and assessed harshly. Multiple times, instructors said "the next part is much better and you'll get better grade," to which many students still performed badly. Unless you're willing to commit multiple days to exams and the essay, to which you write massive essays per question that you must do conclusive research on, you won't be geared for success in this course, even if you focus in all lectures and ask questions. Feedback on every assignment was insufficient and no sense of guidance existed for students who performed poorly. Do not take this course unless you're willing to commit an extreme amount of time to its assignments and still perform poorly, while having instructors who expect you to be an expert in their field in four weeks. All in all, it is impossible to know your grade and true understanding of the concepts because grades aren't all shared and assessments do not reflect taught material.

The ideal student for this course would be one that is interested in some aspect of paleontology/fossils/dinosaurs/evolution and is seeking a relatively low workload credit (although the essay is the one assignment of the semester that requires a good amount of time).
