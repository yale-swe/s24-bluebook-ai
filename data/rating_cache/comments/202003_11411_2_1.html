Â A major strength of this course was the small number of students. Anyone who wanted to contribute to the discussion had the opportunity to. Another strength was the selection of interesting essays from a diverse array of fields. The professor also gave great feedback, and held regular office hours that I found very helpful. There were very few course weaknesses, and the weaknesses that I did note seemed to be a result of the changes to the learning environment that COVID-19 has precipitated. In particular, because I learn better when I write things down, I feel like there could have been more written activities. I also wish that the creative writing unit, which existed in previous semesters but was cut this semester, had been retained. However, I recognize that time constraints prevented this.

Â Strengths: 1) There is an excellent structure that guides students through the learning, with each stage and each assignment guiding the student along the process of how to write an academic essay. The deadlines for the course are also well spaced-out, with nothing feeling too rushed and the work evenly balanced. 2) The earlier assignments have a far lower weight than the later ones, allowing students' grades to reflect the feedback they've incorporated and skills they've picked up as the course progressed. 3) Professor Newton. The best thing about this class, she took no time at all to get to know the student and understand the specific guidance and input that each individual's writing required. Her feedback on essays is also particularly detailed and helpful. 4) The class is divided into multiple units, each based on a different theme. This allows students to understand multiple features and aspects of cities. 5) The size of the class was just right: we were able to interact with each individual at length on at least one occasion and get a sense of each other's strengths and weaknesses as writers. Through peer-reviewing others' papers and watching creative project presentations, we were given a brief but meaningful understanding of many urban issues we might not otherwise have heard about. Weaknesses 1) Class discussions can occasionally become too centred around American cities, particularly New York. 2) There could be a slightly wider range of ideologies and perspectives reflected in the assigned readings on cities. While I happened to agree with most of the authors that we were introduced to, it would have been interesting also to be exposed to opposite views.
