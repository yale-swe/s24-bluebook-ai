Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 10958/11028/11029 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 Strengths: Shelly is an incredible teacher, and nobody has pushed me to critically evaluate my own moral beliefs like he has. Further, Shelly leaves incredibly detailed feedback for essays. Weaknesses: Seminars were often dominated by the same 2 or 3 people, oftentimes turning the class into a type of back-and-forth Q&A session between those people, or between other students who would jump in and those same 2-3 people. This made it somewhat uncomfortable for those of us who were not in that group to jump in. Shelly did this once or twice, but it would have been nice if he told those who'd been dominating the conversation to be more conscientious of, giving more students a chance to contribute instead of allowing the same group to dominate our conversations, especially when all of our participation grade comes from our seminars. I also think the "jump into the discussion" approach, while I understand where it comes from, is not optimal for zoom. I know Shelly is opposed to this, but I genuinely think raising hands for when we want to speak would improve the discussion aspect of this course. I also thought that having an essay due during election week, without any adjustments to accommodate students' wellbeing, was not the most effective decision. For those of us who are members of marginalized communities and for whom politics is more than just a debate/conversation, this was a legitimately stress-inducing time. Coupled with the fact that we are in a literal pandemic, I felt like even slightly adjusting the due date would have greatly helped us.
 On Grading: it's harsh but fair. I don't think you'll be super ~surprised by any grade you get. But yes those bad ones can come as a shock for sure. Make appointments with him. If your ideas get totally destroyed in the first one, book another appointment with him. If your argument is "approved of" by him, you'll do fine. Besides, you'll also get the best feedback you could ever hope for in a class. He wrote me 2100 words of feedback on my 2200 word paper. In terms of weaknesses, the flowing discussion in seminars is nice some of the time, but it's also very susceptible to being taken advantage of. Some people will certainly talk a little bit too much because of the way the conversation is totally open. I don't know if the drawbacks outweigh the strengths of the "talk at will" approach, but there certainly ARE drawbacks.
 Improve by: actually discussing the secondary readings.
 Shelly's feedback is incredibly deep and engages with your idea, actually seriously responding to the paper and your arguments. Discussions touched on a wide variety of topics most of which were interesting, but at the same time sometimes those discussions felt a little bit too surface level and could have benefited from everyone more carefully reflecting beforehand.
 Excellent seminar discussion format led by a capable and engaged Professor Kagan.
 This class works very well as a seminar. However, I wish there was more engagement and collaboration among students outside of the seminar. Of course, some of that would happen naturally in-person, but the instructor might consider including assignments that are more interactive than readings and essays. For example, Perusall has worked well in another class of mine as an alternative to solitary reading.
 I really liked the critical and engaging discursive style of the course, and how driven by student contributions it was. That said, the fact that participation was a large portion of the grade, along with the large number of students in the class, meant that there were always multiple people jumping in very quickly after someone would stop talking. I found this very difficult personally, as a person not comfortable talking over others. The result was that, although our discussions were scintillating and engaging, the small group of people who were more assertive in starting to talk ended up talking much more than others who were less comfortable talking over others. I think this class could be improved by a little more structure to guide the discussion - maybe Shelly could call on people so that those who aren't as comfortable talking over others would still be able to comfortably contribute.
 Discussion is amazing. Perhaps to favor one person less would have opened the discussion more; but then again, they favored themselves.
 Strengths: clarity of explanations, a lot of feedback. Weaknesses: pressure to speak in seminar led to common subpar contributions, priority of breadth of subject matter sometimes led to unfortunate deprioritization of depth.
 Shelly assigns interesting readings that dont take years to comb through, as they display the same clarity hes looking for in a student paper. Relatively short in comparison with many other classes at Yale, in my opinion theyre the perfect length to keep all students engaged throughout the semester, which in turn helps facilitate a strong seminar discussion. More often that not, students previously unfamiliar with complex philosophical concepts came to class with an understanding of them rare to see people gain so quickly an undergraduate course.
