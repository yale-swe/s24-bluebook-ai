2020-Summer ANTH S473 01/ARCG S473 01/NELC S473 01/EVST S473 01 Climate Change

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

ID: SU100 CRN: 30066/30067/30068/30065 Term: 202002

123Forward
 Nature of societal collapse and its causes.
 The course introduced me to a field I knew nothing about: anthropology and climate change. Through the course, I feel that I learned multiple classes worth of material while also seeing how Harvey Weiss, a professional in the field, approached his work.
 -An excellent understanding of current debates surrounding the causes of societal collapse -Where societal collapse occurs in the archaeological record -Correspondences between the paleoclimatological record of abrupt climate shifts and societal change
 In this course, we studied the various paleoclimate records that historians use to track climate in the past. We learned to read textbooks critically and analyze the arguments that historians have made about different collapse events across history.
 I had no understanding of anything that was taught in this class prior to taking it, and thus everything I learnt was new to me. The connection between climate events and ancient collapse events was fascinating.
 I learned a great deal about historical collapses and civilizations and how all of this relates to natural and anthropogenic climate change. I learned to critically analyze data and develop my own conclusions about why societies collapsed.
 The factors contributing to ancient society collapses and what makes societies fragile. Understanding collapse usually does not involve one cause. Helps reflect on modern day -- are we declining and heading toward societal collapse?
 This course gave me comprehensive knowledge of climatological change collapse events throughout human history. We looked at multiple events from a variety of scholarly perspectives, and I was surprised by the number of different conclusions that can be drawn from the same data about the same collapse event.
 I learned about the tools (paleoclimate proxies) that archaeologists use to determine rainfall and other climatic conditions during the past. I also learned about the perspectives of different authors regarding collapse, defining collapse, and the various factors that can contribute to collapse.
 This course covers the history of ancient societal collapses: the theories for why they occurred, and the archaeological and paleoclimate evidence that exists to support those theories. The material covered gives you the ability to analyze collapse theories critically, and has interesting implications on the future of anthropogenic climate change.
 I learned about 5 paleoclimate proxies and what they can tell us about 7 major societal collapses. It was difficult to read the articles at first, but with practice I was able to extract the relevant information from the scientific data. The final essay, a two-page synopsis of the entire course, was challenging. It was a great exercise in concision and deciding what to include and what to leave out.
 I was never well informed in empires and throughout this course it taught me many things that I didnt know before abut societal collapse and collapse of empires. The discussions we have in class has helped developed my critical thinking and gives me a different perceptive on the reason these empires failed. I also got to learn that many empires during this time their reason for their collapses was a sustained drought throughout that area and it wasnt adaptable so they ended up habitat tracking to different settlements.
 A deeper understanding of ancient civilizations and the reasons for their collapse, as well as research skills.
 I learned about the impacts of climate change on societies in the past, and the implications of droughts on collapse or resilience, especially in areas that relied on rain-fed agriculture. This is especially applicable today, where anthropogenic causes of climate change can have similar effects on communities. I enjoyed learning about different societies (from Mesopotamia to the Mayans to modern ones such as India under British colonialism). I also appreciated the multi-disciplanary approach to climate changewe discussed archeology, environmental science, humanities, social theory, and history.
 Through this course, I was able to understand the quantifiable aspects of climate change better, as well as its implications on the collapse of certain developed societies. I was also able to analyze a number of different theories regarding collapse and assess the viability of each based on the paleoclimate data that has been found in the past century.
