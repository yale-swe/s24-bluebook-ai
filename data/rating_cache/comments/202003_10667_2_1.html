2020-Fall RUSS 125 01 Intensive Elementary Russian

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 10667 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 Lots of work and very quick pace - ultimately rewarding but know what you are getting yourself into
 I think most of the weakness lies in the textbook layout, but additionally I wish learning the material and verbal exercise emphasis could be more synonymous. But the strengths of the course are literally everything else!
 The class was very engaging--I found myself somewhat unfocused due to the Zoom format in all of my classes except this one. Every student participated in every class. The teachers and professors were also highly involved in our learning process, and they were very open to taking questions and meeting with students. I was impressed by how much Russian I was able to learn in this relatively short period of time. I though the class was bit disorganized, although this certainly improved as term went on. I didn't always get feedback on the assignments I turned in, and I felt that could have improved my skills a bit.
 The strengths of the courses were definitely the amount of material we were able to cover in such a short amount of time. The only weakness I can think of would be that the course sometimes felt a bit chaotic in terms of the schedule and the modules tab on Canvas.
 The strength of this course is the intensity and timeliness in which content is presented. The instructors are very thorough and each have a different style of teaching. I felt that the level of engagement and the personal attention given to each student by the instructors created a safe environment to speak freely (especially if mistakes were made) in order to correct them quickly and efficiently. In terms of improvement, I believe it would be best to require students to compile a "master document" for the cases in order to memorize and distinguish each more efficiently.
 Strengths: Professor Muravnik and Ilia! Both of them were very good at explaining difficult concepts and were always willing to give extra help. I think they did a really good job of helping us build a foundation in Russian that we can later build on, rather than just having us memorize vocabulary and conjugations. Weaknesses: It wasn't always clear what material would be on our quizzes.
 This class has a ton of strengths. For starters, Constantine is an absolute gem of a professor. He epitomizes what any language professor should be: his knowledge is vast and far-reaching, and he really, really enjoys teaching. He also wants to get to know his students, and while it was certainly difficult to do that especially in an online format, he tried his best, and I really appreciated that. He made himself available and was very responsive to our feedback. I say all this to preface the fact that this is perhaps one of the most important parts of a language learning environment: comradery, but also overall cohesiveness among the class, something at which this course excels. The way the course is designed forces you to get to know the students with whom you are taking it, especially through conversation and speaking exercises in class. Outside of simply the class environment, the speed is perfect for learning. Sure, there were bumps along the way where it felt like we jumped from topic to topic, but I don't think I'd have had it any other way. It's truly a privilege to be able to take an intensive language course, especially with a language like Russian, and you are getting top-notch instruction from one of the best professors at this university. The grammar is hard, but Constantine knows how to teach it. However, that is not to say that there aren't any weaknesses, and I wish previous students had written more about their experience in the class. Perhaps it was different due to the class being on Zoom, but I think this class works better with a smaller class size. There was what seemed like a record number of students interested in taking this class, and so it was split into two sections; mine, with Constantine, had anywhere from 10-12 students. Minjin's section has 4-5. Because of that, it was difficult to receive the type of individualized attention you need from a language class, already made harder by the Zoom format. Also, the textbook is good in some respects, but really, it's absolute garbage. The videos are incredibly outdated and frankly irrelevant to classroom learning. Constantine and Ilia had us implement them into class in the second half, but they served no purpose. The textbook's method of teaching vocabulary is absolute trash, too. The vocab lists at the end of the units are way too long, and the method of teaching them without any clear progression makes it impossible for any student to acquire the skills necessary to not only memorize the words, but also use them correctly. There was no implementation of learning the vocabulary actively in class, and the textbook homework and examples didn't do a great job of reinforcing the new words, so at the time of this writing, I've certainly lost many of the words I had already known and learned. Speaking is also a problem; the final exam was switched to an oral exam, which is perhaps the weakest skill we had worked on throughout the semester, so that made it a pretty daunting task. Another of the biggest issues we encountered was typing and learning the Russian keyboard; one cannot simply learn the Russian alphabet and then be expected to type flawlessly in a matter of weeks. And this compounded my final weakness of this course, the online exams. Language learning thrives on tactile experience; we're already at a disadvantage because we are on Zoom and everything is digitized. There was an attempt to switch homework to an online format too, which was the worst idea broached throughout the entire semester. We're tired of technology, and having to do homework on Canvas would've made it worse than it already was. There needs to be more handwritten tasks incorporated, regardless of whether it's on Zoom or in a classroom. And the exams: they did not match the material in class. That's a huge issue in a language learning class. You can't quiz us on something we literally don't know. That's not fair to us, and means you forgot to teach us something important.
