 Strengths- Interesting material largely new to all students Weaknesses- **The TA team** completely unresponsive/borderline disrespectful Section participation as a grade turns into a competitive learning environment Grading is harsh..not appropriate for an introductory course Overall, I actually wouldn't mind the challenge of harsh grading if TA's were willing to advise/help with material But they are unwilling to read drafts, discuss any real points of the material, or really meet at ALL even on zoom.
 I think the double edged sword of this class is the harsh grading. It can be a huge motivator when you do poorly, and then very gratifying when you do better, however, it can also make people shut down or give up. We all have our different thresholds for challenges that can cause us to try harder or stop altogether and I feel lucky that for me I worked harder, although I could have just as easily quit.
 This course is fantastically taught. It's fantastically TA-ed, and all the topics are interesting. However, I don't agree with Prof. Kagan's grading policy. I've spent more time stressing about getting a good grade in this class than I have about actually understanding the material. When your comments are being graded in section, it's hard to feel like the conversation is going anywhere because everyone's just butting in to try and get more comments in to boost their grade.
 strengths: compelling course material, compelling teacher, responsive TFs weaknesses: can't think of any
 I loved the course content but discussion section always felt like a rat race of jumping into the discussion to quickly make a somewhat coherent point or two. Overall it wasn't really a good learning environment because it didn't really promote "discussion" when everyone is just trying to get a word in, and it definitely didn't reward active listening -- sometimes it even just felt outright hostile! I also didn't really like how discussions were always structured as debates between two ethical frameworks because while I understand the argumentative value of picking a side and sticking with it, I also think there should be room for meaningful contribution to discussion that isn't along the lines of "utilitarianism sucks and here's why, Kantianism rules and here's why".
 This is a very engaging class. The lectures are presented impressively and they really do force you to think and evaluate your assumptions. The main weakness is that the class can at times seem daunting, however, it is rather fulfilling when that emotion is overcome.
 The strength of the course is in Shelly's lectures and the enthusiasm with which he teaches. I liked the way we covered utilitarianism, free will, and contractarianism. A weakness would probably be the grading. It's harder to genuinely learn in discussion sections when your comments have to be good enough to merit a good grade.
 Strengths: Shelly is one of the best lecturers you will ever have. He is engaging, enthusiastic, and witty, and he always encourages students to stay on after class to discuss philosophy with him (an opportunity you should not miss). He also did well to mask his own personal views on the topics, which is important in as controversial a field as ethics. Also, this class only requires three papers (albeit adding up to many hours of thought and writing). Weaknesses: although this might be a strength to an odd few (case in point: Shelly), Shelly's grading is gruelling. "C is for satisfactory work" was the first disclaimer of the semester. That being said, As and A-s aren't too hard to come by if enough thought is put into each paper; anyway, using up a Cr/D/F on this class is definitely worth it.
 I think there should be a better way to actually make sure students do all the readings. I definitely wish Id had more oversight in that department.
