2019-Fall HIST 103J 01/AFAM 202 01 Life&WritingsFrederickDouglass

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401-YCWR CRN: 13923/13924 Term: 201903

123Forward
 I learned how to think critically about a singular historical figure and how complex history is. I learned a lot from the professor as well as my classmates during each class. I learned how to take apart certain texts and how to apply them to our understanding.
 So much knowledge about Frederick Douglass. If you are interested in learning about Frederick Douglass there is no other course to take (this goes without saying!!) I also learned a lot about research this was far from my first junior history seminar, but one interesting thing about this course is that you develop a base of knowledge over the course of the semester in a really narrow subject area, so your research paper is really starting from a different place than papers in other seminars tend to (in many other junior seminars, you need to acquire that base knowledge yourself, because the course is broader). That was something I wasn't expecting, and it was great to write a research paper within this context.
 This was an absolutely fantastic course. I learned so much about Frederick Douglass, the period, and the practice of writing American history.
 An understanding of this era of American history through the lens of Frederick Douglass's writings, speeches, etc. A valuable historical and academic exercise.
 I learned about Frederick Douglass's life in very great detail. We read all of Prof. Blight's biography of Douglass, Leigh Fought's book about women in Douglass's life, James Oakes's book about Douglass and Lincoln, parts of a book about Douglass and photography, Douglass's three autobiographies, his novella, and several of his speeches and editorials. We discussed Douglass as an abolitionist, political actor, journalist, and autobiographer, in addition to his personal life and things that are harder to know. This class is more structured than Prof. Blight's seminar on historical memory in the spring because it takes you through Douglass's life chronologically. Since he lived through most of the nineteenth century, I also felt like I learned a lot about northern abolitionism before the Civil War, nineteenth century politics, and the Civil War that I didn't know before coming into the course (I haven't taken Prof. Blight's lecture on the Civil War and Reconstruction). That's not to say that this course gives a comprehensive treatment of nineteenth century American history, it definitely doesn't, but I do think that taking this class can teach you a lot about it if you don't already have a great deal of knowledge about that time period, and it's interesting to learn history by studying one person. There was also a 20-25 page final paper, so that was a pretty open-ended opportunity to dig into something or someone related to Douglass's life in more detail while learning more about historical research.
 In this class, we learned all about Frederick Douglass that a person could hope to know. I feel like one of the skills this class really helped me develop was critical reading. There is so much material in this class that it was impossible for me to read everything as meticulously as I would have liked to, so I had to learn how to read more quickly and in a more economical way.
 I learned an incredible amount about Frederick Douglass, who is just an incredible figure in American history. I also learned a great deal about biography and autobiography and how use a wide range of resources to understand one person.
 An incredible opportunity to delve deeply into the life of one of the greatest Americans, guided by perhaps his greatest biographer. Every history student should at some point take a class on a single individual - it is an incredible prism through which to understand a historical period and helps to humanize the political, social, and economic forces you are trying to grapple with as a historian.
 This class was a deep dive into the life of Frederick Douglass. I came away with a much better understanding of Douglass and the history of his time.
 I learned not only a ton about Frederick Douglass but about the attitudes and debates raging during the 1800s surrounding slavery and American governance, especially during the Civil War. This class was also analyzed the prose, style, message, and evolution of Frederick Douglass's autobiographies in depth while giving us the opportunity to engage critically with Douglass's ideas and language. Having the chance to study David Blight's biography while asking questions and engaging in debates along the way was a phenomenal experience. Hearing other peoples' term paper topics and discussing those various aspects of Douglass's public and private life really gave me the opportunity to study this time period holistically as we tried to build a clearer picture of Douglass as an abolitionist, politician, intellectual, husband, and father.
 I learned about the biography of Frederick Douglass and his relevance to the 19th century and beyond. But beyond that, I learned how to read the voice of one of America's most important and best writers. This class taught me much about primary historical research for long writing projects.
