This class focused much more on the public policy around science, such as how public funds are obtained for research. A shift in focus would have made the class more interesting.
Â I think the guest speakers are a real strength of the course. Collectively, they give a really comprehensive overview of different interactions between science and policy and effectively give real-world examples of the content from lectures and the textbooks. I think the manner in which the policy memos are approached is a bit of a weakness. Prior to this course, I had never written policy memos before and I felt a bit like we were thrown into the deep end on the first memo. We were given a lot more guidance after the first because the grades were so poor and across the board, students did much better on the final two. To account for this, the professors did adjust the grading so that the first memo counted for less than the final two. In future years, I think it would be helpful to dedicate a class to detailing policy memo best practices.
Â I definitely liked how the assessments in the course were based pretty abstractly on the material we learned in lecture. The assessments were three memos and a research paper, which were less about determining how much material you learned in lecture and more about making you do the research necessary to get an understanding of how science policy is actually made using practical examples. I also definitely enjoyed the guest lecturers. They were all both interesting and very relevant for the course material.
Â This course covered a broad topic, a survey of all of the players and issues in science policy. Strengths: By discussing science in the news at the beginning of each class, this course was well-structured for student engagement. Furthermore, the guest speakers invited were very experienced and inspiring people. Their perspectives on modern issues was something unique this class gave me. Weaknesses: I wish the assignments, writing policy memos, were more geared toward learning the course material. One could get a good grade in this class without ever reading the textbook or paying attention in lecture so long as they wrote an effective policy memo. Luckily, the lectures are engaging enough to draw people in.
Â The readings were interesting, but they were long and dense and would take me many hours per week to do. It was really hard to engage with the reading and stay focused on it. The lectures basically covered every reading, so it didn't seem too necessary to do the readings thoroughly. It would have been nice if there were more case examples or something extra in the lectures so they wouldn't just be the readings in lecture form. These were still very interesting topics and lectures, but some diversity in class activities would have been appreciated and increased engagement in my opinion. The only assignments for the semester were three memos, and one long research paper on a topic of our choice. However, I felt we were never taught how to write a memo, so this was incredibly difficult for me. I think lots of students in my discussion section were confused on what exactly was expected from our memos, especially because we were never even given rubrics before they were due, or a filled in rubric after. We were given some notes by our TAs on things we should do, but these were never ranked by importance level or anything. I also would have liked to go through some example memo strategies in class or in discussion section, where we could have read a memo together and made an outline of a successful memo or something. I really struggled with having no clear expectations for the memos or an example template or something to follow. Other students I spoke to had the same concerns and questions as I did.
