2020-Fall ECON 123 01 IntmdDataAnalysis&Econometrics

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 Learnt about econometric methods and applications in real, published research
 I learned various econometric techniques and properties ordinary least squares, unbiasedness and consistency, heteroskedasticity, omitted variable bias, Gauss-Markov Theorem, classical and non-classical measurement error, generalized least squares, two stage least squares and instrumental variables, fixed effects and within groups estimator, T and F hypothesis testing, power calculations, endogeneity and treatment effects models, difference-in-differences, deterministic dynamic processes such as moving average and autoregressive, panel data methods and clustering, and more. This was a broad survey course that really allowed in-depth understanding of various econometric properties that is very useful for understanding the academic literature and coming to your own conclusions regarding the internal and external validity of economic studies and their approaches.
 Built upon econometrics and data analysis methods first learned in ECON 117. The class went more in-depth and was a lot more theoretical.
 A really good grasp on a whole range of econometric methods; we genuinely learned so much in this course and I feel like it really equipped us well to understand the statistics portion of research papers in any field / analyze data ourselves.
 I learned a lot about different econometric models and their applications. I think this course did a wonderful job making the econometric concepts feel very tangible and helping us understand how they could be useful in practice.
 Very practical data analysis skills - highly recommend!
 Learned about statistics techniques and applications to economics.
 Strengthened my reasoning and application skills in the areas of econometrics we covered in the course.
 Learned methods of econometrics and how to perform causal inference in economics research. Methods included randomized controlled experiments, Difference-in-Difference, within-groups estimators, and time-series methods such as autoregressive models and moving-average models.
 I learned about so many of the mechanics behind econometrics and how to apply econometrics in real-world situations where we have to think about time/money constraints, unobservables, precision, etc. I feel so, so much more confident in entering the grad world where a lot of these skills are expected. Also, if you are doing any sort of quant thesis, this class is a fantastic aid. A great assistant in thinking thoughtfully about one's own research.
 As someone who previously took Glbl 121, Glbl 122, and S&DS 230, this course gave a deeper understanding of quantitative methods used in social science/economics work. I got to practice interpreting output, analyzing the design of experiments, and replicating economic research papers. Importantly, this course helped develop my understanding of what can go wrong with economic research (i.e. general equilibrium concerns, omitted variable bias, unbalanced samples, etc.), which I believe will help me critically assess the work of others.
 I learned different methods of analyzing data such as dynamic modeling and expanded upon concepts learned in Introduction to Econometrics such as instrumental variables and endogeneity
 I learned how to analyze economic data. The core of the course was linear regression (OLS) and the issues associated with it. I learned techniques to address these issues including instrumental variables, randomized experiments, and quasi-experimental methods.
 OLS + OLS-related analysis, Instrumental Variables, Panel Data, different ways of analyses depending on some characteristic of the data
