What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

 The course is a good reinforcement of some concepts taught in Linear Algebra and S&DS 242. If you understood those the first time, and want to learn anything actually useful, this might not be the best course for you.
 Clear lectures, very doable and reasonable psets, overall great class and great workload
 I am an S&DS major (third-year), and this is the best course I have taken in the department so far. This is largely thanks to Professor Brinda's dedication to student learning/educational pedagogy as well as the comprehensible, well-organized textbook he wrote for the course. Unlike most other professors in the S&DS department, Professor Brinda eschews long, grueling problem sets in favor of meaningful, basics-driven assignments. It was clear from almost every homework assignment (almost all involving coding in R) that Professor Brinda put thought into the most effective ways to reinforce concepts from class through example. Rather than being frustrating and borderline learning-inhibitive like some of the 40-hour psets I've had in other courses, I enjoyed our assignments and felt that I genuinely benefitted from doing each one. I cannot emphasize enough how incredible this was, and how surprised I was that shorter, slightly less challenging homework was actually more conducive to learning. I wish other classes would follow suit! Another strength of this course was the spaced repetition via Anki flashcards we did each week. Professor Brinda prepared us weekly batches of exercises from his textbook that reinforced core concepts from lectures, and we were encouraged to practice these regularly in exchange for a perfect score on one of the week's two in-class quizzes. I had not tried a method like this for a math course before, and it was really effective. I enjoyed how the decks were not too challenging and thus not discouraging, but rather built up muscle memory so we could better apply fundamentals (like properties of orthogonal projection matrices) to new material. I only wish this course were longer, or that there were a Linear Models 2 offered at Yale. We spent a fair amount of time building strong foundational understandings of key linear algebra concepts, which I fully appreciated. But this means we had less time left at the end to go over statistical concepts related to randomness and predictions. It would be great to have a second linear models course taught by Professor Brinda on offer, as I felt this was the first S&DS course at Yale where I was instructed well enough that I would leave feeling confident about the material and excited to apply it. There are certainly more complex linear modeling topics that one could cover in a second course and it would likely be fantastic to study those with Professor Brinda. Finally, the twice-weekly quizzes were short and often not made to be difficult, but I found it occasionally stressful to try and complete a question on the spot, photograph and upload it to Gradescope within a timed (short) window. Luckily, Professor Brinda always asked for feedback on quiz difficulty and I felt he responded well to student input.
 The primary strengths of this course are: 1) Prof Brinda, who is extremely helpful with assignments and very skilled at explaining difficult/abstract/theoretical concepts; 2) the material, which will allow you to see linear regression and linear models in a completely new, geometric way; 3) Prof Brinda's textbook, which is very concise and very clear; 4) the homework assignments, which are reasonably short and give you the opportunity to apply the theoretical concepts of the course to real-world data sets. There are no real weaknesses.

 Strengths: Professor Brinda is such a strength for this course. It's clear he's been honing the syllabus and course format over the several years he's been teaching it, committed to making it better for students with each rendition. The textbook and "variables picture" approach was so helpful in being able to visualize what is happening when fitting a model (vectors projected into subspaces). I feel leaps and bounds above where I was in terms of interpreting and understanding what is happening when running "lm" in R. Professor Brinda also spends the first portion of the class going over the important linear algebra concepts to set the stage before introducing random vectors, which was helpful both for those who took linear algebra a while ago, but also to set a clear foundation before introducing randomness. There were 2 weekly quizzes after class for most of the semester, but if you practiced the flashcards with the given exercises, your lowest score for the week was given a perfect score (and, of course, you were more likely to remember the concepts/be prepared to do well on the quizzes). The homework assignments were structured really well, with the clear emphasis being to reinforce the ideas of class, rather than have you reinvent the wheel. I liked the incorporation of R as well. Weaknesses: We didn't receive grades back for some of our quizzes until the very end of the semester (though one of the original graders needed to step away from the course). There were answers posted, but not specific feedback for ours until much later. Beyond that, I genuinely can't think of any. This course was well structured for my learning style.
