2020-Summer PLSC S228 01/EP&E S306 01 FirstAmendment&EthicsofLaw

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

ID: SU103 CRN: 30114/30115 Term: 202002

Back123Forward
 This course was truly fantastic, owing to Karen's and the teaching staff's deep investment in our learning. I am in awe of how intellectually exciting our class discussions were, and I wish we could continue them through the year. I am immensely grateful to Karen and our TFs.
 I sometimes wished that the class had been more explicitly legal and less abstractly ethical, or at least had made more of a connection between the two. To my mind, as the practical application of morals, ethics are inseparable from law, and I didn't always feel that was clear. When our midterm asked us what we thought about hate speech, and not at all about what the law dictated, it felt like I missed an opportunity to show how much I had learned about the first amendment in favor of the chance to share an opinion I could have defended before the course. I love Karen, and my TF (Allison) was similarly wonderful. Especially as illiberalism creeps deeper into the mainstream of our institutions, I was grateful for the chance to learn about this subject matter.
 Strengths include the robust discussions that were held; weaknesses were the quantities of pages in certain articles assigned.
 Strengths: Both small section and larger full class discussions. The narrow focus on the First Amendment/free speech is a topical issue for both college students entering a world of critical discourse and for citizens in a country facing dual-narrative media, which we contextualized in very recent cases! We discussed the recent case of U.S. v John Bolton, in which the Trump Administration tried to prevent the publication of his ex-National Security Advisers political memoir. Weakness: the only one that naturally comes to mind is that as a large course online, full class discussions were at times difficult to participate in, as we had ~66 people on the call at once.
 One strength of this course was how we were encouraged to question courts' decisions. Because the overarching question was "Is this ethical?" in addition to "Is this legal?", I was encouraged to criticize courts when I disagreed with their decision and was able to develop my own ethical and legal standards. However, the amount of reading in preparation for each class could have been spread out more evenly, especially considering the Monday-Wednesday schedule. This schedule meant that we had either one or four days to do typically equal amounts of work between classes. Also, it was sometimes unclear who could be speaking during the large-group discussions (just the members of the relevant small group(s) or anyone interested in speaking).
 Strengths: Covered a lot of cases, examples, and hypotheticals. Class and section discussions were fascinating. Midterm and final exam were open-ended and enjoyable to do. Weaknesses: Sometimes, the amount of readings felt overwhelming and it may have been more helpful to go in detail about a few cases rather than talk briefly about many cases.
 I thought there was too much reading at the beginning, but I see now that that was crucial to having a background of cases to refer to and add into discussion. I thought it might have been good, for beginners like me, for there to be a quick explanation of how one gets sued, like the legal process behind that, and how these things get decided in court, like with a jury and what not, but I understand its a 300 level class and maybe I was the only one without any background in all of this. Other than those things, I really loved everything about this course, my group discussions were fantastic, and I do feel like I learned so much in such a short period of time
 i think it was a great course, the professor was amazing. i wish that lecture had a tad more structure, or that legal vocabulary and terms were covered at the start of the course
 Well organized and run discussion groups Good TAs Engaging professor A bit heavy workload and readings
 The breakout sessions were facilitated extremely well and both the readings and reading response prompts supplemented class discussion well. I would say the one thing to note was that the size of the class really undermined our large group discussions. Because the lecturer and TAs were always trying to make sure that everyone was heard, we rarely delved deep into the topic at hand and had to move through the discussion questions relatively quickly.
 The biggest strength is Judge Goodrow; she was phenomenal. The only weakness was the online format.
 It was a lot of readings, but I liked the format of the course (short lecture, small group discussions, bigger group discussions). It also definitely made me thinker about the issues of free speech and the First Amendment. I didn't get any feedback on my midterm or final, though, only the grade.
 Its overall an extremely engaging course. Readings could be more feasible.
 Although it was strange to take the course online, it was organized in a way that maximized class discussion. Very engaging, insanely interesting.
 Strengths: The class and accompanying readings had a consistent focus, lectures were informative, and the professor/TA did a good job of making a 3 hour 15 min block as engaging as a 3 hour 15 min block can realistically be. Weaknesses: The types of readings assigned could have been more varied without weakening the tight focus of the course. For instance, in the readings dealing with true threat/fighting words doctrine, several legal cases were assigned that differed minimally in relevant aspects of form (core issue, outcome, judicial reasoning, applicability, etc.). Having several of these relatively redundant cases replaced with readings on the philosophical justifications of and/or contemporary debates surrounding true threat/fighting words doctrine would have led to a more informative and engaging class. I would have also been happy to see readings offering more heterodox views of the First Amendment; for instance, an author willing to challenge originalist interpretations or even the "marketplace of ideas" would have been a refreshing contrast to what we mostly read.
 Strengths: Karen is amazing, great TAs, adapted well online with a lecture, section and then group discussions, very engaging Weaknesses: sometimes A LOT of reading that isn't always discussed. Some weeks I would have liked to have focused on fewer readings. no feedback so no indication of how well you are doing.
 This course worked really well as an online class, and everybody worked together to ensure that people had a chance to speak when they wanted to. One weakness, which is specific to the Summer Session, is that some weeks there was a really high volume of reading that made it more difficult to deeply interact with the material. I also wish that we had received more feedback on the essay assignments.
