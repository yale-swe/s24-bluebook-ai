2021-Spring PLSC 029 01 Constitutional Errors

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC409 CRN: 23571 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 Yes. Despite the kinks in the grading and feedback, I would still recommend this course to anyone who is interested in the content and does not obsess over grades-- because you learn so much. I think the cons aren't hard to fix. This course really opened my eyes to how intricate the Constitution and the system the framers built is. This is in my perspective, crucial to understanding how to solve problems we see today (or at least, understand what the root of those problems are).
 Absolutely. This is a relatively relaxed class where students get to discuss constitutional issues in both a historical and contemporary context. The readings can sometimes be too long, but students can probably still engage in class discussions having not read them in too much detail, given that many of the discussions involve broader themes. That said, doing the readings is both very intellectually stimulating and helpful. Additionally, the overall workload is not too bad; there are three short essays and a couple of discussion blog posts.
 If you are interested in talking about the Constitution and studying it for an entire semester this would be a great course. If not, this is probably not the course for you.
 Yes! Learned a lot of things
 I would recommend this course to another student, but I do think this course is harder for people that do not have a political science background. The professor is great and very approachable. The seminar structure of the class allows you to really interact with the ideas explored. The material is interesting but some can be dry and at times it was too much material at once.
 If you are interested in the topic or want to learn more about the flaws of the country and its government.
 Yes! Even if you're not normally into this stuff this is a really cool class. The readings are really interesting, and Prof. Silverstein is really good at leading discussions. The grade is based on 3 essays so as long as you're ok with that I would say absolutely, take this class.
 Yes! This was my favorite class during my first-year. Some may recommend this course only to students who are interested in politics or the law, but I think this is a great class for anyone who would like a greater understanding of the American political, social, and judicial scene. With that being said, I think anyone taking this course should be prepared to complete a lot of reading (100-200 pages a week) and consume a lot of information related to current and historical politics and the law. I do not think that this course should be recommended on a basis of how familiar a student is with the law and politics but rather on a student's willingness to dedicate adequate time and effort to better understand the foundations of American society. And this recommendation would be incomplete without mentioning that the success and enjoyability of the class is almost entirely made possible by Professor Silverstein. He is an excellent teacher, very knowledgeable of the material, and an eager aide to his students.
 Yes, it's a first year seminar and every Yale College first-year should take one! The professor is great!
 Yes! Its an incredible course; whether or not you plan to enter politics, its a must-the for residents of the U.S. Its well-designed to take modern political and social dilemmas and put them in the context of Constitutional history. It took us a while to actually identify some errors in class, but the journey is worth it! While theres a lot of reading, you dont need to read everything. If you get the gist of what the reading is saying, youll be prepared for class discussions.
 If only for the fact of it being a first-year seminar, I would recommend it. In regards to the content of the course, seeing how often and readily different conclusions can be reached by two esteemed, educated individuals presented with the same evidence can be extremely valuable (from the founding of our country through today). I also found the course helpful in understanding more the beliefs of several high school friends with different political tendencies.
