 I loved Professor Gendron. She's amazing and always so ready to help students. Strengths include the grace days, the reading quizzes, and only one exam which was open note. I liked the optional draft options for the final paper.
 I really liked the textbook and the online quiz format. I also received really good feedback on my papers, which was great! I think the main drawback of the course was that the instructions for the papers were sometimes very confusing, and it was hard to keep track of all of them. It was also difficult to know what information to include on the final paper, as we discussed so many different things, but I think Professor Gendron was pretty lenient, especially with the stats sections, which is great.

 Strengths: good hands-on experience and you get to pick topics you're interested in; lectures and textbook all covered very helpful materials; Prof. Gendron and our TF were really available, patient, and warm Weaknesses: As part of the course design, we received less feedback on the 2nd research project. But since our class study (the 2nd project) was much more complicated than the group study (the first project), I wish we had spent more time debriefing it.

 Professor and TA were very involved in student success. I can suggest no improvements.
 I really liked that the class provided us with a baseline. I hadn't had much experience with scientific writing or with conducting experiments, and this course helped me get the baseline that I needed. The professor didn't assume that students had any prior knowledge, which was super awesome. I liked the material that we covered (we specifically talked about how WASPy psychological research is). We also got lots of feedback on our writing, which was super helpful even if it was shocking at first to see the amount of comments on your page. The textbook was helpful, although it contained so much information that I wish we had covered some of it in class. We were expected to read the textbook and take quizzes on it, which was helpful but we never got to apply the information we learned to what we were doing in class. The quizzes and the one exam were good ways to test what I had learned, although the amount of reading and work this class had was often overwhelming. It was especially frustrating when we'd get assigned lots of work and then it wasn't graded until the end of the semester. Because there was so much work, I sometimes felt like I didn't absorb the important information as much as I'd have liked to. For example, we did two in-class exercises on SPSS, but we mostly worked on these independently, and I would've liked a class with the professor orienting us in SPSS instead of the worksheet. When I analyzed data one-on-one with the professor and a few other students, I understood what we were doing and why; when we did the in-class assignments earlier in the semester, I had no idea what our results meant or what we were really doing. It would've been helpful to have some orientation in this aspect and to have it applied practically, since a lot of this class doesn't make sense if it's not applied practically. I just feel like a lot of those in-class assignments should have had some more guidance from the professor.
