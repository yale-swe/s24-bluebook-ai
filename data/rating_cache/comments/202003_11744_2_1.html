2020-Fall ENAS 513 01/MATH 301 01 Introduction to Analysis

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 11869/11744 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 More homework exercises are needed to help understand and solidify lecture content.
 Although I liked the instructor, I feel like there just wasn't a lot of structure to the class. I had difficulty understanding the importance of the different things we were learning and their applicability to different math problems. The fact that this class had no consistent homework made it difficult for me to put theory into practice.
 I think having psets or at least practice problems here and there would have helped me digest the material better. But I also do think the focus on presentation and teamwork challenged me a lot (I learned LaTeX for this class and I'm forever grateful!!).
 The topics were interesting and the project-based format was a unique and fun way to learn them. We were divided into groups for the midterm and final and worked on a few problems together as a group and then presented it to the class. It was definitely more engaging than doing a regular pset. Weaknesses -- it's sometimes hard to understand Professor Jones' teaching style and honestly, it was very hard to stay engaged with the class throughout the semester and I didn't learn that much.
 One weakness of this course is that there was not weekly homework. This can make it hard to really apply the material and learn it well, and you kind of just move along passively with the course until it is your turn to do a weekly homework.
 Strengths: group projects are great for solidifying knowledge of certain material / problems. I think it's great to avoid exams in the online format because they are not conducive to learning. peter jones is super super kind and caring and open to meeting with students individually. Weaknesses: very poor lectures. Textbook sucked. didn't receive any feedback or grades the whole class.
 The lectures could have a little more structure. Sometimes they are hard to follow
 Great professor Challenging but doable Not much weekly work
 Strengths- easy grading, self guided, slow paced Weakness: Could have a little more pressure
 The strengths of the course consist of a good level of interaction thanks to the virtual whiteboard, interesting presentation sessions where one can learn of applications of certain theorems and work with other members of the class. It was probably the one class I had this semester where I met most of the people in the class and had to collaborate with them on a presentation.
 I'm sure this course is taught very differently during a normal, in-person semester, but it just didn't work this time around. The only assignments I was ever given were two group projects (for which I never received feedback or a grade), and the lack of graded weekly p-sets meant most students got by without having to learn analysis (me included, to my shame). But really, what do you expect when students have other hard classes with regular assignments, as well as all the other commitments we typically make -- most of us need grades as a motivator (even just for completion). Now, the projects were pretty interesting, but I do not feel even remotely prepared for Math 305, which I was looking forward to taking next semester. All this said, the professor is really sweet and clearly enjoys teaching. I guess he just didn't think any of this through when planning this class.
 Honestly, I was very disappointed by this course. The lack of structure and very confusing notes the written by the professor made it impossible to build a strong foundation of analysis. I plan to take Math 305 next semester, but feel in no way feel that 301 somehow prepared me for it. If you're a math major, or just someone looking to learn higher level math, I'd recommend skipping this course and taking something more engaging. The only "upside" was the workload was incredibly low, if you absolutely need this course for whatever reason. But, overall, stay away from this course.
 Maybe it was just because it was online, but this class had basically no strengths and plenty of weaknesses. Professor Jones repeated himself often, and the course didnt really feel like it had any kind of progression.
 I think the strength of this course is definitely Professor Jones, who is highly enthusiastic and has a wonderful sense of humor. The course could have been improved by adding more small assessments (ex: homework submissions).
 This course is a great exploratory math course for someone looking for a reasonably low-workload pure math class
 Professor Jones cares a lot about his students and is always willing to help. He is very kind and made mathematics interesting. I think the course can be improved if the lectures were more organized.
 The subject material was fascinating and well-chosen. I feel like I learned about exactly what I was hoping to. It was difficult to learn in an online environment, but that was inevitable for this semester.
 The instructor worked with students on their projects, and this was very helpful in pointing the students in the right direction for the proofs. The class goes over a lot of historical and famous proofs, and the professor is enthusiastic about math and its history.
