 Weakness: Book per week; I had to rush some readings, so I feel guilty. A lot of Mexican-focused content. Strengths: TA's, content, Professor Pitti
 The course itself was very interesting and covered a variety of subjects, ranging from the history of Spanish in the United States to Puerto Rican student protests. The readings we completed tended to be interesting and generated fruitful discussion in class. I appreciated having Professor Pitti invite the authors of several books we read to class. The final project, which consisted of creating an exhibit proposal, was also one of the highlights of the class. While the assignments themselves were not too burdensome, the reading is this class was excessive. Having students read a 200-300 page book a week, while it allowed us to delve into the topics covered, was quite unreasonable, particularly in the middle of a pandemic. Shortening the readings, or at least suggesting that students focus on a particular section, would have led to more cohesive and productive discussions overall. The structure of the class was also a bit confusing and at times frustrating. This class is NOT a seminar. It is very much a small lecture, the only difference being that sections were built into class time. I found myself having very limited interactions with students outside of my section and Professor Pitti. While TAs for this class were absolutely wonderful, it was also a bit weird to see one group have Professor Pitti as their section leader. Clearer guidance would have also been helpful when completing the course assignments, particularly our primary source analysis.
 ALOT of reading, it's a little unrealistic to expect 300+ pages of reading a week when students are taking several other courses
 A strength of this course is that we are split into discussion sections, giving us the opportunity to be more engaged with the topics being discussed, contribute to discussion more frequently, and have a tighter-knit learning community. A weakness of this course is that the number of pages assigned for reading each week (mostly entire books) is not necessarily feasible and it makes discussions less specific due to the fact that in an attempt to cover everything we read, we talk about the books more broadly. It would be more beneficial to read specific chapters from the books.

 The strengths of this course lie in the instructor (Prof. Pitti), its structure, and the skill-building assignments. Prof. Pitti is an extremely knowledgeable and empathetic listener and mentor; he always makes himself available to clarify course content, respond to your weekly reading response, and provide guidance for any of the assignments. The class has an easy structure to follow -- you read that week's book or chapters, write a response, and come to class ready to discuss it. There are overall three main assignments that build on each other -- the first teaches you how to search for primary sources in a database, the second teaches you how to analyze several sources on a topic, and the third asks you to build a museum exhibit using many primary sources. It could be improved with better timing of the assignments, in my opinion. The museum exhibit project felt rushed toward the end of the semester, even with the prospectus several weeks prior to the final project's deadline. It'd be great to have more guidance as far as when we should have our primary sources picked out, when to meet with a librarian, when to have a draft done; unfortunately, since this project was our final, many of us procrastinated to the point of doing all of these steps in the last week of classes. It would have been so much more enjoyable to have started finding sources earlier on in the semester should we have been more proactive; while taking this initiative was definitely our responsibility as students, it would have been great to structure the earlier assignments around the exhibit project, perhaps, or to provide "guidance" deadlines to keep us on track throughout the semester.
