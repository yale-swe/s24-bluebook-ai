2020-Fall EAST 260 01/EALL 280 01/FILM 307 01 East Asian Martial Arts Film

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 How to write Film Analysis papers. How to watch a film while thinking of the elements that were used and which were not and what the reasons are for these decisions made by the director.
 I really gained a deep understanding of the history and impact that martial arts films had. It created a rich cinematic landscape that had a lot of nuances to explore. I also learned how to analyze films, an area of study I was previously unfamiliar with.
 I learned a lot about the history of martial arts film and the different elements that make up the genre. Also, I learned a lot about the cultural context behind the genre.
 I really learned a lot about East Asian Films and dived deeper into a culture that I did not know about until now. Professor Gerow really teaches this class beautifuly, weaving between different countries and genres as cinema evolves over time in East Asia.
 If anything, this class exposed me to so many amazing martial arts films that I didn't even know existed and am really glad I got the opportunity to watch. The East Asian martial arts genre has so much more nuance and depth than what meets the eye. The class focuses a lot on themes in martial arts films like sexuality, honor, masculinity, etc. I also learned how to write a film analysis paper for the midterm and final. As a non-film major, I enjoyed learning about how to analyze films and also learning about different filming techniques when it comes to sound, lighting and camera angles.
 Knowledge about the history of martial arts cinema, genre studies, and how to write film analysis.
 This course helped me understand why martial arts films are famous internationally and gave me a strong foundation to analyze media that I consume.
 I have gained a deeper understanding of East Asian martial arts film and film studies in general. I've never previously taken a film class, and this is great for anyone who is remotely interested in learning more about film.
 Basic film analyses and history of the East Asian martial arts genre
 i developed an understanding of the themes of gender, nationalism, race, foreignness, and other themes present in Chinese wuxia and modern kungu fu films, Japanese jidaigeki historical dramas, and Korean film.
 I learned a lot about different East Asian cultures as well as their filmmaking techniques and styles.
 I learned about how martial arts in cinema differs based on the culture and the time period. I learned about different styles of filming martial arts. I learned about martial arts tropes in film. I learned to question if a film is a martial arts movie or a just movie that contains some martial arts.
 I gained knowledge of various genres of East Asian martial arts film genres, mostly from China and Japan, of films from the early 20th century to present. I was able to analyze martial arts films in regards to its genre and other martial arts films.
 Learned a whole lot about martial arts, film, gender, cinematography, and different cultures!
 Coming in I had no background in film analysis, but this class introduced not only in-depth visual analysis for film but also more higher-level issues that provide background, context, and further grounds for analysis specific to East Asian martial arts cinema. As a survey course, I was exposed to many key works in the corpus of martial arts cinema and the various historical and cultural developments that affected this overarching genre as it evolved in the many different places they were produced in. This class is not just a survey of film, but also an examination of several evolving cultural dialogues.
 knowledge about various east asian martial arts films including wuxia and jidaigeki. Also learned more about film analysis and looked at the social implications of films such as masculinity/femininity in Bruce Lee's character and the female knight errant.
 I developed a new found appreciation for Martial Arts films and the culture that comes from it. I also learned how to critique and analyze films on a fundamental level.
 I was exposed to a wide range of knowledge surrounding East Asian Martial Arts films and their intersection with other topics such as gender, race, sexuality, and ethnic/national identity.
 A solid understanding of Hong Kong history and its relative film history. The traditional forms of martial arts film. A deep understanding of themes and narratives within the genre of film.
 I learned how to analyze film scenes and techniques. I also learned a lot about the historical context in which many martial arts films were produced.
