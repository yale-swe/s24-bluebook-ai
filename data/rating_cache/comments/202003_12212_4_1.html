Â Overall, I would recommend this course with some caveats. I also have to say that I would recommend any course with Dr. Kim as he's great. This course has a lot of strengths and there is a lot I liked about the course. For staters, this course had interesting material, and I learned a good amount about North Korea that I had never known previously. Dr. Kim is a very sweet and approachable professor. He structured this class well in terms of order of units where it felt like it followed a logical progression. People were very ready to participate so it never felt like there was a lull in conversation. I really enjoyed being broken out into breakout rooms with my peers because everyone wanted to participate. I also liked that there was such an explicit effort made to switch up class structure. The assignment workload was quite light. We just had one seminar to lead, weekly 500 word discussion posts and a final paper. One of the best and coolest facets of this course was getting to meet Monique Macias on the last day of class. Getting to meet and interact with someone who was raised in North Korea and has close ties to the Kim family was incredible and truly once in a lifetime. Bringing more speakers next time this class is taught will only elevate it further. At the same time, this course had several weaknesses in my eyes that should only get better as it's taught more and more. The biggest is that this class was very anti-North Korea, pro-Western world. I felt many of our discussions got into bashing the North Korean way of life which is not fair (and also not the point of this course). Iljea (our TA) was the great balancing force in this dimension always forcing us to rationalize the biases we have against the DPRK and for the Western World. North Korea is far from perfect (what place is), but it's not our job to make an executive decision or summary judgement on their way of life as so many people seemed to try to do. On that same note, it was easy to tell that the class was made up of almost all Christians. As a result, our discussions on issues like "missionaries" were not in the least bit critical. There's something seriously problematic about a missionary saying he "saw the need for saving in the DPRK people," yet no one wanted to acknowledge this (especially in thinking about how imperial powers have messed with the Korean Peninsula for centuries). Although I thought the class was organized, I felt we were given too much in reading/watching assignments every week. We often had a whole book and a film. In truth, that just means not everyone will read which will only hurt the conversation. Further, it felt like the same conversations were repeated class after class. In particular, "Is Juche a religion?" was asked in basically every class. Relying on different students every week to craft the conversation undoubtedly resulted in some of that repetition. On the note of seminar structure, I felt the non-student led portion also became repetitive and somewhat disorganized. In the beginning, we used that time to talk about religion in North Korea, North Korean political structure, and Korean history all of which I found interesting. After we had gotten through that material, the early lecture portion of the course became more disorganized. All to say, I think this course is 100% worth taking, but it's by no means a perfect class which will hopefully shift to being less Western focused in the future. This course is a great 5th class with a professor who really cares about his students focused on a country that isn't often spoken about.
