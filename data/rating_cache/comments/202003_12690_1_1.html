2020-Fall ER&M 353 01/HIST 323J 01/AMST 317 01 Latinx History

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401 CRN: 12690/12692/12691 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 A lot of knowledge and insights about Latinx history stemming all the way back to the 1840's. The class was also insightful is helping to see how progress can be made, but also how institutions have continuously failed Latinx and Black people throughout history.
 The class covers Latinx history in the United States beginning in the mid-nineteenth century. The class itself focuses mostly on Mexican/Mexican-American histories (and should honestly be advertised as such). We did spend a short period of time covering Puerto Rican histories as well as Central American histories. The class sadly makes no mention at all of South American histories.
 By taking this course, I have learned about race construction, social formations, organizing, radical community care, etc. among/from Latine groups, such as Mexican-Americans and Caribbean peoples, as well as briefly Central Americans. I have also gained the skill of being able to research primary and secondary sources, engage with them, and write papers from this research!
 This class helped me understand the deep history of Latinx presence in the United States. It helped me think about the Latinx communities in the United States and their ability to adapt to various political landscapes of persecution in US.
 I learned about Latinx community organizing, identity building, migrations history, and other concepts. We did cover a lot of concepts, but the focus of the course is mostly on Mexican histories within the United States.
 increased knowledge of Latinx histories (Mexican-American immigrant history, with some history of central american immigration to the United States).
 I learned a lot about Latinx history, and also gained insight into the ways that I study (Latinx) history. This class helped me find greater clarity about how I differentiate between different histories while still seeing their interconnectedness (i.e. neither homogenizing nor being too primordialist). Also, this was the first time that I've designed a (hypothetical) museum exhibit, which was cool!
 I learned so much about the history of Latinx peoples in the US and the complex transnational politics that shape their lives. This class really changed the way I think about (im)migration and the place of the Spanish language in this country.
 I learned about the history of Latinx peoples in the U.S. from 1848 to present-day around the southwest, the northeast, and Puerto Rico. By reading really informative and well-crafted books on different topics/time periods/populations every week, I learned about history as a discipline and its methodologies and limitations. I also learned so much about different tensions and issues about a history that I did not know about at all. Prof. Pitti really helped contextualize the books we read by inviting the books' authors to our Zoom class, showing related videos and materials, and helping us navigate databases to find more information on that week's topic.
 Knowledge of US Latinx history via various books and paths of different ethnic and racial groups of Latin America. Not a textbook course; rather, you learn selected histories (e.g. Puerto Ricans and state violence, or indigenous labor organizing in the US South) that give specific and broader insights into the history of Latinx people in the US (and other LA nations) from 1840-ish to present. How to analyze history from the grassroots, rather than top-down.
 Learned how to research history and discuss/evaluate history works.
 I took so much away from this course. Everything from old Latinx history to how my story fits within that narrative. I learned how to do proper research with the support of great graduate TFs and it was great to have a Professor who also understands that struggle.
 I learned so much about Latinx history, stories and topics that are hardly ever discussed in traditional American education despite the impact of Latinx immigration on the development of the United States.
 Generally, I developed my historical analysis skills, but specifically this course introduces you to some of the recent landmark works in Latinx history.
 Reading and writing about history, finding and analyzing primary sources, making an exhibit, finding primary sources and archives
 Learned a lot about migration from Mexico to the United States and about Mexican immigrants' lives and hardship from the 19th-21st centuries along the American Southwest. We also learned about immigrant communities from Guatemala, the Dominican Republic, Puerto Rico, and Cuba. Additionally, the assignments stressed historical methodology and primary source analysis, which was especially insightful for history majors, though likely useful for all students. I also enjoyed the readings' focus on oral histories and the uncovering of often erased/ ignored histories more broadly.
 As the course name suggests, I definitely learned a lot about Latinx history, much of which I'd never learned about. In addition, I also learned to think critically about the ways that historians frame historical events.
 I developed a wide understanding of Latinx history in the United States and from select countries in Latin America. I also learned how to research and analyze primary sources from historical archives.
 This course was an excellent introduction to Latinx History and made a good effort at explaining the narrative that many of us are familiar with. It introduced us not only to different events and concepts relating to Latinx history but also different approaches at story telling and sharing these histories.
 A very broad overview to Latinx history in the US, especially focused on the Mexican-American/SW experience. As someone not from a history background, I was exposed to the many types of history research there are.
 The traditional essay assignments offered in this course were different than the standard paper because the rubrics called for more description and flexibility with prose. In other words, the instructors allowed students to play with their writing voice in a way that worked with academic writing while encouraging deep and complex research topics. An example of this is the final Museum Exhibit project, which was a research assignment but the focus of this paper was to (re)imagine the presentation of history through a museum exhibit. Thus, research and creative writing merged in this unique assignment.
 This class provides the opportunity to gain more skills in the deep analysis of literature and the exploration of past events/history that still impact us to this day. The weekly readings forced me to learn how to read large pieces of texts in a short amount of time, while still understanding what the author is trying to show us. The weekly discussion posts, while sometimes frustrating (to come up with something to write), were encouraging for active participation in each week's book and in sections.
 This course enabled me to get a far more comprehensive study of migration, social mobilization and acts of resistance, Latin American diasporic sites in the US, among several other topics. Beyond the expansive knowledge I developed in this course, I also honed and learned research and archival skills through the course's final project.
