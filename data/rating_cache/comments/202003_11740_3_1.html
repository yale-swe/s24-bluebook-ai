2020-Fall MATH 250 01 Vector Analysis

Q:

Among the practices in this course geared toward learning during the COVID-19 pandemic, which succeeded and which could be improved? Please comment on course practices that allowed for virtual engagement.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC602 CRN: 11740 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 Recording the lectures and uploading on canvas was great! The lectures were engaging and worked well in a virtual setting where Professor Neitzke screen-shared his iPad.
 Success: engaging lectures; drawing things out by hand instead of clicking through a PowerPoint (so we can keep up with the pace and take notes); keeping the students engaged during lectures for those who were there; and for those students like me, who inevitably slept through the lecture each morning and watched the recordings later, making lectures interesting and engaging enough to where I could watch multiple and not get tired of them. Seriously - while other course's recordings/live lectures were long, drawn out, and boring (often causing me to set them to 1.5x speed to get through), I would plan to sit down and watch one lecture for this course to stay on track, but instead get excited for the next topic and watch 2-3 lectures at a time. I'm not entirely sure how, but this class was engaging and it's the only course where I feel I did not lose any engagement learning online instead of in person. Weaknesses: Just in general, I don't think sending students to breakout rooms to discuss examples is particularly helpful. This course's material can be challenging, and unfortunately in an online environment the instructor can only monitor one group at a time. Fortunately, Prof. Neitzke noticed this and abandoned the idea, making the latter classes even more engaging than those at the beginning (as if that was even possible).
 Having recorded lectures and posted notes on canvas was great! Virtual office hours were done well, both by the peer tutor and the professor. Having the Slack channels for quick questions was also helpful.
 The slack channel was nice, even if I didn't use it that much
 Recording lectures definitely helped for people with time zone differences. Office hours ended up conflicting with other classes and it was difficult for me personally to go to all the office hours I wanted to, as all the other classes also wanted to schedule office hours and sections at times that was suitable for people from different time zones.
 I really think this class was built around the pandemic very well. The live lectures kept things interesting and made sure students who could attend would stay engaged, while the recorded lectures and clear notes made the material accessible for people in different time zones. (I would imagine) the take home exams also kept things simple for people in other time zones.
 The recorded lectures functioned very well, and Professor Neitzke did a great job organizing and presenting his notes, and posting the PDFs of his notes after class. There were occasional delays in PDF and lecture uploads, but not significantly. I was pleased with how the course was run from a COVID perspective.
 I enjoyed the way that live lectures had brief sessions of breakout rooms where we could discuss example problems. I also relied heavily on the Slack chat throughout the semester.

 Recording lectures was helpful for when it was not always possible to attend live. Slack was also helpful for asking questions outside lectures and office hours.
