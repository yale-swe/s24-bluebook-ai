 Virtual learning was fine in this course. The one big issue is the way that exams functioned. The exams were really long, so students really had to rush through them. This made it feel like they were less an accurate assessment of how well you knew the material and more just a test of how quickly you could work.
 I appreciate that JT encouraged multiple forms of virtual engagement, including answering questions in class or on Canvas afterwards.  The lecture and participation scheme (cameras on, screenshots taken every so often, participation recorded, and an opportunity to answer questions on Canvas for participation credit) definitely helped with engagement.
 JT kept the class super entertained and taught super well, so the lecture was able to move very quickly and well. The requirement to speak was fixed and that is definitely the way to continue moving forward allowing students to answer questions after or speak in class.
 I think having a fully virtual class with 65+ people does not provide the greatest environment for learning.
 I really enjoyed this class, and I appreciate JT's method of keeping us engaged. In GLBL 121, there was no participation. However, in GLBL 122 there was a participation factor. I think it was actually useful. I was a fan. However, I think it should be graded more harshly in order to encourage participation. Rather than having one block of text, there should be a response due every Monday and Wednesday after class so it forces students to go over the material again. It should be graded on completion, and count for a fair few points in order to incentivize students. I think it was a good addition, it just wasn't executed as well as it could have been.
 The requirement for camera-on, graded attendance led to a competitive atmosphere that was not conducive to learning. It also didn't take into account the myriad of situations that different students may have been in.
 The lecture format and discussion sections were successful
 Fully online classes should not be this large. It truly does not work, particularly for participation. It was a good class because JT is a good teacher, but even he really struggled to keep us engaged.
 Success: One of the notable advantages of the zoom lecture was video recording. Since all the lectures were well recorded and made available for the students, those videos became great tools for the students to review the entire class materials so much easier than before. It is particularly true because the course is heavily dependent on the instructor's logical pathway to address each statistical method and method. For this reason, I hope that the instructor considers providing the same recording service for the future course as well even after the pandemic. Improvement: Based on my semi-in-person classes last semester, I felt that the virtual setting was better suited for this course because of the advantage described above. I found that it was easier to engage with the instructor through zoom in the class despite the size of the class. Although this comment does not entirely fall into the category of "things to be improved," I would like to leave this feedback to point out that the instructor pulled out really in the virtual setting, even better than the in-person setting.
 Both the lectures and sections allow students to actively participate and engage in class by answering relevant questions posed by the professor and TAs.
