Â This is an excellent course for anyone interested in public health, I really enjoyed it. The good thing about this course is that it covers many topics, including some that go beyond the first level Statistics, so it really provides a very strong base. Conversely, my criticism of this course is that it taught at a level for students who intend in doing some level of research (or would be researchers), while many of the individuals in the class are not intending to be researchers but rather administrators. That is where the other methods of statistics would be handy such as Quantative Methods of Business Analysis (figuring out risk/reward/compensation/cost & benefit scenarios, line queue's, etc.), these would be more helpful for healthcare administrators. My other potential criticism of this course is that although majority of the students will do well in taking this course, some of us who are non-science will not do as well, so although I really liked this course a lot, I do not want my grades/passing to suffer so much because this course is really intended to challenge students with science based backgrounds. If Yale wants to produce the senior leaders of tomorrow, my suggestion at this juncture would be to try to gear this course more toward healthcare administrators. Note: From my experience, one of the hardest things for a healthcare leaders is to figure out (from a statistical strategic perspective) whether: (I) a merger or acquisition will work out well; or (ii) whether litigating or settling a conflict is preferred, or whether to use product A versus product B for a particular type of procedure given their cost, benefits and risks, or (iii) whether to add a wing to the hospital for specific purposes like neonatal unit or whether to change a wing in a hospital from being preventative medicine based to being more cancer or more orthopedic issues based; (iv) whether to build a hospital in a certain area given the population and demographics, (v) the impact on healthcare costs and benefit of a change in policy or law, etc. That is something all senior healthcare administrators need to understand better and be able to strategize to lead the organizations. Most administrators do not have a comprehensive understanding of these issues, although I think they are relatively easy to teach. Please note, I offer the above suggestions with two things in mind: this is only my first semester at Yale, so I don't really know the depth and breadth of what lies ahead till I take more courses; and conversely, I join this program after having many years of business and law experience and I do believe I have an idea of what the future leaders in healthcare need.
