2021-Spring PHIL 128 01 Philosophy of Gender

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

 strengths: PROFESSOR DEMBROFF! what a person! loved how they lectured so well and i loved the TA, moya, as well. both of them did a phenomenal job creating a community and classroom over zoom. the range of texts, the lectures' clarity, the guest lecturers weaknesses: none (except maybe that if you aren't a philosophy student, the argument mapping is hard and the software is like from the early 2000s.)
 This is a very strong course! The structure is very clear and every reading is purposefully and deliberately chosen to help you think about issues of gender and sex. One strength is definitely Robin and Moya! They are some of the greatest teachers and people I have ever met! Not only do they provide insightful perspectives on the readings, they are also so kind and accommodating, especially during this crazy online semester. Another strength is the focus on argumentation. This course really taught me to analyze arguments effectively and break down complex philosophical works in a way that makes it easier for me to understand. Also, the guest speakers we had throughout the terms were incredible. It was so cool to read these incredible works of feminist philosophy and then actually get to ask the author questions about it. Definitely a highlight of the course! I'd say the only weakness was the time. The 50-minute lecture period would often go by so fast that we wouldn't have enough time to get to all the material. Moving from 50-minute to 75-minute lectures would greatly improve the course. Also, I wish lecture slides would have been posted before lecture. During lecture, I often found myself scrambling to jot down notes instead of actively taking in Robin's insights. Also, I would've loved the chance to go back to earlier lectures to refer to points previously discussed.
 The professor and the T.A Moya were the best part of the class. They were very knowledgeable and approachable
 This class has so many strengths! First, Dembroff is an amazing teacher who is obviously very passionate and knowledgeable about the topics discussed. One of my favorite parts of the course was the opportunity to meet and discuss with some of the authors we read on the syllabus. Even on zoom, this class was one that I engaged with the most, which says a lot about how Dembroff structures classes and the student-teacher dynamic that is cultivated. I loved the discussion board assignments.
 One of the strengths of this class was the reasonable reading load and accountability for the reading. As a result, this was the only course I took this semester where I genuinely read everything assigned to us. The weekly discussion boards were also really helpful in facilitating discussion with other students in a virtual context, and making me think a bit more about what I had read before lecture. The discussion session I had with Professor Dembroff was also run exceptionally well, with them doing a good job of drawing responses out of more hesitant students and helping us all collect our thoughts and connect them to one another. Honestly I can't say enough good about the class. Most of the weaknesses in my opinion had to do with the virtual format.
 Strengths included: high level of support from professor and TA's, engaging class and readings, guest lecturers from pieces we read.
 I thought the class was a great introductory class into philosophical thinking and how to write philosophical papers. The focus is primarily on assessing arguments as opposed to creating ones own philosophy in relation to already existing feminist philosophy.
 I think that this course could be better if more time was dedicated to teaching there students argument mapping.
 Strengths: Prof. Dembroff is an incredible lecturer, and the material is extremely interesting. Weaknesses: None really. Maybe that the class is too short. Would do better in an hour 15 time slot + section.
 Strengths: I learned so much and the lectures were incredibly insightful and engaging, even with the virtual format. Prof. Dembroff used polls to help ensure we were understanding the material and every lecture was so interesting!
 I think the extent to which students were encouraged to engage with and formulate their own opinions about the material was probably the strongest part of the course.
 This course only had strengths! I loved the material, the professor, and my TF. If I could change one thing, I would get rid of all of the argument mapping/Rationale stuff. It only confused me. Otherwise, this was one of the most amazing classes I got to take in my Yale education!
 This course provided many useful insights into gender oppression and made philosophy extremely palatable. However, I think it could have benefited from a more in-depth discussion about argumentation for those of us who came in with little or no experience.
 So many strenghts. All of it was a strength. the once a week short, but well thought out question was GREAT because it was more analytical than just a comment, and it made you really think about what you'd read. Honestly the only weakness would be maybe that the class didn't have much writing. If you're me, and you want to work on your writing, this is a weakness. if you don't want to write a lot, than the lack of writing is a plus!
 The classes were all incredibly engaging and informative. the constant reassurance you get from professor Dembroff and Moya was always encouraging and motivating. You also always get feedback and assistance on the written assignments which makes them less stressful. In addition, the weekly discussion posts were a great way for us to hear our classmate's thoughts on the readings, and formulating our answers to others' questions was great prep for the assignments. The only thing I would have wanted to change was the length of the classes. I felt that 5o minutes weren't always enough to discuss everything every week.
