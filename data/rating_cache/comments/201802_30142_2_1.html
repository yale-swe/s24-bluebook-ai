2018-Summer ITAL S110 01 Elementary Italian I

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

ID: SU109 CRN: 30142 Term: 201802

Back123
 Perhaps. It definitely depends on what the student is looking to get out of the program. If the student wants to get through L1-L2 and see something different, its probably a pretty good choice, but I would also warn them to temper their expectations about how fun the program will be. My working evaluation of the program (which is definitely changing and developing as distance from the program grows) is that Im glad I did it, but I wouldnt want to relive those 8 weeks. I feel really sad and isolated for most of them, and definitely was waiting for time to run its course. It is a program that taught me what I needed to know, however, and so I dont regret doing it. I just wish I would have come in with lower expectations If a students did do this program, though, I would offer them this advice (and yes, all of these are implicit recommendations of how to change the program): First, be very specific with your dietary needs with your host family. They will probably ask you, Do you eat everything? Wanting to be a good student, you will say yes. But if the thought of pasta every day disturbs you, maybe dont be so accommodating. Likewise, grow thick skin. They will likely try to make you eat more than you want every. single. day. If you dont want that or want to remain health-conscious you will have to be pretty aggressive about pushing back. Its tough, but necessary (I do wish, however, that program coordinators were more helpful in laying down the law to host families that force feeding is not okay!). Second, try to get involved early. Finding places to study in Siena is tough (read: nearly impossible), but I urge you to try to make friends early so that you can perhaps find that elusive slice of home in Siena that I never found At the same time though, be careful who you travel with. Traveling & sharing an airbnb with a relative stranger is a fast path to an explosive fight. Third, be kind to yourself. You will probably be homesick at times, and may not enjoy the program much at all. Thats okay/normal. It felt really reassuring for me when I realized that I wasnt the only one having issues with my host family or the program as a wholeessentially everyone was in the same place.

 I would definitely not recommend the course itself. I speak 5 languages fluently and essentially spend all of my free time learning languages, so I have some experience with this and in my opinion this course has many quite obvious and significant issues. The positive was that we were in Siena and it is a gorgeous town to spend a summer; however, the course itself (which I assume is the same when conducted at Yale) is a quite poorly executed. The textbook is terrible. 4 pages out of 5 are skipped entirely during the course. It is a 300 page textbook with literally 26 useful pages (I counted). There are so many issues with the way that the Italian language is taught at Yale that I couldn't write them all in this course review even if I tried. Just to shed a small light on the problem I'll give a minor example: all the vocabulary lists are organized in the alphabetical order of the Italian words, with the corresponding English translation next to each italian word. This means that you cannot quiz yourself using the textbook because you automatically know what letter that the Italian definition will start with since you see that the word above it started with the letter 'C' and the one below also with the letter 'C'. It is such a minor thing when considering the 300 pages of stupidity that is that textbook, but it is the small things that make the biggest differences when you are attempting to learn a foreign language. I want to be clear, I am not ranting to be mean. I am ranting because I am trying to help and I think the Italian department could greatly improve its efficiency if it fixed some of these problems. Instead of having a textbook that has all of the vocab, grammar, culture, and passages for reading. There should be different books for each of the 4. There should be a vocab book, a separate grammar book, a different book for reading, and then culture should be taught by teachers through powerpoint presentations or articles. This course was lazily organized and many students suffered because of it. I was lucky because I knew, through experience, how to teach myself Italian while I was in Siena, in spite of this course. But most other students did not, and spent a ridiculous amount of money and 2 months in Italy and came out of the course without the ability to speak the slightest amount of Italian. They also did not have enough vocabulary to be able to read or write coherent passages that did not sound like Google Translate.
