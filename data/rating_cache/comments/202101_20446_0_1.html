2021-Spring PHIL 326 01/RLST 402 01 The Philosophy of Religion

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401 CRN: 20446/20453 Term: 202101

12345Forward
 I learned about questions concerning the existence of God, including arguments for and against his existence.
 ability to assess and justify claims on theism, especially in the Christian tradition small introduction to Akkan, Islamic, Buddhist, and Hindu traditions understanding of theistic argumentation as it stands today, main players, and the primary arguments for and against theism an appreciation of the diversity of first-principles and motivating factors which go into forming a theistic/atheistic argument
 Understanding of the basic issues, ideas, and arguments in Philosophy of Religion, as well as common responses or issues with those arguments. Some topics covered include: Possible characteristics of God (and whether those are even coherent), arguments for/against God's existence, the place of faith and reason. In order to understand those arguments often there were substantive digressions into more general epistemology, so also gained some knowledge in that area. Gained skill in writing Philosophically through the essays and discussing Phil. Religion topics in section.
 I became familiar with important philosophical and religious questions.
 This was my first real philosophy class and I definitely now understand how analytical philosophy is done and wish I took this class earlier at Yale to do more philosophy.
 Survey of important views in philosophy of religion, plus a lot of exposure to related views in metaphysics and epistemology that were interesting on their own terms (i.e., even without considering their interaction with religious questions).
 A survey of the basic questions and issued embedded in the discourse known as "philosophy of religion."
 I developed a good background knowledge of the arguments for and against theism. I also was able to explore questions concerning the nature of religion, epistemic certainty, etc. Skill-wise, I improved in clear and focused philosophical writing.
 This course helped me think critically about large philosophical and metaphysical questions, and the arguments made regarding them. It also helped me greatly with the condensing of long texts into short analysis and using that kind of analysis to construct and compose arguments.
 The course covers major issues in philosophy of religion: divine attributes (what is God like? how can we prove it?), proofs of God's existence (cosmological, ontological arguments), problem of evil and related issues, faith and reason, moral implications of belief in God, and the interaction of science and religion. This class MAJORLY expanded my mind and understanding of religion, and encouraged me to consider problems I'd never thought of before. It's an incredibly creative class (more so than other philosophy classes) because Prof. Pittard is really open to different styles of argumentation. The sheer quantity covered is also really impressive, and I'm glad I took this over a philosophy seminar. Prof. Pittard (I'm pretty sure) is a theist, but he treats both sides really fairly and again is a super expansive and creative thinker..
 This class is a fantastic introduction to philosophy of religion. It gives you a good sense of the sorts of questions that the field considers. Though we went through the topics quite quickly,
 So much about the philosophy of religion. It made me a better thinker and writer. The course covered material in both depth and breadth which made the course very worthwhile.
