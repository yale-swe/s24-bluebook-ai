2020-Fall HIST 311 01/CLCV 219 01/NELC 311 01 Egypt of the Pharaohs

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 Yes, but my answer is highly caveated. This was without question the most frustrating course I have ever been enrolled in. Not because of a challenging workload or difficult assignments, but because of the lack of organization and direction. I was so excited going into the course, as I had never taken a non-western-centric history course. I found the material to be incredibly interesting, but the lack of clear structure ultimately made the class so much more difficult than it had to be. There was what seemed to be a complete disconnect between the professors and students. For example, the professors refused to post lecture slides prior to the lectures out of fear that it would incentivize students to skip lectures. This made no sense to me, and I found it to be quite the opposite. I would rather skip the live lecture and watch the recording with the slides in front of me, than watch the live lecture with no slides. When I raised this point to my TA, they explained that the TAs had tried to make this case to the professors, who were absolutely opposed to the idea. That lack of flexibility made lectures incredibly difficult to follow. Additionally, there was no clear purpose to each lecture, as they all went off on tangents about specific artifacts rather than establishing clear points about historical events/trends. Given the virtual learning environment, I would have thought that professors would do everything in their power to make the lectures as accessible as possible. I was wrong. My TA, Jen, proved to be incredibly helpful to that end; I think she understood the issues that students were raising, and it was clear that she did her absolute best to help give us a sense of direction and orientation. This class has so much potential, and I would hate to see it continue on this trend. It's cool to look at and learn about all the different artifacts, but at the end of the day, I just didn't find it to be a great learning environment. I have learned more about general ancient Egyptian history in the 2 days that I have studied for finals than I did throughout the entire semester.
 If you have a genuine interest in Ancient Egypt, this is a great survey-style course to get acquainted with it.
 Unless you already know a decent amount about the topics or are committed to doing all the assigned reading you will find the course difficult. Lectures were a bit random, and topics were not always chronologically covered. For the final exam we had to know the information and historical context of most of the kings, monuments, and events from 3,500 years worth of history.
 All in all, I would recommend this class to another student. I truly believe that some of the issues I had with the course stemmed from the fact that it was an online semester and the professors' first time teaching virtually. The class will feel really relaxed for most of the semester, but take good notes and actually do the readings because you need to be prepared for the final. Just know that it's coming. But the course itself was super interesting, and I don't regret taking it.
