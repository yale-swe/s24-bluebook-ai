Â Strengths: The small group structure was great. It gave more space for discussion, the readings were great, and there were consistent central questions about strategy that drove our conversations. It was also great to have Professor Wolfson with us, who obviously has had a lot of real-life movement experience. I also appreciated the policy brief and presentation. Weaknesses: The main thing that comes to mind is that when it came to the policy work, there was an unspoken air of, like: "We're pretending to be government officials in this presentation so we have to take account of REAL constraints and we can't be too 'radical' or 'outlandish' with our requests, and that's good because that makes us more realistic and effective, and that's why we're closer to the source of power, e.g. the mayor of NYC or the president-elect" That makes sense to an extent, but the problem is 1) I think it would have been good to have more discussion about why some political constraints exist, rather than accepting them as a concrete given, and 2) Some of us in the course might actually want a career on the activist side of things rather than in the government office, and arguably neither of those two options is superior to the other in any objective way (it's never said explicitly that one is, but that's the general ambiance I personally felt)
