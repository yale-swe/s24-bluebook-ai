Â Prof. Hinton is a strength! She brought a wealth of knowledge and energy to our class discussions every week, and it was really exciting to get to learn from her around the time that her new book came out. Prof. Hinton always seemed to be genuinely interested in everything that we had to say during our class discussions, so I appreciated the dedication that she brought to teaching in that regard. Prof. Hinton also offered to look at drafts of our assignments before we turned them in, and I found it helpful to meet with her and get feedback on my writing. I appreciate it when professors offer to do this, so that was a part of the course that I found especially helpful. The theoretical readings that we opened the course with were really strong and provided a lot of great things for us to talk about seminar. Regarding the readings in other weeks, I didn't enjoy some of the reported narrative pieces that we read quite as much, such as the Kotlowitz book and the Anson book. Since I didn't have a thorough background in the topics that this books were intended to address on the syllabus, I think that I would have appreciated reading a scholarly history book (or sections from a book) that provided a more thorough historical grounding than the narratives about individual people and families did. I'm not sure if other students might disagree, so perhaps having one of the reported narrative-style books on the syllabus instead of two could be a compromise adjustment. I also think that some of the weeks of this course had too much reading to keep up with. If this course is taught again, I would recommend trying to pare down the reading in a few of the heavier weeks. The two major writing assignments that we had in this course were helpful and fun to write (the media analysis and the final paper). It was nice that we were given a lot of flexibility in choosing what to write about, so we could choose to address whatever interested us most. On the other hand, I thought that the reading responses were a bit stressful. Personally, I would have preferred having a second short writing assignment or being asked to write a slightly longer final paper instead of having to do the reading responses several times during the semester. I did appreciate that we didn't need to write them every week, though, and that we had some flexibility because we could choose which of the five weeks we were going to do the responses for.
Â There are many strengths of this course. First, the time period of this course was slightly more recent than many history courses at Yale. In addition, the enthusiasm and knowledge of the instructor, Professor Elizabeth Hinton, is a major strength. Hinton's use of a pre-class email discussion chain also helped improve class discussion. She also often led class (rather than relying on student discussion leaders). Finally, the topics were interesting, and her in class mini lectures often provided essential background information. The weaknesses of the course were the too-heavy reading load, and slight disorganization of the topics. Some weeks, the reading was manageable and wonderful, but other weeks (particularly toward the end of the term), the reading was near (or even exceeded) 300 pages/week. This was extremely hard to complete, and sometimes hindered class discussion, as the class struggled to finish all the reading, and we rarely had enough time to discuss everything. It would have been helpful to slightly reduce the number of pages per week. Finally, slightly more attention to the divisions in weekly topics might have helped bring the course together-- some weeks, the discussion seemed focused on a place (detroit, chicago), others, the discussion seemed focused on a time period (the great migration & early 1900s, the reagan years), and still others, we focused on a type of policy (housing, welfare). It might have been helpful to either choose one method of topic division (for instance, making the course chronological, then tracking these different themes through time) or just to be more explicit about the organization of that week or that segment of the course.
