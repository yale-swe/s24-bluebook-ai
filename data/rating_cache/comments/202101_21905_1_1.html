 Strengths: Study halls and TA office hours translated extremely well over Zoom, in my opinion. I'd love to see them continued. The class is organized into modules that correspond to the chapters in the textbook, which also makes it easy to organize notes. Weaknesses: Just about everything else. Prof. Nagai was good at explaining things, but he would lose himself sometimes in the middle of a problem and confuse everyone. Additionally, he rarely went through the entire problem in class and asked us to derive equations or problems by ourselves using the textbook module. However, the textbook wasn't much help. The diagrams were messy and the explanations convoluted. Many of our problem sets this semester (compared to PHYS170) were literally impossible to try without a TA present, which was discouraging. I rarely learned anything in lecture and instead credit most of my learning this semester to a particular TA's office hours.
 This class was very strange since the simple fundaments are taught in lecture but then the assigned work and examinations focus on complicated upper-level problems. In some classes, this method can stretch students' thinking and help them learn, however in this class, for the most part (in part due to the circumstances with COVID), the massive discrepancy between class and PSETs only served to leave students utterly reliant on TA help for problem sets. On the exams that were open-note, students did well when the assigned problem was similar to a PSET problem, but did poorly on all other problems, which were often excessively challenging; on the harder problems, students basically just received credit when their guesswork was on the right track. This often advantaged students with more confidence/high school background in physics even though basically everyone was confused to the same degree. These criticisms were present but to a much lower extent in Phys 170 (fall semester). While the in-class teaching in the spring was more clear, it failed to go to the depth needed to even come close to solving homework and exam problems, so ultimately I preferred how the course was designed in the fall. That being said, the course took up an unreasonable amount of time in the fall, so at least spring had that going for it. Despite these flaws, I do appreciate the flexibility of Professor Mochrie and his willingness to adapt the course. There are many wonderful things about this class, and I think that a few adjustments can make this a wonderful course. Overall, I am still glad I took it over 180, I think.

 Daisuke was very kind, and he and Simon were both flexible - if students felt unprepared for an exam, accommodations were often made. That being said, I feel like I learned nothing from the class. Daisuke handled lectures exclusively while Simon wrote PSETs and exams. As a result, I would leave Daisuke's lectures (interspersed with PollEverywhere questions and practice problems) feeling like the material made perfect sense. However, the lectures were NOTHING like the PSETs or exams. I had to go through every one of Simon's PSET problems with a TA to even begin to understand the material, and the exams were twice as difficult. More often than not, the exams tested material that was never even covered in the class to begin with (exams covered material that was never even in the TEXTBOOK, which was written and provided by Simon). Because of that, even though I understood the material as it was taught in Daisuke's lectures, PSETs and exams took this material to a ridiculous extreme beyond the scope of the textbook, which left me and other students feeling frustrated and demoralized. Furthermore, because Simon wrote all the material for the course/textbook, it was impossible to get help beyond TAs - I couldn't even get clarity from a different physics textbook. Even some TAs admitted that they sometimes couldn't understand Simon's answer keys for the PSETs. The instructors are really trying to make it possible for students to learn by offering corrections and group quizzes, but the material taught and the material tested upon are too different for that to happen.
