2021-Spring AFAM 297 01/HIST 162J 01 Urbn Inequal After Civil Right

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC409 CRN: 22131/22132 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 Yes--Professor Hinton is an amazing professor, and I had a wonderful time learning from her and my peers!
 Definitely! If you have a chance to take a class with Professor Hinton, do not pass it up. She is one of the kindest professors that I've had the chance to take a class with at Yale -- aside from genuinely caring about her students, she is also just a wealth of knowledge on Black history, the post-1960s era, and urban inequality (among many more things). The syllabus for this class is a gift in-and-of itself, and is one that you probably won't find in many other courses at Yale. All in all, however, the biggest reason I'd recommend that someone take this class is because I'll genuinely miss it. Each and every discussion was stimulating and made me think about society in some new way. Conversations in this class never felt boring or stale, or like we were saying things just to fill up space. Professor Hinton really pushes you to think in more expansive and imaginative ways during each seminar, to not let the current state of the world limit what it can become.
 Absolutely! If you have the chance, I highly recommend it. Professor Hinton provides an excellent learning environment, and the readings provide the foundation for incredible class discussion.
 I would definitely recommend this class to another student. Not only do you learn a lot from the readings and from Professor Hinton, but you do so alongside other folks who typically are at the same starting point as you are with many of the topics we discuss. The best past about this class is the seminar discussions, so if you're a seminar-oriented kind of person I think this class has a lot to offer.
 Yes!!
 Yes! This course is so important. I appreciated that this course started where other AFAM courses tend to leave off (in the 1960s) and talked about how the post Civil Rights era created the environment we see today. Discussion was always fruitful and Prof H does a great job of making everyone feel comfortable to share in class. I would just say that if you're thinking about taking this course, be ready to do a LOT of readings. Per week, it was about 200 pages of readings, including full books that we'd have to read in a week and comment on the day before class. That being said, it was worth it for me! Prof H gives plenty of feedback on assignments and was always available for support (plus, it's just awesome to be able to learn from her, and seeing the strides she's making in the field). And, for other ND students searching for accommodating professors, Prof Hinton was always willing to work with me on deadlines and other support.
 Absolutely. Every single person should take this class. I will genuinely miss going to this class every week and would leave every class with a million thoughts running through my head and felt so excited about everything we had discussed. Having this class to keep me grounded in a crazy year was unbelievable valuable, and the lessons I learned in this course will continue to be relevant for many years to come.
 Yes, I would. Prof. Hinton was a great professor to get to meet and learn from, so I would recommend checking out anything that she happens to be teaching. For this course, I thought it was really great to get to take a course that focused on post-1965 Black history. I feel like a lot of Yale students expect other students to already know about some of topics that were addressed in this course, but this material lies in an area that a lot of history courses don't cover. I thought this course was very accessible though, so I didn't feel like I needed a lot of background knowledge coming in.
 Yes! This is a wonderful course with a terrific professor! I'd absolutely recommend this, particularly to someone interested in recent American history with a policy-leaning interest.
 Yes, I would! This course is so important to understanding the problems we face in society today and the racist policies that shape the life outcomes of individuals. Everyone should know this history. I also felt like the structure of the course was conducive to building community. It was a small group and we often did small group projects that helped us connect with one another.
 Yes! I would definitely recommend this course to another student if you're interested in post-civil rights history, urban inequality, or any issue related to education, housing, health care, etc. This course covers a lot of material but provides a really great historical foundation for understanding the inequality we encounter today. It is also a privilege to learn from Professor Hinton who is an expert in some of these very topics.
