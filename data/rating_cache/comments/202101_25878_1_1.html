Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 The course bit off more than it could chew. It tried to deal with too much course material to actually go deeply into any one portion of liberal thought. The strengths of the course were the beginning, when we would read one thinker a week (like Tocqueville) and discuss their influence on liberalism. This made sense to me, and felt like we were actually developing an understanding of what "Liberalism" even means. But the second half of the course was mired in far too many readings every week. This is not some college-student philistinism or laziness. This is, instead, a criticism of the way the course was structured: in my case, the problem wasn't that I was averse to doing reading, it was that I had no idea how to integrate the disparate array of articles we would be given every week into some sort of coherent broader narrative. Surely the instructors were just giving us more than we needed to actually read, so that we could just get a set of working impressions from all of the articles, but I found this strategy extremely unsuccessful. I was left feeling completely unmoored, without any real understanding of where the course was tending, nor any sense of the philosophical development of Liberalism past Cold War American Liberals like Niebuhr. The course was in search of a sensibility. Because of its winding and overwrought structure, I don't think it found it.
