2021-Spring ENAS 514 01/MATH 305 01 Real Analysis

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

 The lectures are clear, and the office hours (both from Minsky and the TAs) are helpful. It seems like they all really care about the class and want the students to understand the math. My only suggestion would be to switch from two days a week to three, as absorbing 75 minutes of new math in one sitting can be difficult, and it's often easier over more shorter periods.
 Strengths: this is an absolutely incredible class. It starts with Professor Minsky. He is so dedicated in teaching his students. He always responds on the Ed very quickly, and he is always seeking feedback. Furthermore, he held two office hours every week where he answered all of our questions. In class, he blends the perfect amount of rigor in the beautiful subject of measure theory, while also giving us a fair amount of intuition, problem-solving strategies, and examples. He is an extremely clear lecturer and has great handwriting on the iPad. Furthermore, his problem sets will challenge you to the brink. However, every problem reinforces learning and feels great to solve. Weaknesses: Not many. Maybe a minor tweak is to spend more time on general measure theory because it felt a little rushed. Another thing is the textbook Beals. I understand that the course follows chapters 10, 11, 12, and 13 pretty closely before going off to do other things. However, it might be worth looking for another textbook. Beals has a lot of errors. Also, many of the proofs in Beals aren't actually all written out, so it was a little annoying if I was confused by something in class. Furthermore, a textbook for Caratheodory/Hahn-Kolmogorov would be extremely useful.
 Strengths: Professor Minsky! Professor Minsky is an amazing lecturer who explains everything clearly (and with lots of pictures). He is also very approachable and helpful in office hours. The class was also extremely well-organized, so it was easy to follow the progression of topics. Weaknesses: The homework grading was a bit...pedantic at the beginning of the class, but got better towards the end.
 Strengths: excellent lecturer Weaknesses: harsh problem set grading
 The biggest weakness of this course was the combination of grading and psets. Psets were very tricky and required you to "get in the weeds" very often, i.e. work out small details and edge cases for pages on end. Grading was insanely anal graders would take off 1/3-1/2 of the points for a question if you missed even one of those details/edge cases (e.g. if you forgot to check that certain functions are measurable, a fairly easy task, you might get 3/5 points for that question). The instructor also failed to fix this until around 3/4 of the way through the course, which is disappointing since the students were giving him overwhelming feedback about their experience with grading pretty early on. However, lectures were well done and the exams were doable.
 This course strengthened my mathematical thinking immeasurably. The material was beautiful and useful. Everything was explained with clarity and vision. I truly can't think of any weaknesses (it was a fair bit of work at times, but this wasn't a weakness, it was a strength; we had all the support we needed, and every single pset/exam problem was valuable). Towards the end of the course I struggled to maintain my attention span during long proofs in live Zoom lectures, but this was just Zoom fatigue, and not the course's fault.

 Strengths: Professor Minsky's engaging and well-organized lectures contributed to my excitement about the course material [he added polls to assess student understanding, short videos to provide an introduction to course concepts, and answered questions throughout class]. However, the real star of this course was our Undergraduate Learning Assistant (ULA) Richard. Although it is easy to feel inadequate or overwhelmed in an upper-level math course, Richard went out of his way to clarify concepts in a way that was welcoming rather than belittling [unlike some of the other office hours]. I do not know how I would have made it through this course had it not been for Richard's patience and willingness to talk through concepts in a way that solidified my understanding. :) Weaknesses: Professor Minsky assigns too much work for a class that has weekly problem sets. I do not mind 20+ hour homework assignments when students are given adequate time to complete them, but expecting us to finish a **20/30+ hour problem set** in a week's time is simply inconsiderate. I have taken other difficult courses at Yale (MATH 350, CPSC 223), but none of them hit me as hard as this one. My mental health was greatly affected [as what that of many of my classmates to whom I spoke] and Professor Minsky made little effort to adjust his course in response to student concerns. Professor Minsky moves *so* fast that there is never any chancebesides the homeworkto review what you have learned or internalize the material.  I would be hard-pressed to discuss the strengths of the course without mentioning Prof. Minsky and the efforts he undertook to make this course as great as it was. The course certainly is thorough in discussing the material and covers foundational concepts in real analysis, but I think that's really where the strengths of the course itself stops and the strength that is Prof. Minsky begins. Prof. Minsky is fantastic at explaining the material. His lectures are clear and concise. He makes an effort to address each student's questions and concerns, whether that about the material or the course itself. But most importantly, he fosters an environment that is conducive toward learning. He really places an emphasis on learning the material and works so that students's learning isn't impeded. As a result, students were willing to participate in class and in discussion during office hours by sharing ideas and asking questions. The only "weakness" that I can think of is that in the beginning, the grading was somewhat strict. As a result, I, and from what I've heard and seen, other students, were spending a considerable amount of time writing up our problem sets (with actually solving the problem sets taking up only a smaller portion of that time).
