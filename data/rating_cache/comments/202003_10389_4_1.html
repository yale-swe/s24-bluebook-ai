2020-Fall EAST 303 01/HIST 303J 01 Hong Kong and China

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC409-YCWR CRN: 10417/10389 Term: 202003

Back12345
 Yes. This course covers material pertaining to Hong Kong and China from the 19th century to the present, and connects current issues in Hong Kong and Southern China to historical background. There is a good balance between analyzing current affairs and historical events/context, as well as a good balance between more Hong Kong centered topics and South China centered topics. Additionally, Professor Ho gives good feedback on papers and is clearly passionate about the course!
 Yes, lots of interesting insight that can be drawn from coming together with students and Professor Ho in a small class setting and talking about readings for the week. Standards aren't too demanding but also enough to keep me engaged.
 No
 Absolutely NOT. She's heard feedback time and time again and has failed to implement it every time. And it has proven disastrously. First, she reordered the readings so as to provide some continuity in the timeline. Fine. But where were the background or contextual readings? Yes, it's a seminar, but for the love of all that is holy, that does not mean that when all we do all day is stare into boxes on a screen that you can't use visual materials??? Put a picture up for god's sake! Play a video! Share your screen when we look at specific quotes! Do some lecturing so we don't make baseless claims! Answer a question with actual substance! She literally set us up for failure. We talked about protest art but never looked at any together. We talked about the Ta Kung Pao coverage of the 1967 riots, but didn't collectively view the pictures we were assigned to look at. We listened to podcasts about the 2019 and 2020 protests, but never watched any of the media coverage. There was no color among this vastly colorful city. There was no vibrancy in the dullness of the pandemic. There was nothing hopeful amidst hopelessness. Ho is utterly as non-personable as you can get. She's as cold as dry ice and as bland as unsalted pretzels. Her personality is a deep as a rusty screwdriver, and the class format is as stale as the piece of bread you forgot about in your dorm refrigerator. You go to office hours to say "Hi! How are you!" and you're met with disdain and nothingness. She's there because she has to be, or at least that's what it looks like. Any of my excitement or joy that would've come from studying HK was thrown out the door as soon I took her class. She sucked the jubilation out of thin air. If we went on a tangent for an extended period of time, a bunch of us considered that a win b/c it was more engaging than the regularly scheduled programming. This class was not a history of Hong Kong. It was a disarray of various episodes in southern Chinese history, with little continuity. Governor Patten, his reforms, the Opium Wars, the relationship with China, refugee crises in both directions, economic strife, etc. were never discussed. It was just assumed that we knew how HK became HK and how the government worked. It was assumed that we knew about the creation of the CCP and the Chinese Revolution, when on the syllabus it said "no prerequisites." No wonder after the first class, when both sections were completely enrolled, 75% of the people dropped it. Literally no guidelines on how to write a good paper. Arbitrary grading (someone please tell me what a dual grade with a slash means?? still don't know). And grading took forever. Like two months forever. And feedback was useless. This class was just a disappointment. In her course video, she seemed so engaged. The syllabus looked fantastic. The primary source documents specially curated during her research in HK were a unique touch. The seminar style looked great. Assignment grading seemed fair. Result: not engaging, breakout rooms were pointless. When we didn't get that the Shenzhen Museum would have been patronized by wealthy HK citizens she gritted her teeth, laughed angrily, and looked as if "you guys have literally learned nothing!" Well guess what, I haven't! I'm literally heartbroken over the fact that I thought this class was going to be my favorite one this semester and it turned out to be the one where I could have read all the materials and talked to an imaginary friend about them--I would've gotten the same out of it. Please get rid of discussion leaders. It's lazy teaching. There. I said it. Fix discussion posts so we don't say everything we have to say on them. Give actual prompts for the papers so we aren't in the dark. Have a rubric with numerical grade values. Explain where on earth HK is. And rename the course b/c it's a lie to say the class is a border history of HK. Yale history dept is famous, but this class was just not it chief.
