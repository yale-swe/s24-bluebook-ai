 It will take a drastic restructuring to make introductory lab classes into a useful experience, rather than something people only ever do to complete a requirement and forget about. I think all of the skills we learned could have just as well been learned within a week of working in an actual lab, and it felt pointless to do activities like mixing to solids together and spending a couple hours separating them again. You are sure to be depressed at some point during the week thinking about this lab and the vast amount of graded assignments that are mostly useless. The class would be far better organized if an exam at the end wasn't a quarter of your grade, if students were provided the hazards of materials and the procedure of the lab rather than having to write it themselves, and if the TAs did not grade so arbitrarily and harshly.

 An immediate strength that comes to mind is the high level of organization and comprehensiveness within the course; course materials on Canvasincluding assignments, reading assignments, lab videos, quizzes, and other fileswere largely consistent, readily available, and navigable. However, there were some discrepancies in chemical compound names and lab procedures between some weeks' reading assignments and lab videos, and the small differences led to confusion and the need for clarification throughout the semester. Ensuring that the information presented in the reading assignments and videos is identical will aid in making the course more user-friendly, especially in a virtual format. I also find thatwhile the exams and quizzes accurately apply concepts discussed in sections and reading assignmentsstudents were not allotted enough time for assessments, especially the final exam. Providing only 60 seconds (quizzes) or 96 seconds (exam) per question creates a significant time deficit, which induces high levels of stress that can cloud rational thinking. Even as a student who read thoroughly and studied consistently to ensure full comprehension of the course material, I found the quizzes and exam to be rushed, and I do not feel that testing conditions allow for an accurate reflection of student comprehension and effort across the semester. Given these timing issues, I found the weighting of the final exam to be particularly severe; missing a single question on the exam equates to getting a 0/10 on an experimental plan or lab notes assignment. Given that these assignments often take several hours to complete (in contrast with the 96 seconds per exam question), I found the 10-point penalty for missing an exam question to be disproportionately high. A student with exceptional performance throughout the semester and mediocre performance on the exam due to timing issues could watch his or her grade turn from an A to a B+ or B within minutes. I find this alarming. I am sure that I am not alone in my concerns, and I urge the course coordinators and/or instructors to reconsider the merits of allowing students more time to formulate their answers on quizzes and exams. If this leads to retroactive adjustments for students enrolled in the course in the most recent semester, those changes would be equally welcomed and appreciated.
