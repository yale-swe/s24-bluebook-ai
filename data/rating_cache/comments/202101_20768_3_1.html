Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

 Yes! Be warned that for a Shelly class, there is a substantial amount more reading, but that's to be expected in what I believe is the highest-level course he teaches. The amount of reading is still on par with other lower-level philosophy classes, and, I imagine, less than other upper-level courses, but if you have taken other courses with him it may still be helpful to know when setting your schedule to expect a bit more reading. But this is a fantastic course that anyone with interest in ethics should take.
 There's not much I can say about Shelly Kagan that's new. The course and his instruction are incredibly well done. He is truly at the top of his field as a philosopher. If you take this course, you will learn. But you will not do well academically. Your work will compared at least to much more experienced graduate students and at most to established philosophers with much more space and information to make their arguments. If you care about your GPA less than philosophy, I would recommend taking this course. If you want to learn about long-term ethics without sacrificing your chance at graduate school, you may be better served to simply peruse the reading list.
 Yes, but make sure you come in knowing how tough Shelly's criticism can be. The class covers a topic which could be of potentially overriding concern, and it is not clear to me what the right take on the issue is. Given these two facts, it is pretty important to sit down and carefully think about the issue, and this class is probably one of the best possible ways to do that with other people who care about about the topic (there is obviously some self-selection in who chooses to take the course). The class introduces you to more nuanced understandings of several interesting sub-questions in ethics, and also forces you to seriously engage with that material since otherwise the papers you write won't be any good. The opportunity to thus have your ideas critiqued by Shelly is pretty special, just be ready for harsh criticism if he thinks your papers/ideas warrant it.
 Absolutely, as long as you've done some non-trivial work with ethics in the past. It was so rewarding to get to explore a big, unresolved question in ethics with Shelly. The topics of population ethics and longtermism are truly baffling, and it is worth taking this class to see just how difficult it is to find satisfying answers to the hardest questions in ethics. As with many philosophy classes, the conversation is sometimes dominated by a small number of students which is annoying, but not a dealbreaker.
 Yes, if you're interesting in ethics and/or effective altruism.
