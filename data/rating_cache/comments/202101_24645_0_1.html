 I learned a lot about the history of medicine, particularly in Greece and Rome. We learned about connections across cultures and up til today, which were very interesting.
 This was the first course in Greco-Roman history that I've ever taken, and I learned a great deal. I gained insight into society and culture in ancient Greece, Rome, and Egypt, through the lens of disease and healing. For example, we learned about how medicine reflected and reinforced gender biases in society, about ancient responses to disability, and about the socioeconomic and other factors that might motivate an individual or couple to expose an infant. We learned that the themes present in each ancient society were often relevant to the themes present in other ancient societies, and also to themes in modern medicine and society. We learned about the Hippocratic corpus, and how its legacy was cemented for centuries in medicine practiced both in the western Europe and the Arabic speaking East. More broadly, we learned about various camps of healing in the ancient mediterranean, and how they interacted with one another. Throughout this course, I also gained confidence in my ability to approach both primary and secondary sources relating to history. I learned to be conscientious about the kinds of sources that historians use to study ancient medicine, and about what kinds of questions can be asked of those sources. I learned how to read primary sources closely, and how to understand the claims and structure of complex secondary sources.
 This course was a really interesting survey of the ways that health, medicine, and illness were treated in the ancient world, as well as parallels in the modern world. The assignments focus mainly on engaging with works of secondary source material either evaluating a certain aspect of ancient medicine or articles discussing the social repercussions of medicine today.
