2020-Summer MUSI S290E 01 EDM: History & Evolution

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

ID: SU103 CRN: 30148 Term: 202002

Back123Forward
 This course is a gut.
 Strengths were all of the information I learned about the history of technology used in making this kind of music. As well as the structure and techniques of how songs are built. Biggest weakness I would say is that a lot of this software would be best learned by using it. It would have been helpful to have interactive sessions where we not only learned intellectually how each tool in the software worked, but how to use it personally.
 My favorite parts of the course were hands-on exploration of Ableton Live. I actually would've preferred if the course did not include the 'history of EDM' section, and was instead entirely focused on creating music and analyzing current songs.
 I think offering this class with more than one DAW would be EXTREMELY beneficial as not everyone uses Ableton live to produce.
 Super interesting content, with some nice opportunities to actually use Ableton and make music Wished that the Zoom lectures were different from the video lectures. They were really just a repeat, so felt unnecessary to do both
 Strengths: I think Dr. A did an amazing job at blending the fundamental skills for content creation with teaching the history of the genre. It was an enjoyable and meaningful course format. Weaknesses: It felt like sometimes the lectures would repeat information heard in pre-class videos.
 Each class during the week was a good combination of getting lecture material information and interactive exercises that kept everyone involved.
 This course goes over everything. Seriously. EDM music history, Ableton, reading notes, major/minor chord progressions, synths, etc, etc. It will probably be more difficult for students who have no music background. If I hadn't known how to play piano and drums/read music, I would've probably struggled in this class. It's pretty fast-paced, but Dr. A and the TAs were always very nice and happy to answer any questions. The lectures were a bit dry, but the material was interesting.
 I thought this course was thoroughly enjoyable and well taught. Some of the strengths I think were the portfolio assignments and the final project, which really gave us an opportunity to drive home our learning. I also thought the quality of the video lectures was excellent. I would be entirely satisfied if this course remained as is.
 Homeworks, midterms, projects, were strengths. Weakness was some of the video structure.
 Strengths: fun class, not too challenging, and lots of resources to be successful Weaknesses: lectures spent too much time on history and not enough on music theory
 Overall, this is an extremely great class. I would've liked a little more instruction on using Ableton Live software, though.
 *Strengths:* Dr. A is a wonderful professor! Its clear shes an expert on electronic music composition techniques, as well as the breadth of history behind it. The TAs were also great and helpful! Everything was clearly explained, and the course itself was structured very logically. I also appreciated the options and freedom we were given for the final project to choose between reharmonization, analysis of a favorite tune, or making an original composition in Ableton. Also, overall the class was very transparently and fairly graded with clear feedback, and there were bonus-point opportunities as well. Dr. A made herself available after class for additional questions and scheduled TA office hours at convenient times. They were also easily reachable by email. *Weaknesses:* I wish that for the lecture portion we spent more time either going over more music theory, or spending more time doing examples in Ableton. A substantial amount of the course was dedicated to the history of electronic music, which is important, but we really got into the weeds in terms of the successive iterations of synthesizers developed 1970-90. Given the rapid pace of the summer session courses, I really wish we had more time to work on developing skills like working with Ableton and music theory which we can take with us afterward, as opposed to developments of synthesizers that I will likely soon forget. Also, it felt like there was a substantial amount of redundancy across the course materials: lecture slides were posted, then there were the pre-recorded lectures reading directly off the slides, then a substantial amount of the live zoom lecture was dedicated to reading through the slides as well. I feel like the slides and pre-recorded lectures were enough, and students should be accountable for that material before coming to class, with the live lectures exploring the material from another angle. When dealing with Ableton, I think the live lectures could have spent a bit less time pointing out and explaining what each control does (since that was covered *extensively* in the slides which were basically comprehensive Ableton documentation), and using the time instead for things that are best done with a teachers live guidance like composing in Ableton or developing aural skills. Dont get me wrong; these were covered as well, but a lot of the time during lecture was used for things that could be learned equally well outside of class. I wish more of the work had us use Ableton. We only really used it twice, for the midterm and for the final. Its clear this would be more time consuming to grade, but it would be to the benefit of the class.
 The course was laid out in a very organized manner with clear notes. The only issue was as an online course, technology could sometimes go wrong and connection would be lost.
