 Strengths: the content on the exams is identical to that of the psets, which makes studying very accessible and no-surprises. JT makes the lectures rather engaging and fun for a math class. The TF's and JT provide very very little insight when asking help on specific questions, and frequently tell you to just go back and watch the lecture which isn't helpful or constructive; you're very much on your own in this class beyond the lecture and section so there's a fair amount of independent learning.

 Strengths: JT is very approachable, well-informed, and generally engaging teacher; exams reflected classwork Weaknesses: problem sets were always posted a day later than they should have been, which reduced the time we could think deeply about them; TAs occasionally gave different answers to questions (meaning that we would get questions wrong by applying the logic one TA had told us); class seemed rushed (it felt like too much time was spent answering questions/giving opportunities for feedback vs learning material) and did not delve into deeper understanding of the topics, only surface level understanding (would have liked to consistently critically evaluate the data of actual research papers on problem sets/exams)
 Cheating runs rampant among the students in this class and it is incredibly upsetting to the few students, like myself, that believe in integrity and choose not to be academically dishonest. As is often the case with the global major, the students all know each other very very well from various global-oriented extracurriculars. Thus, even when JT assigns 5-6 different versions of the midterms/finals, students in the class easily coordinate groups for each version and complete the assessments together on a zoom call. If I had known about this occurrence earlier, I would have reported it anonymously a long time ago; however, I was not made aware of it until this past week. The person who told me about it said that it has occurred during every midterm and final in both AQA I and AQA II. My understanding is that about 35-40 students are involved. I know that during COVID, it is incredibly hard to monitor cheating so I do not at all blame the instructor. That being said, as one of the students in the class that is not involved in the widespread cheating, I am incredibly disheartened. I have worked very hard in this class and am so frustrated that my peers don't do the same. Aside from it being a blatant and punishable violation of Yale's academic guidelines, it is very unfair to the students in class who did not choose to be academically dishonest. I am sure my exam grades would have been better if I had a group of friends helping me, but I care about integrity and it's frustrating to know that put me at a distinct disadvantage in this class. I hope that the university can look into this somehow, especially considering that many of the cheating sessions took place on Yale zoom and Yale wifi. I am a firm believer in accountability and think that these students have the responsibility to own up to their actions.
