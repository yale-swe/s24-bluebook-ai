Â First, I learned a lot about southern history. We read about (among other things) slavery immediately before and during the Revolutionary War, American expansion and Native dispossession in what is now the Deep South, enslaved Black womens resistance during slavery, secession in Virginia, politics in the Confederacy (particularly womens politics), Reconstruction and Redemption, reformism in the South during the Progressive Era and the rise of the New South, Black organizing in early twentieth-century Florida, communism and the early civil rights movement, segregation and violence, the civil rights movement and backlash in Mississippi, and twentieth-century Miami. It was really great that a lot of the readings engaged with topics, ideas, or perspectives that are often left out of mainstream discussions of each of the time periods that we looked at. It would be impossible to give a comprehensive survey of southern history in a single semester, so I appreciated that this class didn't attempt to do that. Instead, it focused on teaching us all something new by going beyond what you might expect. Second, this class helped me learn how to critique academic books. It was structured so that we read a book (or excerpts from 2-3 books) and a journal article and/or primary source every week, and we always spent part of our class talking about the books as works of narrative writing and historical scholarship. This was super helpful because it gave us a sense of some of the current themes in southern historiography while also allowing us to discuss sources, narrative, perspective, what the authors chose to include/exclude, and other considerations that go into historical research and writing. This all helped me think more carefully about the ways in which historians' decisions about how to write up their research impact the analyses that they produce and present to the world as scholars. This course also helped drive home for me that the South has always been an extremely dynamic place with vibrant and persistent traditions of resistance to oppressive rule. From our readings, it felt like there was has never been a period of southern history when that resistance died out. In addition, this class helped me think about the centrality of violence in so much of southern (and American) history. These feel like important insights to me, and I'm really glad that this class gave me the chance to discuss them with other students throughout the semester.
