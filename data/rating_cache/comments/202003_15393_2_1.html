I felt that the grading system ensured that I was challenged enough to always strive to produce my best work and go out of my comfort zone (class presentations, workshopping final paper, incisive grading comments which challenged me to go beyond superficial thought) while having the scaffolding and support I needed in order to not feel overwhelmed. I thought that the organization and structure of the syllabus was great; we were exposed to so many abstract social theory frameworks that were grounded in tangible concepts that we were studying. We also had discussions about ways to add to the syllabus (ex: by covering diaspora in the Caribbean), which I thought was really interesting. Sasha also did a great job of referencing previous class material and reiterating broader themes of the class throughout the semester. The environment of the class was also conducive to creating a friendly, connected class environment despite the challenges of this virtual semester.
Â Strengths: Each seminar delved deeply into the topic designated to that week, whether it was due to insights from the instructor or from a fellow classmate. As such, discussion was usually rich and engaging without much bland conversation in-between. At some points, it truly felt like we were "making" knowledge. To me, the material was captivating and actually interesting to read and engage with. The instructor also assigned relatively short reading responses that I believed were helpful; we were able to choose which weeks' readings to respond to, and they only constituted a small portion of our overall grade. They essentially acted as a low-stakes means for us to improve our writing skills. Weaknesses: If you have no prior experience with racial and diasporic theory, the introduction to the class is quite jarring. While the instructor did provide support in analyzing the assigned academic readings, there is definitely still a large adjustment period when it comes to digesting academic journal articles pertaining to the class and its inclusive themes. I also found that the class did not cover (or brushed over) some parts of South Asian diasporic history that pertain to the United States, but it seems to me that this was the trade-off in electing to explore other aspects of South Asian American diasporic history in-depth.
