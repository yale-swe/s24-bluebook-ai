2021-Spring AFAM 212 01/ENGL 221 01 Af Am Lit in the Archives

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401 CRN: 20216/20172 Term: 202101

12345Forward
 I learned how to be flexible and responsible when engaging in archival research.
 I gained a lot of important skills on how to do research both in the archives and out of the archives. But, more importantly, I gained important knowledge about how to think critically about my sources and engage with them in dynamic ways, rather than just accepting their biases and methodologies.
 Deep dive into archival work. We learned a lot about the archival process. Also, read and discussed some high quality literature in African American Studies.
 This class exposed me to various works of African American Literature in the James Weldon Johnson Collection and, in turn, I feel like I have more knowledge of the content, politics, and meaning of the works studied as well as an understanding of how connected Black writers in the 20th century were. I learned how to successfully navigate the Beinecke library system, build my analytical ability when engaging with archival material, and greatly improve my writing skills with the reading room exercises. One of the most interesting insights from this class was our acknowledgment of Yale's complicated history regarding the acquisition of the James Weldon Johnson Collection and subsequent discussion about the place of students to engage with this collection ethically.
 We learned how to use the archives for scholarly research; how to interpret archival material from letters to photos to handwriting; how to use the archives as a resource alongside our readings of notable African American literature (e.g. Zora Neale Hurston, Richard Wright, Langston Hughes, etc). One of the most rewarding and tangible practices was going into the Beinecke reading room for various writing assignments throughout the semester. There, we learned how to physically interact with archival material and form our own conjectures about what the author's original intentions, relationships, etc.
 I learned how to use the Beinecke archives and how to best honestly and responsibly use archival materials. I also developed my research writing, as well as trying critical fabulation.
 I got great practice doing research in the Beinecke, and a fascinating look at how archives are put together, what they mean, and the ethical questions involved in archivism that will inform every other academic project I engage with in Yale and beyond.
