2020-Fall HSHM 220 01/HIST 417 01/AFST 220 01 Histories of Confinement

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 11430/11431/12562 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 I liked how this course covered a lot of material and topics. It was interesting to learn how confinement developed from pawnship and slavery to the present day, across the units of asylum, prisons, rehabilitation and hospitals. Our discussions were interesting and encouraged critical thinking. This course's deadline could have been clearer and I would have preferred more frequent smaller assignments throughout the semester rather than 5 papers (including the final worth 30%).
 Everything we covered in the course was engaging and both the professor and the teaching fellows gave us a lot of room to discuss with them and our peers. I think one of the biggest strengths of the course was how much discussion we actually had because it really challenged me to develop my own ideas. I think the only thing that could be improved were lecture slides. Professor Quarshie did not put too much emphasis on dates or titles or names or expect us to memorize them, but I think it would have been helpful to have in my notes for my own reference. The lecture slides were usually only pictures and I think some of those key words and phrases should have been put up on the slides as well
 The class covers some fascinating topics and Professor Quarshie is quite passionate and knowledgeable about everything he teaches. However, sometimes he went quite quickly during lectures and it was difficult to follow. Though this course claims you don't need any prior knowledge, I found that you were more likely to succeed if you had taken history classes that covered these topics before. One thing that could really improve the class is better powerpoints. Prod. Quarshie primarily used them to show images. It was frequently difficult to tell where he was going with his lectures and thus you didn't know what you should or should not be taking notes on. It would be much more useful if he gave an outline of his points in a powerpoint (as well as writing out or specifically defining some of the more difficult terms).
 This class could be improved by having greater engagement. Although everyone seemed intrigue by the topic to some level, at times engagement seemed lacking and it seemed some got disinterested.
 I thought the combination of lectures and the case study breakout sessions along with discussion sections was a unique and effective way of teaching the course! As someone who's unfamiliar with this field, I definitely had a learning curve with the terms and concepts that were talked about in lecture, but the breakout sessions and discussion sections helped to gain a better understanding of the concepts. I also appreciated the Q + A period after lectures for students to ask for clarifications on anything that was discussed.
 It is incredible as it is.
 I found the lectures to be fascinating and sometimes wished we'd had a few more lectures. I also wished the readings had been incorporated more into full-class discussions. I loved the interactive applications of concepts in class and found these very productive.
 Strengths: captivating lectures, discussion-style lecture once a week where we got to observe/analyze archival works and incorporate ideas from our readings and lectures in these analyses, discussion sections were meaningful, and the course content was superb.
 Strengths: response papers and readings are manageable lengths, especially with help from the wonderful TFs; lectures are AMAZING and never boring, really helpful Q&As after lectures with the prof, prof Quarshie knows so much about super niche and fascinating topics, and the 2nd lecture each week is larger discussion/activity that is super rewarding. Weaknesses: I wish we went into ideas of confinement as it pertains to migration/other topics, although it seems like prof Quarshie will try to integrate that into next year's syllabus!
 While the information was very interesting, some of the readings were hard to follow.
 This course excels in outside-the-box thinking. Professor Quarshie brilliantly questions answers that may be technically right, constantly pushing his students to think critically.
 Strengths: -covers a wide range of cases (geographical & historically); from the Asante Empire to present-day life amid the Covid Pandemic Weaknesses: -presupposes prior knowledge about Biopolitics; better grounding readings would have been helpful in addition to the lectures
 Strengths: the topic is incredible. Prof. Quarshie has incredible perspectives about how to weave together very disparate aspects of confinement (from pawning to quarantine). TA's were highly invested in this course and were a great resource for help beyond Professor Quarshie. The assignments were very fair, papers were rather short for a Yale course, and reading was not overwhelming. Weaknesses: Professor Quarshie does read his lectures, which can be a bit monotonous as a result. However, a lot of the things he talks about are very complicated and the slow pace provided by him reading rather than freely lecturing was actually a bit helpful when topics were harder to understand. I am unsure if every week a discussion class instead of a lecture class was helpful. Not everyone came prepared and it was a bit difficult when you were not with people who you knew from your discussion section. Therefore, they could be a bit painful to work through. I think they would be more useful every other week.
 I wish the lectures were recorded. In this way, I could review concepts I did not fully understand from the live lectures. However, the discussion sections were very helpful in clarifying lecture topics. The readings assigned for this class were amazing. I enjoyed them very much and learned a lot from them. I wish we could have read more!
 The course challenges your critical thinking abilities as well as your ability to think outside the the box and view things from different perspectives. One weakness I think was the lecture style present within the first session of the week. At times the information presented didn't seem specific or relevant to the units they were discussing.
 Strengths: the teaching team was very understanding and invested. Weaknesses: sometimes lectures were a bit fast or confusing.
 This course was at times a bit broad and a bit sweeping, so perhaps restricting the topics just slightly and cutting down on reading would help to make it more manageable.
 The strengths of this course were the patience and flexibility of the professor and teaching aides, as well as the opportunity to engage with primary-source material. Essay prompts were relevant to their matching units, and the two books included in the syllabus were very interesting to read through! One weakness was the way lectures were structured. I often found myself getting lost in the note-taking. While I appreciate that Professor Quarshie tried to use more visuals than blocks of texts on his slides, I think it would have been helpful to have slides that explicitly defined key terms and concepts. He did a good job of providing lots of context and quotes, as his main points were rooted in complexity, but it was easy to get lost in the little details. This isn't to minimize the importance of context and background, but rather to suggest a re-organization of the slides so that visuals more clearly connected to main ideas. Students learn differently, and I think the aural approach isn't always enough for everyone to retain the information. Perhaps we could have access to lecture notes to refer back to when reviewing, or when we want to cite his teaching in our papers.
