2021-Spring PHYS 345 01 Intro to Quantum Information

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

 I love that there are weekly Psets to keep me on track with the material. The Psets also directly test for understanding, and I believe are just right in difficulty to make sure we grasp the basics firmly instead of flailing around in confusion. Prof Girvin is also such a wonderful teacher. His love for the subject is truly infectious. I took this class just to fulfill distributional requirements, but Prof Girvin's course (all of it) actually makes me seriously consider taking the field into consideration in my future prospects! The TAs are also the most patient and kindest life savers in the class. No weaknesses!
 Excellent professor! Excellent TAs and wonderful peer tutors! The content is also great! The one thing I would change is that the problem sets were sometimes much harder/ complicated, so I would appreciate if more of that material was covered on the lectures.
 Strengths: Good lectures and notes Weaknesses: Teaching staff provided less assistance than expected.
 Overall, the course was really well taught. As with every class this year, it would have benefited from being in person, where it is easier to discuss and ask questions. The course notes were excellent, and there were prewritten ones posted with the live notes so that students could prepare before lecture, and then review the same material after lecture. The lectures were very interesting, and didn't move too fast or slow.
 I thought it was a very good, and especially for its first year being taught it was very well done.

By the end of the course, I don't know precisely what quantum computing is. The first half of the course barely touched on it, and I wish I had waited for the CS department to offer this course. 3. Midterm had 4 short questions. One silly mistake and you're sitting at a B.

 Prof. Girvin knows his stuff, and for that reason the class was a joy to take. I wish we had had more opportunities to work with the IBM Quantum Composer on our problem sets. A version of this class with Physics 439/440/441 as a prerequisite would be amazing for students who already have a background in Quantum Mechanics/the vector space formalism, because a lot of the first half of the class was devoted to review of those concepts. But I also understand the value of having a class accessible to Computer Science majors or Physics majors who haven't taken QM yet. That said, from my conversations with fellow students it seems like the survey of QM was too fast for students outside the Physics major to really get it, but slow enough that students with QM experience didn't really feel challenged.
 I think the course was very well structured at a high level, starting with basic information theory, and moving on to the basics of quantum information. Lectures followed the course notes mostly, and were easy to follow. Professor Girvin was clear in his explanations of many quantum concepts, and I though the pace of course was just slow enough to properly gain some intuition for a lot of the concepts, like entanglement and phase kickback. The course was a bit weaker near the end, when we were exploring different quantum algorithms. Organization was bit more lacking, although understandably so, and lectures covered high-level concepts rather than details.
 This class gives you a solid understanding of quantum computing and inspires you to be the one to press our progress forward. The content is presented at a good pace and curiosity is encouraged. PSETs get to the essentials and provide good practice; the tests are also very fair. This was easily one of my favorite classes! Professor Girvin is absolutely amazing! He is incredibly passionate, helpful, and cares about the learning of his students. It serves as an amazing introduction to the subject. The course notes and copious provided resources were exceedingly useful. It is a wonderful class and there are not any weaknesses.

 This course explains clearly and in detail all concepts required to understand state-of-the-art quantum computing research, in an order that clearly builds upon previous topics. It also lays a good foundation for the linear algebra and probability knowledge required to analyze quantum systems. The algorithms discussed in class give a good idea of where the quantum advantage lies. The independent project was a great opportunity for students to pursue their own interests in this broad and exciting field. However, it would have been helpful to know the grading criteria and expectations for the project more clearly in advance. It would have also been very interesting to hear more about real quantum algorithms instead of only proof-of-concept ones, as well as about quantum hardware.
