Â This course was structured as an argumentative arena, and that helped stretch my abilities to think about philosophical ideas and express my thoughts better. The price of this approach was that we did not always discuss everything that we wanted to in a given text, but the lectures and the readings themselves easily made up for this.

I feel like I left Philosophy with a thorough understanding of pre-modern philosophy. Aristotle is the one exception to this. The reading of the Categories and the time spent on the Physics were a bit overkill. My only other complaint is the final. It was a tonnnn of work, especially relative to the other classes. It was so hard to fit within the word limit.
Â This class provided a great introduction to a wide variety of philosophy genres and beliefs and was really good at exposing and introducing philosophy to someone who had never studied it before in an interesting and digestible way. A weakness would just be the sheer amount of reading that made it difficult to dive into some interesting aspects of the reading.

Â I appreciated the broad range of authors we read, but it did feel as though all the authors past Aristotle and Plato were crammed together such that we couldn't really get a deep understanding of their individual works and did a lot of reading that didn't end up getting discussed. I would personally benefit a lot more from two class seminars spent on one or two authors than two seminars split between 5 different texts spanning two different schools (I'm thinking of the week that we covered Epicureanism and Stoicism).
Â Very fun collaborative experience, where we all interacted well and formed great bonds. The dialogue within the class was very special and something I really enjoyed. The dialogue aspect really helped me understand philosophy, since I had to make and defend assertions I had.
Â The strengths of DS philosophy are the insightful seminar discussions and rich array of texts to read. As I was studying for finals in a group zoom it was really exciting to consider how different philosophers would respond to questions (like how the soul relates to the body or what the focus should be on in life and if the mean is really best). At times there is an overlap between DS philosophy and DS historical and political thought and while this does provide a bit of a break from the heavy reading load it also gives some texts a lot more time than others. In philosophy especially there is quite an array in experience, background, and comfort with different philosophers which is not a fault with the course by any means, but it can be a bit confusing if, in a seminar, names and ideas you have never heard of are being referenced. In my DS section Professor Levy-Eichel allowed discussions to grow and develop but he also would offer clarification when necessary which helped.
Â Obviously, part of the point of DS is the pace; however, it was sometimes disappointing when we weren't able to truly delve more deeply into some of the fascinating texts that we read--we glossed over a couple of them completely in seminar. I know that some of my classmates suggested spending a little less time on Aristotle and spending a little more on some of the other philosophers that we read; I would echo that sentiment. I don't feel particularly qualified to recommend other philosophers that should be added to the syllabus; I enjoyed most of the authors that we read, and I appreciated the way that later texts would respond to earlier ones that we had read. If anything, I wish we had spent more time trying to really concretely emphasize those connections.
Â I really enjoyed the class discussions and the dynamic between students and professor. Perhaps there could be more frequent small writing assessments over the course of the semester in order to ensure that we are really grasping the arguments presented in the works we are discussing. Sometimes I felt that after a class discussion I retained what we had discussed, but not very much from the rest of the text, because it was either very difficult or I hadn't had the willpower to read it closely enough.
