Â Yes, I would. This course has the potential to be one of the legendary classes Yale is known for - a once niche subject now exploding in relevance taught by the GOAT in his field (Girvin) - but I'd say at the moment the course is good and not quite great. There are definitely still a few kinks to work out, which is totally understandable since this is the first time 345 has been offered (for example, Prof. Girvin revised his course notes, sometimes even retroactively, as the class went on), but quantum computing is such a cool subject that the class was still extremely worth taking. Since it's an intro class with very few prerequisites, people came into the semester with vastly different backgrounds and got vastly different stuff out of it because of that. For example, I took this class after taking all the probability, CS, and linear algebra that 345 assumed but not any of the quantum mechanics (though I did have classical mechanics from 260). The quantum stuff we covered all flew entirely over my head so it really felt like a randomized algorithms class. But it was possible to do all the work without conceptually grasping all the physics, so I still did well despite feeling kinda lost in the lectures. I imagine the flip side is true as well: you can do well in the course with lots of quantum background and learning the more mathy stuff on the fly. But it does mean that people are confused about very different things in office hours so I sometimes started freaking out when someone asked a question seemingly unrelated to anything I had been thinking about. The actual work you have to do is heavily computational and there isn't much of it, so it really made a perfect science credit for me, and I'd highly recommend it to anyone else taking math/stats/CS looking for a relevant and relatively chill Sc class. (If his plans haven't changed, Prof. Girvin will be on leave next spring but plans to offer 345 again in the 2022-23 school year.)
