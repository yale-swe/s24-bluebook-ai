2020-Fall HIST 164J 01 Foxes, Hedgehogs, and History

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 Definitely take this course if you can get in. John Gaddis is one of Yale's greatest thinkers and taking a class with him is a high privilege. The workload is very low, simply consisting of 4 papers, all worth 25% of the grade. The papers are creative and will force you to take the lessons you learn from the class and apply them elsewhere. There is a lot of reading, but it is generally quite interesting and includes a lot of recent literature. If nothing else, take this class to get a peek into the mind of Professor Gaddis and learn from other driven students.
 Yes! Workload is not too bad and Gaddis is a phenomenal professor.
 This is an excellent course, and I would recommend it to other students. I was the only girl in this class -- which was not a big deal, but I only mention it to encourage more women to sign up next time it's taught. Foxes and Hedgehogs sort of evades categories or simple description. We started with Berlin's essay on Tolstoy and the Fox and the Hedgehog, and spent the first half of the course on the theory of grand strategy. In the second half we put the theory into practice, reading books on US foreign policy for the past 100 years or so, and discussing how to apply Grand Stategy to US/China relations and National Security policy. So along the way we covered many disciplines, from literature to philosophy to political theory to politics. I note this because I think I came into the course not completely understanding what we'd be learning, and expecting more politics, and it was a pleasant surprise how much Gaddis emphasized philosophy, art and literature. The assignments are very hands-on, such as crafting our own national security policy and coming up with our own Fox/Hedgehog metaphor. Be prepared to think creatively and spend time on your assignments. If you're worried about grades, you're going to have to work very hard, but it's definitely worth it. Gaddis rewards effort and new ways of thinking. I loved this course, and the other students in the class I talked to also enjoyed it. Email Professor Gaddis early on for a spot!
 I will be recommending this course for my remaining years at Yale. During my freshman year, my advisor and a handful of friends recommended I take a course with Professor Gaddis. The former knew of my future plans with the military and spoke of his serious involvement with student-veterans on campus. The courseload itself was demanding. Never had I been asked to read a book a week. I'm proud of myself for holding up my end of the bargain and took more out of the course because of the strict requirements. My peers were extremely impressive--a handful of them above and beyond--and I learned as much from them as from Professor Gaddis. For me, this was one of those "defining" courses that each student has during their undergraduate years. The combination of the reading list, Professor Gaddis' teaching style, and the crucial fact that everybody wanted to be there made this course a success. It should be noted that on the first day there were three female students "shopping" foxes, hedgehogs, and history. The next week, only one female student, Eliza, returned. I have no idea what that's like, but I imagine it's quite difficult to be the only woman in a room. Diversity of opinion, particularly being open to disagreement, was a leadership principle we covered in-depth this fall. However, given that most of the history we read was written by men, and there were fifteen men (including Professor Gaddis and the Lt. Colonel) in a sixteen-person seminar, we likely had a biased interpretation of the material. There must be underlying reasons why our course had far more males than females--in a similar way that courses in the History of Science and Medicine program are female-dominated. I encourage Professor Gaddis to investigate this because I think it would only add to class discussion.
