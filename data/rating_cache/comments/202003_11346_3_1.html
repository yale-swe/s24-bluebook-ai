Â Professor Chalioti managed to create a culture of cameras on, which I really appreciated. At the beginning of every single class, she asked us to turn on our cameras, and I think that this constant reminder really encouraged/compelled everyone to do so. I would encourage other professors to give their students this constant reminder as well. In some of my other lecture classes, although everyone had their cameras on at the beginning of the semester, by the end, only a handful did, and it was as if the professor was talking into an empty void. This did not happen in Professor Chalioti's class. As a student, I know how desirable it is to turn off the camera during a Zoom call and just listen to others talk. However, I also know that when you do this, it is so easy to just zone out and maybe do other things on the computer or walk around the room. Im definitely a more active listener and participant when my camera is on. When everyone's camera is on, it makes the class feel more like an in-person class, and it makes the class more engaging. In Professor Chalioti's class, since everyone had their cameras on, it felt more like a community, and there was more class participation when it came to asking/answering questions. Professor Chalioti also stayed on the Zoom call for 30 min after each class, but stopped the recording first so that only the lecture, and not these 30 min, would be recorded. I think that this was really helpful because it gave students the opportunity to ask any questions they thought of during lecture, without the pressure/nervousness of asking in front of the entire class. These 30 min kind of simulated a student walking up to the professor at the end of lecture, or walking with the professor back to their office after lecture, in order to ask questions.
