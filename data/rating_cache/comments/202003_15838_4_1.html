 Yes, there valuable information to be learned if you put in the time and effort
 If you're interesting in Classics/the Near East/Egypt/economic history, I would recommend this course, with several caveats. The readings can be really interesting, but a lot of class time is just reinforcing the readings. Some interesting elements of the class (like the object of the day) petered out after a certain point in the semester. It also seemed like a lot of people stopped doing the readings, so our discussions got worse at the end of the semester. We didn't get a lot of feedback on essays (since there was no TA). Without being genuinely interested in the class material, it would be easy to lose motivation. However, the readings will spark many new ideas if you are interested in the material.
 Yes, Prof. Manning is very engaging and knows a lot about ancient history, specifically the Graeco-Roman world and the eastern Med. His lectures are engaging and the guest speakers were very interesting. The essays aren't particularly challenging, so this makes a good 5th class if you find the topic interesting.
 Yes. Manning is a great Professor. If you dont love ancient history, topic is a bit dry, but still a great survey course. Manning is a Yale gem.
 Yes. The course is great for both history and econ students I think
 Overall this class was pretty good. The prof was really nice and the material was interesting and simple. However, note that you do have to put some thought into all the assignments - especially the prompt for the final essay was rough, since you had to cite at least 10 syllabus readings and write a new history of the ancient economy (which is not easy). Would recommend as a chill and interesting 5th class, especially if you like writing essays.
 Yes because as an economics student this was a very refreshing course that allowed me to look at the development of economies and our very definition of them.
 Yes. This course provides a unique perspective of ancient history, one that not only covers what historically happened, but also analyzes why & how changes were made. The readings are interesting, and this course can be a great choice to anyone who is interested in learning more about the unique ancient economies.
 Yes, especially for the student interested in Antiquity; there is so much economic thinking that the ancients had that we do not realize. I would say that it is advisable to have at least some background or interest in Antiquity (even a non-History major could have enough) to engage more fully with the material, but it is definitely fine for a first class in Antiquity.
 I absolutely would! I knew nothing about the Ancient Economy and took this class because a friend spoke wonders of Professor Manning. They were right! Professor Manning managed to make a topic I knew nothing about accessible, without erasing the complexities and nuances of the field (which are many). He was always willing to clarify, to explain things further. He also humanized people who frankly previously felt very foreign to me. Ancient people left a far greater historical record than I had realized, and Professor Manning really helped make that come to life. I would absolutely recommend this class to a friend, but I would also tell them to take any class with Professor Manning. He is great.

 Yes I would. There were things I weren't enthusiastic about, but I think this class has so much potential and might be improved upon in later years. The class material itself is absolutely fascinating and I think provides an invaluable perspective on antiquity and economics that one simply won't get elsewhere. The workload was relatively low--only 3 short papers and readings were average length. I think the class format/structure could be improved, as I think it was easy to fall behind and miss the big picture of Professor's points. There weren't many "lectures" or slides giving us facts about the ancient economy as much as it was seminar discussion about the readings we did, although it was unclear at times why we were doing the readings in the order we did or how it related to the other readings. Overall though, it's a fun, valuable class with not a large workload, and I'm very glad I took it.
 This course is probably best suited for students with some prior exposure in a related field (not that it is required for enjoying or understanding the course content) because the most fun/exciting approach of this course is to combine seemingly unrelated fields of study. Without background in ancient history/economics, this course still is a fine survey of one specific school of thought in ancient economics, possible comparisons would be limited to provided course material.
 Yes I would totally recommend this, especially if you're interested in economic history or pre-industrial history more broadly. And I really think econ majors should take this, to sort of break your mind out of the paradigm of modern economies and force yourself to understand different ways of economic organization. The workload is very reasonable, but the work is very intellectually stimulating and the readings are incredibly interesting and you come away with some great insight every day.
