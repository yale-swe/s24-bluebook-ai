Â So many strengths! First, James Shinn was a huge strength. He did a fantastic job of choosing readings that exposed us to many different ways of writing and researching history, as well as different ways of interpreting various themes in southern history (such as regional variation, what counts as politics, violence, race and class as categories of analysis). He always did a great job guiding our class discussions, and I thought that he struck a good balance between students speaking and him providing additional context or interpretation when helpful. James is clearly someone who's very invested in teaching, and he was very thoughtful about trying to make this class as useful for us as possible; the writing assignments were adaptable to our own research interests within southern history, and he held weekly drop-in office hours where we could come by to talk about the readings or to talk through any of our assignments. I always thought that his feedback on my work was helpful and showed that he was interested in my writing. Personally, I think that the course content was another great strength of this course. Southern history is super interesting, and the readings were engaging and always provided great material for class discussions. (I didnt like the Natalie Ring book that we read quite as much, but that's like one of out of fifteen books that we read at least part of.) This is always up in the air, of course, but I also thought that the students in this class and our class dynamic were big strengths! People seemed really engaged in this class and always had really interesting stuff to say. I had fun with this class and looked forward to it every week! Honestly not sure of any weaknesses. The material was super interesting, we had great class discussions, the instructor was a fantastic teacher and accessibleall great things to look for in a seminar! I suppose that some weeks were geographically focused (i.e., we did a deep dive into secession in Virginia and we talked about the civil rights movement and backlash in Mississippi specifically), so that could be a strength or a weakness depending on your perspective. I thought this case study approach worked well for a seminar, but I could understand it leaving people wanting to learn more about other states. That may not necessarily be a bad thing, though!
