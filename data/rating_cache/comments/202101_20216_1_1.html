2021-Spring AFAM 212 01/ENGL 221 01 Af Am Lit in the Archives

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 20216/20172 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 Strengths of this class were definitely 1) Melissa and Kassidi, who deeply care for the success of their students both in and out of the classroom; and 2) the days we spent discussing different aspects of archival artifacts, which usually occurred on Wednesday. The only weakness was that I wish we had time to discuss the literary texts themselves more, but I also think that that sort of analysis is not the object of this class, so once I realized that I did not see it so much as a weakness.
 The strength of this course is definitely how flexible and friendly Melissa and Kassidi were while also facilitating interesting, fun, and useful discussion. The only weakness was that we didn't get to be in person!
 Strengths: Dr. Barton was a huge strength. She's empathetic, flexible and just an incredible human being. I always felt like I could talk to her about anything and she went above and beyond to make sure everyone was comfortable in the class. She also put a lot of care into the syllabus and the extensive class pages she made for every class. The material in this class was also AMAZING! We read a lot of texts I had been hoping to read at some point anyway -- great pieces of literature like Native Son. I loved all my visits to Beinecke and interaction with archival pieces as well. It was really cool to be physically touching such historic and original pieces -- especially during a pandemic. I loved the assignments we had to do. The workload was high enough that I felt I was putting things together but not so much so that it felt overwhelming. I will also say (and this is big for me), this class has a very respectable and manageable reading load. There were very few weeks I didn't finish all of the reading. This is INCREDIBLY uncommon at Yale. Most classes give out more reading than anyone can possibly do and that usually means I read none of it. Weaknesses: 1000% the people in my class. This was the worst classroom environment of my time at Yale and it had nothing to do with the professor or TA (who's also great). From my first day in this class, I felt people thoroughly judged what others said. They seemed to self-separate into the academic elite. There was a clear divide between those studying the humanities who were well versed in what we were reading and those who were trying to learn from the ground up in this class. Further, NO ONE participated even though they thought they were so much smarter than their peers. This included in breakout rooms which were often quite awkward. Further, I understand and LOVE that we interrogated the ethics of archives including Yale's. However, I often felt like in these conversations, students antagonized Dr. Barton which was unfair. She is not the institution. And the issues with Beinecke's accessibility, history, etc are much more deeply rooted institutional issues. I've never felt such an unwelcoming environment in a class (and again this has nothing to do with the prof and TA).
 I think the workload of this course was really well thought out. Having three page papers removes a burden of stress but simultaneously helped build my concise reasoning/writing skills, which I feel makes me a better writer all around. I also appreciated that there were two final project options and that neither was an overwhelming amount of work. Further, the reading content of the course and reliance on outside primary sources was incredibly interesting and engaging, though I do think it could be difficult to keep up with some of the longer novels we read.
 The strength of the course is in the premise itself. There is a wealth of knowledge and material in the archives at Yale, and tapping into that space is incredibly fulfilling and rewarding. Especially during a semester of Zoom, being able to go into the Beinecke and interact with material there, see material during class (via screen share), and share ideas/material with peers was something I began looking forward to doing each week. A suggestion that I would propose is for more written feedback on our weekly Canvas posts and/or questions. Forming conclusions about what we read/see in the archives is difficult, and I think having more regular feedback as we learn the ropes would be helpful! I think it'd be also interesting to learn about what other classmates have been looking at in the archives -- more info-sharing would be a great added element as well.
 Strengths - very engaging course, good mix of lecturing and discussion. Assignments allow for a lot of freedom of focus, with very detailed feedback. There is also a good balance of the amount of reading we do with the written assignments, and the course moves very smoothly through each topic. Weaknesses - the example projects we were given for the final assignment were very helpful, so I think that some kind of example could have been helpful for the earlier assignments as well.
 This course was fantastic. Professor Barton adapted incredibly well to the challenges of meeting virtually, and made better use of zoom rooms and canvas pages than any other professor I've had. With that said, the course will be even better in some ways, I'm sure, when people can meet in person to look at physical documents together.
