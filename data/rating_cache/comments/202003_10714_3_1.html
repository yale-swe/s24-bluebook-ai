2020-Fall S&DS 555 01/S&DS 355 01 Introductory Machine Learning

Q:

Among the practices in this course geared toward learning during the COVID-19 pandemic, which succeeded and which could be improved? Please comment on course practices that allowed for virtual engagement.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 Format of tests was great. Lectures with jupyter notebooks were great.
 The office hours could be improved to provide more one-on-one support to the students struggling with the coding part of the homework. Also, please get rid of all the ULAs. They were utterly useless. I tried to go to a few of their office hours, and they had no idea what was going on in the class. They were of ZERO help with the assignments (and actually led me down the wrong path multiple times). You don't need the ULAs. You only need the TFs, Wendy and Jerome, who are absolute gems.
 I think the private office hours were crucial to my success, and should be kept in place as long as virtual learning continues. I don't think anything could really be improved.
 Again, one weakness was that lectures were still up an an hour and 30 minutes long. Watching an online lecture is not the same as watching a recorded lecture. I wish professor lafferty broke up the lectures a little more, or did maybe inserted time for "breaks" in the middle of lecture.
 I thought that lectures were pretty well structured, and I appreciated the sample notebooks given to us that we went over during lecture together and could take notes on and run on our own.
 Par for the course
 I watched the recordings of lectures because of a timezone difference, and it worked out well I think. Lafferty encourages questions during lecture still, and the course overall seemed well adapted to pandemic times.
 This is a difficult course and COVID was not helping at all. Usually when I am struggling in a STEM course, I go to office hours, but the feeling of virtual office hours was completely different from in-person office hours, and I found them much less helpful. The benefit of office hours, in my opinion, is the opportunity to receive help from your classmates as well as the TA or ULA. You can be flexible and "float around" different groups of students to get the help you need. This feeling was completely lost in virtual office hours, since only one person can talk at a time. Recorded lectures were actually pretty cool. I liked having the opportunity to fast forward or pause videos and watch them according to your needs. I wish I had been able to attend them live, but since I am in California, they would have been at 6 AM. I would have appreciated it if the class could have been at a more multiple timezone friendly time.
 The recorded lectures, detailed slides, and example code helped teach us the material during a time like this - given the nature of the material, it worked out wonderfully.
 I think that the online recorded lectures were a very good format for this class. It was really nice to be able to reference lectures before exams to re-watch and study. I think that having recorded lectures for programming classes is really helpful even in non-pandemic times!
 The large number of office hours were extremely helpful; Piazza was another really helpful tool.
 This class was taught very well in a Covid semester.
 I think more in-class demos would have been helpful. In the second class, we had the covid data demo in breakout rooms and I think that would have been more useful. I think that you could focus more on applications. The first couple of homework assignments were a lot better because they were more challenging and useful. I think that you could also shorten the beginning of the course so that there can be more time spent on the more useful and challenging methods.
 I think the recorded lectures and extensive office hours provided by TAs, ULAs, and the professor were great. However, I wonder if more basic Python code could be provided in class, because the coding aspect of this course was overwhelming, especially for students with no Python experience. I also suggest that the professor minimize the coding burden of midterm exams
 I definitely support the lectures being recorded - it allowed me to take another course at the same time without sacrificing any flexibility. I thought that the professor and TA's were really adept at virtual teaching.
 The super active class Piazza was a huge boon! Professor Lafferty and the TAs were very responsive and helpful, making it a great forum to ask for clarification about lecture material, help on problem sets, etc. Notes being posted ahead of time and everything being recorded and nicely trimmed were also hugely helpful.
 I much prefer required synchronous with cameras on, and this course was neither.
 Quizzes were confusing.
 The lectures were great in a virtual setting, I would often have breakfast and coffee as I tuned in and took notes. Although that was a benefit of the mic-off & video-off model, I think it also made students more disconnected from others in the class, and made teaching difficult for the professor as well who lacked real-time feedback of students' understandings. In a virtual setting, I find there as also not enough support for assignments, especially for students learning Python for the first time. I'm sure that was different with in-person office hours, but it was more challenging to achieve over Zoom.
 There werent any particular practices that increased engagement
 Posting recorded lectures and python code to review was helpful. It was also very helpful when the professor shared his screen to walk through the Python code.
 I really loved the recorded lectures and the recorded review sessions. This tremendously helped my learning when time zones made it difficult to attend every class event.
