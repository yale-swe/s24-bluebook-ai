2020-Fall GLBL 121 01 Applied Quantitative Analysis

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 The course is good as it is. The only suggestion I have is not to put time pressure on students in exams. The objective should be to test a student's mastery on the subject and time pressure unnecessarily puts stress on the students in the exam, which either leads to errors or inability to answer all the questions , even though the student knows how to solve it. It is unlikely that in the real world anyone has to solve problems on a regular basis with extremely tight deadlines. Those scenarios will be the exceptions and not the norm. Therefore, I would prefer that students are given adequate time to complete the exam by either reducing the number of questions or increasing the time allowed. Let the questions be tough, but give the student time to apply what they learnt in a less stressful environment.

 Strengths: JT and my TF Brian Chung's energy really helped me engage with the class as a less naturally quantitative thinker. Going to office hours with JT when I could was also a very pleasant experiences and I feel that he was very invested in our growth as a learner, meeting us where we are. Weaknesses: I know JT mentioned briefly that many of these choices were related to his own pedagogical style, but taking the course in conjunction with the very differently organized class of ECON 116 was jarring. In ECON, some of the most useful resources were the class piazza board, peer tutors, open office hours where I could listen in to other student's questions and learn from them, and posting content from sections and pset corrections each week. I think I lost a bit of my engagement with AQA because I felt like I was going through the class alone with just one of my friends as a study buddy without those class features that foster community learning. I also didn't love the cold calling in class because sometimes I spent more time being anxious about being called on than thinking about the theory questions. While some may find this engaging or positive pressure to do the readings, it felt a little bit like it was stoking the infamous competitive culture of global affairs. This may seem overdramatic, and it really wasn't too bad getting called on when you didn't know the answer, that it did add on that mental component.
