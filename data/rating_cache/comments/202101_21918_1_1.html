 The course material is presented in an extremely logically and precise manner. However, sometimes too much emphasis is placed on mathematical derivations and not enough on conceptual understanding.
 good class on e&m and moore is a great professor. alot of class felt like it was of derivations, but that is as much as I expected.
 The textbook (Griffiths) is really great, and the professor is really good at keeping up engagement and explaining things in a clear manner. When I got to the exams, I really felt like I understood what we had covered and why, which was in part due to the layout of the textbook. Also, the problems on the problem sets were creative and had cool relations to actual lab work going on at Yale. The psets seemed pretty long some weeks, but overall I think the pacing and everything was good.
 Professor Moore is fantastic, he is a great lecturer and explains concepts very clearly. I looked forward to coming to every class because he made the material interesting and understandable.

A very no-nonsense class that you can get a lot out of if you wish. Weaknesses: I would definitely have made more use of the TF office hours if they were longer or more frequent (an hour a week is the lowest I've ever had for a Yale STEM class).
 I think Professor Moore is an amazing professor. He was extremely talented at breaking down tough concepts and simply explaining the examples. Furthermore, I think that this course was structured well, and the material naturally built upon itself. Over time, I was amazed at how much I learned in the class. The course is tough (exams were always extremely hard), but interesting and well organized. I think something that could make the class more manageable and allow for more interaction is to have more office hour and study hall sessions.
 The class is taught very closely to Griffiths which is a strength given the textbook is a great treatment of E&M. Moreover, Moore does a great job at covering the textbook and even his pset questions are super interesting and full of footnotes that show you the real world applications of what you are learning.
 This class was organized very well and did a great job working parallel with the textbook. Professor Moore is as good as any physics professor at Yale in explaining complex topics and preparing very interesting lectures.
