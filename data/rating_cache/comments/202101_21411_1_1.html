2021-Spring ANTH 110 01 Intro to Cultural Anthropology

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 21411 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 I believe the breadth of readings for this course was definitely a strength. I also thought the exams were well thought out and an accurate test of my knowledge.
 This course brought students around the world to provide examples of societal phenomenons and issues that are applicable to multiple societies. I wish that we read or learned more about native anthropologists, or read more texts that did not come from a eurocentric colonialist view, even though historically the socio-cultural anthropology field consisted predominantly of white men.
 This course was fantastically taught by Prof. Harms and the TA's. They were always welcoming and able to help, and they assigned projects that were helpful for understanding the material but also enjoyable. I wish some of the readings were cut short. For a class that seemed to be an overview of a wide range of anthropological topics, some readings went in depth to the point where it felt like a lot of the material was simply unneeded, which cut time away from actual analysis/thinking I could've done.
 Professor Harms is very funny and kind. One potential weakness is that the readings are sometimes not discussed enough in lecture/section
 Strengths: The readings were very interesting and engaging. Lectures were super fun and came in a multi-media format (listened to some songs, saw videos and a couple movies, class participation and storytelling, etc.). The professor was very engaging and the choice of topics was diverse; we got a good taste of everything. I enjoyed attending discussion sections. Weaknesses: Sometimes I wished the assignments were not so Covid-centered. Overall amazing course!
 The strengths was the professor who was very knowledgeable and also the very clearly structured course. I cant think of any weaknesses.
 Strengths: Very understanding professor and engaging assignments and sections. Weaknesses: A lot of weekly reading that is hard to keep up with in addition to other classes.
 Really engaging and informative lectures and concepts, professor Harms and all the TAs are super nice and helpful. The ethnographic assignments were also really interesting to do!
 Professor Harms is an amazing person, and his passion for anthropology comes out whenever he teaches. He takes great care with words, and breaks down concepts in a way that is understandable.The readings were interesting and tied in with what was taught in the lectures, and that continuity was really nice.
 Professor Harms is a gem! He's an incredibly good lecturer, is deeply passionate about anthropology, and truly makes you care about the subject. The assignments are also quite fun, since they involve talking to and observing people. The readings are very interesting, though often lengthy. No real weaknesses imo, perhaps the timed exam that was a bit stressful, but Professor Harms is very understanding and tried to relieve lots of the stress.
 This course was very structured around student learning which was a major strength
 Section was most definitely needed in this course. I feel like it could have been even more discussion-based.
 The main strength of this course is that the assignments due are all hands-on projects that allow students to actually experience the kinds of things that a cultural anthropologist does. The class has less of an emphasis on rote memorization and more of an emphasis on actually experiencing things.
 Professor Harms and the TFs were wonderful. They understood that this semester was incredibly difficult for students and were so kind and empathetic. Their flexibility made the class and by extension my learning more enjoyable.
 The readings that professor Harms picks are fantastic and always are worth reading. Even when I was strapped for time, I never regretted taking the time to look through the readings before class because they are either quality ethnographies that challenge problematic anthropological norms, or they are critical texts which also present interesting critiques of ethnographic research. I feel like this could be a strength or a weakness, but the class feels very broad and unfocused at times. There is a lot of theory and sometimes feels devoid of tangible application. However, these moments are rare for the most part and Harms does a good job connecting the theory to the real world and telling us what all the theory he is telling us about really means in a practical sense. But it is an intro class so there is less ethnography and more theory about it.
 The lectures and sections were very engaging. I enjoyed being able to discuss with the professors and TAs after the lecture and section.
 I think the course was very engaging and in some ways always related to us either as students or humans. One thing I think could be improved would be to make the selection of readings and examples more well-rounded and global, as most of it was related to Asia. this is understandable considering professor Harms' expertise but it could benefit from other examples from Africa or Latin America as those backgrounds make up a population of students , as well.
 The course was so thoughtfully planned, and also adapted well to talk about different real-world events that were going on at the time. Immaculate planning.
 The course has meaningful assignments that incorporate lecture content, readings, and fieldwork experiences. The professor provides clear expectations for each assignment and extremely detailed instructions. One improvement for the course is to remind students of an upcoming written assignment deadline during lecture. This reminder could just be a slide at the beginning of the PowerPoint, or on the side of the title slide, that students can see (10 seconds is all).
 Strengths of this course include the professor. He was absolutely brilliant and extremely understanding in a time that demanded some compassion. Weaknesses of the course would be how abstract it is. This might just be the nature of Anthropology itself, but there were many abstract concepts that all seemed very scattered and difficult to understand, and we spent more time on examples than the concepts themselves.
 I think one of the strengths is that we get to read, analyze, and learn about various ethnographies and cultures, which I found very interesting and always kept me engaged. I think a little more time could have been spent on the key ideas such as structuralism and structural functionalism.
 I found some of the earlier concepts, readings, and lectures to be a bit pedantic. It was kind of hard thinking about anthropology in the abstract. But when we started discussing real-world applications of anthropology (Investment bankers, homeless drug users, Prof. Harms experiences in Vietnam) in the latter half of the course, I got much more engaged.
 The strengths of this course were its curriculum and the wonderful teaching team. Every lecture was so carefully thought out and respectful toward its subject matter. Professor Harms and the teaching team were the same, guiding us with empathy through complex material in a difficult semester. The weaknesses of the course were that, sometimes, Professor Harms didn't always explain the assignment during class, leading to some confusion on guidelines and due dates throughout the semester. But these were minor concerns in an overall great semester!
