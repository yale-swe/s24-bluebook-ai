 Lots of real analysis (Lebesgue measure and measurability, Lebesgue integration, rigorous Fourier analysis and an application in the heat equation, and some miscellaneous topics like some general measure theory, Hausdorff measure, and the ergodic theorem.
 Knowledge about real analysis and measure theory.
 So, so much. We began with Lebesgue outer measure, Lebesgue measurability, Lebesgue integrals, and convergence theorems. Then, we did applications like Fourier series, the heat equation, and Weyl equidistribution. Finally, we did more general measure theory, including the Carathodory Extension Theorem and the Hahn-Kolmogorov extension theorem. We got a little taste of the Ergodic theorem at the end.
 Lebesgue measure, Lebesgue integral, Fourier series, some general measure theory
 We began the class by developing notions of measurable sets and constructing the Lebesgue measure. Lebesgue integration and the related convergence theorems. A good chunk on Fourier analysis - namely, we learned a lot about the behavior of Fourier coefficients and the convergence of Fourier series. Then, a fun hodge-podge of topics related to measure theory: applications of Fourier analysis in solving the heat equation; Weyl's equidistribution theorem; introduction to general measure theory; fractals and fractal dimension (this was a lot of fun); basic concepts in ergodic theory.
 How to write rigorous proofs
 Measure theory (mostly focusing on Lebesgue, though a nice intro to general measure theory towards the end), integration (in the measure-theoretic sense; so touching on lots of pathological examples that you don't see in earlier classes, as well as tools to deal with these) for the first half of the course, and L1 & L2 spaces. The second half was a fair amount of Fourier analysis, including an application to the heat equation; a week on fractal/Hausdorff dimension; a week on general measure theory; and an amazing week on ergodic theory.
 L1 and L2 spaces, lebesgue measure, etc
 I learned about Lebesgue measure and integration on the real line, including outer measure, measurable sets and functions, integral convergence theorems, the connection to Riemann integration, the vector spaces L^1 and L^2 on R and on [-, ], Fourier series of integrable periodic functions and when these converge to the functions themselves, and other assorted topics including solutions to Fourier's heat equation, general measure spaces, Hausdorff measure and the ergodic theorem.
 Most of this class was spent building up machinery to work with the Lebesgue integral. We started by coming up with a reasonable definition for Lebesgue measure, and proving some basic results about it. Then we used this measure to define an integral. Then we spent a decent chunk of time proving some convergence theorems for Lebesgue integrals and relating it to the Riemann integral and the derivative. Then we used our new integral to do build some Fourier series, and applied our results fro Fourier series to the heat equation. We finished the semester with some generalizations to general measure spaces, and a brief look at Hausdorff measure and at dynamical systems.
 In this course I learned everything there possible could be to learn about lebesgue measure, integration, and Fourier analysis. We also finished that material with a good month left, so we had time to learn an application of Fourier analysis (the heat equation), about general measures and measure spaces, algebras and sigma algebras, Hausdorff measure and ergodicity.
 - intro to measure theory - Lebesgue integration - fourier series - Heat equation
 Learned a lot about fundamental theorems and ideas in analysis, got a lot better at proof writing.
 MATH 305 provides a thorough study of measure theory and integration. In the first half of the semester, we learned about Lebesgue outer measure, Lebesgue measurability, the Lebesgue integral, convergence theorems, and the function spaces L1 and L2. In the latter portion, we focused on the convergence of Fourier series, the heat equation, general measure spaces, Hausdorff measure, and ergodicity for dynamical systems.
 Learned proof techniques in analysis. Analysis proofs feel so much more intuitive now. I find them more fun than proofs in any other field of math I have studied.

 Lebesgue Measure Theory and Integration, Fourier Series, General Measure Theory, and some Ergodic Theory/other miscellaneous topics (like the Heat equation)
 Learned real analysis with an emphasis on measure
 Those course is a rigorous introduction to serious analysis, not just basic epsilon-delta proofs but seriously understanding little details about approximation techniques and the power of an epsilon of room. This course also teaches Lebesgue measure as well as the integral over the real line and the corresponding convergence thms. After we gain a good foundation in Lebesgue integrals we move on to Fourier series and then selected topics using the new powerful integral and measure we have worked so hard to understand. These topics included hausdorf dimension, general measure theory/sigma-algebras, as well as some ergodic systems and the heat equation. Throughout the course I rarely felt that I fully understood a topic after lecture, this is no fault of Minsky, he is wonderfully clear, but rather just that this material is difficult but very fundamental and necessary if you want to truly start to understand the importance of analysis. Also the psets in this course are beautifully design to make sure you learn the material well, down to the last epsilon of detail.
