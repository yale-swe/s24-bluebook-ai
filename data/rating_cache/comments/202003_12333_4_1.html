 This class is an example of how Yale excels in theory -- there are some practical applications in the course, but mainly it's about the title: automata theory. If you interested in math, want to get some more practice writing proofs, or need a good CS elective that doesn't require programming, 10/10 would recommend this course. There are weekly psets which can get to be a little much at times, but they really aren't that difficult and do a good job of illustrating the concepts in class.
 I would recommend this to any student interested in the crossover between computer science and mathematics. This class blurs the line between the two more satisfyingly than any other I've taken. I would recommend this to any computer science student interesting in the theoretical side of the field. This class won't improve your programming in a very meaningful way, but I am left with a better understanding of what computers are capable of. If you've ever wondered about the absolute theoretical problem-solving capacity of a computer, or loved the Turing machine problem set in CPSC 201, or thought the halting problem was interesting to think about, this is the class for you. This class is very low-workload - none of the weekly problem sets took me longer than 90 minutes or so to complete. This is honestly perhaps a negative of the course - it definitely could afford to go a bit faster and cover more material.
 Absolutely recommend this course to anyone in the CS major, but especially those who enjoyed the parsing/grammar/turing machine sections of CS 201, or are generally interested in programming languages, low level computer architecture, etc. The class doesn't get super practical or technical about these, but the concepts taught are enjoyable and really cool.
 I would definitely recommend this course to any student looking for an introduction to theoretical computer science and formal languages, or really to anyone looking for a fun and approachable mathy and/or logicy computer science class. It is not incredibly difficult or time-consuming, but it is definitely very engaging and interesting. It is certainly a good choice if you want something different from traditional computer science classes, yet it is not anything like most math courses either. Its really a unique and fulfilling experience. Meanwhile, due to its reasonable workload, it is a good choice for pairing with an intense programming course like 323. Furthermore, I think it provides a good entry to a more in-depth study of the field. However, I would not recommend it to someone with a lot of experience in these topics, as it is not super rigorous or formal. On the other hand, it is definitely useful to have some previous exposure to proofs and theoretical reasoning, but it does not have to be from the direct prerequisites (just ask Professor Bridy if you are unsureI almost didnt take it for this reason until I sent him an email). But as an introduction to a new field of computer science, as it was for me, I would highly recommend it!
