Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

 classes were slightly unorganized, with little text on the slides (just images to just follow). classes often moved too quickly, and having the lectures not be recorded did not help studying at all. second half of class mostly focused on oil and renewable energy, but seemed to be repeating the same information/themes over and over again.
 I think the strengths of the course are that the syllabus is very well designed, it is a very thorough and comprehensive course, and it really helps teach writing skills and the whole research process. Additionally, this course draws people from a wide variety of backgrounds, making discussions particularly interesting. The only weakness I could see someone stating is the workload: there is a lot of reading, weekly writing, and both a final paper and a final exam. I will say though that none of the reading necessarily felt extraneous, and every piece of it was interesting. But it is something for prospective students to be aware of, particularly those looking for a lighter workload or not used to heavy reading.
 Strengths: The content is interesting Weaknesses: leminar format, often felt like we would spend a lot of time on one thing and skim or skip over multiple decades
 I really liked the research element of the class and how Prof Sabin guided us through completing our own research for the final paper. The readings were also great. I would suggest more structure in the second half of the semester to help students make sense of the complex international relations surrounding the teaching units on oil.
 I honestly really enjoyed this course! Classes were just the right length to cover a lot of material, but not be too overwhelming. Although readings were sometimes long, I felt that they all provided meaningful insight into class topics. I found the primary sources we studied in class especially fascinating. Dr. Sabin is a really engaging lecturer, and discussion section was genuinely very fun and added to my engagement with the course. Plus, the guests he invited to class were super interesting! The one aspect of this course that was a bit negative was the final -- for most of the semester, we were supposed to have a take-home essay that was a primary source analysis. But, about a week before the final, the professor changed it to a timed Canvas exam with many essays based on class content. This was quite a stressful change, but I'm not sure how relevant it would be when this course is no longer taught online.
 Strengths: learned a lot, tons of material to refer to, TFs were great Weaknesses: sometimes so much work and so much reading that to feel prepared for the course you'd need to be reading hundreds of pages a week.
 A lot of people dislike the leminar style but it's one of those things that in the end is better for you. While it might feel like it interrupts the flow of lecture, it forces you to engage with the ideas, and at least for me, that's a strength! Some would say this is the weakness though.
 The classes did not feel long enough to get a complete understanding of everything involved and would have greatly benefitted from being 90 minutes long. For what it was, the course crammed a lot of information into 50 minutes and made it easily accessible.
 Incredibly thoughtful class construction and highly knowledgeable
 Strengths: super interesting, writing exercises helpful, teaching team was great, class and reading materials were all varied and insightful Weaknesses: not enough writing structure for the final essay (deadlines for pre-draft items weren't super useful)
 The break-out rooms gaurenteed a good level of participation and synthesis. I also liked talking to classmates because it made me more likely to raise my hand in lecture. Lecture sometimes ran over a few minutes, but overall the class was really good. I also really liked the sequence of preparation for our final research papers. I was very prepared well in advance.
 Strengths include the interesting & informative readings, Prof. Sabin and his amazing TF team, the engaging lecture slideshows, and the American Energy History website. One notable improvement could be recording the lectures and/or making the slideshows available on a lecture-by-lecture basis.
 Pretty good class overall, there were a lot of breakout discussions which were nice for discussing concepts together. The content is interesting even if you're not invested in history or energy history. It's also a light WR only like 2 proper essays and weekly reading responses that are participation-based. Prof. Sabin does go through content quickly, but slides are shared before the midterm essay and the final exam.
 I think this course was great! I would have loved a bit more of instruction on the process for writing final papers, as I had never written a major paper like this before and felt a like I was flying by the seat of my pants at times.
 I loved how this course had breakout rooms. I thought that it nicely wove in the readings into the lecture through this format.
 I liked the fact that we looked at culture, technology, government, and social movements as a whole. I think section discussions could be more reflective of the examination materials.
