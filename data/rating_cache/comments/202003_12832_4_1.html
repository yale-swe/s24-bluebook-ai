2020-Fall EPS 101 01 Climate Change

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC409 CRN: 12832 Term: 202003

Back12345
 Absolutely. Whether you're interested in EPS or you're looking for good science credit, it's literally not possible to be a citizen of the world without having a good background in climate science and climate change. The instructors are super personable and genuinely care about any questions or concerns you have, and this course has really helped me solidify my understandings while also exposing me to a lot of the uncertainty that is actually out there about climate change and ways to adapt or mitigate it.
 I would recommend this course if you're interested in the science behind climate change. However, this course doesn't cover the societal aspect of climate change (policies, effects on people, environmental injustice, etc.). I would've liked for this class to discuss more about these aspects. The workload is manageablereadings aren't too long and response papers (while annoying) are fairly easy, especially because you work with a partner. The midterm and final papers are definitely stressful, considering this is an intro class. The professors are very chill and kind, which is what made class enjoyable.
 Definitely. I got a comprehensive understanding of the basics of the science of climate change and can now read and write papers on climatology more effectively.
 I would 100% recommend this course to another student. Whether you are interested in majoring in earth and planetary sciences or a non-major, this course is a wonderful introduction to the field in a small, seminar-like format. There were so many unforgettable experiences we shared this semester. Being over Zoom, we were able to hear from Hurricane Hunters directly, talk to the primary authors of major headline-driving climate science studies, and speak with Ph.D. candidates who are in the field conducting research. Mary-Louise and Noah are amazing professors and people, and are great mentors and advisors. For someone like me who had very little experience writing scientific papers, they were really helpful in getting me to narrow down my topic of interest.
 Absolutely. Unfortunately this class was a limited enrollment seminar, but everyone who can take this class should. While those focused more on policy and the humanities might find the class too scientific and technical (in which case, look elsewhere), those interested in learning about climate change in its interdisciplinary scientific nature will love this class. Intersecting with some geology, biology, chemistry, and physics, climate change is the perfect way to integrate multiple fields of scientific study with an all-too urgent and consequential global issue. You will also learn a lot about how various types of climate change research is done, how to read research papers, and how to communicate scientific and technical findings and knowledge with the general public. It's an all-around really good class.
 I would only recommend this course to another student if they were really interested in learning about climate change from a scientific perspective. Do not go into this course expecting to learn about it from a policy perspective because you will be disappointed. Other than that, the professors really tried to make this course interesting and it was clear that they had a passion of the subject. This class just wasn't the right fit for me.
 I would absolutely recommend this course to every student. It was a challenging but not at all overwhelming science credit that helped me think critically about climate change. Both of the professors are so engaged and give clear and exciting lectures. Loved this class
 Yes, absolutely! This class is worth every minute; and there might not be another class at Yale that you remember more! The material is so important, and it is brought to life in an exciting way. The professors encourage you to ask questions, and this opens up so many new avenues of explorration As a STEM major I loved this class and learned something new in each lecture/paper.
 Yes! I think during a non-virtual semester, this class would be a lot better, but I still learned so much about climate change and also just research papers in general, so I think it's a great course for both STEM and non-STEM students alike. The workload is relatively light (just a single 1-2 page paper a week with a partner and two longer research papers throughout the semester) and the topics are really engaging and also incredibly important for every student to know.
 YES!! If you're interested in climate change and want to learn about the science of climate change, this is the class for you! You don't need much of a science background, this class is made for people who are in humanities. But you learn a lot. You learn to read scientific papers, think critically about the science of climate change and how to write about complex scientific topics for the general reader. If you have the chance to take this class, take it!!!
