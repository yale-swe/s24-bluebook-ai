2021-Spring HSHM 206 01/HIST 114 01 History of Reproductive Health

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401 CRN: 20259/20277 Term: 202101

12345Forward
 I learned SO MUCH in this class! This course is full of incredibly important history that really is not taught in other classes (which, after taking this class, is absolutely infuriating and unacceptable). Every single unit was full of new and really really fascinating information that was delivered in such a thoughtful way. Rich also does an amazing job incorporating primary sources in a way that makes students comfortable using them. I really learned how to analyze these sources well in this class. This class also really challenges students to think about this history really critically, constantly connecting it to our current health systems.
 I learned more about the racial biases in medicine, specifically womens reproductive medicine, from the 1800s through present day.
 I learned about the history of reproduction in America from the 17th century until now. We examined this history from the lens of various races and ethnicities of women, physicians, midwives, activists, and men. We also focused on the changes in attitudes relating to birthgivers' positions in society, physicians' statuses in society, birth control's place in reproductive rights, and advancements in reproductive science and technology.
 Having never before studied history of reproductive health, this course opened my eyes to the depth of social and political history around various topics ranging from the development of birth control to the shift from home to hospital births, to the ways in which race and class deeply impact reproductive health outcomes and histories. Professor Rich offered an intersectional lens on reproductive health, connecting it to US histories around slavery, Puerto Rico, and colonization/imperialism.
 I learned so much in this course: multiple histories of reproductive health in the US, how to think critically about historical content and reproductive health, and how these histories affect reproductive health in the present day.
 This course created space for us to think critically about reproduction and the ways in which historical context frames how the process of pregnancy, birthing, and women's bodies are conceptualized and regulated. As someone studying biology, I found this course to be so incredibly important for future physicians and researchers, as well as policymakers and honestly every community member. I am so appreciative of the frameworks that this class introduced me to and illuminated how deeply issues of reproduction affect society and individuals' experiences.
 I learned so much in this class. I have always been interested in reproductive health care but this class provided such an in depth and nuanced perspective on the history of reproductive health. Everything I learned in this class built on past knowledge to give me a really well rounded understanding. Loved!
 A sense of the themes in American reproductive medicine and healthcare from the early 1800s to the present day and how legacies from the past continue to influence today's healthcare system; close-reading of primary sources; drawing connections between time periods and looking for moments of rupture/continuity.
 I learned so much about the history of reproductive medicine and reproductive activism and historical conceptions of motherhood and childbirth, and gained really valuable insight into the origins of the medical institutions I engage with regularly.
 A general history of women's reproductive health in the U.S., a plethora of resources to continue exploring this topic beyond the classroom, an abilityi to contextualize this history within U.S. history and legacies of eugenics, a new habit of ending lectures with the intent to ask myself what questions regarding this topic can I now attempt to answer.
 I learned so much about the foundations of medical authority in the United States such that I view medical topics critically now.
 I didn't realize how little I knew about the complexities of reproductive health history in America, but I feel as if I know a lot more than I did before about the areas of history typically left out. I have a lot of new respect for the black women we learned about in this class, and the struggle that was given to get to the place we are today. I think this class was extremely enlightening (and sobering) in that regard.
 a really great introduction to all the major parts of history of reproductive health in US, starting from slavery to current news
