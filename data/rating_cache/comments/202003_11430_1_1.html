2020-Fall HSHM 220 01/HIST 417 01/AFST 220 01 Histories of Confinement

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

 How to compare multiple readings and integrate them with my thesis for papers
 This course challenged me to think about all of the structures that exist in our society, and that we take as normal, in a different way. I loved this course because it was a great combination between a survey course and a more focused course. We covered multiple different types of confinement in a very deep way.
 This class does a great job of introducing various types of literature and media to get a more holistic view of what confinement is and how and by who confinement has been used.
 I learned about various institutions of confinement throughout history and ones today and the power dynamics/imbalances that exist within those. I also learned to challenge ideas/concepts of confinement being simply a physical space or as the opposite of freedom which allowed me to reflect upon institutions or spaces of confinement in everyday life. I learned important terms and concepts which allowed me to critically think about the social and cultural contexts of those being confined and institutions doing the confining such as biopower, pawnship and panyarring, spritual pawnship, austerity, performance of power, utilitarianism, and many more. I will definitely be able to apply many of these concepts and queries from this class to future courses and also thinking about the world around me.
 I feel I have a completely new perspective on so many things. By far one of the most insightful courses I have taken.
 I gained a broad understanding of how methods of confinement have come into being in context of colonial West Africa, the USA, Latin America, and Canada. I learned about the neoliberal state, welfare v. workfare, insurance, pawnship, slavery, rehabilitation, re-education, and many other iterations of state-individual confinement. I gained many new insights on how confinement has been shaped through time, governments, and systems of power.
 Great course on the histories of systems of confinement, including the asylum, prison, rehabilitation centers, and hospitals. The course provided insight into the means by which confinement shapes our every day societies, are often rooted in power and control, and can be punitive and/or beneficent. The course also provided tools to help us analyze mechanisms of confinement including the structures used to confine individuals, the institutions at play, and the tools/systems/ideologies used by these institutions.
 Analyzing notions of confinement in a variety of settings (psychiatric institutions, prisons, asylums, shrines, schools, hospitals, social distancing). Being able to dissect complex ideas about what confinement is, understanding systems of confinement and how they are perpetuated
 I learned how to define the concept of confinement in its design and application.
 The history of confinement is far more nuanced than if someone is actually confined. The powers that result in confinement are far more than the dyadic 'ruler' versus 'ruled,' rather they are everywhere and exist in a state of ongoing negotiations.
 I gained a wide array of knowledge regarding the valuation of human life, manhunting, biopower, and confinement. My writing skills were strengthened by a focus on political philosophies and other frameworks.
 I learned about different examples and modes of confinement throughout history. This course showed me how to view society and its systems from a new perspective. I also practiced my critical reading and writing skills.
 I believe I gained a keen perspective as to evaluating the mechanisms and conditions that surround structures of oppression. It introduced me to ways of thinking about and engaging them beyond their immediate concrete makeup. That is to say it showed me how oppression and confinement, specifically, are not clear cut.
 I learned a lot about philosophy and how to analyze history from a critical lens. We spent a lot of time discussing confinement, which was extremely relevant in many different ways during 2020.
 This course was so information dense. I had previously had no exposure to the subject matter, and we undertook an extremely sweeping survey of the topic of confinement across time and the entire world, and yet Professor Quarshie did not sacrifice the intellectual rigor of the way in which we discussed the topics. At times it felt like whiplash, but I'm so glad to have been exposed to these ideas.
 We covered systems of confinement including West African slavery, prisons, hospitals, asylums, rehabilitation centers, re-education/integration schools, and racial quarantining/social distancing during the bubonic plague. Our discussions centered on manhunting; we complicated the binary between confinement and freedom and came to the conclusion that they're not polar opposites, but rather conditions within a larger cycle. Other large themes included the effects of austerity, the work of performance and power-relations, memory as an alternate source of history (thus, histories, plural!), theory versus practice, the value of human life, colonialism, neoliberalism, projects of modernity, the establishment of order, and the disruption of time as linear.
