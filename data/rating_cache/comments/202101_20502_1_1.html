Â Strengths: the class' pace. Some students clearly struggled with the pace of the course, but I thrived. I think if it were any slower I wouldn't have as strong a grasp on the grammar, or more generally, the language. You really have to know what you're getting yourself into when you take a class called "Intensive Russian," especially when marketed as a course for students with "superior linguistic ability." This is no joke. Also, Constantine and Ilia are fantastic teachers. I think Zoom certainly hindered their abilities greatly, but Constantine knows his stuff inside and out, and coupled with Ilia's engaging teaching style and use of multimedia, it worked really well. Weaknesses: the amount of homework became a chore. Felt tedious. Some days we had upwards of 15-20 pages of workbook work to complete, and it wasn't teaching or reinforcing anything. Also, we started having to teach ourselves a lot of the material, then to be tested/quizzed on it, which is not right in a language class. The point of class time is to learn the material, and we did not always do that. We'd spend needless minutes answering inane questions or going over extremely simple concepts, to the detriment of actually learning the most important and most difficult things. We never really spent adequate time on participle formation and usage. And reported speech was barely touched upon in class; I learned most of it through the quizzes/tests -- which is definitely not how it should be. And much like the previous iteration in the fall, the disconnect between Constantine and Ilia was palpable. Especially when it came to homework -- turn in homework to Constantine, and w/o fail in 48 hours you'd have it back. Turning it in to Ilia, however, was a futile effort, as it'd be a real game of Russian roulette. Is he going to give it back or not? Did I waste my time or not? And lastly, the breakout room partnering up stuff -- some students were really, quite frankly, sucky partners to have in a language class. You could get a delightfully engaged partner, or someone completely checked out, lost, and not aware of what was going on. Really doesn't serve any party well in a language class when you have to carry the weight and then report back to the whole class what you discussed... if you didn't discuss anything! But alas, that is a problem with most language classes.
Â The class does a fantastic job of leading you to understand grammar in a deep and instinctual way. We practiced it a lot in class and it was really cool to see early on in the semester my peers using all 6 cases in a sentence naturally after learning them only last semester. After finishing this class (including last semester's 125), I realized that although we got speaking practice and sentence-construction practice in class and homework, we are kind of trained to only speak in a particular way; although we're very familiar with certain constructions and talking about specific topics like those treated in the plot of the video companion to this class or units that we spent lots of time on like travel and city life, I felt at the end that I am somewhat limited by these topics. This is all to say that I think there should be a much more heavy emphasis on being creative with your speech and writing -- one presentation about politics with tons of unfamiliar vocab and a couple non-templatic writing prompts in the last few homeworks doesn't quite cut it. The major rebuttal to this point is that we don't have time for this in this class, and that's valid, but I think the class would be truly intensive and get you up to an advanced level if it was restructured to consistently integrate this. This is OK though -- I presume that L5 courses help expand your range of ability and in general this class is a superb foundation for further Russian study and practice. After only one year, it's great to feel somewhat close to fluency and feel like I just need one more push, like a trip to Russia or a structurally different L5 class, to get there.
