2020-Fall ANTH 242 01 HumanEvolutionaryBio &LifeHist

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401 CRN: 12550 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 By taking this course, I was able to appreciate human diversity and learn how to reject the standards of normalcy, through an evolutionary lens. Professor Valeggia structures the course in a manner where everyone will leave with a general takeaway. If you want to get into fine details explaining concepts, she provides the framework for this as well. She is very honest when she says students are in charge of their own learning. Throughout the semester there were many moments to genuinely reflect on what we learned and connect content into our lives. I have learned more in her course than I have in most courses at Yale because she knows how to facilitate real learning without stressing students out.
 Exploring biology from an evolutionary perspective.
 This course will teach you some of the basics of evolutionary biology.
 I have greatly expanded my knowledge of evolutionary biology, and have gained skills in reading scientific journals, experiments, and graphs.
 Concepts of human evolutionary biology, as well as sociocultural aspects
 Evolutionary medicine, epigentics, reproductive ecology, life history theory, etc.
 As someone who had little experience in evolutionary biology, I gained a much better understanding of various evolutionary biology topics like epigenetics, biological rhythms, human evolution, human diets, etc.
 I developed a simple knowledge about human evolutionary biology. The effects of stress on human health. One topic that stood out for me was the Race based medicine and its effects on the society
 I gained a holistic understanding of how biology and science is socially situated.
 About human evolutionary biology history
 Learned about life history, evolution, epigenetics, reproduction, ethics, and biological rhythms. We also talked about the social determinants of health.
 I learned a great deal about human evolutionary biology and life history and got a better perspective on how different evolutionary concepts and theories can be applied to the things we take for granted in our daily lives.
 I learned more about ethics in human research studies, epigenetics, life history theory, and the implications that race have on medicine today. The course also allowed me to revaluate how I view myself and view other.
 The course reoriented the way in which I think about and engage with science as a discipline, and made me think more deeply about assumptions we hold to be fact.
 Through the study of human evolutionary biology, we come to intimately understand topics that are relevant to our lives. The interdisciplinary nature of human evolutionary biology is especially important because it allows us to look at human biology from a perspective that accounts for both social and biological realities. Biology has societal implications, just as social environments can affect one's biology. Therefore, in regard to society, the study of human evolutionary biology is useful because it allows us to understand the scientific root of certain societal problems so that we may be more adept at solving them. Personally, the study of human biology has helped me understand the links between evolutionary biology and my everyday life. Therefore, in studying this subject, I have learned what lifestyle choices I can make to maximize my body and minds daily performance.
 Learned about super cool topics like epigenetics and female reproductive biology and ecology, as well as the impact of race on biology (& much more!)
 For non-stem majors, you learn how to read a scientific paper, filter through key points, and critically think about assertions based off of scientific studies. You come to view science as a more dynamic and interactive academic space rather than a stiff and factual field of study. In terms of the actual material, you gain insight into basic human evolutionary theory and specific important phenomena within the field. It's a good introductory course.
 I learned a basic overview of what human evolutionary biology is and why it is relevant to day to day life. We covered a lot of basic issues that humans face and why the human body works in the way that it does based on evolution over time.
 We learned various aspects of evolutionary biology, including why our bodies developed certain behavioral characteristics in order to survive. We also learned the ways the environment can affect our biology and how to incorporate evolutionary ideas into medicine in order to create new and more effective treatments. Thanks to this course, I had a much better understanding of my body in reference to the whole of nature and the history of evolution. Because of the weekly questions we had to answer, my writing ability and my ability to synthesize and use information increased dramatically.
 I definitely expanded my understanding of the ways that human society has impacted our evolution, especially the ways that scientific fields have overlooked (either purposefully or through negligence) several key factors that have impacted the way humans have changed over time.
 I developed a body of knowledge about human evolutionary anthropologyall information essential to making decisions about my own body in the future, and understanding more about the history of the human race.
 A sense of how human evolutionary biology impacts our daily life
 I gained valuable new perspectives on human health, particularly from a social standpoint backed by science. There were many aspects of human biology and medicine that I had not previously considered. One especially important aspect of this class is that it intentionally funneled STEM and humanities students into the same space, balancing each other's perspectives.
 This course gave someone with very little science experience, a foundational level of anthropology that changed the way I looked at the world. It taught me the ways through which social motives alter scientific understandings, the purpose of our evolved traits and countless other topics that are essential in understanding the human race.
 This course allowed me to revisit and expand on a lot of concepts that I learned back in high school while adding nuance and complexity to the explanations.
