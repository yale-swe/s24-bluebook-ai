Â This course spent far too much time on Chapter 3, which highlights quantum formalism essential to further study. I understand that knowledge of this notation and formalism is important, but it didnt seem directly connected to solving quantum mechanical problems. It felt more like a detour into the quantum analogue of linear algebra and the principles of bra-ket notation. It makes sense to cover these topics, as they form the foundation for analysis of other, more advanced problems in quantum mechanics. But when the course never actually gets to those other, more advanced problems, I dont see the point in spending so much time establishing the formalism to solve them. The main weakness of this course was its incoherence between topics. Throughout the semester, I felt that I was learning concepts, but didnt see how they all fit together in the larger picture. The material was difficult, and that made it hard to focus on the implications of what I was learning. Instead, I was just trying to wrap my head around the ideas and make it through the time-consuming problem sets. I think Prof. Rakich could have done a much better job of occasionally taking a step back and putting our knowledge and skills into perspective - relating them to other ideas or real-world applications, showing us how they link together or how they might arise in the real world, and making sure we understand why we are learning what were learning. The course would have been much better had this concept of coherence between topics been more of an emphasis.
