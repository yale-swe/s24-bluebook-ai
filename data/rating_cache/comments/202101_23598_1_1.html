Â One of the primary weaknesses of this course is that there are no prerequisites listed when in fact the readings are quite dense and use a fair amount of economic language. I thought it was somewhat unfair to expect students to have familiarity with financial systems and macroeconomics without any sort of basic introduction to those concepts at the beginning of the course. For that reason, there was a definite divide in section between students with no background in economics and students who had obviously taken advanced economic courses. Next, the course was poorly organized at times, with lectures and readings named different things. I think that could be easily fixed by taking advantage of the Modules tab on Canvas. I believe the course also advertises itself as doing more than it does. When students think of Capitalism and Crisis, I think they anticipate material which is a bit more engaging and which includes other perspectives more relevant to the pandemic and current financial crisis. The focus on only a few advanced industrialized economies was at times a bit dull and could be seen as perpetuating the idea that the world has to learn from advanced (Western) industrialized economies and not vice versa. Overall, I think the reading was often too long and the lectures unnecessarily detailed. Overall, I think the material could be very interesting but the design of the course hindered my ability to appreciate that material at times.
