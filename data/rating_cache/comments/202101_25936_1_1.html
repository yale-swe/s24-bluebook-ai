2021-Spring SOCY 100 02 Intro to Population Studies

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 25936 Term: 202101

 A strength of this course was in the interesting subject matter and Professor Zang's knowledge on it. Several of the papers presented in the class were co-authored by Professor Zang and she was very available to answer questions during lectures. A weakness was the volume of required readings for section, though those were diminished over the course of the semester. I also thought the length of class during the project presentations was too long. The professor and TAs for this class were very accommodating, largely because this was a newer course and it was online.

 The best thing about this course was how focused Professor Zang was on helping students engage with the material. She was very receptive to feedback and was always looking for the best way to help us learn.
 A big strength of the course was its breadth -- it was a pretty good intro into the field of demography if you had never learned about it before (I hadn't). My TF was super helpful for the final project, and the final project was actually a really great opportunity to work with other students in the class (it's a pretty sizable undertaking, so I would recommend doing it as a group). One challenge was the calculation based content like life tables, which are very integral to the field but require a lot of practice to get the hang of. At first, there wasn't enough guidance on that but Prof Zang made sure to slow down.
 Strengths: lecture moves very quickly, topics were interesting and had real world examples Weaknesses: too much reading (though you could get by in the class without ever doing it)
 This course was very well organized, making it easy to follow along with the themes and concepts being discussed. Discussion sections did an excellent job of reinforcing knowledge gained during lectures, and academic readings helped to supplement the class's textbook with real-world examples of demographic work. With the combination of academic papers and textbook chapters, occasionally the reading load for the course felt heavy. Additionally, lectures often covered many of the same subjects which had been discussed in the assigned textbook readings. I think that giving more clear expectations of what students should look for in readings would be helpful here, and having a more clear distinction between lectures and textbook work. Lectures could move very quickly, which was mad manageable by the fact that lecture slides were available on Canvas. However, the more quantitative concepts learned were more difficult to understand from only the slides, and I think that devoting more external resources to understand them would be helpful as well. The course would also benefit from a more clear outlining of expectations for student quantitative and scientific knowledge. Many students came from different academic backgrounds, and it was sometimes unclear how much we were expected to know or learn about the math or science underlying certain demographic concepts.
 Strengths: fantastic professor with equally as fantastic lecturing. Wonderfully resplendent slides with lots of information, very interesting content, and not too many assignments. Weaknesses: while the lectures were very engaging, they were very dense. Without having implemented the recording halfway through the semester, I would have been unable to learn adequately. The exams were okay -- the first one was rough, because two questions wrong = 130/150, which can tank a grade very fast. They did provide a bonus points opportunity, which was very generous and greatly appreciated. The lectures were simply very, very fast. And the readings -- very long. Like, really long. Very relevant, but super super long. Final project was also a huge undertaking, with not a whole lot of guidance, especially for an introductory course. Assumed statistics background if choosing to do a quantitative project.
