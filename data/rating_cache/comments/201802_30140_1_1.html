2018-Summer LITR S176 01/FREN S305 01/HUMS S267 01 Age of Cathedrals

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

ID: SU103 CRN: 30141/30140/30139 Term: 201802

Back123Forward
 One strength of the course was its broad scope of learning. It was also engaging and the majority of the material was interesting and clearly relevant to the point of the course. The course also helps foster a closer connection between the professor and students.
 Strengths, classwas interesting and well taught and overall a great experience Weaknesses: ambiguity in grading and a lack of transparency on what we were missing on worksheets
 Maile's analysis of architecture and medieval history was fascinating. She did a wonderful job of connecting the texts required for the course with their historical context. She also did a wonderful job of explaining the Christian ideology and history in the sculpture and stained glass artwork in cathedrals during excursions. There was a lot of confusion about Maile's grading policy. The questions on the worksheet seemed to be designed to provide a general understanding of the text (we only had 1-3 sentences to answer them and Maile indicated at the beginning of the course that the purpose of the worksheets was to merely confirm that we read), but they were graded harshly. Students started to panic because it wasn't clear how %s translated to letter grades/indications of academic performance since %s given for the worksheets were very low (ex: some worksheets resulting in a 79 average). The course could have done with some clarification about grading policy and expectations for the worksheet.
 The final paper at the end of the course id very rushed with the last week of class, excursions, and travels either home or to other destinations. I think it would be beneficial to push the final due date back to aid students who are leaving immediately after the course is finished, not to mention dealing with jet lag, etc..
 The program is strong in its day-excursions to seven different Gothic cathedrals and churches around France and in its interesting readings. The class was a comfortable mix of lecture and seminar-discussion and allowed us some time to spend in Paris with classmates. Although The Age of Cathedrals covered many topics, including Gothic Architecture, Medieval French Literature, and History, it was mostly graded as a literature course. The only big weakness in this course was Prof. Hutterers unclear grading policy. Halfway into our 2018 summer term, she replaced the participation grade which used to be a substantial part of the course under Prof. Bloch and instead increased the importance of weekly worksheets and the final paper. This course and its syllabus was designed by Prof. Bloch specifically to emphasis student learning while abroad, and participation was therefore a major part of this class. Because Prof. Hutterer thinks the participation grade is subjective, she arbitrarily removed the participation grade. Furthermore, the class is a seminar, and we go on one or two class excursions a week. Clearly, participation is an important factor; I have never seen a seminar without a participation grade. Weekly assignments, 6 questions to be answered in 3 sentences, have completely taken the place of the participation grade, and taken together, these worksheets represent the majority of the grade in a class that is more focused on student engagement than short-response writing. These two categories - worksheets and participation - are not replaceable as they demonstrate different skills and points of improvement. This is especially important to me because I am very interested in the subject and participating, yet this does not help my grade at all. This significant change to the grading policy should have been made clear on the first day of class, not halfway into it, leaving my classmates and I confused as to what was expected.
