2020-Fall S&DS 612 01/S&DS 312 01 Linear Models

Q:

Among the practices in this course geared toward learning during the COVID-19 pandemic, which succeeded and which could be improved? Please comment on course practices that allowed for virtual engagement.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC602 CRN: 10739/10713 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 1. Anki cards: Spaced repetition flashcards with short exercises from the textbook. These were really helpful in keeping us engaged with course material outside of homework and lecture, and easy to do at home in between other activities. 2. Piazza: I appreciated that this course had a forum for questions and discussion, as well as Professor Brinda and the TFs' thoughtful responses. One challenge with Piazza I've seen in other classes has been students feeling intimidated out of asking "stupid" questions, and the forums end up dominated by a few "top" students. This wasn't the case for SDS 312, and I appreciated the teaching staff giving clear, helpful answers to even basic questions. I also liked that we could post anonymously to teachers, as this made me comfortable asking a few questions that I may have been embarrassed to post otherwise. 3. Clear accompanying textbook material: This was especially important given that some students cannot attend office hours or live class due to time zone differences. Having a companion reference in the form of a textbook helped make up for some of this loss as I knew where to look when I didn't fully understand something covered in lecture or homework. For classes without one textbook, having access to detailed slides or other resources for each topic would likely be as effective. Things to improve: The only thing that might need changing is the twice-weekly in-class quiz policy. I am on the west coast, so an 11:35 EST start time means I begin class a bit earlier at 8:35. That was fine for me, but others in Asia or Europe likely struggled to attend live lectures so they could complete quizzes in class. I think Professor Brinda realized this and will likely adjust if this is taught online in the future.
 I thought the virtual lectures worked well! I have no further suggestions.
 Use of the online whiteboard was great.
 Class lectures were recorded, which was nice, especially for students who were in different time zones, or for people who just wanted to go back and rewatch some lessons. We had take-home exams for the midterm and the final. I think these succeeded because we were given an ample window of time to be able to work on them. The after-class quizzes were the only synchronous activity that had to be completed in the same 30 minute window of time after class. Course engagement was definitely subject to an individual and their particular situation.
 I think more office hours and TA would have been nice.
 Live zoom lectures worked out well, the scanned notes and recorded lectures are very helpful as well. Piazza was really useful for getting questioned answered.
 Live virtual lectures were helpful because they allowed students to be engaged. Piazza was really useful to cover questions outside of lecture and work through homework problems.
 I really enjoyed the take home midterm and final, actually. It was really conducive to reviewing the course materials as a whole, and gave me extra experience programming applications of what we've learned. And of course having a week-long submission window really lessened the stress of the exam. Honestly, I think that this is how exams should be done even in a normal year.
 Please stop having timed 15-minute quizzes after each lecture. They're not great for people in very different time zones and not fair for people with poor connection. There were times when I couldn't see the quiz for 5-10 minutes because my connection would cut out, and by then I couldn't really finish the quiz. Thank you for recording lectures--they are a blessing when internet goes out.
 The recording of lectures was successful, it was easier to understand lectures if there was a way to come back to the lectures and rewind whenever the concepts were confusing.
 Brinda's lecture style was very good, only small comment is that he moved the tablet window a lot so I didn't always have time to write what he said. Office hours were also at fairly inopportune times.
 Recording lectures and uploading the lecture notes was great for online learning, but also helped when I wanted to go back and review the material. That was a major plus. Having online quizzes at the end of the lecture helped synthesize material, but that may have taken up a significant portion of lecture time which ultimately led to not enough time for more difficult concepts at the end of the course (e.g. power, prediction intervals etc.)
 I particularly liked the daily-quiz model. I think including a low-stakes quiz at the end of every lecture was a good way to encourage students to stay on-track throughout the virtual semester. I also appreciated the heavy use of Piazza for questions and discussions.
