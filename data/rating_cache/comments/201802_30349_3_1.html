Â This is an excellent course for anyone interested in public health, I really enjoyed it. The good thing about this course is that it covers many topics, including some that go beyond the first level Epidemiology, so it really provides a very strong base on how to critically analyze epidemiological issues. This course really helped me understand how to put on my critical thinking hat and view healthcare decision making from different angels. Dr Desai was excellent in helping us understand that. I really liked his socratic method of teaching. Conversely, my criticism of this course is that it taught at a level for students who intend in doing some level of research (or would be researchers), while many of the individuals in the class are not intending to be researchers but rather administrators. That is where the other methods of understanding diseases would be handy such as qualitative and quantitative techniques for analyzing issues related to diseases; (figuring out risk/reward/compensation/cost & benefit scenarios, implementation of standards of care, etc.), these would be more helpful for healthcare administrators. My other potential criticism of this course is that although majority of the students will do well in taking this course, some of us who are non-science will not do as well, so although I really liked this course a lot, I do not want my grades/passing to suffer so much because this course is really intended to challenge students with science based backgrounds. If Yale wants to produce the senior leaders of tomorrow, my suggestion at this juncture would be to try to gear this course more toward healthcare administrators including for those who are of science based backgrounds. I believe that the overall purpose of most of the people in this program (regardless of whether they are physicians or business people or lawyers) is to be better administrators in their respective organizations. Note: From my experience, one of the hardest things for a healthcare leaders is to figure out (from a statistical strategic perspective) whether: (I) a merger or acquisition will work out well; or (ii) whether to use product A versus product B for a particular type of cure/procedure given their cost, benefits and risks, or (iii) whether to add a wing to the hospital for specific purposes like neonatal unit or whether to change a wing in a hospital from being preventative medicine based to being more cancer or more orthopedic issues based; (iv) whether to build a hospital in a certain area given the population and demographics, (v) the impact on healthcare costs and benefit of a change in standard of care, policy or law, etc. That is something all senior healthcare administrators need to understand better and be able to strategize to lead public health organizations. Most administrators do not have a comprehensive understanding of these issues. Please note, I offer the above suggestions with two things in mind: this is only my first semester at Yale, so I don't really know the depth and breadth of what lies ahead till I take more courses; and conversely, I join this program after having many years of business and law experience and I do believe I have an idea of what the future leaders in healthcare need.
