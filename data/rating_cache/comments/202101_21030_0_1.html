 Survey of American Economic History.
 How to read economic history papers, and economics papers in general. I learned how to make arguments on topics such as slavery and inequality by citing the work of economic historians and their data.
 We were introduced to the field of economic history, which seeks to answer questions about historical economic phenomena (examples include the impact of the railroad on American development, the impact of the 1918 flu pandemic on economic outcomes like income, the evolution of working women based on economic models, the ghettoization of black Americans, etc.). Answering these questions may involve using certain econometric techniques, employing economic models, addressing endogenous variables and finding causal explanations, and the selection of papers (which spanned the antebellum period to the present in America) employed a variety of these methods.
 A dive into approximately 25 economic research papers in topics such as slavery, female labor force participation, and ghettoization in the US since the 1800s.
 I definitely improved my analysis skills. This course was very good at trying to weigh different perspectives and explanations for historical events or patterns.
 I learned how to critique and analyze economics papers, specifically those related to American history.
 I learned about many significant pieces of research providing insight into America's growth and international relationships. I learned how to critically evaluate research as well.
 Knowledge-wise, students will learn about multiple economic perspectives on a wide variety topics, including (but not limited to) the efficiency of slavery, what factors led to the vast improvements in health in recent centuries, the labor market, wage inequality, the causes of the Great Depression and recovery, and the development of the urban ghetto. Skill- and insight-wise, I think I learned a lot about how economists work with data and the ways they work to make sure that their measurements are accurate (and certainly how difficult it is to ensure this!), both from readings and from working on my own analyses for the essays.
 The field of economic history was certainly a mystery before this class; while this class is structured essentially entirely around a set of published papers focused on quite a number of subjects in economic history, you get an appreciation of the kinds of methods and analyses economic historians have turned to or innovated in order to test and answer fundamental questions in American history through an economics lens.
