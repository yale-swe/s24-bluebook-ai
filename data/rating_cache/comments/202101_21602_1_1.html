2021-Spring MCDB 109 01 Immunity and Contagion

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 21602 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 The strengths are the Professor. Dr. Kavathas is an angel! She is so attentive to students and sweet, not to mention how willing she is to help you outside of class and during office hours if you need it. The discussion section was also great! Some lectures I've taken have very lackluster discussion sections, but this class had very lively ones that actually complimented the lecture well. In terms of weaknesses, a bit more clarification at the beginning would work well for lectures. Especially for non-stem students, launching into microbiology from the get-go can be very overwhelming. Perhaps an initial overview lecture of everything we should know going in would be helpful to preface the content itself.
 Fantastic course. I took this course as a non-science student and found it to be the perfect balance of accessible and challenging.
 Strength: great class for non-science majors. Professor Kavathas is awesome -- so nice, so fun, so entertaining, and a great teacher of course. Weakness: sometimes lectures felt rushed and like we tried to cover too much
 The only weakness of the course was that I wish when we got our grades and notes back for our psets, they told us what we were missing from questions where points were docked. Otherwise there are many strengths: The TAs Kavathas chose were super helpful and knowledgeable and one even hosted a really helpful study session during finals to answer questions and go over key concepts. Lectures are well organized and posted right after class. One possible improvement though would be to post lectures before class so that you can take notes before class and add to them during because its hard to keep up with everything on the slides. Professor Kavathas is a nice grader, the TAs can be a bit more strict with grades thought but they only grade the Psets.
 Strengths: this course is incredibly holistic and focuses on both the biological and social elements of pandemics which I think is really important. There is a good amount of biology involved but Professor Kavathas was really good with keeping the social and political components of biology in the syllabus. For example, we discussed not only the structure of the HIV virus but also the discrimination the pandemic caused. Weaknesses: sometimes the readings for discussion section felt random. I really felt this with the Polio book which taught me a lot but was confusingly written and therefore discussing it was sometimes difficult. Also- sometimes the grading of the assignments felt very subjective to which TA was grading. While most of the assignments did add to my understanding of the material, some like the paper did not.
 The only suggestion I have for improvement of this course is that the grading of the essay and the presentation seemed somewhat arbitrary to me. I realize that these are the two non-objectively graded components of the course, but I wish there were some more explicit guideline on what to include.
 Strengths: tfs were all wonderful, I loved the interactive aspect of lecture (made it feel like a large seminar), appreciated the inclusion of the arts and social issues with the focus on the science Weaknesses: sometimes the powerpoints were a bit confusing and didn't explain the concepts clearly which made taking notes in class a bit difficult
 Strengths: well-organized, divided into relevant units, comprehensive booklet that you MUST read as you go along for best outcomes, a wonderful professor and TFs who are generous with their time and care about their students and learning, a diversity of assignments (manageable PSETs, guest speakers, presentations, one very short essay, etc.), very insightful reading assignments Weaknesses: hard to think of any! The rhythm and instructional methods of this course were all very positive in my opinion.
 Gained a better understanding of my own body, the pandemic, diseases, etc. Looked at art and history related to immunity and epidemics Helped me unpack readings about science - helpful as a humanities major Teaching assistants were amazing - appreciated after class tutoring events
 Strengths: interesting topic and Prof. Kavathas and TFs really care, and they're flexible in meeting you and also they're flexible in talking with you about assignments. Also, it is very non-science major friendly, with humanities-related topics and readings like when you focus on polio and the different social and political implications of the development of the polio vaccine, as one example.
 I think there were a good amount of topics covered and a good amount of time spent on them. The professor did a good job at explaining concepts. I also liked that she had so many different types of assignments and the grade was distributed among them, rather than having high stakes exams that determine your grade as many other STEM classes tend to do. She also did a lot of breakout rooms and activities, and though I am not a fan of those, they were good practices for engagement and integration of the material. The TA's were also great resources, and I liked that she had them do lectures on their own work. One thing that I did think could be improved was that sometimes the professor would go through the material really fast, and have a lot of time left over at the end of class. There was time for questions then, but when no one had any she would let us go a little early. It was nice, but I would have preferred a slower run through the lecture. I think she might have noticed it though and fixed this issue, as I only remember thinking this towards the first half of the semester.
 Strengths: There's a focus on equipping students to be scientifically literate. Professor Kavathas really dedicates herself to students. Weaknesses: I think feedback could have been more detailed on assignments in order to help students improve. Sometimes feedback was only a couple words.
