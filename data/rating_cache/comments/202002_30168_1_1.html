2020-Summer GMAN S999E 01 German for Reading

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

ID: SU103 CRN: 30168 Term: 202002

Back123Forward
 Strengths: The TAs! Both of them were very generous and hardworking. I learned a lot from them. The textbook is very good too, although some texts are somewhat ethnocentric and even problematic. Weakness: the instructor and her lack of empathy. This is the first time at Yale that I come across such an insensitive and unfair instructor. Although I had many pleasant and efficient interactions with her, one sufficed to destroy my whole experience in this course. The materials and activities offered were useful. However, it is my believe that much more preparation is needed for a good asynchronous course.
 I would have liked more quizzes on grammar (specifically on different tenses, and especially on passive constructions). It would have also been good to have more frequent vocabulary quizzes.
 Strengths were the intensive focus provided by the summer session. The video recordings of lessons were the most helpful tool. They were rather short though, ranging from 10-20 minutes a day, so I felt at times like I was having to teach myself the material. The class could be improved through longer recording video sessions. I strongly suggest recommending another textbook, or finding a cheaper option. The book ended up costing me $137 to order from Amazon, which is by far the most expensive book I have purchased for a class. Making an electronic PDF available to the class would be ideal.
 I felt that the course focused too heavily on translation, as the graders were incredibly scrupulous about exact word choice in translations. Too much attention was put in making students translate German texts into English, and not enough attention was put into helping students read and understand advanced German texts.
 The strengths of the course include: a well-selected textbook and concisely presented lectures. I certainly consider this course a successful one in that it reaches its aim of developing students' ability to read German. One drawback to the course is its timeline: had I even one more week, I feel as though I would have been able to better internalize the material. The pace of the course was sometimes difficult for me to meet, as I work almost full-time. This is not a weakness inherent in the course, but a corollary arising from the speed of the course.
 Online with flexible due dates. I wished that the midterm was done after Chapter 15.
 The course had a lot of homework and it was very fast-paced (Expected), but learning all the material is just very challenging.
 strengths: asynchronous format, quick turn around time for comments on assignments weaknesses: occasionally unclear instructions for assignments (ex: how long video posts should be), the types of questions that would be on the midterm were unclear (it was called a "grammar quiz" but also had multiple longer passages and vocabulary)
 The course's main stregth is its modular and organized structure, which makes it possible to squeeze in a few weeks many grammar points and to advance quickly. It weakness is that the deadline of the two final translations coincided with the one for the last five chapters of the textbook. I feel that it would have been better to have a couple of days after finishing the textbook to focus just on the final translations.
 I think that the course is really good, however, I feel that the intensity of the assignments and homeworks prevents students from sitting with the material and internalizing the Grammar better. I have observed this phenomenon in all the language courses I have partcipated in or visited throughout my time as a graduate student at Yale. This makes me believe it is an institutional policy which Yale needs to revise along with its pedagogical philosophy when it comes to language learning. The university should realize that the demographics at GSAS have changed and that there are more people like myself who try to balance Graduate school and parenting while also fighting a chronic illness, therefore expecting us to dedicate five or six hours each day for a language course sets unrealistic expectations. I would not have complained if I think that this is the only way for one to learn a language, in fact, I have majored in modern languages as an undergrad and I am proficient in a number of languages other than my native tongue. Therefore, based on my previous experiences learning foreign languages I can confidently say that I do not believe that the way language is taught at this institution is geared towards achieving optimal pedagogical outcomes and sincerely think that similar if not better outcomes can be achieved through courses that do not stress or burden students as much. This criticism is not directed at this particular course- which was relatively good- but at the institution and how it is choosing to conduct language courses that seem to ignore the diversity within the student body as well as privilege certain groups over others.
 I think the format of German for Reading worked incredibly well: I learned a lot, despite the fact that I typically absorb information better when conversing with groups of people in person. However, I sometimes wished there was a greater variety of assignments. The typical routine of sentence, then full text translations did not always feel sufficient as the grammar concepts to which we were introduced became more complex.
 My only complaint about the course were the weekly video assignments in which we were supposed to upload videos of ourselves answering questions about that week's material. I don't see any reason that we could not have just uploaded written replies to a Canvas discussion instead of recording a video. Other than that, I was very pleased with the course and was surprised by how much my German reading comprehension improved.
 The course was very well designed and organized, and the instructor as well as the TFs were engaged and effective teachers. The textbook was excellent and well-structured, with easy to follow assignments that gradually built up skills and knowledge. My only recommendation for improvement is to make the video presentations more engaging - they generally repeated the material in the textbook, which was useful, but harder to engage with having just read the same material. More interactive online content (e.g. non-graded fun quizzes) or additional insights in the video presentations would have made an excellent course even better.
