2021-Spring CHEM 175 01 Organic Chem for First-YearsII

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 21529 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 The course time was way too early for students in different time zones. 75 minutes of lecture at 6 or 7 am twice a week, every single week, is brutal.
 I believe that changing the style of PSets to a less stress-inducing and learning-focused format would improve the experience.
 I swear, there was zero communication between the professor and the TAs/peer tutors. There were so many problems assigned in the PSET that were not covered until later in class (e.g., Baeyer-Villiger, Wittig reaction, malonic ester synthesis). Test content was fair and I appreciated having 75 minutes rather than the 50 minutes that wed get in 174, though Im pretty sure there were several people who cheated on them this year because they were held over Zoom, so fair can only go so far. This class is curved up to a B+/A- average, so I guess thats not terrible if youre doing average in the class. There was only one TA that gave practice problems reflective of the exam, and there was one TA who grade significantly harsher than the others, but thats probably not going to be applicable in future years.
 Strengths: THE PROFESSOR!! In terms of organic chem, Prof Spiegel is the best professor you could ask for. The class is perfectly-paced, and he is very thorough and clear in his lectures. This class is not a memorization class - you actually LEARN about organic chemistry. Prof Spiegel is also very welcoming and open to questions. He makes time to chat with students and holds review sessions before every exam. Beyond teaching, he is also a terrific human. He is so relatable, funny, and approachable. It makes for the perfect class. Weakness: NONE
 There was some miscommunication between TAs and the professor that made some logistical things confusing, and sections between TAs were very different. TAs and peer tutors were a great help.
 Strengths: TAs really carried, and the weakly discussion sections really helped to solidify the material covered in class. Also the extra sessions that some of the TAs conducted helped a lot in exam preparation. Weaknesses: The practice exams sometimes did not match the actual exams, which was only concerning for the final exam. At times I felt lost as to why we were covering a certain subject, and for the greater half of the semester we didn't follow what was on the syllabus. The pacing at times was also kind of slow, especially in the beginning of the semester.
 Strengths: A lot of opportunities for learning and studying, good support network (peer tutors were always there to help). TFs were very dedicated and helpful. Weaknesses: It felt like there was a little lack of communication or organization between the professor and the TFs.
 The online lectures were chaotic and the professor was often not prepared/showed up late. He explained reaction mechanisms well, but the PSETs and exams were incredibly difficult and the material covered in lectures was not enough. We rarely received guidance on how to study for this course and it was hard to get a sense of how prepared we were before exams.
 I learned the most through the office hours held weekly with peer tutors. They really did a lovely job breaking down course material into more simple, easy-to-understand terms. I also thought the course was well-organized, and I thought that the problem sets allowed me to grasp material a bit better. I thought exams were significantly more difficult than problem sets, examples in class, and practice problems. I personally believe that neither the totally flipped classroom approach nor a standard lecture is the best format of the class, but rather a mix, explaining concepts and then providing more examples of each reaction. Lectures often went into tangents, which were sometimes interesting, but not always very relevant and often just made the concepts more confusing for me. I really think focusing on explaining the concepts and providing a couple examples, especially for more complex/difficult reactions would be helpful. Often I found myself making mistakes in assignments because I did not fully understand the rules or conditions of reactions. Additionally, I think that the discussion sections could be redundant at times, and it would be nice to see the sections catered a bit more towards students' needs -- addressing common pitfalls, practicing with examples similar to those on assignments, and really explaining the "why" behind some of the key reactions.
 A major weakness of the course is that students are expected to intuit whether or not a certain reaction or mechanistic step will take place, even when no analogous processes have been presented in class. Worse, much of organic chemistry is educated guesswork, and it's extremely frustrating as a student when the TAs, professor, and peer tutors all disagree about the answer to a practice problem and none of them can definitively prove the others wrong. That being said, I did get better over the course of the semester at making these educated guesses, but I wish this had been through specific explanations rather than a gradual process of pattern-recognition on what types of answers tended to be correct.
 Sometimes, I felt as if the exam content was disconnected from psets and what was being taught in lecture. Specifically, exam 2 contained a lot of mechanism questions but we had not done much practice with that prior to the exam. It was only after exam 2 where we had extensive practice with mechanisms on the problem sets. Overall, it was a well-taught course and I could tell that the professor cared about our learning and well-being.
 The lecture format was not very engaging over Zoom, but Professor Spiegel tried to make it more interesting with breaks which was nice. There was a lot of support in terms of peer tutor study halls and discussion sections, which was very helpful.
 Much of the weakness came from the virtual aspect of the learning environment.
 Strengths: There is no homework other than 6 psets throughout the semester, so the relative day-to-day workload is pretty small if there isn't an exam coming up. The lecturer also tries to be engaging with the students and includes a break in the middle of the lecture to lighten the mood. Weaknesses: This isn't necessarily a weakness persay, but the exams and problem sets will challenge you, ESPECIALLY the exams since they are under time pressure and you can't simply go to office hours during an exam to get help on how to solve a problem (you can do that for psets, but obviously not during an exam). Some questions on the exam will feel impossible and sometimes the molecular behavior in the mechanism will also look unfamiliar, which may throw you off during that exam. In fact, for this semester, in my experience, the exams always had at least one question like this, where one has to really think in order to solve the question. Suffice it to say, the exams are hard and you may do worse than you had hoped coming into the exam - the answers aren't always straightforward and you will be challenged. Some of the TAs also grade harshly on the exams, which has definitely happened to me at least twice this semester, so beware. However, the exams are curved though and you do get one exam drop, which is useful to know if you happen to receive a particularly bad grade. Personally, I stressed a lot whenever an exam came up because of the difficulty level of the exams, if the difficulty level for these exams could be lowered, that would be nice for future students.
 Strengths: TAs were very helpful and there were lots of opportunities to get help. Weaknesses: Lots of miscommunications between the professor, TAs, and peer tutors on solutions to practice problems as well as what to know for the exams. Large lecture with no group work wasn't great for engagement. Very hard to gauge the level of difficulty of the exams because it wasn't usually like the practice exams or the psets so it felt like we were going in blind sometimes.
 Strengths: nice, funny professor; TAs are good at explaining material; professor did study sessions before tests; peer tutors were helpful Weaknesses: there are a few weaknesses to this course. For one, the tests often were often more complicated than the material we covered in lecture. In lecture, we only covered the basic reactions, while the tests sometimes had mechanisms that we had never heard of in lecture. The TAs sometimes covered this extra material in the discussion section, but I would have appreciated practicing coming up with new mechanisms in lecture, which often was chalked up to having "chemical intuition." Second, I thought there was somewhat of a disconnect between what the professor was saying in lecture and what the TAs were doing in section, which could sometimes be seen in the grading or writing of assignments, especially the final exam, which was described very differently and ended up being quite confusing. Third, in the grading sometimes I had questions about what I could have done better, but I sometimes did not receive a clear response from the TA in charge of that question, or even a response at all.
 The class was very disorganized and unnecessarily stressful. Professor Spiegel, the TAs, and the peer tutors often explained concepts in a contradictory manner.

 Strengths: For what organic chemistry seems to be about, Spiegel does a decent job of teaching it. Often he would go over mechanisms for reactions without explaining fully the driving forces behind it, specific details, etc. Also, we were never taught some material (such as protecting groups), but at some point we were just expected to know it. But overall, the weaknesses are not overwhelming, I learned a lot about orgo, and the class is pretty reasonable.
