Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

 The recorded lectures were a lot at times on top of the readings and I tended to not listen to them if short on time. I would suggest doing more during the nonrecorded live lectures to promote engagement and participation.
 Strengths: The pre-recorded lectures and readings were helpful and interesting. I especially enjoyed the lectures on ecology, India, Japan, the Comanche, and the material world. The readings were also helpful, such as the the Pomerance reading and the Naoroji one. Weakness: I would appreciate it if the class was structured a little better. Having a set schedule of when we actually met in class and when we had the day off would make it easier to plan ahead. Also, the breakout rooms in class time were sometimes quite awkward because no one would say anything and we would just be staring at blank space. I think class time could have been better if Professor Semmel did a little lecturing and we could ask him questions and have some back-and-forth.
 STRENGTH: Prof Semmel cares a lot about his students, lighter workload, goes beyond Euro-centric POV (even at times actively criticizes it) despite that being Prof Semmel's field, TAs are quite helpful, readings were not overwhelming, genuinely enjoyable subject matter. WEAKNESSES: Assignment dates got pushed back a lot which is normally fine but I picked the class because I thought I'd have the final paper done and turned in before reading period and instead it was due right at the start of reading period. Lectures felt a bit unorganized at times. I do not know if we made it to the end of the century. I think we only went from around like 1790-1860 with most content being from the early 1800s but it was hard to tell because the class was organized mores based on theme than chronology. I think just making it a bit more clear where we are situated would be nice.
