2021-Spring MUSI 115 01 The Mathematics of Music

Q:

Would you recommend this course to another student? Please explain.

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC409 CRN: 22413 Term: 202101

Back12345Forward
 Yes! Professor Cohn cares about his students very much and does his best to teach the musical concepts to everyone, even if it means taking a detour to explain something in a different way. I am not a music major and still felt like this course was valuable.
 For people who love music, this class will make you look at it from a completely new angle. Many of these mathematical connections are absolutely beautiful.
 Super interesting class that will make you think about music theory in a new way. I found the class / psets kind of tough, and there are a lot of people who are really strong in math who take the class. Professor Cohn is such a gem and definitely the highlight of the class!
 Yes and no...I would recommend this course to someone who enjoys theorizing about mathematics and applying those concepts to music; however, I would not recommend this course to someone like me, who just enjoys music and needed a QR credit. I often struggled to feel like I understood what I was doing in this class.
 Maybe. I definitely stretched my mind thinking about the math concepts and learned about some cool musical structures, and the workload is definitely light (2-3 hours per pset, 10 psets). But the material feels like more of a curiosity than anything really relevant or captivating. It's an easy Hu credit if you're decent at thinking about math, but in an easier semester I would've liked to take something more true-humanities.
 Yes, if the student has a good handle on music theory and wants to think about math in a more abstract manner.
 This is a wonderful course for students of all experience levels in both math and music. Prof. Cohn is extremely knowledgeable and his textbook, problem sets, and exam were all interesting, fair, and fun. While other courses at Yale have "hidden prerequisites" due to some students having significant preparation and the class catering only to those students, this class is wholly meant for those who are interested in the topic. If this class interests you, I urge you to take it!
 I want to preface this recommendation by giving some background about myself: I play an instrument, don't have music theory background, and despite my love of music, I am more of an enthusiast than a serious musician/composer. I also enjoy math a lot. I gained a lot of curiosities from taking this course. It was certainly neat to see the generality of some ideas in music and how we could use math to understanding some underlying structures of music that are conserved between different cultures of music. However I worry that in the future if somebody asks me to apply my knowledge, the conversation will go somewhat along the lines of: "What do you notice about this music?" "You can trace the chord progression out on a tonnetz!" "So what?" "I don't know" Perhaps this feeling stems from my lack of music theory knowledge and music in general. If you have time and are curious about music, I would still recommend this class. Just make sure you are asking the right questions so it remains interesting to you.
 I would recommend this course to anyone who has an interest in music or math (ideally both!).
 This is a pretty good fifth or sixth credit, especially if you have a decent background in mathematics (and to a lesser extent music).
 No
 I would recommend this to someone that is particularly interested in the mathematics of music and not the two subjects by themselves. Although I love music and math, the combination of the two did not feel very meaningful. The problem sets were also quite difficult so you do have to put a good amount of effort in. If you think it would be cool to know about an extremely obscure topic and also want the distributional credits, I would recommend this class.
 Ehhh. Im a CS major interested in music. At times I was frustrated at how handwavy the course felt. It wasnt a lot of work, and the grading was pretty fair, so if you want a chill credit, go for it.
 It's a pretty interesting class that really gives you a new perspective in many musical concepts! However, prior knowledge of basic music theory, e.g. reading treble and bass clef key signatures, and chords, is EXTREMELY recommended.
 Absolutely. I really enjoyed the material and found the workload manageable. Perhaps would have been more difficult for a grade (I took it Credit/D/fail) but I had a very good experience.
 It depends on what they are looking for. I would recommend this course to anyone who wants an interesting, off-the-beaten-path investigation into the intersection of math and music, but I would not recommend it to those who want to stay more focused on core subjects and applicable learning.
 It depends. I would recommend the class to math-inclined students, but not music-inclined ones. When I talked to my music friends, they were often much more confused about topics than my math friends, who had an easier time grasping topics.
 I would certainly recommend this course to another student, assuming the changes to grading I mentioned were made. Otherwise, I would recommend another music theory class. (This is nothing against Prof. Cohn or the course content, but the high sensitivity of the course grade to small errors made it unnecessarily stressful).
 A hesitant yes. The material is interesting but it is difficult to wrap your head around. THIS IS NOT A QR GUT! I could see it as a humanities gut but definitely not QR.
 I would only recommend this course if you have a decent grasp on music notation and basic theory (able to immediately know the notes in all the major and minor scales, able to quickly determine the interval between two notes, etc.), and if you want to spend a lot of time doing set and graph theory calculations on music-theoretical concepts. Taking the course will give you a new perspective on music theory, and a lot of the concepts you touch are really interesting, but you should be prepared for a very heavy workload. Additionally, (and this may change with Zoom vs in-person classes) the classes are fairly intense (cold-calling is quite common), and the grading was also rather opaque.
 Unlikely. This class may work ok for students that are looking for a 5th class that is relatively light in workload, interested in and have backgrounds in both math and music, and are simply looking for a class that might have interesting bits of information every once in a while. Otherwise, I wouldn't recommend this class, as I didn't feel very engaged (may be due to the online format) during the semester, and there are probably better options out there.
 I would greatly recommend this course to anyone who enjoys music, especially to musicians who might find music theory boring, and most of all to composers who seek the formula for a captivating piece.
 Yes, I took this course as a "gut" for me since I have a strong background in both math and music, and for that purpose it was great. It took up very little time in my schedule but was still interesting. It might be challenging for people with very poor math backgrounds.
