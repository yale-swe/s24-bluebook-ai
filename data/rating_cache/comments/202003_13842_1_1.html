2020-Fall LITR 028 01 Medicine and the Humanities

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC401 CRN: 13842 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 I learned a lot about medicine through new lenses (philosophy, art, music)
 radically changed the way I perceived the field of medicine, taught the value of thinking about traditionally "scientific" concepts in a more human context
 I developed my knowledge about the medical profession and the uncertainty that lies within it. I also became more familiar with philosophical works and how their underlying principles relate to the medical field as well. Additionally, the midterm and final papers allowed me to build my skills as a writer.
 I learned about the human side of medicine. This class really opened my eyes to the humanity of doctors--they're not just robots who never make mistakes. I learned a lot about how important it is for doctors to have empathy and how important it is for doctors to embrace uncertainty. I had never really read texts written from the perspective of doctors, so it was really insightful to be able to see how they think and also the cognitive biases they may fall prey to.
 I learned more about the role of uncertainty in medicine, empathy, patient care, ethics, and philosophy.
 This class teaches you more how to think about medicine as a whole rather than about any specific topic. Its readings cover everything from Aristotle's writings on ethics to 1900s Russian authors to articles documenting clinical errors in medicine, but each lesson proves relevant to the field of healthcare. Moreover, nearly every contemporary author we read came to guest lecture, Henry Marsh and Lisa Sanders probably most notable among these. An absolute must-take class for anyone considering going into premed! This class gave me confidence that medicine was right for me, even while I struggled in STEM pre-reqs. Dr. Morrison is a wonderful, kind, uplifting human being, and I am extremely grateful for his lessons on how to be a better physician (or even just a better person).
 I learned a lot about how nebulous medicine (and science in general) is, and how that's not necessarily a bad thing. I definitely view medicine in a different light, and it's really encouraging to know a bit more about where I'm going.
