2019-Summer SPAN S242 01 Lang, Cult, Soc Spain

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

ID: SU103-SA CRN: 30254 Term: 201902

Back123Forward
 Debate activity could be improved/ more relevant to coursework
 I wish there were more quizzes so that the questions and vocabulary that accompanied readings were more rewarded. I often crammed for the tests which isnt the best for long term memory.
 Strengths: the Professor, the excursions, the readings Weakness: a lot of work during the last week
 Strengths: The professor and the utilization of Valencia and its surroundings. The variety of assessments. Weaknesses: The fact that all of the work is piled onto the last week makes the last week very stressful. For example, a recording, essay, debate, exam.
 Host family was an absolute strength, as well as the excursion. More in-class emphasis on grammar and vocab could have been helpful.
 The course has a lot of homework, however it is not graded, so it feels like the amount of time you spend on it is not rewarded, and you can only prove your knowledge of it on the test. I felt that the grades are so heavily concentrated on in class essays and tests that it is very stressful, especially because they are graded toughly. In fact, I felt frustrated a few times by the way tests were graded because they felt very subjective even if the teacher and I agreed my answer was correct, it was not good enough for her.
 I thought the course was fantastic. Motivated students can put in a lot of work in the areas that interest them. I spent a lot of time focused on anthropology, religion, and history. A student interested in improving their grammar may invest time in that, or students interested in improving their conversation skills can go out an talk to people. The Spaniards tend to be really chatty, and there are a lot of students and student hang-outs in Valencia. There is a lot of unscheduled time, which means that motivated students can pursue what is most interesting to them at the pace that they want. I personally took advantage of it. I will say that it would be great to do more coursework with Valencian students. I know it's their summer, but if they have summer courses, it would be great to have an option to sit in on class with the UCV students. Since our class was Yale-only, the standard for conversation and comments were different than being in a classroom with students working in their own language at a high academic standard. Having our class was great because we could go at our own pace and the topics were flexible each day, but to also have the opportunity to test my abilities in a Spanish-speaking university would have been awesome. My one other suggestion is that those students who have home placements more than a 25 minute walk from campus be subsided for a bus pass. For me the walk to class was around 35 minutes; because I am a fast walker, I could make it in 25 if I hustled. For the first two weeks, I varied my routes every day so that I would always be walking a different way each time. After that, I wanted more time to explore the farther areas of the city and going from class to lunch at home each day (the standard cultural practice) and then back to the city took a large chunk out of the day. I started taking the bus to and from class, which cost around 45 euros on bus passes, which was the majority of what I spent out-of-pocket. I noticed all the university students and professors in the area used university bus passes; perhaps being able to get the student discount would be helpful. The homestay and meals each day are provided for within the cost of the program; I think it is reasonable to also consider subsidizing public transport for those students who do live far away from the classroom.
 The greatest strength is the homestay program. One weakness could be not having excursions every Tuesday and Thursday.
 I really did not see any weaknesses in this course. The material was well facilitated for student learning; everything (assignments, excursions, and due dates) were outlined in the syllabus prior to the start of the program. Specific and important information was also posted on Canvas, so that all would know what to do when. The professor and teaching assistant were extremely encouraging, cared about all in class, and worked hard to ensure that students were learning the information and had a safe trip. If anybody had a question, both were quick to reply and very helpful.
 I think that the strengths of this course are that it is complete immersion in Spain through everyday life, in the classroom and at home, which forces us to practice and think more in Spanish. We gain exposure through class but our best learning moments our when we are together on excursions or out in the city. It allows us to keep speaking to each other in Spanish and soon enough it becomes easier to converse with others. I think that the class readings were mildly difficult later on in the course which made class discussion difficult.
 I loved pretty much everything, especially the home stay. One thing I'd fix is the debate. I wish we all got to participate, as only half the class was able to, and it also seemed like a lot at the end of the fifth week. It would've been nice to incorporate the theme of the debate into the whole class (also, I'm not sure if pro/against immigration reform is really a great topic for debate). I think the debate could've been a lot more engaging had we covered another issue, like that of week 1 with the water crisis in el Mediterrneo.
