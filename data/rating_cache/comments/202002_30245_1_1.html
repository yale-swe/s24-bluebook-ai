2020-Summer CENG S150E 01 Eng.Improv:IntrotoEngAnalysis

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

ID: SU103 CRN: 30245 Term: 202002

Back123Forward
 Great caring professor/TA, interesting content but zoom was inefficient
 I think that one of the strengths of the course was that collaboration was strongly encouraged for breakout rooms during class as well as Piazza for problem sets. Also, office hours to go over hard questions on the problem set was really helpful. I believe that both Professor Loewenberg and Rodrigo were very accessible and helpful. This wasn't entirely a weakness, because it was pretty helpful, but I think that learning the majority of the material by ourselves through the pre-recorded lectures was a bit difficult. Overall, this may be just a fault with online learning as a whole but I found it hard to retain the new information and knowledge when I just had to watch a bunch of pre-recorded lectures in a row in preparation for our classes. I think that it could be helpful to spend part of our synchronous time together just reviewing the key information that we had learned from the videos before diving into examples. Also, one weakness of the course in my mind was that even though it is supposed to be an introductory course that even non-chemistry majors can take, I found it difficult at times to keep up with the at-times heavy math and notations. Especially in the last week, I was a bit confused with the partial derivatives because I had only taken AP calculus before this course so I was not familiar at all with multivariable calculus.
 Strengths: Prof Loewenberg was clear, patient, and accommodating of everyone, and a great lecturer. Rodrigo is an excellent TA. The dynamic between the Prof and the TA is really great and definitely a really enjoyable course overall. The flipped classroom format doesn't always work for some other courses, but in this course it worked well: lecture notes were posted beforehand so you could annotate them while watching the videos.
 Professor Loewenberg and the TA Rodrigo are prompt and very helpful. They make the course enjoyable.
 I felt like the course was taught well. Strengths would include the examples taught in lecture (helped a lot with understanding general topics), being able to apply concepts learned in different engineering problems through problem sets and the review of topics in office hours. While I had a really great experience in the class, I feel like the way group work was structured could be improved, given that the breakout rooms did not always provide the intended experience of being able to work with others in small groups on problems.
 The structure and overview of class content could be sharper and more organized, eg clear learning goals and example problems we should be able to solve
 The online version of this course could have benefitted from a half lecture half group work structure, since it was sometimes difficult if we did all practice problems or all lecture-type materials. One huge strength of this class were the lecture videos, which were incredibly clear and helpful, especially those that were from the classroom (which reinforced me wishing I could have taken this class in person, but I still loved it!) One thing I'd recommend, which I didn't think of until I saw how effective it was in another online class, is forming study groups within the class which are also used as the default breakout rooms during class (which you can program beforehand in Zoom, as long as all students are logged into Zoom with their Yale accounts). This makes breakout rooms a lot less awkward, and encourages students to actually work together a lot more.
 I thought the lecture videos were helpful. I think it would have been helpful to go over confusing topics during one class (i.e. Monday) and then do practice problems on another (i.e. Tuesday).
 A strength is that the professor uploads videos before each class and I think that's very helpful.
 The flipped classroom model works well for the class environment, and the problem sets were extremely well designed. Exams were not hard, but problem sets arguably were, which is conducive to learning. If there were any weaknesses, it would be that Professor Loewenberg could have prepared for some classes better just because it felt impromptu at times.
