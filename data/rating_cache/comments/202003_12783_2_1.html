Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 12609/12783 Term: 202003

 One unexpected strength is the community that you build throughout the course; I thoroughly enjoyed the reading groups, dinners, group paper etc. for building it.
 Howard Dean is, without a doubt, the most refreshing and enjoyable professor I have ever had at Yale. If you're unsure about applying to this course, like I was, do it. I hate reviews like this, and I have often been the reviewer amid 20 fawning praises for a professor who talks about the disadvantages of the course. This class is really that enjoyable. After years of getting increasingly cynical about making change in the world as I dealt with constant toxic posturing from my classmates and the structures that a lot of Yale establishes, Howard gave me hope in making change again. The memos were also incredibly enjoyable; they made me enjoy the process of toiling with the course's ideas. More courses should adopt a many-short-memos evaluation!
 Professor Dean asks for students to complete a lot of reading but he assigns everyone into reading groups. Additionally, he speaks a lot during the seminars but that is both a strength and a weakness of the course.
 Strengths: Both Howard and Caroline made the virtual classroom environment both welcoming and engaging. They are really accessible and open to talking about anything. Moreover, the reading list was really strong and included some of my personal favorite memoirs. The topics and chronology covered by the course was comprehensive and didn't leave much out. Great guest speakers and a fun simulation make this course the highlight of my semester. Weaknesses: Every week, there are 2-3 presentations given by students on a topic unrelated to that week's readings. These presentations are interesting but often run for well over half of the class time, taking time away from touching on the class content.
 Howard Dean has a lot of experiences and perspectives to share from his personal career in government and in terms of policy decision-making. He is very accessible to students. There are also guest speakers throughout the semester, like Ambassador Samantha Powers, who have extensive experiences and unique perspectives to share.
 Howard! He truly cares about his students
 I think the strengths of this course are Howard Dean, and the discussions we have in class. It's incredible to hear Howard's stories and insights on the content we read, and to have him guide the discussion and ask the difficult questions. The other students in my class were also amazing, which I think greatly contributed to a high level of discussion. I think a weakness is probably the lack of structure in these discussions. Sometimes, it felt like we just moved on from one aspect/question to another without fully addressing the details.
 The course was really amazing not only because Professor Dean knows the subject so well, but also because my fellow students contributed so much to my understanding of the issues. Professor Dean required us to give weekly presentations, and I learned so much from my peers' research.
 The strength is professor Howard Dean. He puts so much into this class and devotes so much time to his students. He helps form a real connection with the class and between the students. He brings in fascinating speakers and asks intriguing questions. No weaknesses.
 Professor Dean is awesome, I loved being in his class. The course has a ton of reading but you aren't expected to do all of it. There is no busy work - the bulk of the course is the fantastic class discussions. The strength of the course is also the caliber of the students - many students are really experts in specific areas and regions and I found I learned a ton from my peers.
 Great adaptation of an upper level seminar to an online format. That being said, the virtual work with groups was certainly harder/less natural via Zoom but also necessary to build that seminar camaraderie.
