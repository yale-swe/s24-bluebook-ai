Â I think this was a wonderful course in that the texts and lectures were fantastic and enlightening, but I think it was also not designed well for the format of a summer class, or for Zoom. The class ultimately consisted of about 15 hours together on zoom, with only 5 of those being for discussion of 5 texts. Prof. Iheka emphasized multiple times in the first few lectures that he did not want to take too much of our time by having additional time on Zoom because it is the summer. I would have greatly preferred even just 1 extra hour a week of discussion, which would double our time. I didnt dislike the weekly written forum discussion, but it wasnt a replacement for conversation. My responses were stilted, for I had the impression that I was being graded and evaluated, much more so than I did when I was talking in class. Further, the posts tended to be long, ask multiple questions, and make multiple observations. The responses to those posts typically only took up one question or observation, leaving an enormous amount hanging. They also had little bearing on our in-class discussions. Because of the exceedingly limited time, we had to cover enormous ground very quickly, with discussions which merited a whole hour being passed over in ten minutes. This created disconnected and chaotic conversation, with each person cutting in to make their (very good!) but entirely unrelated point. The limited time also meant we were unable to adequately address questions of intertextuality, which were hinted at but not explored. I think these problems could be helped by adding 1-2 more hours of discussion a week (summer classes ought to be short and intense, in my opinion - this class was not intense) and either reducing the lecture component or including instruction during the discussion sections in a guided-seminar style. I felt somewhat patronized at times, even with the knowledge that this is an introductory level class. One example is the multiple choice question we were given to pick which one of two theses was better, with one of them beginning with In this essay I will discuss..., which everyone got right. A second example is that we were supposed to underline our theses in our final paper which - as was made clear in the lecture - was not just to facilitate grading but to ensure that we were clearly focused on one thesis while we wrote. These things might be reasonable in an introductory level course, but if so, they would also necessitate much deeper student engagement (built into the course via discussion section, not just optionally meeting with TAs) and more discussion of craft in essay writing. It was the combination of the suggestion that the students were having difficulty incorporating their theses in their papers with the strong you can figure it out yourself attitude fostered by the limited direct student engagement that felt patronizing, which made me feel slightly antagonistic when referencing my thesis in my final paper. Again, I think this problem could be addressed by improving student engagement. Lectures on zoom are difficult to follow (though Prof. Ihekas lectures certainly rose to the challenge!). I find that theyre much easier to follow if everyone who is able has their mic on, as well as their camera. It creates a much stronger feeling of being in a classroom. I think this is much more important in discussion sections, where everyone was muted except the speaker, which stunted conversation.
