 Strength: great professor Weakness: a lot of reading
 This course was incredibly well taught with a variety of content. However, there was a slight excess in articles to read, making it difficult to dive deeply into any individual article.
 The class is fairly broad in terms of the topics it tackles, so there is a good chance you may have a topic or two which you find particularly interesting. This makes the essays and midterms easier since you have the ability to optimize for what you see as the most interesting topics. One of the weaknesses of the class is that it is mostly focused on the late 19th century and early/mid 20th century, so if this is not the most interesting period of American history to you this class might not be ideal.
 Professor Eli is one of the best professors I've had at Yale. Really cares about the wellbeing and success of the students. Really laid-back lecture that encourages learning in a low-stake environment. Great Class.
 The course covered many great research papers that introduced new analysis techniques. A weakness was the class wasn't very engaging, but the virtual format made that particular detail difficult.
 Maybe add discussion sections. Would help give extra practice with the material
 I think the main strength of this course was the teacher. I had never been a history person in the past, but Prof Eli truly made it engaging in fun. I think the main fault is that there wasn't much student engagement. I feel if we had weekly discussion sections, that would be a lot more helpful to learning. Additionally, we only had two assignments, a midterm, and a final. Because of this, it was hard to receive feedback about how we were doing in the course and if we fully grasped the readings. In the first half of the semester, there were also quite a bit of readings that were quite long.

 The strength of this course (besides the course material) is that it is straightforward in its expectations. Between Professor Eli's lectures, the slides, and reading the papers, you can get a solid understanding of the important aspects of a paper. The assignments and exams simply ask you to be able to explain these aspects (e.g., question, hypothesis, methods, results, conclusions). That said, I never felt like I was regurgitating the material; I truly understood it. I think this course gives you a reasonable amount of information to learn in a semester and doing just what is expected of you is not only enough for you to do well but also to appreciate and understand the material.
