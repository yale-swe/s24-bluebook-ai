2020-Fall HIST 202 01 EuropeanCivilization 1648-1945

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 10375 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 I saw no strengths to this course. The lectures were occasionally interesting with the professor giving tidbits of information that one wouldn't find In a textbook. However, he seemed to be more interested in talking about his experiences and visits to the places related to that week's lecture than he was in actually teaching. I think a recorded lecture would have been better because it would have ensured that prof stayed on topic. The discussion sections were terrible...The TA sent out a document with questions the night before, students answered the questions in the doc, and on the day of the discussion section just read out their answers. WHAT A HUGE WASTE OF TIME!!!
 Weaknesses: The lectures aren't based off of the reading material so it's not really anything that is particularly important or useful. I think talking about the reading material could help the lectures, but it kind of forces you into doing the reading if you want to get anything out of the course. The paper assignment felt really vague, and the discussion sections weren't really that structured, although that can also be a strength. Strengths: The paper assignment was also part of our midterm, which was honestly really great because it gave me an advance on the writing process that I otherwise definitely would not have had.
 The strengths of this course are that the professor and TF's were absolutely wonderful, and the content covered was very interesting.
 strengths: interesting material, assignments helped learning weaknesses: lectures were disorganized and I felt like a lot of the learning came from the textbook
 I thought that the course was very interesting and also casual, so it was easy to take for a good foundation of European history. I loved the professor and my TA and thought that they were very invested in our learning, which I very much appreciated. However, it was relatively disorganized, as I had no sense of my grade or even what the final would be like until right before it. I enjoyed the course but would have appreciated a little more structure.
 Strengths: Prof Merriman is engaging and fun, and very knowledgeable about every subject you can think of in the class. Also, he would send out a list of key points before lectures with which we could follow along. The TFs were kind and knowledgeable as well. My first survey course at Yale, but I enjoyed it. Weaknesses: Lectures were sometimes a bit hard to follow. The readings were long and full of information, which is to be expected, but it was difficult to know what information needed to be retained. Only 3 main assignments to be graded.
 Only having a final paper and final exam make it difficult to determine how well you understand the material, not a lot of feedback
 All you really needed to do was to read one textbook he provided, so it was a pretty relaxing course. Sometimes the lectures can feel a bit off topic because the professor veers off into whatever he likes the most, but his passion can make such tangents fun. The sections also do a surprisingly good job of keeping one engaged with the material.
 The course is disorganized and the reading load is unreasonable for an introductory class. One of the assignments is a 10-page research paper, which is not specified in the syllabus (it is only referred to as a 'short paper'), so students had no idea about this when they enrolled. Besides the fact that this was not mentioned anywhere on the syllabus, expecting students to write 10 pages of original research seems contrary to the very idea of an introductory lecture course. I have taken a few introductory level courses at Yale in different departments, and none of them have ever asked their students to complete such a substantial assignment. It would be all well and good if it were an advanced seminar, but it's not.
