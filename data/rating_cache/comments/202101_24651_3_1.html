 Yes, although I think it depends on the student. I would definitely recommend taking this for the content and the design questions - I learned about so many different artists and histories that never appear in other architecture courses. However, it took some getting used to the structure of changing groups bi-weekly and preparing the projects on a short timeline. In total, the workload is not too much and because of the frequent projects, there is no larger final at the end. However, the work is asymmetrically distributed in that lecture weeks have almost no outside prep and pin-up weeks require outside meetings with a completely new group and the development of a project in a couple of days.

 No, not in its current form. As a new class with visiting professors, I was really excited, especially because it was taught with Howard students as well. All the Howard classmates were lovely, as were the Yale students as well. I feel like we bonded through the struggle that was this class. Vague prompts with harsh criticism, no consistent weekly readings or organized syllabus, and professors that were disorganized and made students pay the price for that disorganization (multiple times throughout the semester, we received angry and passive-aggressive emails from the professor about missing assignments, only to receive a follow-up email that he wasn't looking in the correct submission folder). Also asked rather personal questions about a Black women student's hair in front of the class. The professors also seemed specifically critical of women of color, and Black women in particular. I really wanted to like this class, because there is a wealth of Black and Indigenous history and design methodologies that I'd love to know more about, and I'm sad that my experience of learning was tainted by the mess that was this class. I would have liked to see more intention and grace and compassion for students, especially during a pandemic.
 Only if it becomes more organized. It was not only clear that this was the first time this course was being taught, but the first time that either professor had led a seminar (or at least it seemed that way.) Prior to this course Yale devoted I think 0 of its courses to Black and/or Indigenous architecture, so this course sounded so cool from the get-go, but I think between the disorganized nature of it and Jerome's harshness and unreasonable expectations (especially for students in a pandemic!!!) I was disappointed. I did leave with a lot of references, articles, and book titles I definitely want to look more into, but oddly the structure and nature of teaching for this course was not inclusive. For example, calling students out for not having their cameras on or being late or needing extensions...this is an elective course where most of its students are already spending a lot of time on their architecture projects. The last thing we want is to be berated in a class where we're hoping to feel seen.
 I would absolutely recommend this course to any student interested in design.
