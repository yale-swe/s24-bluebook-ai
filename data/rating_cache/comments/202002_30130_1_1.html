2020-Summer ECON S115E 01 Introduction to Microeconomics

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

ID: SU103 CRN: 30130 Term: 202002

Back123Forward
 One of the challenges of the online model was that there were three avenues through which we were learning new information (textbook chapters, prerecorded lectures, live lectures). Sometimes it felt like there was a lack of continuity between the three. I think that increasing the amount of time spent on practice problems would be helpful, as well as some sort of practice problem review sheet for the exams.
 I liked the professor, and I thought the prerecorded lectures were excellent. The textbook was dull and repetitive. The in person lectures were good. I thought the problem sets were poor. The questions were often unclear and confusing and all the answers were given in the section meetings with the TA.
 This course is an okay intro to microeconomics. However, it is excessively demanding. Professor Koker expects students to watch six hours of prerecorded video lectures, spend five hours on zoom calls, read at least four textbook chapters, study for at least nine hours, and do a problem set each week. While this is all intended to facilitate student learning, the information gets redundant, and actively begins to impede the process, very quickly. The live zoom sessions would be much more helpful if conducted in a more question-based, office hours style rather than serving as a vehicle for Tolga to hear himself talk about unrelated tangencies for hours on end.
 I felt that the course was extremely well down and well paced prior to the first midterm but the second half of the course was much too rushed. The professor even mentioned that the second half of the material was more difficult. I think that in order for the students that have the adequate time to understand the material, the course must be restructured to provide more explanation of the more challenging material covered after the midterm
 The material covered in this class gives an excellent grounding in economics. The pace of the class is understandably quick, as it is a condensed summer course. However, it was made worse by the fact that both the midterm and the final were scheduled for during the Thursday class times of the third and final weeks of class respectively so we effectively lost that teaching time. It's a lot of material to cover in 10 classes but doing it in 8 definitely left me feeling like certain topics had been brushed over. An obvious improvement would be to fix this scheduling!
 Strengths: Real-life examples (super helpful for contextualizing principles and seeing how they apply - usually very broadly), Tolga (he shares the best examples and truly is a very dedicated professor!), TA hours (Marianne is really good and patient in going through answers). Weaknesses: The textbook can get dry and difficult to get through (especially when the text describes a mathematical graph in words), but i guess that's just something we learn to get through.
 The course had a very fast pace and a lot of content was covered. I felt a high level of engagement though, despite the online medium. The psets and TA help were very useful.
 Very well taught and organized. Only weakness is the pace, which is too quick.
 Recorded lectures were great but the live sessions always felt a bit rushed.
 The strength of this course was the teaching from Professor Koker and support from the TA. The course was difficult because it was a high-intensity workload in a short amount of time. On average, I was doing econ-related work at least 40 hours a week. Furthermore, for people who are looking to take this as a QR credit I recommend at least some calc knowledge before you enroll because there are math sections that require calc and are not necessarily explained in class. With that being said, Professor Koker has an excellent organization to the course that, if you follow, will always keep you at a high level of understanding. Furthermore, the TA we had for the first session did a great job of answering specific problem set questions and explaining the context and knowledge behind it so we were constantly encouraged to think about answers in a broader context. The course was difficult because of the time constraint but is organized well.
 Strengths: Great, engaging and truly passionate professor. Platform with multiple streams on content and lots of help available was great. Weaknesses: Intermediate level content was at times quite challenging, this is more of a labeling concern from a non-Yale summer school student.
 The strengths of the course were the video lectures and Zoom discussions. I found them to be very clear and Professor Koker did an excellent job of describing/illustrating the concepts. The textbook readings however, were a weakness for the course. I found them to be very long, confusing, and dry.
 Weakenss: Brutal pace and lack of practice problems makes understanding concepts difficult.
 Strengths: engaging lecture videos, reinforcement of important topics in the live session, fair exams, relevant and helpful textbook Weaknesses: None
 This course was hard because it was taught online and in a short amount of time (summer session). We watched ~6 hours of pre-recorded lectures a week, then attended 4 hours of Zoom a week (prof held us overtime each time), where all of the lecture content was simply repeated in brief with no new content or really any practice problems, and then we spent hours reading the textbook for info we were responsible for but that was not necessarily covered in lectures, doing psets and office hours to get pset answers was necessary. Overall, it was a ton of time that was not planned out efficiently. Also, the lack of practice problems made me feel less prepared for the exams and less able to check my understanding and if I could really apply the chapter concepts.
