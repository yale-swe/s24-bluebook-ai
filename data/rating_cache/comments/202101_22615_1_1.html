2021-Spring PLSC 111 01 Intro International Relations

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 22615 Term: 202101

 This course provides a great introduction to the topic of IR but doesnt dive especially deep into any area (went I wouldve liked to study it even more). That is likely an inherent issue with intro classes and I certainly wouldnt fault the instructor or syllabus.
 My only criticism of the class is that the readings were simply too excessive. I ended up having to skip the majority of them, which I very much regret because the ones that I was able to read were incredibly interesting. But the sheet amount of reading expected was much too high for an introductory class. Beyond that, my experience was excellent. I enjoyed the class, the professor and my TF were both great, and overall I think it was well-organized and expertly conducted.
 so many assignments that are needlessly long, felt like we had a quiz every other week that took up a lot of time. Professor Scheve also spoke very very quickly during lecture and it was often difficult to keep up
 STRENGTHS: Professor Scheve is very knowledgable, TAs are incredibly helpful, subject matter is very thorough, readings are interesting WEAKNESSES: Felt like we focused on war for a long time, workload was a lot toward the end, lectures could be a bit boring with just the powerpoint slides but it was Zoom so that is to be expected
 Strengths: The course moves quickly with lots of information and readings, which are usually interesting. Professor Scheve is a great lecturer and is really interested in the material. The content is really interesting, especially on international trade. They ask for a lot of feedback and actually use it, which is cool. I thought the course was going to focus more on the isms, but it didn't, which ended up being great. I am also not a very quantitative person, and the class is definitely more quantitive than I thought it would be, but it ended up being really interesting as well. Weaknesses: The readings can be quite long and technical, and sometimes it didn't feel like they were aligning with the lectures very well towards the beginning of class, but that improved towards the end. The course does move very fast.
 the readings were very long and it wasn't always clear what we were supposed to be getting from them. this could be improved by shortening the lengths of the readings.
 Strengths: The class went over a lot of material, if that's what you're looking for Weaknesses: This course is very dense, and the sections often went over new material rather than discussing the lecture explicitly (many section meetings had their own readings)
 Nothing to improve, good course
 This course was very interesting and well-taught, but I often felt that there were too many readings for us to actually absorb any of the information.
 The topics were very interesting and the final paper was very engaging. One weakness is that the pace of lectures often felt too fast.
 Strengths include organization, engaging lectures, clear slides, and a manageable workload. There are not a lot of weaknesses. Perhaps practice questions to help review for the exams would be helpful.
 I wish we had received more guidance on the structure of the final paper. The expectations were very unclear.
 The readings were not as engaging as we didn't speak about them in class and it felt like they were only there for the midterm. Moreover, having a quiz, a midterm and a paper due back to back was overwhelming towards the end. The display and teaching of the material was excellent however and I really enjoyed it!
 This course goes into way too much detail about complex topics for an "intro" PLSC class. At times, the class felt like an upper-level macro or game theory course, and we covered topics like war using counterintuitive methods that very few of us had even heard of before. Exams are open-book and open-note, but they often tasked us with finding minute details in long, advanced readings that we hadn't covered that much. This made it very stressful and hard to tell exactly how each of us was doing in the class. I also felt that we didn't get enough guidance on the final paper (or, in general, how to read and write political science research papers).
 I did not enjoy this course. The lectures were loaded with words which made it difficult to take notes and feel engaged at the same time. The concepts were also more technical and we did not explore any case studies in depth.
 A lot of topics but not a lot of information about them.
 I thought that the professor was very engaging and really enjoyed my sections. However, I found the lectures to be very long and filled with too many details, so they were difficult to follow. I also felt that a large amount of reading was assigned, but it was repetitive and often irrelevant, and even doing all of the reading, grading on assessments was so harsh that I did not do well.
 The course gives a very good comprehensive overview of the field to IR for students who may not have taken a similar course before, but moves very quickly and may be harder to follow for students not considering a major in a related field (e.g. Political Science, Global Affairs or EP&E). It may be worthwhile to slow down the material slightly and spend more time on important conceptual topics being introduced (e.g. tragedy of the commons, democratic peace theory) to ensure all students can follow the course and remain engaged in the material.
 Very interesting content, not always taught in a way that was accessible to students who were new to the subject.

A weakness of the course was that a lot of content was covered in each lecture; it wasn't necessarily an excessively fast class as a whole, but there wasn't always time to finish the lecture every day.
