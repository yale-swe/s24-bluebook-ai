2020-Fall AMST 348 01/EVST 304 01/ER&M 381 01 Space, Place, and Landscape

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 12582/12584/12583 Term: 202003

 I would say that the course is really well organized for online learning and generates really interesting discussions. The readings are hit and miss, but the discussions are always good. The home projects are so much fun if you really dig into it and choose a place that you truly feel connected to. I think maybe even less focus on lecture and starting the discussion and home projects earlier in the semester could be even better because I just felt kinda lost in the concepts early on in the class without grounding in examples, projects, and a model system because the topics early on are super philosophical and intangible which is hard for my stem child mind to grasp.
 The only weakness this class has is not starting the weekly assignments earlier so they could be more spread out. Otherwise, this class is pretty flawless. The readings each had its own purpose and were interesting. I felt like I learned something new from each paper and they were the perfect length for maximizing interest and time. Having the final project be a recollection of all the assignments made it a lot less stressful than other courses. A section on how to create our own geographic media would have been nice, I think that would really tie this course together.
 Professor Barraclough's ability to run an engaging and supportive class is one of the course's key strengths. Additionally, the project-based element of the course was way more fruitful and manageable than courses structured around big papers and reading responses.
 The course really gives a lot of freedom with the projects and also provides a good background of geographic material to get started. I think perhaps focusing a little more on the practical applications of geographic concepts rather than their abstract definitions might make the class more engaging.
 The seminar sessions were really strong because we were able to have fruitful discussions. Splitting up into groups for some portions of it was really effective as well because everyone had time to discuss their projects/speak their thoughts on the weeks readings. No weaknesses!
 The syllabus for this course is pretty well structured. The readings are engaging and cover a variety of topics from differing authors, offering breath and depth to the topic. The field assignments were a nice change from standard written assignments. Although feedback was given on each assignment, it could perhaps be more specific with suggestions of improvement beyond just adding detail. I believe this course could qualify for a WR credit, in which case I would definitely expect more tailored feedback to the weekly assignments. Because the weekly write-ups are only 1-3 pages, a more clear set of guidelines at the beginning of the semester would help clear up what to prioritize within it, and to establish the level of rigor that is expected.
 strengths: how personalized it can be for each student, the amount of dialogue and small group discussion while also covering a lot of material, assignments in different mediums so that it can play to each student's strengths, class structure felt fairly collaborative and flexible, a couple scheduled individual meetings with prof and TF were nice weaknesses: there was just too much work (at least for now during COVID) even after kristin and professor barraclough made adjustments, didn't get to see much of the work the rest of the class was doing on their personal sites until the last class, sometimes felt like the expectations and feedback for the papers we turned in were unclear
 A strength in this course is definitely the intimate seminar format. I learned a lot from class discussions and break out room discussions with my classmates.
 While I appreciated the broader overview of the field provided by the readings, the discussions were often lacking and often just felt like we were being asked to summarize or restate the main points of the readings.
