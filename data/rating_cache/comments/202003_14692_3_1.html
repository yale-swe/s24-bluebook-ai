Q:

Among the practices in this course geared toward learning during the COVID-19 pandemic, which succeeded and which could be improved? Please comment on course practices that allowed for virtual engagement.

 Prof Wittenstein did a great job.
 I loved how hard he worked to get the in person component going!
 I really appreciated the opportunity to attend live, in-person lectures as it was my only in-person class. This helped my engagement in the course tremendously and I always looked forward to attending class in-person.
 I really liked how Professor Wittenstein used class time for discussion rather than pure lecture. This was especially helpful during the pandemic since Zoom lectures can get boring very quickly. Because students were encouraged to talk, I felt more engaged, and the class ended up being like a leminar. Professor Wittenstein recorded a 20-30 min-long lecture for us to watch before each class period. Each 75 min class period was then split into 2 parts. The first 55 min were for class discussion. We reviewed the material from the pre-recorded lecture/readings, asked questions about that material, or answered new discussion questions posed by the professor. In the final 20 min we broke out into discussion sections with the TAs. Additionally, this class was cross-listed as a graduate school course, so there was a socially distanced, in-person option with Professor Wittenstein. Virtual students could still participate since everyone, even if they were sitting in the lecture hall, logged onto the class Zoom call. I unfortunately could not attend the in-person lectures, but I think that was a nice option to have. Being in-person is always more engaging than a being online. I plan to suggest this to all of my professors: professors should encourage a culture of cameras on. For this class, everyone had their cameras on at the beginning of the semester. By the end, however, only a handful did, and this made the class less engaging. As a student, I know how desirable it is to turn off the camera during a Zoom call and just listen to others talk. However, I also know that when you do this, it is so easy to just zone out and maybe do other things on the computer or walk around the room. Im definitely a more active listener and participant when my camera is on because I feel that others are aware of my presence, kind of like when youre sitting in a lecture hall. I know that professors cant force students to turn on their cameras, but maybe if professors ask students to do so at the beginning of every single lecture, more students will feel encouraged or compelled to do so. Maybe the constant reminder will make turning on cameras into a habit. As soon as one student turns off his/her camera, everyone starts doing it, so my hope is that professors can try to stop this trend.
 This class was the most accomodating for COVID and also had the most realistic expectations (see earlier commente). Some classes think: overworking=learning a lot. But that is not the case here. Students learned a lot without having to sacrifice being overworked! The class had manageable readings, relatively short pre-recorded lectures, and meaningful live discussions. Additionally, the class was organized immensely well. First off, all the readings were posted on Canvas way before the first live meeting, so you could download them and get a feel for the content. Additionally, the lecture slides and lecture videos were posted in sections, so maybe 3-4 weeks of lecture material would get uploaded at a time. These uploads were very organized and timely, somtimes 2-3 weeks in advance! This gave students a lot of flexibility and agency which was great for time management. This class was the most organized class I've ever taken; and this is especially important considerint the uncertainities of COVID.
 Zoom breakout rooms each class made it much more interactive
 Virtual office hours and Zoom course set up were great for this course.
 Pre-recorded + live session discussion was good model; enjoyed breakout sessions at the end of class (kept me engaged)
 This class navigated the COVID situation better than any I experienced or heard of. I especially appreciated the hybrid synchronous/asynchronous model, which additionally allowed for lecture time to include more student engagement and for discussion sections to be conducted during class time.
 I thought the course was successful in adapting to the pandemic, I especially appreciated the opportunity to participate in in-person lectures. Although it was hard to hear the in-person students while online, I thought Professor Wittenstein did a nice job restating each question and making the combination of in-person/virtual lecture as efficient as possible.
 The split-class format made engagement difficult as sometimes it felt like there were two parallel classes happening at once.
 Prof. Wittenstein did a good job making the course a hybrid in-person and virtual lecture. Students in person and remote could pay attention the same, I just think that when students in-person speak, Prof. W could turn his mic off and the student could turn theirs on, because that would prevent Prof. W from having to repeat the question or comment.
 I really thought that Professor Wittenstein made a great effort to engage members of the course through the pandemic - he offered a hybrid in person model which allowed some students to actually go into lecture. However, I thought that his use of breakout groups could be improved - there was never really time to discuss key course concepts and they didnt work well in a hybrid model.
 Prerecorded lectures for preparation were a total hit, we loved them. Also, I liked the inclusion of discussion sections every day to keep us engaged.
 Professor Wittenstein did a great job adapting the course to learning during the pandemic. The pre-recorded lectures were a great way to communicate information, allowing students to watch them on their own schedule. This meant that we could do a more seminar format during lecture. The only piece of feedback that I would have about learning during the pandemic is on the format of the exams. Both the midterm and final were take home essays with a 48 hour window to complete the exam, but Professor Wittenstein said to not use the entire time (don't pull an all nighter etc.) and that it was meant as a curtesy. While I appreciate the additional time and know that it only comes from good intentions on Professor Wittenstein's part, this creates an issue for students who have many things going on during those 48 hours. For example, due to how my schedule fell this semester, I had every single one of my classes within that 48 hour window, which meant I was working on the essay during the night, right before it was due. I felt like I was not able to spend as much time on the midterm as other students in the course. Some alternatives have worked well in other virtual classes of mine. One is to have the exam be available for 48 hours, but restrict the amount of time students can work on it (I believe this is an option through canvas). This gives students necessary flexibility but still standardizes amount of time spent on the course. Alternatively, give a longer time window, perhaps even a week, to complete the exam. Again, I have to stress that Professor Wittenstein worked extremely hard to adapt this course to the pandemic and I know that this 48 hour take home came from a place of understanding.
 I really appreciated the breakout rooms towards the end of each class session as it allowed for us to discuss in smaller groups the larger topics from lecture and the readings.
 During the breakout group sections we never really had enough time to fully devote our attention to the week's memo or Canvas questions, let alone discuss what had been brought up in class earlier, but this is really a function of the pandemic's influence on the course design. Otherwise, the pre-recorded lectures were a great addition, and I thought the structure of the midterm and final was very fair.
 Most definitely the hybrid concept was a success. For virtual engagement, I thought breakout rooms were executed well, although my breakout room never had enough time to cover both the memos and the topic posed in class.
 Due to mostly online learning, the course was switched to having about a 20-30min lecture pre-recorded to watch sometime before the scheduled class time. Then, during class time, you could attend in-person or virtually, and it was formatted like something between a lecture and a full class discussion. While this might sound hectic, it was *very* well fascinated, and allowed us to talk more about concepts that we were interested or confused about. I really enjoyed this format, and I felt it made the content a lot easier to understand and made the live portion of the class all the more interesting.
