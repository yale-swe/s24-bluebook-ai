Â The main strengths of the course were the overall curriculum, the interesting problem sets, the reasonable exams, and the strong instruction. The course was very well organized, with nicely-typed notes slides and carefully-planned examples to illustrate the theoretical concepts. It provided a thorough yet broad introduction to theoretical computer science, which is a field not often covered in detail in earlier (lower-numbered) courses. Even with a more theoretical/mathematical background, I found the proofs and thought-processes from this class to be eye-opening and very thought-provoking. Along those lines, I always loved doing the problem sets, which often felt somewhere between logic puzzles and formal proofs. They perfectly reinforced concepts from class, with most of the problems being extensions of examples from class that were still relatively challenging. Meanwhile, while weekly, they were not very long, only taking somewhere between 4 and 8 hours each week. Similarly, since the class was virtual, the exams were a take-home format in which we had 48-72 hours to complete them; however, they were only meant to take 2-3 hours, a far more generous and reasonable approach to virtual exams than many other courses. Finally, Professor Bridy himself was exceptional, providing an amusing and relaxed atmosphere and being very open to questions, whether live in class or via email. Unlike most classes which are scrambling to finish in time, he usually finished a few minutes early, and the problem sets related to the previous week of class, so they could be finished as soon as they were released. Meanwhile, his actual teaching style was excellent, with theoretical explanations followed by clarifying examples that related directly to the homework. It was honestly a breath of fresh air compared to the normal confused lectures followed by partially unrelated homeworks of many STEM classes. Furthermore, Professor Bridy taught in a very calm yet engaging way, so class always felt like a nice reprieve from the stresses of life (and 2020). The main weakness of the class was spotty grading, as people with very similar answers received drastically different grades depending on how they explained it. The desired level of detail was very unclear, and overall it seemed quite random. Maybe the rubrics could have been improved, or more grading could have been done by the professor, but this was definitely a source of stress for a lot of people.
