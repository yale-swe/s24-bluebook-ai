2020-Fall LITR 028 01 Medicine and the Humanities

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 13842 Term: 202003

Back12345Forward
 It was extremely eye-opening in connecting unexpected aspects of literature/philosophy with STEM and medicine. It was very engaging and Dr. Morrison one of the best/most understanding professors I've had so far.
 It's discussion based which was great, but I wish we had more opportunities for assignments.
 Strengths: discussion format, the workload was very reasonable, definitely a breath of fresh air in comparison to my other courses. Weaknesses: There is a lot of reading (but definitely manageable and rewarding). Oftentimes the philosophical readings are hard to understand, but thats why the discussions are there to help you understand and ask questions! I don't really consider this a weakness, but if you are not fond of reading and do not see yourself dedicating time to do so, I would not recommend this course for you.
 The course contains a lot of interesting readings and topics that will change how you think about what it means to be a doctor. The lectures/seminars were always very interesting and engaging. Numerous authors, professors, and other experts in their respective fields were invited to speak. Even if you're not a pre-med, the course covers broadly applicable ideas that can be of use to anyone.
 This class was extremely engaging; the literature that Dr. Morrison selected for us to read was extremely interesting and made for thought-provoking class discussions. I also really enjoyed getting to meet the authors of many of the works we had read, from Dr. Lisa Sanders to Dr. Henry Marsh. I think the one weakness of the class was the length of lectures; though Dr. Morrison's lectures were extremely informative and stimulating, my one comment was that they ran a little long sometimes. There were a few days where the entire class period was taken up by lectures and the students didn't really have a chance to engage with the material. Perhaps those lectures could be shortened a little bit so as to leave some more room for discussions during class.
 Strengths: Dr. Morrison. I have never seen someone who is so passionate about what they're doing. He started off his emails with "hello dears," which always warmed my heart. He was always so excited about class and to see us and to share his thoughts. It was a much needed break from the stresses of all my other classes. He also brought in special guests and it was so cool to have the authors of the books we read come visit us and answer our questions. The workload was VERY fair, and his grading was also VERY fair. Weaknesses: Sometimes the lectures at the beginning class were a bit long, so they cut into discussion time.
 StrengthsDr. Morrison is a gem. He's such a caring professor and puts a lot of effort into making each class memorable. The readings were also super interesting and Dr. Morrison invited some incredible guest lecturers, like the legendary neurosurgeon Dr. Henry Marsh and Dr. Lisa Sanders, the creator the NY Times Diagnosis column. Weaknessessometimes the discussions felt slightly circular (although this may be exacerbated by the zoom format)
 The course's strengths lie in its engaging readings, Dr. Morrison's lectures (you can clearly tell he has put many hours of effort into researching and organizing the topics he presents), and the wonderful discussions in class each week. I learned so much from the perspectives of other students, and Dr. Morrison always guided discussions masterfully. Its weaknesses are few and far between. The only thing that comes to mind are the weeks that had a heavier reading load, but the readings were always so interesting that even higher page counts never seemed like a burden. This class was wonderful in so many ways it is hard to quantify them all - I just wish I could take it again next semester!
 There was a lot of reading! Nevertheless, it was definitely engaging. I started reading everything 1 month before the term started, and taking the time to think things through for myself was really valuable. I will say sometimes we didn't have much of an opportunity to talk about what we had read because there was so much other stuff to say/talk about.
