Â Strengths are that you receive a wide and varied breadth of information, and I felt like I learned a lot about French writers and artists who were looking to the Middle East for inspiration. Weaknesses: I don't know if it's because this semester was particularly difficult, but this class sucked out my soul. Be warned: the readings are all a bunch of French white guys who don't know what they're talking about, and it's aggravating to have to read stereotypes week after week without any critical examination. I felt like the professors saw no problem with this given the way that they taught the course. There was hardly any interrogation of the power dynamics between France and the Middle East; the professors seemed to suggest that they were historically on an equal playing field even though colonization happened because "France borrowed some things from the Middle East too." It felt like a very tone-deaf class to me personally, and I wish that I had known beforehand that we were just going to read a bunch of white French men. When I say that this class made me want to never pick up another book by a white French man, I mean it. If I never hear Voltaire's name again, it'll be too soon. I would rather drink ten jugs of Yale Spa Water mixed with 5-Hour Energy than take this class again. I would rather lick ten people's face masks than take this class again. I would rather eat ten copies of the book Les Miserables page by page than sit through this class again.

Â This class had quite a few strengths, but on the other end, quite a few weaknesses -- however, I think a lot of the weaknesses stem from the Zoom format, and that this course was taught by two brilliant professors who seemed to be at odds rather frequently. The selection of readings was fantastic -- never did I know there was just so much stuff to read about French people and the Orient. When Madame Sanjabi actually had time to speak, I held onto every single word she said. And yet the breadth of the reading list also inhibits any great deal of close reading or analysis, because you spend anywhere from half a class to only one class discussing one particular author or one particular work, to the detriment of actually deeply learning about or analyzing the piece. Both professors Sanjabi and Girard are brilliant, without a doubt, perhaps of the most brilliant at this university, and so listening to them speak about these writings was a joy and a delight. And yet there was little room for discussion, if at all, and when there was room, no one had anything to say, because we had not adequately addressed the readings. Girard teaches in a very 'French style,' wherein she lectures for the majority of the class; Sanjabi is the opposite, she asks questions, provides a little context, and entertains a discussion. The class time was unevenly divided between the two professors, so I spent most of the time bored or not paying attention, simply because we never really got into the meat of these readings. Travel journals and travel writings are incredibly difficult to ground oneself in, and then being asked to write about them in paper assignments is a monumental task, one that I felt wholeheartedly unprepared for. This class could have been so much more, and yet I felt like I missed out. The level of engagement was fairly low, with a lack of participation to boot -- but also, there was nothing to say, nothing to ask. Class also frequently went 10-15 minutes over time, which could be quite frustrating. We also spent waaaay too much time going over the biographical details of the authors/writers, and too little time actually discussing the texts. Sanjabi got cut off on a few occasions, so we didn't even discuss some of the poems we read; and frankly, I lost interest during those periods, which was almost every class. I took away bits and pieces from each class, and it pains me to say, but I don't think I gained a lot from each class.
