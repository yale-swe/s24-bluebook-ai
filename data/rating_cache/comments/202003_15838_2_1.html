Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

 The strengths of the course were the following: We read a mix of secondary and primary sources; we read current and older (20th c.) scholarship; we looked at different kinds of evidence and discussed their implications; we also enjoyed four guest lectures throughout the term. One weakness was that Professor Manning tended to repeat the material of the readings during lecture. Also, some classes were pure lecture while others were all discussion the balance felt off. Part of this was due to the fact that we didn't have section, so Professor Manning was essentially combining a lecture with a section every week.

 Strengths: in class seminars were recorded. Yes, thats it Weaknesses: disorganized, unclear and harsh in terms of grading. In class discussions have very little to do with the assignments. Half the time we were behind on discussing the readings in class.
 Strengths: Professor Manning's live lecture format which allows students to ask questions in real time as the lecture is happening (something not typical of the other history in-person live lectures I've taken pre-pandemic) Weaknesses: Not all the reading was covered in the class, so I would recommend lessening the reading load per class.
 The course material itself diverges from standard historical thinking sometimes and encourages students to change their pre-conceived notions of the ancient world. Its all very interesting, and the promotion of integrating scientific analysis with conventional history might be good for anyone aspiring to study history. The professors expertise in Ptolemaic Egyptian history also makes some of the material very new for experienced classicists. His guest lecturers also represent some of the most important minds working on the field at this moment. That said, the class can sometimes strawman conventional conceptions of the ancient economy too much, obsessing with Moses Finley when its clear all modern scholarship have abandoned his view. I must also say that his final assignment, a prcis that also needed to include 7 other sources as well as our own opinions, was a disjointed and self-contradictory request. I couldnt figure out what he really wanted there.
 I think the biggest strength of the course is simply the subject matter, which is totally unique and fascinating even among Yale history courses. It really does give you a new perspective on antiquity and arguably even modern economics too. Professor was also very nice and accommodating considering the circumstances of Zoom learning and COVID. Also, the writing workload is relatively quite low, with only 3 papers for the whole semester. Weaknesses: I think the class was too big given the format. We had 30-40 people but the class format was largely seminar-style, so as a result it felt a little difficult to participate in the discussions. Feedback on the essays was not very thorough, although this is hardly surprising given the large class size and no TA's. Also, at times I think the big picture of the class was unclear, as it was easy to get lost since we were considering many different economies from different regions and time period.
 Strength: interactive, exposure to various fields Weakness: lack of independent research-oriented learning/project (thus discussions generally limited to sources provided by Prof)
