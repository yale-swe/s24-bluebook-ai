 The class is among the easiest I've taken at Yale, but it still follows the standard economics major curve, leading to a severe rushing problem (which you learn about in the class - LOL). Professor Mendelsohn claims that because students, especially at Yale, will spend as much effort as they can competing for the top grades of the class, he does not seem to care as much for students' wellbeing (he blatantly refused to drop the lowest problem set grade that most other classes allow to help lower students' stress if they have one really busy or off week). Thus, the class is ironically even more competitive to get the highest grades.
 This course covers quite interesting material, but seems to gloss over the details of many aspects of environmental management. Since classes were only 50 minutes long, it felt like we would talk about 10 concepts per class, with one or two slides dedicated to each concept. Lectures moved at a very slow pace, but it seemed like we moved through the material too rapidly. Furthermore, some concepts taught in this class are at odds with established climate science and other economists. For example, we learned that 4 degrees Celsius was the optimal warming target, and that climate change would affect only a small chunk of the economy. Professor Mendelsohn also claims that $5-10 is the optimal price for carbon, although other economists estimate numbers at least 10 times as high. I do realize that part of taking a course with a researcher in this field is understanding the different approaches that they take to problems. However, at times, it seemed that even the TAs seemed skeptical about the material that Professor Mendelsohn was teaching.

 The strength of the course is the material itself. The course covers important and interesting material while providing a economics perspective on issues we hear about a lot. The course is at its best during lecture and discussion section when the passion of the professor and TAs comes through. Professor Mendelsohn clearly cares about this class a lot, and same goes for the TAs. The weaknesses of the course mainly have to do with course administration, specifically in three areas. First, communication was not easy. The lack of a piazza for the course made it difficult to have questions answered, and email oftentimes did not substitute well. I'd imagine that TAs got multiple questions a lot, and they sometimes seemed frustrated when they received a question more than once (which is totally understandable) but could be alleviated with a course-wide piazza. Second, the structuring and wording of assessments was poor. Oftentimes, it was difficult to understand what a question was asking. This problem was exacerbated by the lack of good communication channels and by a lack of dedicated time to go over practice problems. Some discussion sections went over practice problems while others didn't, and we never went over practice problems in lecture. I think it was good to have discussion sections and lecture cover material instead, but maybe a recorded video of a practice problem would be good. Third, grading was inconsistent. I rarely got good/clear feedback in a timely manner from the class, and I seemed to lose points for things I didn't think warrant a point deduction. We were told at certain points that some TAs carried most of the grading burden on their own, so I hope that's changed in the future so that students can get clearer feedback quicker.

This happened a lot during the final exam and it causes unnecessary stress.
