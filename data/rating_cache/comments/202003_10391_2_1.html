Â This course has so much potential because it covers such an interesting period and civilization. That said, there was what seemed to be a complete lack of organization and a disconnect between the professors and students. For example, the professors refused to post lecture slides prior to the lectures out of fear that it would incentivize students to skip lecture. This made no sense to me, and I found it to be quite the opposite. I would rather skip the live lecture and watch the recording with the slides in front of me than watch the live lecture with no slides. It made lectures incredibly difficult to follow. Additionally, there was no clear purpose to each lecture, as they all went off on tangents about specific artifacts.
Â The class was fairly straightforward in terms of what it required of students. Although lectures at times felt haphazard, with the information in lecture not always aligning with what the professors wanted us to internalize about a given topic.
Â Weakness, some of the lectures seem to cover events randomly. As a student who has never taken an Egyptian history class before I found I learnt a lot more from the textbook than the lectures as the information in lectures was not always chronological. It took me a while to wrap my head around the chronology of the events we covered. I think the course also tries to cover way too much history within such a short class. We covered nearly 3,500 years worth of history within the class, and were expected to know the information and historical context of most of the kings, monuments, and events from this timeline which was very difficult to remember for the final exam.
Â The content of the class itself is really interesting, and the lectures were quite pleasant (despite the fact that they completely lacked organization). I looked forward to going to class each week, and I could feel the passion of the professors and appreciated their dedication to teaching us. The papers we wrote also felt feasible and were not graded too harshly. That being said, the class would have maintained this pleasant nature had it not been for the final exam. This consisted of several essay questions, and although the exam on the day-of was not terrible, the preparation for it was a huge undertaking given the chaotic manner in which the class had been taught throughout the semester. In order to improve this aspect of the class, I would say that the professors should consider making the exam open note, as I do not think my memorization of the topics aided in my understanding of ancient Egyptian history. It would also be more fair, in my opinion, to give us less potential essay questions on the study guide. While this guide was super helpful and definitely necessary, preparing for all fourteen essay questions (when only about 5 were on the exam) seemed like a waste of time after the exam was done. Going back to the lectures and class content, I think the class could be improved by moving chronologically through the course or thematically. The professors attempted to do both first giving us a whirlwind tour through all 4,000 of Egyptian history followed by scattered thematic lectures.
