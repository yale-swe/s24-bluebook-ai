Â The class is solid the whole way through. The semester is paced well and you feel like you really know the topics introduced. However, and this is a commentary on the Spanish program as a whole, percentages for exams must be changed. Fluency is not built in a day, and the idea that high stakes testing is necessary to learning a language is just wrong. It adds unnecessary stress and isn't truly a signifier of a student's ability. Most other language departments, like Hebrew and French, rather have small tests spaced out throughout the semester. This would be much better Spanish's current program.
Â The strengths of this course was definitely the professors and the overall structure of class periods. The material was organized and presented in a manner that was interesting, easy to follow, and conducive to analysis. Further, we were allowed ample collaboration time during class to engage with our peers and to develop conversation skills. The weaknesses of this course was that there wasn't too much of a lecture component so most of the class was just spent in discussion, which was much more enjoyable and still productive, but there were certain topics that I wish would've been explained a little more comprehensively. This could be improved by making sure there is a set lecture time for each topic during the beginning of class complete with Powerpoints.

Â Two strengths of this course were (1) the inclusion of brief peer discussions, such as through breakout rooms, and (2) the inclusion of mini interviews with special guests. The peer discussions helped students stay engaged throughout what was, by nature, a long class session, and they additionally helped create a sense of community among the class. This community was especially appreciated given the isolation of remote learning. Moreover, the interviews with special guests introduced students to new aspects of Latin American culture and challenged the students to actively listen and communicate. However, weaknesses of this course included rapid pacing and a lack of in-class feedback. Given the accelerated nature of summer courses, the rapid pacing is not necessarily something that can be fixed, but it nevertheless caused me to rely on short-term memory rather than truly understanding the material. I recommend that more external listening activities (such as the mini interviews), become incorporated into the curriculum, as these activities challenge students to use their knowledge of material on the spot. I also believe that students grow accustomed to the way in which their professors speak over time, so being introduced to other voices, accents, and paces of speaking again challenges the students to rely on more than short-term memory. Furthermore, I wish I would have gotten more feedback in order to improve my learning. Though I received excellent feedback on my written assignments, I received much less feedback on my communication skills during class, and this class time is pertinent to the goodor badspeaking habits that I develop.
