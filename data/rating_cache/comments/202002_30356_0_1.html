2020-Summer ASTR S120 02 Galaxies and the Universe

Q:

What knowledge, skills, and insights did you develop by taking this course?

ID: SU100 CRN: 30356 Term: 202002

123Forward
 I have learned a basic understanding of cosmology and the story of our galaxy as well as neighboring galaxies. I've been given insight to the many celestial objects that exist within the different galaxies, including stars, planets, moons, pulsars, and so much more.
 I learned a lot about astronomy in this course: the early understanding of the solar system and universe, stellar properties and evolution, black holes, neutron stars, white dwarfs, galaxies, the Big Bang Theory and misconceptions associated with it, dark matter (or at least, our current understanding of it), among other things. I also learned about various scientists and their contributions, as well as the scientific techniques we have available and how astronomical discoveries are generally made.
 I learned about many basic ideas and theories in physics and astronomy. I also learned a lot specifically about science and research of stars, galaxies, and the universe.
 I enjoyed having an accessible kind of STEM class on an interesting topic. I got to review some chemistry and physics concepts, and tie them together through the lens of astronomy. Now if any space-related news come sup, I have a general reference point for what it means.
 A crazy combination of all the sciences, astronomy is! And this course really touched it all in a well-done way. Physics, chemistry, geometry, mathematics, geology, etc etc. I think it really helped build my confidence and passion to get a taste of all of this over the course of ASTR S120.
 From this course, I gained an introductory knowledge of astronomy.
 This class, at least the Summer Session Zoom version of it, is an example of not simply failed but misguided pedagogy. The professor and TAs showed no interest in constructive feedback and seemed more concerned with punishment. Commentary on homework assignments was sparse and sometimes nonexistent, often with many points deducted and no explanation given. The professor claimed toward the beginning of the class that the bi-weekly homework assignments and weekly quizzes were intended as useful tools for mastering the material and addressing areas of confusion. Laughable. A problem set assigned at the beginning of the second week of the class was returned the week of the final. That is to say, of the 9 problem sets assigned over the course of five weeks, only 2 (the simplest, least significant assignments of the bunch, material which would have little or no relevance for the remainder of the class) had been graded by the Monday before the final exam. If you prefer percentages (I know the professor and TAs certainly do) that's 22.2% of the total number of problem sets assigned for the class. For baseball fans, that's batting .222, ripe for a benching or demotion. Total dereliction of their most basic responsibility to the class, or the "audience" as the professor seemed fond of saying. Nice to know we were all spectators to 8 dull hours of introductory science for 5 weeks of our blissful quarantine summer. O joy, o rapture. The format of the quizzes was another example of the backward pedagogy of this backward class: 15 multiple choice and short answer questions, entered directly on Canvas, automatically graded and spit back out as a percent. Let's do the math: 15/15 = 100%, 14/15 = 93.3%, 13/15 = 86.6%, 12/15 = 80%. 3 automatically graded short answer questions wrong, no work shown, and your grade's down 20%. That's punitive. Made a power of ten error? On a homework question maybe that'll be a point off (not a percentage point, a single point, a fraction of a percent). On a quiz? Try 7 percentage points. There is no room for error and the professor is a terribly ungenerous grader. That cannot be overstated. You'll have to turn your desperate appeals elsewhere. Higher spiritual powers, perhaps. You'll need their guidance through these treacherous 5 weeks. To compensate for their completely delinquent grading, the professor and TAs staged nothing short of a last minute carpet bombing of the problem sets (what ever could go wrong when you're racing to grade 40 * 7, that's 280 for those keeping score at home, assignments in a single weekend?). Talk about an extreme distraction in the last and most stressful week of class, in which we had two more assignments due, daily lectures covering vast swathes of new material to attend, and a final at the end of the week to prepare for. Outrageous. Several all-you-can-ask, two-and-a-half hour review sessions were scheduled during the last week of class to make up for the grading delinquency. The TA overslept the most convenient and earliest one, the one that would have been the most helpful and given the most time for internalizing clarifications and review, and missed the entire session. So, let's summarize: knowledge is not the point of this class, whatever minor insights you might come away with after enduring Zoom-engendered eye torture and the professor's droning bass (which is monotony incarnate) and frequent, strange, and protracted asides about the media and the Church's proven historical affinity for all things scientific at lecture will be like the relief felt after vomiting from a bad bout of food poisoning, that is, sudden release at the point of near-surrender.

High school courses (that I have taken) have a lot more structure, and information such as derived formulas are often handed to me. I learned to put in a lot of effort to construct basic things such as deriving equations and day to day summaries. I attended nearly every discussion session, office hours, and also joined a small student-led study group that pushed me to take on both course-related and social challenges. In terms of what I learned from the course content, I was always drawn to the Astronomy field because of my fascination with the interaction of art and science. This class helped me define the parts about Astronomy that correlated with my interest.
