2021-Spring MATH 225 01 Linear Algebra & Matrix Theory

 Strengths: The professor generally does a pretty good job explaining , although sometimes he can sound a little confusing. The psets are pretty interesting and helpful with understanding. Weaknesses: I think the course was a bit rushed towards the end. The professor tried to get through too much material, so we ended up not having enough time to actually understand the materials before moving on. He also had to upload multiple addendums to better explain the material we could not get to in lecture, which sometimes made things more confusing. Also, it would be great if the professor can do a better job at explaining the purpose/goal of the material before we get into it. Sometimes it feels like I'm just copying down whatever theorem/proposition he puts down without knowing how it would lead to anything useful. (Also just a note, having the exam worth 40% was a bit concerning...)
 The strength of this course is probably the support provided by the peer tutors. This course had many weaknesses. First, the course was poorly laid out throughout the semesterwe spent over half of the semester going over minutiae and then had to rush through 4 chapters of the book without much time to process. Second, the discussion sections were of little help, since it was difficult to follow the graduate student's train of thought. This is also usual with the main instructorthey both tended to overcomplicate things, and I believe it is why we didn't get to learn more practical and essential aspects of linear algebra. I feel that we were thrown with a thousand theorems and definitions disconnected from the broader picture in math, let alone in other subjects. Finally, the final exam was in a format very different from what we practiced throughout the semester, a situation that added unnecessary stress to a complicated semester. I don't understand what's the point of memorizing over 170 minor theorems and their proofs. Is memorizing helpful for understanding? No. Will students remember even 20% of those theorems one week after the exam? No. Oh, I forgot. The feedback on our PSets after mid-semester stopped coming until a very short time before the final.

 Strengths: there were a lot of resources in this course (office hours primarily) that made it difficult to "fail" this course and also some addendums on certain topics that filled in blanks. I found that doing proofs in class often inspired "aha!" moments which were really insightful. Also, I found the way that the professor explained concepts very clear. Whenever I struggled to recall a concept I'd written in my notes, I'd go back and watch the lecture to hear how he explained it. I liked how the professor tried his best to engage student suggestions and answers during a lecture too. Weaknesses: this class felt a little depersonalized to me. I didn't feel like I had built a strong community among the students taking this course, other than appreciating the ULAs and instructors for all their work. For me, this is something I attribute mainly to COVID-era learning, but it made this course much harder. I also often struggled to actually do computational problems even though I understood the concepts, and I found that my TA session notes were often not very organized and helpful. Honestly, I think the second half of this course was tougher for me than the first half, and it felt like it went a little faster. My only other remark is that I sometimes felt that the professor assumed we knew more about notation/linear algebra than I actually did (I can't speak for others). My other classes used GradeScope and I would have preferred to have all my course submissions method be the same (I think GradeScope is often easier?) but the Ed Discussion was really well maintained. Overall, strengths outweigh weaknesses but this was a rewarding, albeit challenging course.

 The lectures and problem sets provided excellent motivation for learning the concepts; the Psets in particular were well-cultivated and provided intellectual challenges that cemented my understanding. The exams, however, were a bit of an uneven experience: the first exam had a rather bizarre format of being a 32-hour take home exam and ended up yielding a remarkably low average; I do appreciate, though, that the instructor took this into account and curved the exam. Overall, though, I thought it was a great course!

 This course was taxing beyond belief. The weakness of the course is the lack of help. The material is extremely hard, and our resources were very limited. Reading a textbook about math is not a proper addition to learning the material, as if you cannot understand it through a professor, reading about it will not help.  The strengths of this course include allowing students to drop their 2 lowest pset grades and allowing for late submission on 2 psets, and having the lectures recorded - mostly the structural things about the course. The weaknesses of the course center on how lectures are taught and the decisions made in how to communicate concepts. I did not find the teaching style of the professor to be particularly helpful - in particular, the lecture format involved little to no imagery and handwritten notes. Outside online resources taught me most of what I know conceptually, and helped me visualize and convince myself of things we only discussed abstractly in the course. Much of the teaching style was based on definitions and building upon those definitions to come to a conclusion in the proofs, but I can't say I understood the intuition behind the proofs. The class was a bit frustrating in this way. Especially if you are someone who has a surface level familiarity with mathematical notation, since that was a large part of proof writing and something lightly touched upon at the beginning of the course. I don't think the course had to be as confusing as it was, and highly recommend that if you take this course, to find a resource outside that you understand well. In terms of learning how to write proofs, I think the course could do so much better. As someone who never took a proof writing course before, I did not have an idea of what a proof could look like, and what it would require. I think it would be helpful to future students for the instructor to spend time at the beginning of the course outlining what a proof is, and giving different examples of proofs. While I understand that much of learning how to write proofs comes by practicing, without an idea of what different forms proofs can take, practice isn't particularly useful, and there is an unnecessary amount of self teaching on the basics of proof writing that must occur. Lastly, I'm not sure if this was just a result of the pandemic, but the response time for grading psets was very slow (at one point the past 4 psets hadn't been graded). That is something that could be improved.
 I think there could have been more one on one instruction between teacher and student, and the psets could have corresponded more to the tests. I thought the professor was very friendly and open to questions, but I do think that grading policies could have been more clear throughout.
