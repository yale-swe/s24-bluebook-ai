2020-Fall WGSS 325 01/ER&M 324 01 Asian Diasporas since 1800

Q:

What are the strengths and weaknesses of this course and how could it be improved?

(Your anonymous response to this question may be viewed by Yale College students, faculty, and advisers to aid in course selection and evaluating teaching.)

ID: YC403 CRN: 10311/10299 Term: 202003

 I thought that the pacing and weekly themes of this class were organized wonderfully. I especially appreciated the inclusion of various guest speakers in the curriculum. Professor Tran really cares about her students and provided helpful feedback throughout the semester, as well as during office hours. The course's flexibility with the final project also allowed for many creative final projects, which I appreciated. Can't think of any specific weaknesses, but perhaps more small-group activities during class or having a few students present on a reading or text each week would help with encouraging participation, as there were some moments where the discussion moved a little slowly.
 Strengths: Professor Tran is incredible, interesting readings, guest speakers, and engaging events. No weaknesses.
 Professor Tran was very clear in her expectations for the course, something extra helpful given the online format during the pandemic. The course also was the strongest I've taken in terms of its syllabus. Professor Tran pulled together so many different readings that all worked together so well in exposing us to key tensions in the field of Asian diaspora studies while also continuing to expand our thinking about different Asian diasporas.
 The strength of this course are the diverse selection of readings, the pedagogy of Professor Tran and the opportunities to engage meaningfully with guest lecturers. Each week our reading workload was very heavy, though no text was ever superfluous. The diversity of texts both in content (1800s to the present day, all of Asia, is a lot of time and space to cover) and in style (we read graphic novels, watch films, in addition to history and theory) kept me engaged all semester. Discussions rarely ran dry. Professor Tran always connected student insights with each other, with her own meaningful reflections and with the overarching structure of the lesson and semester. The guest lecturers included a talk with comedian Hari Kondobolu, a performance by comedian Kristina Wong, and an intimate roundtable with children's book author Minh Le and filmmaker-academic Cindy Nguyen. I laughed a lot during the semester, and was meaningfully entertained and intellectually engaged by these cultural creators. Student presentations, though important to adding additional content to the class, were difficult to stay focused on for 20 minutes of lecturing over Zoom. It may be beneficial to enforce a Zoom break (5 minutes off screen) after each student presentation.
 I loved how the seminar cultivated a sense of community. I found that all of our class discussions were deeply honest, and thought-provoking. The only suggestions I have to improve the class would be to include more central/west Asian literatures and more fictional/poetic texts.
 I really appreciated the breadth of literature we read in this class, covering Asian diasporas across the world. I especially liked the analysis of sources beyond the stereotypical academic non-fiction, such as documentary, comedy, art, literature, and my personal favorite - the graphic novels. The critical approach to diasporic studies was also very valuable, and I gained a lot from our discussions questioning the very definition of "Asian diasporas".
 The strengths in this class definitely came from class discussions as well as the outside of class engagements such as guest speakers! I think that this class pulled from a lot of different resources such as academic text/data, artists, guests outside of class, personal experience, research, etc. that provided a well rounded and full mode of learning that was both fun and engaging.
 Prof. Tran is the strength! She created a community that worked hard to learn together. I especially loved the guest speakers she brought in. She is one of the best seminar leaders I've had so great at responding to peoples' points and making sure all voices are heard. Not really any weaknesses, the syllabus is carefully curated and the assignments were all helpful. The class generally self-selects for students who consider themselves part of Asian diasporas, but genuinely anyone would benefit from understanding non-Eurocentric histories.
