 very philosophical aspects of catastrophe; social aspects also. how to think about catastrophe in the past, and what it means for the future.
 This is one of the coolest courses at Yale! A less-sexy title might be "History of Earth and Environmental Sciences," but it's so much more than that. You'll explore how science is intricately entangled with systems of capitalism, politics, and religion; questions about human and natural agency; conservation, preservation, sustainability, and management of resources; environmentalism versus economics; the roots of human ecology; science as socially constructed (and deconstructed); the role of consensus and public opinion; and much more. This has perhaps been the course that has most often made me rethink what I previously understood about the world.
 I learned about the history of global catastrophes and prominent figures throughout the history of science.
 Professor Rankin approaches topics like science, religion, politics, environmentalism, etc. in a very different way compared to other classes here at Yale and demonstrates how largely this topics all intersect with each other. The way he approaches these topics really makes you think about each of these topics really function in your everyday life and really shape the way you may approach these kinds of topics now and in the future.
 I learned about the history of debates surrounding global catastrophes, centered around whether catastrophes exist and how we should deal with them. I learned about the relationship between science and religion, and science and government and economics. I also learned about a bunch of cool historical moments I'd never learn about anywhere else, like the age of the earth debates and the history of plate tectonics.
 A more nuanced understanding of the history of science, as well as conceptual tools for tackling scientific uncertainty and discussions of catastrophe.
 hard to summarize what the class teaches you in a title or course description, it isn't just about climate change or just about geology or just about the oil crisis, it's about how and why we go about trying to answer scientific questions.
 You very quickly learn that this course was not worth taking because every unit is more or less a reiteration of obvious facts about science, backed by gratuitously long readings that are discussed in pointless sections. The minimal feedback you get is arbitrary and punitive.
 Interconnectedness of science, politics, economics/capitalism, social movements. Stylistic history of several world catastrophes including peak oil, overfishing, climate change, ozone layer, etc. A greater than expected emphasis on geology, religion, more mundane topics than the course title might indicate. Heavily American/European centric.
 You learn a lot about how to analyze the history of science and you come to understand that this history is not linear. We go into the "cartoons" and bedtime stories of science and the narrative that this class helps weave is very interesting.
 I learned about the history of catastrophe but also the politics of science.
 Learned about world catastrophes, Earth's history, and current modern-day environmental issues
 Critical thinking of science and history together.
 Helped to develop the way I think about scientific history - something I had not though much about before this course.
 A greater understanding for history of environmental crises and more broadly, history of science.
 I learned about the history of geological study, climate study, and resource extraction among other topics in history. I developed analytical skills conducive to being critical of science (what constitutes it) and its relationship with other disciplines.
 The professor sounds like a nice person in the lecture videos, and Im sure hes a good person, but this class was so brutal. Brutal! The midterm is equivalent of 3-4 pages single spaced, and the final is equivalent of 5-7 pages single spaced. Writing all of this in one sitting is incredibly difficult. Week to week, the workload is heavy for a lecture. It is as demanding, if not more demanding, than seminars. If you take the class credit d it may not be too bad, but thats a strong maybe. Also, the grading is confusing, so it isnt always clear why you get points off.
 I gained a lot of knowledge about catastrophes and how people interpreted the science and what qualified as science. I also learned about the political and social roles of science. I also learned about the importance of different world-views and how that impacts science. The title is a little misleading - you don't really learn about specific catastrophes themselves but rather like how they influenced science and their connection to other things and people.
