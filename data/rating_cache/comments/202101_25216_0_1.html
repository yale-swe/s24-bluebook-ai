Additionally, I also gained considerable insight into the literature of feminist and queer ethnography by reading several ethnographies through the lens of dystopia and disaster, which in turn gave me the analytical skillset to analyze topics related to extinction, climate change, and how they intersect with settler colonialism and transnational economic systems.
Â I think this class was so helpful in learning about different perspectives and ways of imagining the world. Also, helping us establish critical skills in how to engage with the material and the traps of global capitalism (including methods like statecraft).
Â I learned broadly about the practice of ethnography through feminist and queer theory lenses. We read a text a week and discussed the themes, methods, and analyses in both small and big groups. We discussed the moral and ethical implications of doing anthropology in the wake of the violence on which the field is predicated. We also focused on dystopian realities and the alternatives in the midst of the constant end of the world.
Â Exposure to ethnographic texts, how to approach and study such texts, discuss difficult thematic topics in the content and think critically about anthropological work.
Â The course was divided into three units, each of which included a set of ethnographies that we used as occasions for hazarding answers to those questions that headlined each unit. The first unit, "Epistemologies," asked what kinds of forms of knowledge production are both responsible for the perpetual catastrophes in which we find ourselves, as well as inquired into the kinds of epistemologies that might resist it. The second unit, "Etiologies," asked how we might understand the varied causes of the various dystopias, catastrophes, and extinctions that the ethnographies we were reading depicted. The third unit, "Methodologies," asked about what kinds of methodologies are appropriate for ethnographers who are looking to conjure up speculative fabulations for a "world to come." The ethnographies we read covered subjects ranging from hunger in Brazil to US border policy to orangutan rehabilitation and so much more. Beyond these broad divisions, I learned so much from our collective readings and interpretations of each ethnography. Getting to read a full book per week instead of just selected chapters gave us the opportunity to really dissect what the ethnographies we were reading were trying to do, and how they succeeded or failed to achieve their goals. Professor Pepi also was able to get several of the ethnographers to come speak to our class, which was such a special experience.
